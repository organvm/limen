# Performance Rubric — Communication
*Draft rubric skeleton — for practitioner review. Practitioner designs the philosophy; this translates it into structure.*

> **Client:** [name]
> **As of:** [date]
> **Level:** [individual-contributor / manager / executive]

| Level | Beginning | Developing | Accomplished | Exemplary |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** | Messages are unclear or incomplete; requires follow-up to understand intent | Communicates clearly in most situations; occasional ambiguity | Expresses ideas clearly and concisely; tailors message to audience | Consistently communicates complex ideas with precision; raises the clarity standard for the team |
| **Active Listening** | Interrupts or fails to acknowledge others' input | Listens without interrupting; asks clarifying questions | Seeks to understand before responding; synthesizes others' input | Creates psychological safety for open dialogue; draws out quiet voices |
| **Written** | Written work contains errors or lacks structure | Writes clear, structured documents for routine communication | Produces polished written work suitable for external audiences | Writes persuasively and concisely at all levels (email, doc, deck) |
| **Cross-Functional** | Communicates only within immediate team | Engages cross-functional partners when necessary | Proactively aligns cross-functional stakeholders | Builds communication bridges that accelerate org-wide collaboration |
| **Feedback** | Receives feedback defensively or gives feedback rarely | Accepts feedback constructively; gives feedback when asked | Actively solicits feedback; gives timely, specific, actionable feedback | Cultivates a feedback-rich culture; models how to receive and give growth-oriented feedback |

## Practitioner Notes

- Rubrics are guides, not checklists. Manager judgment determines where an employee falls on each dimension.
- The rubric should be shared with the employee before the review period as a transparent standard.
- Evidence (specific examples) should support every rating.
