# Reference — Metabolic monitoring on a second-generation antipsychotic

*Office reference library · general clinical knowledge, true of anyone on this class of
medication · names no patient · not medical advice — the prescriber decides.*

Quetiapine (Seroquel) is a second-generation ("atypical") antipsychotic. Whether taken for
sleep, mood, or psychosis, the drug carries an **FDA hyperglycemia warning**: this class is
well documented to raise blood glucose, weight, and lipids, and can unmask or trigger
type-2 diabetes. Because of that, professional consensus attaches a **standing metabolic
monitoring schedule** to the medication — it is part of the standard of care, not optional,
and it is one of the things a busy patient *and a busy prescriber* most often let slip.

The office's job is to keep this schedule in front of the principal and his clinicians and
to track which items have actually been done.

## The ADA/APA monitoring schedule

| When | What should be checked |
|---|---|
| **Baseline** (at start) | Personal & family history (diabetes, heart disease); weight / BMI; waist circumference; blood pressure; **fasting glucose**; **fasting lipid panel** |
| **4 weeks** | Weight / BMI; (for quetiapine, olanzapine, clozapine: fasting lipids) |
| **8 weeks** | Weight / BMI |
| **12 weeks** | Weight / BMI; waist circumference; blood pressure; **fasting glucose or HbA1c**; **fasting lipid panel** |
| **Every 3 months** | Weight / BMI |
| **Annually** | The full panel (weight/BMI, waist, BP, fasting glucose or A1c, fasting lipids) |

## Why it matters here specifically

New, frequent night-time urination (nocturia) is a classic early sign of **high blood
sugar** — the body dumps excess glucose into the urine and pulls water with it (osmotic
diuresis). When nocturia appears *after* starting a drug with a known hyperglycemia signal,
the baseline + 12-week glucose checks stop being routine box-ticking and become the
specific test that could explain the symptom. This is checkable with a single fasting blood
draw.

## What the principal does with this

Nothing clinical on his own. He brings the schedule to whoever ordered the medication (or
to a primary-care doctor) and asks for the baseline labs that were missed and the 12-week
labs when they come due. **He never stops or changes the dose on his own** — any medication
change is routed through the prescriber.
