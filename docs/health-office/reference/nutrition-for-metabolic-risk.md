# Eating well on a weight-gaining medication

*General clinical reference. Names no patient. The office's KITCHEN department reads this to
set the *emphasis* of the meal plan. This is general healthy-eating guidance, not a
therapeutic diet — a tailored medical diet is set with a clinician or registered dietitian.*

---

## Why nutrition is load-bearing here

Second-generation antipsychotics (quetiapine among them) **cause weight gain in the
majority of patients**, and most of that gain lands **in the first 6–12 weeks** of
treatment — the same early window the SURVEILLANCE department is watching for the metabolic
panel. They also push blood sugar and lipids up. That makes the *first weeks* the highest-
leverage time to get eating dialed in, and it makes "I'm not thinking about what to eat"
a genuine clinical intervention, not a lifestyle nicety: the easier the right food is to
reach, the less the medication's metabolic pull wins.

## The emphasis the office uses

Guidance from the Royal College of Psychiatrists' *Positive Cardiometabolic Health
Resource* points lifestyle counselling at standard national healthy-eating guides (NHS Eat
Well / the equivalent of the USDA plate). The office distills that to a few rules it can
actually plan meals around:

1. **Protein and fiber at every meal.** Both blunt the blood-sugar spike and keep you full,
   which is the real defense against medication-driven appetite. Eggs, fish, poultry,
   beans, lentils, Greek yogurt; vegetables, fruit, whole grains.
2. **Lower the added sugar and refined carbs.** Sugary drinks first (they do the most
   damage for the least fullness), then white bread/pasta/pastry. Swap, don't suffer:
   whole grains, fruit for dessert, water/seltzer for soda.
3. **The plate method.** Half the plate vegetables, a quarter lean protein, a quarter whole
   grain or starchy veg. No counting required — it's a picture, not arithmetic.
4. **Simple and repeatable beats perfect.** A small rotation of easy meals you'll actually
   make defeats an elaborate plan you won't. The KITCHEN department optimizes for *low
   cooking effort*, because the goal is to remove the decision, not add a project.
5. **Steady timing.** Regular meals (vs. skipping then over-eating at night) keep blood
   sugar even and protect sleep — which loops back to the SLEEP department.

## What the office does with this

- Builds a **weekly menu** from a small rotation of simple, higher-protein/higher-fiber,
  lower-added-sugar meals, filtered around stated likes / dislikes / allergies.
- Generates a consolidated **grocery list** so the week is bought in one pass.
- Frames it all as a **starting rotation to refine**, and routes any therapeutic diet
  (e.g. if labs come back abnormal) to a clinician or dietitian.

## Where this stops

The office does not prescribe a medical diet, count calories for you, or treat an eating
disorder. If weight or labs warrant medication-level help (e.g. metformin or a GLP-1),
that is a clinician's call — the office surfaces the conversation, never the prescription.
