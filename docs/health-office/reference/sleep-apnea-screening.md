# Reference — Screening for obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)

*Office reference library · general clinical knowledge · names no patient · not medical
advice — a clinician evaluates and decides.*

Obstructive sleep apnea is the airway repeatedly collapsing during sleep. It is common,
under-diagnosed, strongly associated with excess weight — and very treatable. It matters to
this office for a specific reason: **OSA itself causes nighttime urination.** During apnea
the heart releases a hormone (atrial natriuretic peptide) that signals the kidneys to make
more urine, so people with untreated OSA often wake repeatedly to pee. So the same symptom
that points at blood sugar *also* points here, and both deserve to be checked.

## STOP-BANG — the standard screen

A widely used screening questionnaire. Each "yes" is a point:

- **S** — Snoring loudly (louder than talking / heard through a door)?
- **T** — Tiredness / daytime sleepiness most days?
- **O** — Observed to stop breathing / gasp / choke in sleep?
- **P** — high blood Pressure (or treated for it)?
- **B** — BMI over 35?
- **A** — Age over 50?
- **N** — Neck circumference large (collar >16–17 in)?
- **G** — male (Gender)?

**0–2 = low risk · 3–4 = intermediate · 5–8 = high risk.** A score of 3 or more is the
usual threshold to ask a clinician for a formal evaluation.

## What evaluation looks like

- A clinician reviews the screen and symptoms.
- A **home sleep apnea test** (a small device worn overnight at home) or an in-lab sleep
  study confirms it and grades severity.
- If positive, treatment (commonly CPAP, plus weight management) typically improves sleep,
  daytime energy, blood pressure — and often the nighttime urination itself.

## What the principal does with this

Raise it at the next medical contact and ask for a sleep-apnea evaluation or a home sleep
test. The office holds the screen and the symptom history so the conversation is two
minutes, not a cold start.
