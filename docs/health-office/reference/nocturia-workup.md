# Reference — Nocturia (waking repeatedly at night to urinate)

*Office reference library · general clinical knowledge · names no patient · not medical
advice — a clinician evaluates and decides.*

Nocturia means waking from sleep one or more times a night specifically to urinate. Waking
**4+ times a night** is significant: it fragments sleep, and it usually has a findable,
treatable cause. The office's role is not to diagnose it — it is to make sure the right
questions get asked at the next medical contact, in roughly the order a clinician would
work the differential.

## The differential — leading, checkable causes first

1. **High blood sugar (hyperglycemia / new diabetes).** Excess glucose spills into the
   urine and drags water with it (osmotic diuresis), so the bladder fills overnight.
   Especially relevant on a medication with a hyperglycemia signal (see
   [quetiapine-metabolic-monitoring](quetiapine-metabolic-monitoring.md)). **Checkable** with
   a fasting glucose and an HbA1c.

2. **Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA).** Apnea raises a heart hormone (ANP) overnight that
   tells the kidneys to make more urine, so OSA *itself causes nocturia* — and it travels
   with excess weight. Strongly worth screening when nocturia, snoring, and excess weight
   appear together. See [sleep-apnea-screening](sleep-apnea-screening.md). **Checkable** with
   a screening questionnaire → home or lab sleep study.

3. **Medication / fluid timing.** Some medications and evening fluid, caffeine, or alcohol
   load the overnight bladder. A new drug starting around the same time as the nocturia is
   a temporal clue worth flagging to the prescriber.

4. **Other** (less likely but a clinician rules them out): urinary or prostate causes,
   heart or kidney issues, diuretics.

## What a workup tends to look like

- A **fasting glucose + HbA1c** blood test (the blood-sugar question).
- A **sleep-apnea screen** (e.g. STOP-BANG) → a sleep study if it scores positive.
- A **medication review** with whoever prescribes the relevant drugs.
- Sometimes a simple **bladder/voiding diary** (the office can hold the nightly count).

The point worth holding onto: the **two leading causes here are both checkable with simple
tests and both very treatable** once found. This is not a "live with it" symptom.
