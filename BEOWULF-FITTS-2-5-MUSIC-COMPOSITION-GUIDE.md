# Beowulf Fitts 2–5: Music Arc Composition Guide
## Thematic Divisions & Scene Progression for Beowulf II–V

This document provides the structured scene breakdown and thematic analysis needed to compose music arcs for Beowulf's second through fifth divisions. Each fitt is presented with its dominant thematic force, major scene anchors, and emotional/dramatic arc.

---

## FITT 2: Grendel's Characterization & Eruption

### Dominant Thematic Force
**Primary:** Grief | **Secondary:** Fate, Wrath

### Scene Structure (3 major anchors)

**Scene 1: Grendel Hears the Joy of Heorot** [Opening]
- *Thematic focus:* Theological exile; envy as motive force
- *Narrative beat:* Establishes Grendel as the poem's antagonist through the lens of spiritual exclusion, not moral evil
- *Key detail:* Grendel dwells in the moors, descended from Cain, cursed to wander. He hears the nightly harp and creation-song from inside the golden hall. His motivation is defined by what he cannot possess.
- *Emotional tone:* Torment, envy, exclusion, the pressure of hearing joy you cannot enter
- *Music anchor:* Dissonance reflecting isolation; grinding, resentful harmonic territory; something outside the light

**Scene 2: The First Night Raid** [Middle]
- *Thematic focus:* Violence as release; catastrophe made concrete
- *Narrative beat:* Converts the threat established in Scene 1 into tangible devastation
- *Key detail:* Grendel seizes thirty sleeping thanes, slaughters them silently, carries bodies back to his lair. At dawn, the benches are emptied; the great hall is shocked into silence.
- *Emotional tone:* Violence, shock, sudden grief, the discovery of loss
- *Music anchor:* Crescendo into violence; percussive attack followed by descent into silence; the music of sleep broken

**Scene 3: Twelve Years of Unrelenting Reign** [Closing]
- *Thematic focus:* Duration as civilizational failure; Grendel as anti-king
- *Narrative beat:* Establishes the scale of suffering: not one attack but a decade-plus of occupation
- *Key detail:* Attacks continue nightly for twelve winters with no truce, no wergild, no remedy. Heorot stands empty after dark. Grendel rules the night; Hrothgar rules the day. Neither can dislodge the other.
- *Emotional tone:* Endurance, despair, the weight of accumulated loss, institutional collapse
- *Music anchor:* Sustained minor/diminished harmonic territory; the music of watching something continue year after year with no resolution

### Dramatic Arc
**Exclusion/Envy → Eruption → Endurance (Twelve Years of Occupation)**

The fitt moves from the psychological (why Grendel acts) through the violent (what he does) to the institutional (what it means for the Danes). The emotional center is Scene 3: not the moment of attack but the duration of suffering.

### Music Arc Potential
1. **Opening:** Dissonant isolation theme — Grendel outside the light, hearing joy he cannot enter
2. **Middle:** Crescendo into violence, then descent into silence — the night raid and discovery
3. **Closing:** Sustained minor/diminished reflection — twelve years of Grendel's rule, the hall emptied every night

### Dominant Force Registry
- **Wrath:** Grendel's eruption (Scene 2)
- **Grief:** The Danes' accumulated loss (Scenes 1, 3)
- **Fate:** The relentlessness of Grendel's occupation (Scene 3)

## FITT 3: Transition from Grendel to Beowulf's Summoning

### Dominant Thematic Force
**Primary:** Grief | **Secondary:** Law, Fate

### Scene Structure (3 major anchors)

**Scene 1: Hrothgar's Helpless Mourning** [Opening]
- *Thematic focus:* The exhaustion of human counsel; kingship hollowed by helplessness
- *Narrative beat:* Shifts focus from the monster to the victim—Hrothgar unable to sleep, unable to find counsel, unable to act
- *Key detail:* The aged king sits joyless and sleepless. His strongest warriors debate in council. All counsel proves useless. He cannot command the creature to cease.
- *Emotional tone:* Helplessness, aging, despair, the king isolated by his own inability to protect
- *Music anchor:* Dirge-like, exhausted—the sound of institutions failing, of counsel reaching its limit

**Scene 2: Heathen Vows at Idol-Shrines** [Middle]
- *Thematic focus:* Spiritual nadir; misplaced hope; the collapse of the Danes' own resources
- *Narrative beat:* The Danes turn to pagan shrines in desperation—the moment where human and divine counsel both fail
- *Key detail:* In despair, the Danes sacrifice at heathen temples, praying to the soul-slayer. The Beowulf-poet notes (with theological double-vision) that their counsel was wrong, that God's grace was absent, that they knew no better but they were lost.
- *Emotional tone:* Desperation, spiritual emptiness, misplaced faith, the texture of a civilization's resources exhausted
- *Music anchor:* Dissonant sacrifice sequence—something breaking down, structure failing, the acoustic threshold of form being breached

**Scene 3: News Reaches Beowulf in Geatland** [Closing]
- *Thematic focus:* The turn; hope rekindled; the hero answers un-summoned
- *Narrative beat:* The narrative pivots. The focus shifts from the Danes' helplessness to the hero's response. Word of Grendel crosses the sea.
- *Key detail:* Beowulf, the strongest man living, hears of the need and resolves to seek Hrothgar. He does not wait to be summoned; he answers an un-summoned need.
- *Emotional tone:* Resolution, hope rekindled, heroic calling, the opening of possibility
- *Music anchor:* Shift to a resolute, ascending heroic motif—something that suggests answering, hope beginning to kindle, the direction of aid

### Dramatic Arc
**Helplessness → Spiritual Failure → The Turn (Hero Hears and Resolves)**

Fitt 3 is the valley—the moment where the Danes' own resources are exhausted. It closes on the opening of rescue, but the arc itself descends, reaches its nadir, then turns upward with the news that reaches Beowulf.

### Music Arc Potential
1. **Opening:** Dirge-like exhaustion theme — Hrothgar sleepless, counsel at its limit
2. **Middle:** Dissonant sacrifice sequence — the Danes turn to idols in desperation, spiritual nadir
3. **Closing:** Shift to ascending heroic motif — news reaches Beowulf, the turn begins

### Dominant Force Registry
- **Grief:** Hrothgar's sustained mourning (Scene 1)
- **Law:** The failure of counsel and order (Scenes 1, 2)
- **Fate:** The inexorability of Grendel's rule, the necessity of rescue (all scenes)

## FITT 4: Beowulf's Voyage Across the Whale-Road

### Dominant Thematic Force
**Primary:** Desire | **Secondary:** Fate, Sacrifice

### Scene Structure (3 major anchors)

**Scene 1: Choosing the Company and Launching** [Opening]
- *Thematic focus:* Will and preparation; the hero gathers his means
- *Narrative beat:* Beowulf resolves to act. He selects fourteen warriors, gathers weapons, prepares the ship.
- *Key detail:* Beowulf chooses the strongest companions. They are guided by a skilled seaman. Warriors bear bright war-gear. The ship is prepared and provisioned. This is the texture of resolve made manifest.
- *Emotional tone:* Resolve, preparation, camaraderie, the gathering of will
- *Music anchor:* Martial gathering theme — the sound of preparation, of will becoming action, of a war-band assembling

**Scene 2: The Voyage Over the Whale-Road** [Middle]
- *Thematic focus:* Threshold-crossing; the liminal space between home and danger
- *Narrative beat:* The hero and his band cross the sea. This is not a march but a passage—the ocean as both obstacle and kinship.
- *Key detail:* The foamy-necked ship runs like a bird over the whale-road. The prow cuts the swell. The voyage is swift and good. The Geats pass through the liminal space between the known world (Geatland) and the imperiled world (Denmark).
- *Emotional tone:* Journey, liminal space, the texture of forward momentum, of moving toward something unknown but called
- *Music anchor:* Flowing, rhythmic seafaring theme — the motion of the ship, the rhythm of oars or sail, the sea-passage as transformation

**Scene 3: Sighting Danish Cliffs and Beaching** [Closing]
- *Thematic focus:* Arrival; the journey resolves into landfall
- *Narrative beat:* On the second day, the Geats sight the gleaming sea-cliffs. They disembark, shake out their mail-shirts, give thanks for the easy passage.
- *Key detail:* The warriors land on the Danish shore. They see the high cliffs. They are grateful for the safe crossing. They are now on imperiled ground.
- *Emotional tone:* Relief, arrival, gratitude, the resolution of journey into place
- *Music anchor:* Triumphant landfall fanfare — something that suggests arrival, the resolution of the voyage, the shift from passage to presence

### Dramatic Arc
**Resolve and Embarkation → Whale-Road Passage → Landfall on Imperiled Shore**

Fitt 4 is the hero's answer given form. The arc moves from will through passage to arrival. It is a movement fitt—the music of motion and transformation.

### Music Arc Potential
1. **Opening:** Martial gathering theme — Beowulf assembles companions, preparation and resolve
2. **Middle:** Flowing, rhythmic seafaring theme — the whale-road passage, the liminal crossing
3. **Closing:** Triumphant landfall fanfare — arrival at Danish cliffs, the journey resolved into place

### Dominant Force Registry
- **Desire:** The unnamed pull toward answering the need (all scenes)
- **Sacrifice:** The hero's willingness to enter danger (Scenes 1, 2)
- **Fate:** The good passage, the arrival (Scene 3)

## FITT 5: Beowulf's Arrival in Denmark

### Dominant Thematic Force
**Primary:** Law | **Secondary:** Kingship, Supplication

### Scene Structure (3 major anchors)

**Scene 1: The Coast-Guard Challenges Strangers** [Opening]
- *Thematic focus:* Law and order at the threshold; protocol as strength
- *Narrative beat:* Hrothgar's watchman sees the armed band on the shore. He challenges them, demands to know who they are and their right to land.
- *Key detail:* The watchman notes one warrior appears to be a noble—he can read rank even in strangers. He performs his duty: he does not assume alliance but tests it.
- *Emotional tone:* Tension, protocol, the assessment of strangers, the formality of the border
- *Music anchor:* Formal challenge theme — the sound of duty being performed, of order testing newcomers, of the border as a place where protocol matters

**Scene 2: Beowulf's Reply—Lineage and Errand** [Middle]
- *Thematic focus:* Supplication in proper forms; the hero honors the order he enters
- *Narrative beat:* Beowulf answers courteously. He states his people (Geats), his king (Hygelac), his purpose (to help Hrothgar overcome the secret destroyer).
- *Key detail:* Beowulf does not seize authority. He does not claim power. He offers aid in the proper forms: he names his lineage, he names his purpose, he supplicates. He honors the law even as he comes to serve it.
- *Emotional tone:* Courtesy, clarity, the offer of aid, the speaker honoring the forms
- *Music anchor:* Clear statement of purpose — the sound of declaration, of a hero stating his identity and intent, of supplication in proper register

**Scene 3: The Coast-Guard Grants Passage** [Closing]
- *Thematic focus:* Hospitality and the opening of the threshold
- *Narrative beat:* The watchman accepts them as friends. He pledges the ship safe. He becomes their guide, leading them toward the gold-roofed hall.
- *Key detail:* The border opens. The watchman transitions from challenger to escort. The threshold is crossed not by force but by proper form honored.
- *Emotional tone:* Acceptance, hospitality, the welcoming of the stranger, transition from border to hall
- *Music anchor:* Welcoming transition theme — the sound of protocol satisfied, the threshold opening, the guide leading the hero forward

### Dramatic Arc
**Challenge at Border → Declaration and Offered Help → Acceptance and Guidance Toward King**

Fitt 5 is the ritual of arrival—the poem's insistence that Beowulf enters not by force but by proper form. The arc moves from tension through declaration to acceptance. It is the threshold fitt.

### Music Arc Potential
1. **Opening:** Formal challenge theme — the coast-guard tests the strangers, protocol at the border
2. **Middle:** Clear statement of purpose — Beowulf declares identity, lineage, errand
3. **Closing:** Welcoming transition theme — the watchman becomes guide, the threshold opens toward Hrothgar

### Dominant Force Registry
- **Law:** Protocol and proper form at the threshold (Scenes 1, 2, 3)
- **Kingship:** Beowulf's nobility, Hrothgar's authority, the hierarchy being honored (Scene 2)
- **Supplication:** The offer of aid in proper forms (Scene 2)

## Four-Fitt Synthesis: Fitts 2–5 as a Continuous Arc

### Overarching Structure
1. **Fitt 2:** Monster's exclusion and eruption (grief, wrath, fate)
2. **Fitt 3:** Kingdom's helplessness and the hero's summoning (grief, law, fate)
3. **Fitt 4:** Hero's embarkation and whale-road passage (desire, fate, sacrifice)
4. **Fitt 5:** Hero's arrival and formal greeting at the Danish threshold (law, kingship, supplication)

### Thematic Progression
From **monster-alienation** through **civilizational failure**, to **hero-answered calling**, to the **re-establishment of lawful order and supplication**.

### Dominant Emotional Trajectory
**Despair → Hope → Journey → Arrival & Protocol**

The four fitts trace a complete movement: the Danes reach their nadir (Fitts 2–3), the hero answers (Fitt 4), and the threshold opens properly (Fitt 5). The structure is narrative but also psychological—it moves from the stuck (twelve years of Grendel's rule) through the turning (Beowulf hears) to the arrival (proper forms honored).

### Music Composition Guidance

**Structural approach:** Consider a four-movement structure mirroring the four fitts.

**Movement I (Fitt 2):** *Grendel's Isolation and Eruption*
- Dissonant, isolated monster-music reflecting Grendel's exclusion and torment
- Crescendo into violence, then sustained minor/diminished harmony reflecting the twelve-year occupation
- Emotional center: the texture of something that cannot be stopped, that rules at night

**Movement II (Fitt 3):** *The Kingdom's Exhaustion*
- Exhausted, dirge-like civic collapse reflecting Hrothgar's helplessness
- Dissonant sacrifice sequence reflecting spiritual nadir
- Shift upward into ascending heroic motif as news reaches Beowulf
- Emotional center: the valley—the moment where all resources are exhausted

**Movement III (Fitt 4):** *The Hero's Embarkation and Passage*
- Martial gathering theme for the assembly of will and means
- Flowing, rhythmic seafaring theme reflecting the whale-road passage
- Triumphant landfall fanfare for arrival on Danish shore
- Emotional center: forward momentum, the hero answering the need

**Movement IV (Fitt 5):** *Threshold and Recognition*
- Formal challenge theme at the border reflecting law and protocol
- Clear, noble declaration of identity and purpose
- Welcoming transition reflecting the opening of the threshold
- Emotional center: proper form honored, the stranger accepted, the entry to Hrothgar's world

## Notes for Composition

1. **Continuity across fitts:** While each fitt has its own dramatic arc, the four together form a larger arc. The music should reflect both the discrete moments and the continuous movement from despair to arrival.

2. **The transition moments are crucial:** 
   - Fitt 2→3: The shift from Grendel's eruption to Hrothgar's helplessness (the question becomes: can this be endured?)
   - Fitt 3→4: The shift from nadir to hope (Beowulf hears; the hero answers)
   - Fitt 4→5: The shift from passage to threshold (the hero arrives; law tests him)

3. **Grendel's presence extends beyond Fitt 2:** While Fitt 2 establishes Grendel, his occupation continues through Fitts 3–5 as the unspoken background. The music of Fitts 3–5 should carry some harmonic memory of Fitt 2's dissonance.

4. **The dominant emotional center of the four fitts is Fitt 3:** This is where the Danes reach their deepest despair. Fitts 2 and 4 are movement fitts (action); Fitts 3 and 5 are threshold fitts (transition and arrival). The music should reflect this structure.

5. **Law and protocol are the poem's structural values:** Fitt 5's insistence on proper form (the coast-guard challenges; Beowulf answers courteously) is not decoration—it is the poem's claim that order, even under threat, is what holds civilization together. The music should reflect this: law is not weakness but strength.

## Comparative Reference (Fitt I Music Arc)

For reference, Fitt I employed this force progression:
**kingship → grief → fate → kingship → kingship → grief → fate → grief → fate → fate**

Fitts 2–5 employ different force-progressions that reflect the new narrative register:
- **Fitt 2:** Wrath-grief-fate (the monster and the suffering it causes)
- **Fitt 3:** Grief-law-fate (the kingdom's resources fail)
- **Fitt 4:** Desire-fate-sacrifice (the hero's answering)
- **Fitt 5:** Law-kingship-supplication (the proper threshold-crossing)

This is a deliberate shift away from kingship-centered forces (Fitts 1 was about the building and maintenance of Hrothgar's hall) toward desire-, law-, and sacrifice-centered forces (Fitts 2–5 are about the testing of both monster and hero, the offering of aid in proper forms, the crossing of the threshold).
