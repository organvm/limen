# Empire: The Weight That Power Carries

What is empire? Not the political arrangement — the territory, the legions, the tax rolls. Those
are its instruments. The prior question is structural: what does a tradition make of power that
has organized itself across time and distance and other people's submission? What does it require
of the person who founds it, the lineage that inherits it, the pilgrim who travels beneath it,
the poet who must praise it and cannot quite? Empire is an encoding problem. Five traditions
attempt it. None agrees on what they are looking at.

---

Virgil is where Western literary empire begins, and begins already troubled. The *Aeneid*'s main
question — "What is empire built from?" — is the question the poem keeps answering and refusing
to settle. The founding myth of Rome, written at the moment of its founding in the person of
Augustus, is also the most ambivalent monument a civilization has ever erected to itself. Aeneas
is told his destiny early: he will found the race from which Rome descends, and Rome will be
given *imperium sine fine*, empire without limit. Fatum commands it. He cannot refuse. He leaves
Troy burning, leaves Dido dying, leaves Turnus begging for mercy at the end — and kills him
anyway, furor breaking through pietas at the last possible moment. Virgil set out to write the
national epic of the world's most powerful state and wrote its confession. Empire in the *Aeneid*
is built from the things it cannot admit: from the violence beneath the marble, from the grief
that the destined man is required to suppress, from the cost exacted from everyone who stood in
the way of the fatum that was not theirs to refuse.

The *Shahnameh* is the other great imperial epic, and Ferdowsi asks not about the founding but
about the persistence. Its question — "What is kingship, fate, and lineage across an age?" —
stretches empire across fifty cycles of dynasty, each one turning on the farr: the divine royal
glory that descends on a king, enables his reign, and departs. The *Shahnameh*'s empire is not
a monument but a mechanism. The farr lights on a man, his kingdom rises to greatness, the farr
leaves — for hubris, for weakness, for no visible reason at all — and the cycle begins again.
Empire here is not a destiny but a rhythm, the violence of kingship recurring through
generations as the force that transmits power while consuming every body that carries it. No
king holds the farr forever. The *Aeneid* grieves the cost. The *Shahnameh* studies the pattern.

The Tanakh encodes empire from below — from inside the people on whom empire descends rather
than the people who build it. Its question — "What is covenant, law, creation, exile?" — makes
the united and divided monarchy an episode in a longer story: the Sinai covenant that bound a
people to a God and made any human king a conditional arrangement, always measured against a
prior sovereignty. Saul, David, Solomon, the split, the northern kingdom's fall to Assyria, the
southern kingdom's fall to Babylon — the Tanakh witnesses these as moments in a covenant drama,
not as the rise and decline of a state. Empire in the Tanakh is what happens to a people when
the covenant frays. The question the prophets keep pressing is not whether Babylon is more
powerful than Judah, but whether Israel has kept faith with the terms of its founding. The
covenant is the counter-weight to empire. Empire, in this register, is always a judgment on
something else.

Dante wants the empire sanctified, and the *Divine Comedy*'s main question — "What is ordered
ascent?" — is inseparable from his political vision. He places emperors in Paradise and popes
in Hell. For Dante, right empire — the *imperium* that holds the secular order in justice while
the Church tends to souls — is not just politically preferable but cosmically necessary. The
political vision runs under the visionary poem like a ground bass: the architecture of Inferno
reflects corrupted imperial and civic order, Purgatorio is the education back toward justice,
and the spheres of Paradiso arrange themselves as a hierarchical empire of which the celestial
rose is the throne room. Dante's just empire is not Virgil's confession — it is a program, a
city blueprint for a world that has failed to realize it. He asks whether ordered power is
possible. The *Comedy* is the answer in the form of a journey.

*Journey to the West* places empire at a tilt. The Tang Emperor's great pilgrimage westward to
retrieve the Buddhist scriptures is empire as piety project — state power conscripted into
religious mission. But the real travelers are Tripitaka and his three disciples, above all the
irrepressible Sun Wukong, the Monkey King, who began the novel by assaulting Heaven's imperial
bureaucracy and claiming the title "Great Sage Equal to Heaven." Its question — "What is
pilgrimage, transformation, and comic transcendence?" — frames the entire Tang imperial apparatus
as backdrop for a transformation that happens to individuals, not to states. The pilgrim's
journey is not conquest; it is the acquisition of merit through trial, suffering, and Monkey's
inexhaustible comic defiance of every form of rank. Empire in *Journey to the West* is both the
frame and the thing being gently satirized. The Tang Emperor commissions the journey. He is
barely in the story. What happens on the road is not his.

---

The music engine couples empire to **kingship** and specifies its sonic register as "ceremonial
grandeur, sovereign display." That pairing is exact and harder than it first appears. Ceremonial
grandeur is not triumphalism — it is the sound of power presenting itself, which includes the
sound of what power is presenting itself for. Handel's *Zadok the Priest*, played at every
British coronation since 1727, is not a march of conquest; it is the moment when a person
becomes a function, when an individual is subsumed into an office. Elgar and Walton carry the
same quality — the full orchestral weight of sovereignty on display — but in Walton's *Crown
Imperial*, as in Virgil's twelfth book, the shadow only arrives when you remember what the
display is arraying toward. The color for kingship is a deep gold-bronze. The music does not
celebrate; it enacts the register, which the reading then has to do something with.

---

But the counter-system cuts the problem at a different angle.

There is no empire entry in the counter-systems pairings — which is itself a kind of answer. The
Eastern traditions gathered here do not primarily encode empire as a form to be evaluated or
justified. The *Shahnameh* is the partial exception, but its cycles of the farr are closer to
fate than to political theory. What the Eastern witness to empire reveals is a structural
difference in what power is thought to be. The Tanakh's covenant places human kingship under
divine judgment from the start: "Give us a king," the people ask, and God warns them exactly
what kings do — take your sons for armies, your daughters for kitchens, your fields for estates.
The covenant precedes and outlasts the monarchy. Any empire is, from this register, a temporary
imposition on a prior order. Dante's counter-answer to Virgil is not Eastern but it is the most
radical: the just empire is not the one that actually exists but the one that ought to exist,
and the gap between them is the subject of the poem.

The deepest tension is between the *Aeneid*'s fatum — empire as destiny one cannot refuse, the
violent cost sanctified by the scale of what it produces — and the Tanakh's covenantal frame,
where the greatest empire Israel ever knew (Solomon's) ends in division and disaster because the
covenant was broken. One tradition says empire is what history is for. The other says history is
what empire is judged by. *Fatum sine fine* against the covenant that preceded it. These are not
compatible encodings. Reading them against each other does not resolve the tension; it clarifies
what the tension actually is.

---

What does the chorus reveal that no single work could?

Empire is not one thing. The *Aeneid* finds it necessary and costs everything. The *Shahnameh*
finds it rhythmic — a mechanism that cycles through kings, consuming each while the farr moves
on. The Tanakh finds it conditional, always measured against the covenant it can violate but
cannot abolish. Dante finds it aspirational — the idea of right empire more real than any
actual empire that has existed. *Journey to the West* finds it the frame within which individual
transformation occurs, peripheral to the real journey, occasionally comic.

The conversation the species has been having with itself about empire is not an argument
converging on legitimacy or condemnation. It is a mapping of what organized power across time
does to the people who exercise it, the people who live under it, and the poets who have to
make sense of it — which is to say, all of us. The *Aeneid* is the founding document; the
*Shahnameh* is its long aftermath; the Tanakh is the surviving counter-record; Dante is the
idealist blueprint; *Journey to the West* is the comic escape.

To read all five together is to hold the weight that power carries — not to resolve whether
empire is good or evil, but to understand why every civilization has felt compelled to ask, and
why no single answer has ever been enough.
