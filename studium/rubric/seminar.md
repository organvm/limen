# Rubric — Seminar (the war zone: how an idea survives trial)

A professor uses the class as a breeding zone and a war zone for the ideas of the book they are
writing. The Studium's seminar is that: a place where a *claim* about a passage — an interpretation,
a counter-system reading, a thesis about how a work encodes a force — is staged, defended, attacked,
and either survives or is revised. This rubric scores the **trial**, not the claim's truth. A claim
that is decisively refuted, well, *teaches more than a claim that is never tested.*

**Diagnostic, never gatekeeping. Losing a trial is not a low score — failing to engage one is.** The
built-in opposition is the canon's own counter-systems (`../synthesis/counter-systems.md`): the Gita
against Achilles, the Dao against fatum, the Heike against martial glory. The work is its own adversary.

---

## The four levels

### 1 — Nascent

A claim was made and put on the floor — a thesis about a passage stated plainly enough that it *could*
be argued ("the Iliad makes wrath the truth of heroic identity"). It is asserted, not yet grounded in
the text, and no objection has been engaged.

*Observable sign:* A falsifiable claim exists, tied to a specific work and division. No textual
evidence and no counter-position are present yet.

---

### 2 — Developing

The claim is grounded in the text (a line, a scene, a structural feature) and meets at least one
objection — most naturally the work's counter-system (the Gita's renunciation set against Achilles'
wrath). The defense engages the objection rather than restating the claim; the attack engages the
claim rather than a caricature of it.

*Observable sign:* Textual evidence is cited for the claim AND one genuine counter-position is named
and answered. The exchange is a real exchange, not two assertions side by side.

---

### 3 — Practiced

The trial is adversarial and fair: the strongest objection is sought out, not the weakest; the claim
is pressured at its actual load-bearing point; and the defense either holds with new evidence or
concedes precisely what was refuted and revises. The counter-system is used as designed — not "the
Gita disagrees" but "the Gita's *yoga* shows the Iliad's wrath to be a cultural choice, not a human
universal, which narrows the claim to: *within the Greek heroic frame*, wrath is identity's truth."

*Observable sign:* The claim is sharper *after* the trial than before — narrowed, qualified, or
re-grounded by the strongest available objection. A concession, where made, is exact.

---

### 4 — Fluent

The trial produces transmissible knowledge. The claim, having survived or been revised under the
canon's own opposition, becomes something worth recording: an essay paragraph, a refinement to
`../synthesis/concepts.yaml`, a Watch-Along prompt, a new counter-system pairing. The seminarian can
run *both* sides — state the strongest case for the claim and the strongest case against it — and say
what the collision revealed that neither side held alone. This is the breeding zone working: the idea
is now ready for the book.

*Observable sign:* The trial output is a durable artifact (a sharpened thesis + its strongest
counter + what the collision yielded). It feeds the current inquiry (`../community/current-inquiry.md`)
and emits a `trial_round` event (`../analysis/events-schema.yaml`).

---

## How to use this

Run a trial when a daily note reaches a claim worth testing — not every day. Stage it via
`../community/trial-protocol.md` (claim → defend → attack → score → log). Score the *conduct* of the
trial on this rubric; the claim's fate (held / revised / refuted) is recorded separately as data, not
as a grade. A community of trials, scored and logged, is the curriculum's "packaging as data
analysis": which concepts are most contested, which claims keep surviving, where the canon's
counter-systems bite hardest (`../analysis/`). The war zone is not combat for its own sake — it is the
fastest way an interpretation earns the right to be transmitted.
