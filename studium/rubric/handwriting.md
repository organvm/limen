# Rubric — Handwriting

The hand is a drag brake on reading speed, and that is its whole value here: copying a passage in its original script forces a granular encounter with structure — where a line breaks, how a clause is built, which words fall under the ictus — that silent reading skips. This is not penmanship. It is ductus as attention.

**This rubric is diagnostic, never gatekeeping.** A score of 1 on a given day is information about that day's contact with the text — friction, unfamiliarity, fatigue — not a verdict on the practice. Low days are data.

---

## Level 1 — Nascent

You copied the passage but the hand was mostly transcription: letter by letter, no felt sense of the script's rhythm. You may have lost your place in the original, copied a word twice, or skipped a line. The letterforms are legible but mechanical — drawn, not written. You finished. That is enough for today; the resistance you felt is the thing to note.

**Observable sign:** The page shows effort to reproduce shapes. No formal observation emerges from the copying itself — the observation field (if filled) was drawn from the translation or a prior note, not from the act of writing the original.

---

## Level 2 — Developing

You moved through the passage with some fluency but slowed at unfamiliar letterforms, ligatures, or long compound words. The copying produced at least one noticing you would not have had from reading alone — a syllable boundary you had glossed over, a repeated root, a line-end that changes the syntax. The formal observation may be tentative, but it is traceable to the act of copying.

**Observable sign:** The formal observation field names something specific about meter, word-order, or sound that the hand revealed. The handwriting is uneven but shows the script's direction and proportion, not just letter shapes in isolation.

---

## Level 3 — Engaged

The copying felt like reading in slow motion. You moved through clauses rather than words, and the hand's pace forced you to hold a phrase in suspension long enough to feel its weight. The letterforms have internalized enough that attention was freed for the text rather than consumed by the script. One or more structural features — enjambment, a chiasm, the position of a divine name — became visible through the copying that was invisible in the English.

**Observable sign:** The formal observation is precise and grounded in a specific moment in the passage — a line number, a particular word, a syntactic turn. You could point to the exact place in the manuscript where you noticed it.

---

## Level 4 — Fluent

The hand and the reading were one movement. The script offered no resistance as a script; resistance came entirely from the text itself — its difficulty, its density, its surprise. The copying became a form of memorization by hand: you found yourself writing phrases without consulting the original character by character. The formal observation is layered — structural, sonic, or intertextual — and could not have come from reading alone. The page feels inhabited.

**Observable sign:** The observation field holds something that would belong in a commentary. The handwriting shows the script's inner logic — not uniform, but *inside* the tradition's proportions. You wrote it; you did not draw it.

---

*Score the handwriting line on your daily page 1–4. Over weeks, the pattern shows where the script is becoming transparent and where it is still an object. Transparency is the goal — the script disappears into the text, the way a skilled reader's eye disappears into a page.*
