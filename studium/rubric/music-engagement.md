# Rubric — Music Engagement

Music is the second commentary system: not decoration running alongside the text but an independent
account of the same force — wrath, grief, metamorphosis, law — meeting the passage on its own
terms. The day's KEEP / REPLACE verdict is the unit of engagement: you are asking whether the
force-matched pairing actually delivered what the force required, or whether a better pairing
suggests itself now that you have lived inside the passage.

**This rubric is diagnostic, never gatekeeping. A low day is data — evidence of what the pairing
missed or what the force demanded that you hadn't yet heard — not failure.**

---

## The four levels

### 1 — Nascent

You listened. You wrote something in the *Why this composition fits* field, even if only to note
that it didn't obviously fit. The KEEP / REPLACE verdict is there — even if it's a reflex KEEP
with no reason given, or a REPLACE with no alternative named. The pairing has been received; the
conversation has not yet started.

*Observable sign:* The verdict line is filled. The *Why* field has at least one sentence, even a
tentative or dismissive one. No specific musical moment is cited.

---

### 2 — Developing

You can name at least one concrete musical event — a harmonic shift, a rhythmic insistence, a
timbral change, a moment of silence — and connect it to the dominant force of the day's passage.
Your KEEP / REPLACE is argued, not reflexive: if KEEP, you can say what the piece actually
delivered against the force's requirement (from `dominant-force.yaml`); if REPLACE, you can name
what the requirement was and what the proposed piece would do differently.

*Observable sign:* The *Why* field references a specific moment in the music and maps it to the
dominant force. The verdict is a sentence, not a word.

---

### 3 — Practiced

Your KEEP / REPLACE verdict engages the force's musical requirement precisely — not just "it felt
right" but "the force required rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony; this piece delivered /
failed to deliver because..." You hear the pairing as a reading act: the music illuminates
something in the scene that the translation didn't, or reveals a tension the text kept implicit.
If REPLACE, you can propose a specific alternative piece with a reason grounded in the requirement.

*Observable sign:* The verdict could be read as a note to the synthesizer — it gives enough
information to select or reject a pairing programmatically. You cite the force's requirement
verbatim or in substance.

---

### 4 — Fluent

The pairing has become generative. You've heard the composition as a counter-reading: it either
confirms the force (KEEP — and here is what the confirmation revealed about the scene that a
second read alone would not have), or it fails in a way that teaches you something about the force
(REPLACE — and the better pairing would surface this specific thing the text is doing). A fluent
day may produce a one-sentence insight that goes into an essay draft or refines the curated arc in
`music/<work>/book-NN.yaml`. The KEEP / REPLACE verdict is not a consumer review; it is a
contribution to the transmission record.

*Observable sign:* The *Why* field or an appended note contains a claim about the text — not just
the music — that was made visible by the listening. The verdict is a full thought, not a ruling.

---

## How to use this

Score after the page is complete, not before. The score belongs to the day's ledger entry alongside
the other three rubric scores. A sustained run of 1s on this layer is a signal to change the
listening conditions (headphones instead of speakers, or active listening before annotating, rather
than music as ambient backdrop) — not a signal to simplify the canon or the pairings.

A REPLACE verdict is not a lower score. A considered REPLACE at level 3 or 4 is the rubric working
exactly as intended: the force-matching system self-corrects through your verdicts.
