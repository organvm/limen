# Rubric — Translation

Translation is the layer where the text stops being an object and becomes an encounter: you are not reading about what Homer said, you are discovering what English can and cannot hold of what he said, and which translator decided to sacrifice what. The focus here is the encounter itself — the friction between literal and literary rendering, and the noticing of translator choice: which word is kept first, which force is softened, which syntax is bent to make the target language comfortable.

**This rubric is diagnostic, never gatekeeping.** A score of 1 on a given day means the encounter did not happen at depth — that is data about the day, not a verdict on the reader. Low days are part of the record.

---

## Level 1 — Nascent

You have the passage in front of you and at least one translation open beside it. You read both. You may have written out a word-by-word gloss from a supplied interlinear or vocabulary list. You notice that the translations differ but cannot yet say how. The LITERAL and LITERARY TRANSLATION fields on the daily page exist but feel like two versions of the same thing. The formal observation feels borrowed from the notes rather than seen.

## Level 2 — Forming

You produce a literal rendering — even halting, even wrong — in your own English, sticking close to the word order of the original where you can feel it. You compare it to one published translation and name one specific divergence: a word that translator expanded, a phrase they compressed, an epithet they dropped. You can say *what* is different, though not yet fully *why* the translator made that call.

## Level 3 — Active

You produce both a literal and a literary rendering of the passage yourself, and the two feel distinct on the page — one faithful to syntax and root meaning, one shaped for rhythm or force in English. You compare at least two translators and identify what each weights: one keeps the war-cry blunt, one softens it into dignity; one preserves the genitive absolute, one dissolves it into a clause. You note which weighting serves the dominant force of the day's scene and which mutes it.

## Level 4 — Fluent

The encounter runs in both directions. Your own renderings are purposeful — your literal translation is a critical instrument, revealing something about the original's grammar or sound that the literary translation cannot hold; your literary translation makes a real argument about the force encoded in the scene. You can say what a translator's cumulative *theology* is — their hidden interpretive commitment — based on three or four specific word-choices across the week's passages. You know which word they always keep and which they always soften, and you can name what that costs the text.

---

*Score 1–4 on the daily page alongside comprehension, handwriting, and music engagement. The score is a single integer; the real record is the ONE FORMAL OBSERVATION and the LITERAL / LITERARY fields themselves.*
