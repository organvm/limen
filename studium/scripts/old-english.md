## Layer 1 — Calligraphy / paleography (the hand)

Old English manuscripts use the **Insular script** — a family of hands developed in Irish and Anglo-Saxon
scriptoria from the 7th century onward. The Beowulf manuscript (Cotton Vitellius A.xv, c. 1000 CE) is
written in late Anglo-Saxon minuscule by two scribes. Copy the insular letterforms; the modern "normalized"
alphabet is a scholarly convenience but the manuscript hand is the real encounter.

**Four letters absent from modern English — learn these first:**

| Letter | Name | Sound | Handwriting note |
|--------|------|-------|-----------------|
| **þ** | thorn | /θ/ or /ð/ (both) | A vertical ascender with a lobe on the right mid-shaft — NOT a p; the lobe sits above the baseline |
| **ð** | eth (edh) | /ð/ or /θ/ (both) | A d with a crossbar through the ascender — write d, then slash through diagonally |
| **ƿ** | wynn | /w/ | A tall rune-derived glyph; in editions often silently replaced with w |
| **æ** | ash | /æ/ (as in "cat") | An a with an e ligature fused to its right — write as one stroke |

**The full Old English alphabet in Insular minuscule:**

| a | æ | b | c | d | ð | e | f | g | h |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| i | k | l | m | n | o | p | r | s | t |
| þ | u | ƿ (w) | x | y | |  |  |  |  |

- **a** — open, rounded; the insular form has a single lobe unlike the double-story Roman a
- **c** — always /k/ in Old English (no soft c); the insular c is wide and open
- **g** — the insular **ȝ** (yogh-shape) for most positions; sounds /j/ before front vowels, /g/ elsewhere; palatal /ɣ/ between vowels
- **f** — in medial position between vowels, voiced /v/; insular f has a crossbar at mid-height only
- **s** — the insular long-s (ſ) dominates mid-word; round s at word-end; both acceptable in your hand
- **h** — /h/ initially; /x/ (as in Scottish "loch") after vowels
- **Double consonants** (ff, ss, ll) are pronounced long — hold the sound

**Ductus notes for the hardest letters:**
- **þ** (thorn): ascender first (top to bottom), then loop the lobe rightward from mid-shaft. The critical
  distinction from p is that the lobe starts at mid-shaft, not the top.
- **ð** (eth): write a full insular d (curved body, tall ascender), then cross the ascender with a diagonal
  slash angled upper-left to lower-right.
- **æ** (ash): the a-body flows directly into an e without lifting the pen; think of it as an "ae" ligature
  compressed into one glyph. It represents a front open vowel: between the a of "father" and the a of "cat."
- **ȝ/g**: the insular g has a horizontal top-stroke, a vertical down-stroke, and a curved tail that swings
  left below the baseline — closer to a 3 than to a modern g.

**Manuscript conventions to recognise:**
- Word spacing is inconsistent; the scribes sometimes run words together.
- Abbreviations: a macron (bar over a vowel) signals a following **n** or **m** (e.g., ƿā = wann, "dark").
- Capital letters are decorated with red or green — the two scribes use slightly different hands; Scribe B
  (who writes the second half) is slightly less regular.
- No punctuation as such; a mid-line point (·) marks a half-line caesura — the rhythmic hinge of the
  alliterative line.

**Write each letter 5× before moving to Layer 2. Then copy one full half-line from the passage below
by hand, reproducing þ, ð, and æ each time they appear.**

## Layer 2 — Glossed reading (decode the line)

The opening of Beowulf — the first six lines of the poem. Copy by hand, then work the gloss.

```
Hwæt! ƿē Gār-Dena    in geār-dagum
hƿæt  wee  Gar-Dena  in  gear-dagum

þēod-cyninga    þrym  gefrūnon,
theod-cyninga   thrym  gefrunon,

hū  ðā  æþelingas  ellen  fremedon.
hu  tha  aethelingas  ellen  fremedon.
```

*(Lines 1–3, Cotton Vitellius A.xv, normalized spelling)*

| OE word | Transliteration | Gloss |
|---------|----------------|-------|
| **Hwæt!** | Hwæt | **Listen! / Lo! / What!** — a call to attention opening the performance; the subject of much scholarly debate (Tolkien: "So!") |
| **ƿē** | wē | **we** — nominative plural pronoun; the scop speaks on behalf of the audience |
| **Gār-Dena** | Gār-Dena | **of the Spear-Danes** — genitive plural; Gār = "spear," Dena = "Danes" |
| **in** | in | **in, during** — preposition governing dative |
| **geār-dagum** | geār-dagum | **in the days of yore / in years of old** — dative plural; geār = "year," dagum = "days" (dative pl. of dæg) |
| **þēod-cyninga** | thēod-cyninga | **of nation-kings / of people's kings** — genitive plural; þēod = "people/nation," cyninga = gen. pl. of cyning "king" |
| **þrym** | thrym | **glory, power, majesty** — accusative singular (the object of gefrūnon) |
| **gefrūnon** | gefrunon | **we have heard of** — 1st pl. past indicative of gefrūnan "to learn by asking, hear tell of"; the ge- prefix often marks perfective aspect |
| **hū** | hu | **how** — conjunction introducing indirect question |
| **ðā** | tha | **those, the** — nominative plural demonstrative / definite article |
| **æþelingas** | aethelingas | **princes, noblemen** — nominative plural; æþeling = man of royal lineage |
| **ellen** | ellen | **courage, valor, zeal** — accusative; a core heroic virtue-word |
| **fremedon** | fremedon | **performed, displayed, accomplished** — 3rd pl. past indicative of fremman "to perform, further, do" |

**Literal:** "Listen! We have heard of the glory of the Spear-Danes' nation-kings in days of yore — how those princes performed valor."

**Literary:** "So. The spear-Danes' kings — their glory in the old days, the great men of those clans: we have heard what heroes they were."
*(Seamus Heaney, 2000)*

**Method:** the corpus holds the Old English text alongside the Heaney, Tolkien prose, Hall, and Chickering
translations. Read the OE aloud (the alliteration will lock the rhythm into your ear), copy it by hand
tracing each þ and ð carefully, gloss each word, then COMPARE how each translator renders **hwæt** and
**ellen** — those two words alone reveal every translator's interpretive stance.

**Alliterative formulae to recognise on sight** (the poem's structural building-blocks):

- `Hwæt!` — the oral-performance opener; appears in other OE poems too (Andreas, Elene)
- `bearn Ecgþēowes` — "son of Ecgtheow" (Beowulf's patronymic, recurring epithet)
- `ƿeder-Gēata lēod` — "man of the weather-Geats" (Beowulf's tribal epithet)
- `hringed-stefna` — "the ring-prowed ship" (kenning for a longship)
- `gūð-rinc gold-ƿlanc` — "battle-warrior proud with gold" (alliterating epithet pair)
- `ofer hronrāde` — "over the whale-road" (kenning for the sea — the poem's most famous compound)
- `worold-candel` — "world-candle" (kenning for the sun)

**Kennings** are the poem's signature device: compound noun-metaphors. When you encounter an unfamiliar
compound, split it at its seam and translate each half — the meaning is usually transparent once you have
the vocabulary.

## Layer 3 — Serious grammar (toward unaided translation)

Minimal orientation; expand as you go (recommended companions: Mitchell & Robinson, *A Guide to Old English*;
Klaeber's *Beowulf*, 4th ed.).

### Word order

Old English is **SOV/SVO/VSO** — all three orders occur, controlled by information structure and clause type.
The verb tends toward final position in subordinate clauses (Germanic verb-final) but surfaces second in main
clauses (V2). Because ENDINGS carry grammatical role (like Greek), the order is fluid:

- `þēod-cyninga þrym gefrūnon` = object–subject-verb (the glory [obj] we-heard-of [verb+subj])
- You will not be lost if you find the verb and the case endings first, then assign roles.

### Nouns: four cases, three genders

Old English nouns decline in **Nominative · Genitive · Dative · Accusative** × **Masculine · Feminine · Neuter**
× **Singular / Plural**. There is no ablative (unlike Latin); dative covers most oblique functions.

The two paradigms to learn first:

**Strong masculine a-stem (cyning "king"):**

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | cyning | cyningas |
| Gen. | cyninges | cyninga |
| Dat. | cyninge | cyningum |
| Acc. | cyning | cyningas |

**Strong feminine ō-stem (giefu "gift"):**

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nom. | giefu | giefa |
| Gen. | giefe | giefa |
| Dat. | giefe | giefum |
| Acc. | giefe | giefa |

Key signal: **-um** is the dative plural ending across all genders and stems — when you see -um, the word is
dative plural. **-es** is the strong genitive singular masculine/neuter. **-a** is often genitive plural.

### Adjectives agree in case, gender, and number

Adjectives have both **strong** (indefinite, predicative) and **weak** (definite, after demonstratives) forms.
After ðā ("those/the"): weak forms. Alone: strong forms. The distinction maps onto the/a distinction in
modern English but is grammatically explicit.

### Verbs: two classes

**Strong verbs** (ablaut / vowel-change, like sing/sang/sung):
- Class I: drīfan → drāf → drifon → gedrifen (drive/drove)
- Class II: bēodan → bēad → budon → geboden (command/bid)
- Classes I–VII by vowel pattern; Klaeber's appendix lists them all.

**Weak verbs** (dental past tense, like walk/walked):
- Class I: fremman → fremede (perform) — note double consonant in infinitive
- Class II: lufian → lufode (love)

The past tense of gefrūnon (Class III strong): gefrūnan → gefrān (sg.) / gefrūnon (pl.) — note the vowel
shift. **In/on** = present; **ā/ō** alternation = past.

### The definite article / demonstrative: sē, sēo, þæt

Old English has no true article — sē (masc.) / sēo (fem.) / þæt (neut.) function as demonstrative "that/the."
Declined fully; ðā is the nominative plural of all genders. Recognise this paradigm early — it appears on
almost every line.

| | Masc. | Fem. | Neut. | Plural |
|--|-------|------|-------|--------|
| Nom. | sē | sēo | þæt | ðā |
| Gen. | þæs | þǣre | þæs | þāra |
| Dat. | þǣm | þǣre | þǣm | þǣm |
| Acc. | þone | ðā | þæt | ðā |

### The alliterative half-line (prosody as grammar)

Each line of Beowulf divides at a **caesura** (·) into two half-lines. Each half-line has exactly **two
stressed syllables**; the first stressed syllable of the second half-line (the "head-stave") alliterates with
one or both stressed syllables of the first half-line. Alliteration is on the consonant or any vowel:

```
Hwæt! ƿē Gār-Dena     ·    in geār-dagum
      [Gar]                    [gear]   ← G alliterates with G
```

Recognising the alliterative pattern tells you which words are semantically heavy (they carry the stress) and
helps you parse unfamiliar vocabulary by its metrical position.

**Daily grammar drill:** take today's half-line, (1) identify the two stress-alliterations, (2) parse ONE
noun fully (case · gender · number · why that case), (3) identify the verb's class and tense, and write the
paradigm it belongs to in your ledger. Do this before consulting any translation.
