# Middle English — the script for Chaucer (Canterbury Tales) & Langland (Piers Plowman)

> Script first, grammar second, translation third. The key is not fluency — it is **physical contact
> with the text**. Handwriting slows you down enough to notice structure. Three layers below; go as
> deep as the day allows (set `language_depth` in `logs/studium-state.json`).

---

## Layer 1 — Calligraphy / paleography (the hand)

Middle English was written in **Gothic textura** (formal manuscripts) or **Anglicana** (workaday scribal
hand) — both descended from Caroline minuscule via Insular scripts. Chaucer's Hengwrt and Ellesmere
manuscripts are Anglicana. The alphabet is the Latin alphabet PLUS four letters borrowed from Old English
runic and Insular tradition; these are the first orthographic features to master.

**The four special letters — copy each 5× until the ductus is muscle memory:**

| letter | name | sound | ductus note |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **þ** | thorn | /θ/ or /ð/ (both "th" sounds) | Looks like a ⟨b⟩ with an ascender that curves back left at the top; scribes often write an abbreviated ⟨þe⟩ = *the* |
| **ð** | eth | /ð/ (voiced "th") | A ⟨d⟩ with a crossbar through the ascender; less common in ME than in OE, but appears in northern texts |
| **ƿ** | wynn | /w/ | Resembles a ⟨p⟩ but with a narrower bowl; increasingly replaced by ⟨w⟩ in 14th-c. scribal practice |
| **æ** | ash | /a/ or /æ/ | A ligature of ⟨a⟩+⟨e⟩; common in earlier ME, fading by Chaucer's era; persists in OE-derived words |

**The standard Latin letters with ME-specific forms:**

- **⟨u/v⟩** are NOT distinct in medieval manuscripts: ⟨u⟩ is used in the interior of a word (*loue* = love),
  ⟨v⟩ at the start (*vertu*, *vnto*). Do not impose modern u/v distinction when transcribing.
- **⟨i/j⟩** similarly interchangeable; ⟨j⟩ often marks a final -i in numerals (*iij* = 3).
- **⟨y⟩** frequently stands for ⟨i⟩ or the sound /j/ (*ynogh* = enough; *yet* = yet).
- **⟨gh⟩** renders the velar/palatal fricative /x/ or /ç/ lost in modern English: *nyght*, *knyght*, *doughter*.
- **⟨qu⟩** = /kw/ (*quene* = queen); **⟨sch/sh⟩** = /ʃ/ (*schall* northern, *shal* southern).
- **Long ⟨s⟩ (ſ):** in manuscripts the tall form ⟨ſ⟩ is used everywhere except word-final position,
  where the round ⟨s⟩ appears. Learn to read both without hesitation.

**Scribal abbreviations (the palaeographer's minimum):**

| abbreviation | expansion | example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| superscript vowel | preceding nasal omitted | ā = *an*; õ = *on* |
| ⟨p̄⟩ with bar through descender | *per-* or *par-* | p̄fyt = *parfyt* (perfect) |
| ⟨p⟩ with curl on descender | *pro-* | p̃mise = *promise* |
| ⟨ꝫ⟩ or ⟨ꝑ⟩ | *-et*, *-er* endings | spellings contract the suffix |
| ⟨þt⟩ or ⟨þe⟩ with raised ⟨t/e⟩ | *that*, *the* | the most common sight-abbreviations |

**Handwriting practice order:** Begin with the General Prologue's opening lines. Copy each line once in
print, then once attempting the Anglicana ductus (rounded strokes, connected letters, the tall ⟨ſ⟩).
The muscular commitment to the letter-forms is itself a reading aid — you stop skimming.

## Layer 2 — Glossed reading (decode the line)

The opening of the **General Prologue**, *Canterbury Tales* — the gold-standard daily unit.
Copy it by hand in the original orthography, then work the gloss.

```
Whan that Aprill with his shoures soote
The droghte of March hath perced to the roote,
And bathed every veyne in swich licour
Of which vertu engendred is the flour;
```

*(Hengwrt MS orthography; "shoures soote" = sweet showers)*

**Line 1 gloss:**

| word | pronunciation (IPA approx.) | gloss |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Whan | /hwan/ | **when** — temporal conjunction (OE *hwanne*) |
| that | /θat/ | **that** — subordinating particle, often pleonastic after conjunctions |
| Aprill | /aˈpril/ | **April** — nominative; final -ll is ME doubling convention |
| with | /wɪθ/ | **with** — preposition |
| his | /hɪz/ | **its** — ME uses *his* as neuter possessive (the month is grammatically masculine) |
| shoures | /ˈʃuːrəz/ | **showers** — nominative plural (OE *scūr*) |
| soote | /ˈsoːtə/ | **sweet** — adjective in weak (post-determiner) form (OE *swōte*); note the final -e is *pronounced* /ə/ in Chaucer |

**Line 2 gloss:**

| word | pronunciation | gloss |
| --- | --- | --- |
| droghte | /ˈdrɔxt/ | **drought** — the ⟨gh⟩ is a real /x/ sound; do not silently modernise it |
| hath | /haθ/ | **has** — 3rd-sg present of *haven* (southern dialect form; northern = *has*) |
| perced | /ˈpɛrsəd/ | **pierced** — past participle, used as main verb in *hath perced* |
| to the roote | | **to the root** — prepositional phrase; *roote* final -e again voiced |

- **Literal:** "When that April with its sweet showers / The drought of March has pierced to the root"
- **Literary:** "When April with his sweet showers / Has pierced March's drought to the root"

**Method:** the corpus holds Chaucer's text plus modern English translations (Coghill, Nevill, Benson
glossed edition). Read the ME aloud — the final -e, the /x/ in *droghte*, the /w/ in *whan* — then
COMPARE how translators render *soote* (sweet? gentle? soft?). Translation is where the book becomes an
encounter. Every silent letter you voice is etymology made physical.

**Formulaic openings to recognise on sight** (Chaucer's recurrent registers):

- `A knyght ther was` — the portrait formula (GP lines 43+); ther = existential "there"
- `And eek` — "and also" (additive connective in every portrait)
- `of his port as meeke as is a mayde` — simile construction: *as [adj] as is a [noun]*
- `Ful wel she soong the service dyvyne` — intensifier *ful* + adverb inversion (verb before subject)
- `Heere bigynneth` / `Heere endeth` — the manuscript rubrics that frame each tale

## Layer 3 — Serious grammar (toward unaided translation)

Minimal orientation; expand as you go (recommended companions: Benson's *Riverside Chaucer* glossary;
Davis et al., *A Chaucer Glossary*; Horobin & Smith, *An Introduction to Middle English*).

**Nouns:** ME has largely shed the Old English six-case system. Two cases remain live:
- **Common case** (subject + object): *the knyght*, *the shoures*.
- **Genitive case**: -es suffix (*the knyghtes tale*) or periphrastic *of* phrase.
- Plural is typically **-es** (*shoures*, *rootes*), but irregular OE plurals survive: *men*, *children*, *feet*.

**The final -e:** Chaucer's final -e is NOT silent. It is pronounced /ə/ and carries grammatical
information — weak adjective inflection (*the goode man* vs. *a good man*), dative vestiges (*hire*),
subjunctive mood (*if that it be*). A line that seems metrically broken usually repairs itself when the
final -e is voiced. This single insight unlocks Chaucer's iambic pentameter.

**Adjectives:** "strong" (indefinite: *a goode man*) vs. "weak" (definite/vocative: *the goode man*).
The weak form adds -e; the strong form is bare or adds -e only in plural. Northern dialects often drop
all inflectional -e.

**Pronouns (the critical ones):**

| | Nominative | Accusative/Dative | Genitive |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 3sg masc | he | him | his |
| 3sg fem | she / heo (N) | hire / hir | hire / hir |
| 3sg neut | it / hit | it / him | his ← NOTE: *his* = "its" |
| 3pl | they (N dial.) / hi (S) | hem / them | hir / their |
| 2sg familiar | thou | thee | thy / thine |
| 2pl / polite 2sg | ye | yow | your |

*thy* before consonants, *thine* before vowels (same rule as a/an).

**Verbs:** ME distinguishes strong (vowel-change past: *ride → rood*) and weak (dental suffix:
*love → loved*). Key conjugation of *ben* (to be) in present:

| | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1st | am / be | been / are |
| 2nd | art | been / are |
| 3rd | is / ys | been / are / is (N) |

Infinitive: *ben* or *been*; subjunctive 3sg: *be* (no -th ending — a reliable diagnostic).

**Word order:** Subject–Verb–Object is the default, but ME freely inverts for emphasis or rhyme:
- *Ful wel she soong* = "She sang very well" (adverb–verb–subject inversion)
- *Him thoughte* = "It seemed to him" (impersonal verb; *him* is dative, not subject)
- *There nas no man nowher so vertuous* = "There was no man anywhere so virtuous" (multiple negation
  is grammatical in ME — it intensifies, it does not cancel)

**Dialectal variation:** The four main ME dialects (Northern, East Midlands, West Midlands, Southern)
differ systematically. Chaucer writes East Midlands (London); Langland writes West Midlands; the
*Gawain*-poet writes North-West Midlands. Key shibboleths:

| feature | Northern | E. Midlands (Chaucer) | W. Midlands (*Gawain*) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 3sg pres. ending | -(e)s | -(e)th | -(e)th |
| present plural | -(e)s | -(e)n | -(e)n |
| "they/them/their" | thay/thaim/thair | they/hem/hir | hy/hem/here |
| "such" | slik | swich | such |

**Daily grammar drill:** Take the day's line, parse ONE verb fully (person · number · tense · mood ·
strong or weak) and ONE noun or adjective (case · number · strong or weak inflection), then write
the rule it illustrates in your ledger. Do this before reading any modern translation.
