# Chinese — the script for the Analects (論語)

> Script first, grammar second, translation third. The key is not fluency — it is **physical contact
> with the text**. Handwriting slows you down enough to notice structure. Three layers below; go as
> deep as the day allows (set `language_depth` in `logs/studium-state.json`).

---

## Layer 1 — Calligraphy / paleography (the hand)

Classical Chinese is written in **Hanzi** (漢字) — logographic characters, each one syllable, each one
morpheme. The script has no alphabet; you build vocabulary stroke by stroke. The canonical reference
script for reading classical texts is **Regular Script (楷書 kǎishū)**, the same form used in printed
editions of the Analects since the Tang dynasty.

### The eight fundamental strokes (永字八法 — the eight methods of 永)

The character 永 (yǒng, "eternity / forever") contains all eight canonical strokes. Drill these before
any character:

| # | Stroke name | Shape | Motion |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 側 cè — dot | ﹒ | press down, flick right |
| 2 | 勒 lè — horizontal | — | left to right, slight upward arc |
| 3 | 努 nǔ — vertical | \| | top to bottom, steady pressure |
| 4 | 趯 tì — hook | ⌐ | vertical then flick left |
| 5 | 策 cè — rising | ↗ | short diagonal, left-to-right upward |
| 6 | 掠 lüè — long left diagonal | ↙ | sweep down-left, tapering |
| 7 | 啄 zhuó — short left diagonal | ↙ | short, sharp peck downward-left |
| 8 | 磔 zhé — right diagonal | ↘ | press and flare right |

Write 永 ten times before each session. Every character you will ever write is a permutation of these.

### Radical system (部首 bùshǒu) — the anatomical units

Characters are composed of **radicals** (semantic/etymological components) + **phonetic components**.
The Kangxi dictionary organises all characters under 214 radicals. Key radicals for the Analects:

| Radical | Pinyin | Meaning | Example characters |
|---|---|---|---|
| 人/亻 | rén | person | 仁 (rén, benevolence) 信 (xìn, trust) 他 (tā, he) |
| 言/訁 | yán | speech/words | 語 (yǔ, speech) 論 (lùn, discourse) 謂 (wèi, to call) |
| 心/忄 | xīn | heart/mind | 忠 (zhōng, loyalty) 恕 (shù, empathy) 思 (sī, think) |
| 口 | kǒu | mouth | 曰 (yuē, said) 君 (jūn, lord) 吾 (wú, I) |
| 手/扌 | shǒu | hand | 拜 (bài, bow) 損 (sǔn, diminish) |
| 木 | mù | wood/tree | 本 (běn, root) 樂 (lè, joy / yuè, music) |
| 日 | rì | sun/day | 時 (shí, time) 明 (míng, bright) |
| 水/氵 | shuǐ | water | 洗 (xǐ, wash) 泛 (fàn, overflow) |
| 土 | tǔ | earth | 在 (zài, to be at) 地 (dì, earth) |
| 門 | mén | gate | 問 (wèn, ask) 闕 (quē, watchtower) |

**Ductus discipline:** write characters top-to-bottom, left-to-right within that constraint. The stroke
order is not arbitrary — it is the efficient path the brush takes. Violating it produces characters that
look correct but feel dead on the page. Use a stroke-order reference (Hanzi Writer / Arphic) until
the order becomes automatic.

**Seal script (篆書 zhuànshū) awareness:** the earliest Confucian manuscripts and stone inscriptions
use an older, more pictographic form. When you encounter 子 (zǐ, master/son), note that the seal form
shows a swaddled infant — the etymology is embedded. Occasional exposure to seal script deepens
radical recognition.

### Essential vocabulary: the 50 highest-frequency characters in the Analects

The Analects is ~15,900 characters long. The 50 most frequent account for roughly 40% of tokens.
Write each once daily until you can produce it from the meaning alone:

子 (zǐ — Master/he) 曰 (yuē — said) 仁 (rén — benevolence) 者 (zhě — one who / the person who)
人 (rén — person) 學 (xué — to learn) 不 (bù — not) 也 (yě — sentence-final particle)
君子 (jūnzǐ — the noble person) 禮 (lǐ — ritual propriety) 義 (yì — righteousness)
知 (zhī/zhì — to know / wisdom) 道 (dào — the Way) 德 (dé — virtue) 信 (xìn — trustworthiness)
政 (zhèng — governance) 而 (ér — and/but/then) 之 (zhī — it / him / her / 's) 有 (yǒu — to have)
為 (wéi — to act / to be) 以 (yǐ — by means of) 無 (wú — without / there is no) 於 (yú — at / to)
其 (qí — his / its / their) 則 (zé — then / if…then) 乎 (hū — question particle)

## Layer 2 — Glossed reading (decode the line)

The opening of the Analects, Book I, Chapter 1 (學而篇 第一章). Copy every character by hand, then
work the gloss. This passage is the gold-standard daily unit for classical Chinese.

```
子曰：「學而時習之，不亦說乎？
zǐ yuē:  xué ér shí xí zhī,  bù yì yuè hū?
```

| Character | Pinyin | Part of speech | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| 子 | zǐ | noun (honorific) | **The Master** (here = Confucius; lit. "son/child" → honorific title) |
| 曰 | yuē | verb | **said** (classical "to say / said"; distinct from 說 yuè) |
| 學 | xué | verb | **to learn / to study** |
| 而 | ér | conjunction | **and then / and** (sequence, not contrast here) |
| 時 | shí | adverb | **at the proper times / regularly / at due intervals** |
| 習 | xí | verb | **to practise / to review** (lit. young bird repeatedly taking flight) |
| 之 | zhī | pronoun | **it** (anaphoric: refers back to what was learned) |
| ，| — | pause | — |
| 不 | bù | adverb | **not** |
| 亦 | yì | adverb | **also / indeed** (rhetorical intensifier in the 不亦…乎 pattern) |
| 說 | yuè | adjective/verb | **joyful / delightful** (here = 悅; this graph used in early texts) |
| 乎 | hū | final particle | **question/exclamation marker** (rhetorical: "is it not?") |

**Literal:** "The Master said: 'To learn and then at due intervals to practise it — is this not also a delight?'"

**Literary (D.C. Lau):** "The Master said, 'Is it not a pleasure, having learned something, to try it out at due intervals?'"

**The 不亦…乎 rhetorical frame** — "Is it not also…?" — appears three times in this opening chapter.
It is one of the signature moves of the Analects: a proposition offered gently, not asserted flatly.
Confucius invites agreement rather than commands it. Mark this pattern whenever you encounter it.

```
有朋自遠方來，不亦樂乎？
yǒu péng zì yuǎn fāng lái,  bù yì lè hū?
```

| Character | Pinyin | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| 有 | yǒu | **there is / to have** |
| 朋 | péng | **friends / companions** (originally: two strings of cowrie shells = a pair) |
| 自 | zì | **from** |
| 遠 | yuǎn | **distant / far** |
| 方 | fāng | **direction / quarter / region** |
| 來 | lái | **to come** |
| 不亦樂乎 | bù yì lè hū | **is it not a joy?** (樂 lè = joy; different reading from 樂 yuè = music) |

**Literal:** "When friends come from distant quarters — is that not also a joy?"

**Note on translations to compare:** the local corpus holds the Analects in classical Chinese alongside
renderings by Legge (literal, 1861), Lau (Penguin, 1979), Slingerland (contextual, 2003), and Ames &
Rosemont (process-philosophy, 1998). Read the Chinese aloud. Then watch how each translator handles
the compound 君子 (jūnzǐ) in Chapter 1.3: "noble person" vs. "gentleman" vs. "exemplary person" vs.
"authoritative person." The variance is not noise — it is the philosophical argument made visible.

## Layer 3 — Serious grammar (toward unaided translation)

Classical Chinese (文言文 wényánwén) is the literary register used in the Analects, pre-Qin bronze
inscriptions, and the entire classical canon. It differs sharply from Modern Mandarin. Orientation:

### 1. No inflection — position is everything

Classical Chinese morphology is essentially zero. Nouns do not decline. Verbs do not conjugate for
person, number, or tense. The SAME character can be noun, verb, adjective, or adverb depending on
position. The burden borne by endings in Greek or Latin is borne here by **word order** and **particles**.

Default word order: **Subject — Verb — Object** (SVO), but variations are common and carry emphasis.

> 子愛人 (zǐ ài rén) = "The Master loves people" [SVO]
> 人，子愛之 (rén, zǐ ài zhī) = "People — the Master loves them" [topicalization]

### 2. The key particles (虛詞 xūcí — "empty words")

These carry grammatical weight and must be learned cold:

| Particle | Pinyin | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 之 | zhī | (1) pronoun "it/him/her/them"; (2) subordinating "的" (possessive/relative) | 學而時習**之** = practise **it** |
| 而 | ér | (1) sequential "and then"; (2) adversative "but" | 學**而**時習之 = learn **and then** practise |
| 也 | yě | sentence-final: declarative / definitional pause | 仁者，人**也** = benevolence — it is humaneness |
| 乎 | hū | sentence-final: question or rhetorical exclamation | 不亦說**乎**? |
| 矣 | yǐ | sentence-final: change-of-state, completion ("now it is so") | 吾日三省吾身**矣** |
| 者 | zhě | nominalizer: "the one who…" / "that which…" | 學**者** = a learner / one who learns |
| 所 | suǒ | nominalizer of verb phrases: "that which is verb-ed" | 所學 = that which is learned |
| 以 | yǐ | instrumental: "by means of / using / because of" | **以**禮治國 = govern by ritual |
| 於 | yú | locative/directional: "at / in / to / from / than" | 學**於**孔子 = study with Confucius |
| 則 | zé | conditional/logical: "if/when…then" | 學**則**思 = if/when one learns, then think |
| 其 | qí | pronoun (3rd person, often possessive): "his/its/their" | **其**為人也 = as for **his** being a person |

### 3. Negation

| Form | Gloss | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 不 bù | general negation | negates verbs/adjectives in present/future sense |
| 非 fēi | "is not" | predicative negation; negates nouns ("is not X") |
| 無 wú | "there is no / without" | negates existence/possession |
| 未 wèi | "not yet" | negation with temporal force |
| 莫 mò | "none / no one" | negates an implicit universal subject |

### 4. Questions

Classical Chinese has no question inversion. Questions are formed by:
- Adding 乎 / 與 / 邪 at the end
- Using the question word in situ: 誰 shuí (who), 何 hé (what/why/how), 安 ān (where/how)
- The rhetorical pattern 不亦…乎 ("is it not also…?")

> 何謂也？ (hé wèi yě?) = "What does this mean?" [何 in object position, verb 謂]

### 5. The "coverb" — serial verb constructions

Classical Chinese freely chains verbs without conjunctions. Two or more verb phrases in sequence share
a subject, the second describing means, result, or purpose of the first:

> 學而不思則罔，思而不學則殆
> xué ér bù sī zé wǎng, sī ér bù xué zé dài
> "Learn without thinking → you are lost; think without learning → you are in danger."
> (Analects 2.15 — the axiom for how to read this book)

### 6. Four-character (四字格) and parallel structures

The Analects is built on parallelism. When you see two four-character phrases back to back, the second
almost always mirrors the structure of the first. Use this as a parsing scaffold: if you understand
half, the mirror tells you the grammar of the other half.

### Daily grammar drill

Take one sentence from the day's reading. Do three things:
1. **Identify every particle** — write its function next to it in the margin.
2. **Parse the main verb** — is it stative (adjective-like) or dynamic? What is its subject? Its object?
3. **Identify any nominalizer** (者/所) or rhetorical frame (不亦…乎 / …則… ) and name it.

Write the rule each pattern illustrates in your ledger. After 100 sentences, the grammar is inside you.
