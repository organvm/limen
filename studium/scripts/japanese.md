# Japanese — the script for The Tale of Genji (Genji Monogatari)

> Script first, grammar second, translation third. The key is not fluency — it is **physical contact
> with the text**. Handwriting slows you down enough to notice structure. Three layers below; go as
> deep as the day allows (set `language_depth` in `logs/studium-state.json`).

---

## Layer 1 — Calligraphy / paleography (the hand)

Japanese uses **three interlocking scripts**, all active simultaneously. Command all three; reading
classical literature demands them working together on a single page.

---

### 1a — Hiragana (ひらがな) — the cursive syllabary

Hiragana derives from cursive *man'yōgana* (Chinese characters used phonetically). Each symbol = one
mora (consonant + vowel, or bare vowel). Classical texts are heavily hiragana; Genji is written almost
entirely in it, with kanji woven in.

Write each **5×** tracing the ductus: wrist loose, brush-like pressure with a pen.

| あ a | い i | う u | え e | お o |
|------|------|------|------|------|
| か ka | き ki | く ku | け ke | こ ko |
| さ sa | し shi | す su | せ se | そ so |
| た ta | ち chi | つ tsu | て te | と to |
| な na | に ni | ぬ nu | ね ne | の no |
| は ha | ひ hi | ふ fu | へ he | ほ ho |
| ま ma | み mi | む mu | め me | も mo |
| や ya | — | ゆ yu | — | よ yo |
| ら ra | り ri | る ru | れ re | ろ ro |
| わ wa | ゐ wi† | — | ゑ we† | を wo |
| ん n | | | | |

†ゐ and ゑ are classical forms, obsolete in modern Japanese but present in classical texts.

**Voiced variants (dakuten ゛):** Add two strokes top-right: か→が、さ→ざ、た→だ、は→ば.
**P-row (handakuten ゜):** Circle top-right: は→ぱ、ひ→ぴ、ふ→ぷ、へ→ぺ、ほ→ぽ.
**Combination kana:** Small や/ゆ/よ fused with i-column: きゃ kya · しゅ shu · ちょ cho.

**Ductus note:** あ is the hardest — three strokes: a curving horizontal, then a loop that closes and
trails left, then a descending hook. Copy あ twenty times before moving on. の is a single anticlockwise
spiral; learn it next (it is the possessive particle and appears on every line of Genji).

---

### 1b — Katakana (カタカナ) — the angular syllabary

Katakana derives from isolated *parts* of the same man'yōgana characters that produced hiragana.
In classical texts katakana appears in Buddhist manuscripts and glosses; in Genji editions it
marks certain grammatical suffixes. Modern katakana = foreign words/names, but classical literacy
requires recognising both sets.

| ア a | イ i | ウ u | エ e | オ o |
|------|------|------|------|------|
| カ ka | キ ki | ク ku | ケ ke | コ ko |
| サ sa | シ shi | ス su | セ se | ソ so |
| タ ta | チ chi | ツ tsu | テ te | ト to |
| ナ na | ニ ni | ヌ nu | ネ ne | ノ no |
| ハ ha | ヒ hi | フ fu | ヘ he | ホ ho |
| マ ma | ミ mi | ム mu | メ me | モ mo |
| ヤ ya | — | ユ yu | — | ヨ yo |
| ラ ra | リ ri | ル ru | レ re | ロ ro |
| ワ wa | ヰ wi† | — | ヱ we† | ヲ wo |
| ン n | | | | |

**Ductus note:** Compare parallel pairs — hiragana き (two curved horizontals + descender) vs. katakana
キ (two angular horizontals + vertical). The angular economy of katakana makes it faster to write but
harder to tell apart: ソ vs ン (stroke direction differs), シ vs ツ (same). Go slowly.

---

### 1c — Kanji (漢字) — the logographic layer + radical system

Classical Japanese *kana* texts embed kanji for content words (nouns, verb stems, adjectives). Genji
uses the *man'yōgana* tradition: kanji appear both semantically and phonetically.

**Radicals (部首 bushu):** Kanji are composed of radicals — recurring graphic units that index
dictionaries. Learn these 20 high-frequency radicals first, as they appear constantly in Genji:

| 日 hi/nichi — sun/day | 月 tsuki/gatsu — moon/month | 木 ki/moku — tree |
|---|---|---|
| 水 mizu/sui — water | 火 hi/ka — fire | 土 tsuchi/do — earth |
| 山 yama/san — mountain | 川 kawa/sen — river | 田 ta/den — rice field |
| 人 hito/jin — person (亻 as left radical) | 手 te/shu — hand (扌) | 心 kokoro/shin — heart (忄) |
| 言 koto/gen — speech (訁) | 口 kuchi/kō — mouth | 女 onna/jo — woman |
| 子 ko/shi — child | 門 kado/mon — gate | 糸 ito/shi — thread (糸) |
| 草 kusa/sō — grass (艹) | 金 kane/kin — metal/gold | |

**Stroke order (筆順 hitsujun) rules:**
1. Top before bottom.
2. Left before right.
3. Horizontal before vertical (when they cross).
4. Outside frame before inside, close last: 国 = frame → 玉 → close bottom.
5. Centre stroke before flanking strokes in vertically symmetric characters: 小.

**Classical orthography:** Genji was written in the Heian period (794–1185). The orthography is
*rekishiteki kanazukai* (historical kana usage) — spelling reflects older pronunciation: ゐ/ゑ
are written where modern texts use い/え; は is written where the particle *wa* is now spelled わ;
を marks the object particle. When reading Heian texts, expect this archaic layer.

## Layer 2 — Glossed reading (decode the line)

The opening of *Genji Monogatari* (源氏物語), Chapter 1 (桐壺 Kiritsubo). Murasaki Shikibu,
c. 1008 CE. Copy it by hand, then work the gloss.

```
いづれの御時にか、女御、更衣あまたさぶらひたまひける中に、
いとやむごとなき際にはあらぬが、すぐれて時めきたまふありけり。
```

**Transliteration (historical → modern pronunciation notes):**

```
Izure no on-toki ni ka, nyōgo, kōi amata saburahi tamahi keru naka ni,
ito yamugoto-naki kiwa ni wa aranu ga, sugurete tokimeki tamau arikeri.
```

**Word-by-word gloss:**

| Word | Reading | Gloss |
|------|---------|-------|
| いづれ | izure | **which** — interrogative pronoun (classical; modern: どれ) |
| の | no | **'s / of** — genitive/attributive particle |
| 御時 | on-toki | **august reign** — 御 on (honorific prefix) + 時 toki (time/reign) |
| に | ni | **in / at** — locative particle |
| か | ka | **[rhetorical question particle]** — "I wonder in which reign…" |
| 女御 | nyōgo | **consort of the second rank** — imperial consort below Empress |
| 更衣 | kōi | **lady of the wardrobe** — lower court attendant/concubine |
| あまた | amata | **many / a great number** — classical quantity adverb |
| さぶらひ | saburahi | **serving / in attendance** — *saburahu* (to attend; classical verb stem) |
| たまひ | tamahi | **[honorific auxiliary]** — *tamahu* (adds respect to the subject's action) |
| ける | keru | **[past evidential auxiliary]** — *keri* (recollective past; "it was said/it seems") |
| 中に | naka ni | **among them** — 中 naka (interior/among) + に ni (particle) |
| いと | ito | **very / exceedingly** — classical intensifier (modern: とても totemo) |
| やむごとなき | yamugoto-naki | **incomparable / of no mean standing** — lit. "not able to be set aside"; highest praise |
| 際 | kiwa | **rank / station** — degree of social standing |
| には | ni wa | **as for [her rank]** — contrastive topic marker |
| あらぬ | aranu | **not being** — *ari* (to be/exist) + ぬ nu (classical neg. attributive) |
| が | ga | **but / although** — classical concessive conjunction |
| すぐれて | sugurete | **exceedingly / surpassingly** — *suguru* (to excel/surpass) + て te (gerund) |
| 時めき | tokimeki | **prospering / in great favour** — *tokimeku* (to be in the ascendant, flourish at court) |
| たまふ | tamau | **[honorific auxiliary]** — *tamahu* (she was favoured; subject = the Kiritsubo lady) |
| ありけり | arikeri | **there was** — *ari* (to be) + *keri* (past evidential; "there was, it seems, one such person") |

**Literal:** "In which august reign was it, I wonder — among the many consorts and ladies of the
wardrobe in attendance, there was one who, though she was not of incomparably high station,
exceedingly prospered in the Emperor's favour."

**Literary (Royall Tyler):** "In a certain reign (whose can it have been?) someone of no very great
rank, among all His Majesty's Consorts and Intimates, enjoyed exceptional favour."

**Method:** the corpus holds the Japanese text + translations (Waley · Seidensticker · Tyler · Cranston).
After copying and glossing, COMPARE how each translator renders *tokimeki* — "in favour" (Tyler),
"in imperial favour" (Seidensticker), "singled out for favour" (Waley) — translation is where the
book becomes an encounter.

**Heian court vocabulary to recognise on sight:**
- `たまふ / たまひ` tamahu — the ubiquitous honorific auxiliary; marks subjects of high rank
- `けり` keri — past evidential ("it was so, I understand"); Genji's narrative tense
- `なむ` namu — assertive emphasis particle; "indeed / certainly"
- `やむごとなし` yamugoto-nashi — "incomparable, of the highest quality"
- `いみじ` imiji — "wonderful, terrible, extreme" (the Heian all-purpose intensifier)
- `あはれ` ahare — "poignant beauty, touching sorrow" (the aesthetic key of the whole novel)
- `をかし` wokashi — "charming, interesting, delightful" (Sei Shōnagon's word; Murasaki's is *ahare*)

## Layer 3 — Serious grammar (toward unaided translation)

Minimal orientation. Recommended companion: Haruo Shirane, *Classical Japanese: A Grammar* (Columbia UP).

---

### Word order: SOV + postpositions

Japanese is strictly **Subject–Object–Verb**; the verb always comes last. Nothing corresponds to
English word-order logic. Instead, **particles** (postpositions) mark grammatical roles:

| Particle | Function | Example |
|----------|----------|---------|
| が ga / は wa | subject / topic | 女御は *nyōgo wa* — "as for the consort" |
| の no | genitive / attributive | 御時の *on-toki no* — "of the reign" |
| を wo | direct object | 君を *kimi wo* — "the lord [obj.]" |
| に ni | indirect obj. / location / time | 中に *naka ni* — "among them" |
| と to | quotative / with | と言ふ *to iu* — "says that…" |
| も mo | also / even | 月も *tsuki mo* — "the moon also" |
| か ka | question / rhetorical doubt | にか *ni ka* — "I wonder if…" |

**Topic (は wa) vs. subject (が ga):** は marks what the sentence is *about* (often given/shared info);
が marks the grammatical subject (often new info or contrast). Classical texts use が more narrowly
than modern Japanese; learn to feel the distinction.

---

### Classical verb forms (用言 yōgen)

Classical verbs have **six inflectional bases (活用形 katsuyōkei)**:

| Base | Name | Use |
|------|------|-----|
| 未然形 mizenkei | irrealis | + negation (ず zu), conjecture (む mu), causative |
| 連用形 renyōkei | continuative | + て te (gerund), conjunctions, noun forms |
| 終止形 shūshikei | terminal | ends a sentence |
| 連体形 rentaikei | attributive | modifies a noun |
| 已然形 izenkei | realis/perfective | + ば ba (conditional/causal), など |
| 命令形 meireikei | imperative | commands |

Example — verb *ari* (あり, "to be/exist"):

| Base | Form | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| mizenkei | あら ara | あらぬ *aranu* (not being) |
| renyōkei | あり ari | ありて *arite* (being, and…) |
| shūshikei | あり ari | あり *ari* (exists.) |
| rentaikei | ある aru | あるもの *aru mono* (a thing that exists) |
| izenkei | あれ are | あれば *areba* (since/when it is) |
| meireikei | あれ are | あれ *are* (be!) |

**Key point:** the rentaikei (attributive) form is critical in classical Japanese because entire
clauses modify nouns — "the person who attended" is rendered as the verb in rentaikei + the noun,
with no relative pronoun: `さぶらひたまふ人` *saburahi tamahu hito*.

---

### Classical auxiliaries (助動詞 jodōshi) — the grammar engine

Auxiliaries attach to verb bases and carry tense, mood, aspect, evidentiality, and honorification.
These are the most structurally dense part of classical Japanese:

| Auxiliary | Attaches to | Meaning |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| ぬ nu / ず zu | mizenkei | negation — *aranu* "not being" |
| き ki / けり keri | renyōkei | past / evidential past — *keri* = "it was (so I've heard/recall)" |
| たり tari / り ri | renyōkei / izenkei | perfective aspect — "has done" |
| む mu / むず muzu | mizenkei | conjecture / intention — "probably / will" |
| まし mashi | mizenkei | counterfactual — "would have / if only" |
| らむ ramu | shūshikei | present conjecture — "must be doing now" |
| けむ kemu | renyōkei | past conjecture — "must have done" |
| べし beshi | shūshikei | duty / certainty / strong conjecture — "must / should" |

**Honorific system:** Classical Japanese has layered honorification built into the auxiliary system.
`たまふ tamahu` (renyōkei: たまひ tamahi) raises the subject's action. `さぶらふ saburahu`
humbles the speaker/actor. Murasaki Shikibu deploys these consistently: every character's
grammatical treatment signals their court rank. Parse the honorifics and you parse the social world.

---

### Classical nouns + no declension

Japanese nouns do **not** decline — case is entirely carried by the particle that follows.
The same noun form appears in every syntactic role; only the particle changes:

```
月 tsuki (moon) as subject:   月が出た tsuki ga deta — "the moon rose"
月 tsuki as topic:             月は美し tsuki wa uruwashi — "the moon [as for it] is beautiful"
月 tsuki as object:            月を見る tsuki wo miru — "to look at the moon"
月 tsuki as genitive:          月の光 tsuki no hikari — "moonlight"
```

---

### Classical adjectives (形容詞 keiyōshi)

Classical adjectives inflect like verbs (six bases). Two types:

- **ku-adjectives (く活用):** 美し uruwushi → 美しき uruwushiki (attributive), 美しく uruwushiku (adverbial)
- **shiku-adjectives (しく活用):** いみじ imiji → いみじき imijiki (attr.), いみじく imijiku (adverb)

The attributive form (ending -ki) is used whenever the adjective modifies a noun directly.
The terminal form (ending -shi) closes a sentence: `月美し` *tsuki uruwushi* — "the moon is beautiful."

### Daily grammar drill

Take one sentence from today's Genji passage:
1. Copy it by hand in hiragana.
2. Identify every particle — write its grammatical function next to it.
3. Find the main verb — identify its base form and which base is being used.
4. Identify any honorific auxiliaries (たまひ / さぶらひ) — who is being honoured?
5. Write in your ledger: one auxiliary form + the rule that governs it.

Work **one sentence per day**. Classical Japanese reveals itself slowly; the honorifics, the
evidential *keri*, the attributive modifiers — each repays three readings.
