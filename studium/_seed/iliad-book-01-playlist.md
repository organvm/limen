**Playlist title:** *Iliad Book I: Rage, Plague, Olympus*
**Length:** about **94 minutes**
**Principle:** do not score Book I as "the Trojan War." Score it as the moment where **private insult becomes historical catastrophe**.

Book I is not yet Hector's death, Patroclus' death, Troy's fall, or Achilles' final extremity. It is the ignition: plague, quarrel, hierarchy, divine leverage, withheld murder, wounded honor, withdrawal, supplication, and Olympian pettiness.

## Reasoning for each decision

**1. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*: Prelude** — opening invocation: "Sing, goddess, the rage…" The strongest opening because Book I begins with **rage as causality**, not battle as spectacle. The *Tristan* Prelude is unresolved pressure. It does not discharge. It suspends. That matches Achilles' *mēnis*: a force introduced before it becomes fully visible. Why it replaces immediate "Mars": "Mars" begins too externally — armies already moving. Homer begins deeper: with wrath as an invisible engine.

**2. Gluck — *Iphigénie en Aulide*: Overture to Act I** — the Greek camp as sacrificial machine. The Achaean world is already morally contaminated by sacrifice, rank, command, forced exchange. Not from Book I, but its mythic environment is adjacent: Greek command culture, sacrificial logic, fathers/kings/generals converting persons into instruments. Book I turns on a brutal grammar of exchange: Chryseis, Briseis, priestly ransom, honor-prizes, plague as divine counterpayment.

**3. Berlioz — *Les Troyens*: "Malheureux roi!"** — Trojan doom / Cassandra register. "Les Troyens overture" was not precise enough. Berlioz works best as **mythic atmosphere**, especially Cassandra's prophetic dread. Use a specific number that carries doomed royal knowledge rather than generic epic grandeur.

**4. Berlioz — *Les Troyens*: Act II Introduction** — Apollo's plague, Chryses' prayer, divine punishment. A dark threshold piece. Chryses' prayer is a successful appeal to divine order; Apollo enforces insulted sacred hierarchy. The music gives the feeling of a world where human arrogance has triggered a force above human law.

**5. Holst — "Mars, the Bringer of War"** — Agamemnon vs. Achilles. The best original pairing, stays. The 5/4 pulse is hostile, asymmetrical, hard to settle into — fits the quarrel. A broken heroic economy: prizes, public status, command authority, sexual possession, visible honor. The scene is verbal, but it is already war.

**6. Stravinsky — *The Rite of Spring*: "The Augurs of Spring"** — the quarrel beneath the quarrel. Book I exposes the ritual violence underneath heroic society. Holst gives military aggression; Stravinsky gives ritualized social violence. Together: both levels of the quarrel.

**7. Brahms — Symphony No. 4, I. Allegro non troppo** — Athena restrains Achilles. Moved away from Olympus to the moment Achilles grips the sword and does not draw it. Brahms is form under pressure, passion inside architecture, rage under grammar — exactly Athena's intervention: she redirects wrath from murder into speech, then withdrawal. The poem is haunted by the murder Achilles does not commit.

**8. Debussy — *La mer*: "De l'aube à midi sur la mer"** — Achilles withdraws to the sea. After the masculine assembly, Achilles returns to the shore and calls Thetis. The sea is maternal, pre-human, divine, liminal. Debussy gives the sea as living threshold.

**9. Mahler — Symphony No. 5, IV. Adagietto** — Achilles and Thetis. Replaces **Wagner's "Siegfried's Funeral March"** for Book I (that belongs later — Patroclus, Hector, Book XXIV). In Book I, Achilles is not yet funeral-monumental; he is insulted, wounded, alive, petulant, terrifying, loved by his mother. The *Adagietto* is private, suspended, self-enclosed grief — wounded inwardness, not public heroic death.

**10. Wagner — *Das Rheingold*: Prelude** — Thetis before Zeus. The best Wagner for the divine machinery of Book I. The Prelude grows like a world-system coming into being — Thetis converts Achilles' private injury into cosmic policy. Her appeal is not consolation; it is geopolitical. Zeus' nod becomes a mechanism that will kill men.

**11. Handel — *Music for the Royal Fireworks*: Overture** — Olympus as hierarchy. Ceremonial authority, court splendor, sovereign display — Zeus' world, power staged as grandeur. Olympus needs more than dark Wagnerian metaphysics; it needs court music: rank, order, display, rule.

**12. Rameau — *Platée*: Ouverture** — Olympus as comic divine household. The Olympian ending is grand and ridiculous at once: Zeus and Hera quarrel; Hephaestus mediates; the gods laugh; mortals suffer below. The gods are theatrical, vain, domestic, socially absurd. Book I ends not with pure majesty, but with divine comedy.

**13. Offenbach — *Orpheus in the Underworld*: Overture** — exit from Olympus. The boldest ironic choice; only at the end, after Hephaestus restores laughter among the gods. It exposes the scandal of the Olympians: immortal beings laugh, drink, flirt, quarrel while human beings are arranged for death.

## Removed / demoted

**Wagner — *Götterdämmerung*: "Siegfried's Funeral March"** — removed from Book I; save for later (Patroclus' death, Achilles' return to battle, Hector's death, Priam and Achilles in Book XXIV, the funeral atmosphere of epic mortality). Book I is not yet funeral music. It is **withdrawal music**.

## The dominant arc of Book I
```
wrath → plague → quarrel → restraint → withdrawal → supplication → divine comedy
```
That is why the playlist moves Wagner/Gluck → Berlioz → Holst → Stravinsky → Brahms → Debussy → Mahler → Wagner again → Handel/Rameau/Offenbach.
