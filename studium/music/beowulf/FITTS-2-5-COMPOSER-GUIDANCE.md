# Beowulf Fitts 2–5: Composer & Work Selection Guidance
## Reference Catalog for Music Arc Curation

This document provides guidance on composer and musical-work selection for each scene anchor in Fitts 2–5. It follows the same structure as the Fitt I curation document but offers options rather than fixed selections, allowing flexibility in final composition choices.

---

## FITT 2: Grendel's Characterization & Eruption

### Scene 1: Grendel Hears the Joy of Heorot

**Thematic requirement:** Dissonance as exclusion; envy and torment; something pressing against glass without being able to enter.

**Emotional register:** Grinding, resentful, claustrophobic. The sound of joy heard but not possessed.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Shostakovich (Symphony No. 8, II. Allegretto or String Quartet No. 14)** — The excluded figure pressing toward a light it cannot possess. Sardonic, grotesque, with the quality of something irremediably outside. *Why:* Shostakovich excels at the music of the condemned; Grendel's torment is theological, not emotional, and requires this darkly satirical register.

- **Bartók (String Quartet No. 6, opening)** — Chromatic, unsettled, the texture of something moving without resolution. The strings have the quality of pressing inward. *Why:* Bartók captures alienation without abstraction; the dissonance is specific and fateful.

- **Alban Berg (Wozzeck, Act I, Scene 2 — fragmented, disturbing orchestration)** — The sound of a consciousness fractured by exclusion. The orchestration is deliberately difficult, unsettling. *Why:* Berg's ability to translate psychological torment into orchestral sound.

- **Penderecki (Threnetody for the Victims of Hiroshima, opening section)** — Cluster chords suggesting something beyond human scale. The strings move in microtonally disturbed patterns. *Why:* Grendel's alienation has something cosmic about it (descended from Cain, condemned by God); Penderecki captures cosmic horror without being merely frightening.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Wagner's darker material (too mythologically grand for Grendel's specific spiritual exile)
- Boulez or total serialists (too abstract; Grendel's torment is theological, not merely formal)

---

### Scene 2: The First Night Raid

**Thematic requirement:** Violence as release and eruption; the moment of attack and its silent aftermath.

**Emotional register:** Shock, the breaking of sleep, sudden devastation, the silence of discovery.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Bartók (Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta, III. Adagio)** — Night music of controlled menace. Chromatic string clusters, the celesta introducing something non-human, percussion mapping footfalls. The piece knows what happens in the dark without showing it. *Why:* Bartók uses sound design rather than narrative description; he captures the texture of violence without depicting it.

- **Ligeti (Atmosphères)** — Dense, shifting clusters that suggest presence without form. The sound of something moving through space. *Why:* Ligeti's approach is uncanny and specific; it suggests menace through harmonic density rather than bombast.

- **Shostakovich (Symphony No. 10, II. Allegro)** — A scherzo with grotesque energy. The violins have a quality of urgency and horror. The movement is relentless. *Why:* Shostakovich captures violence as a kind of grimace, not as triumphant rage.

- **Stravinsky (Rite of Spring, specific passages like "Sacrificial Dance")** — Ritualized, driving, relentless percussion. The music treats violence as ceremonial rather than chaotic. *Why:* If you want to emphasize Grendel's attack as a kind of counter-ritual to Heorot's creation-song, Stravinsky's Rite provides that symmetry. However, this risks making Grendel too mythologically central; use with caution.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Anything too explicitly "battle-music" (Wagner, later Tchaikovsky) — Grendel's attack is not military; it is sudden, silent, and devastating
- Symphonic horror (Mahler's darker movements) — too structured for the disruption of sleep

### Scene 3: Twelve Years of Unrelenting Reign

**Thematic requirement:** Sustained minor/diminished harmony reflecting institutional failure; the weight of duration without relief.

**Emotional register:** Endurance, despair, the texture of watching something continue year after year. Not a single catastrophe but accumulated loss.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Barber (Adagio for Strings)** — This is Fitt I's emotional center, but it applies equally to Fitt II, Scene 3. The characteristic motion of pressing upward, intensifying, pressing further, never resolving. The long climax and silence are the right analog to twelve years of loss. *Why:* The Adagio's polyphonic sorrow (multiple voices accumulating) matches the texture of a whole people's exhaustion.

- **Mahler (Symphony No. 9, IV. Adagio)** — Farewell music of the highest order. Mahler treats time as weight, as something that accumulates. The harmony is constantly reaching, never arriving. *Why:* If Fitt II's Scene 3 is to carry the emotional weight of the whole poem's opening movement, Mahler's Ninth offers that terminal quality.

- **Bruckner (Symphony No. 9, III. Scherzo)** — Repetitive, almost obsessive harmonic movement in minor keys. The sense of something returning again and again. *Why:* Bruckner's approach to repetition as spiritual weight.

- **Shostakovich (Symphony No. 14, entire work)** — A cycle of songs on the theme of death. Each movement is a variation on darkness and endurance. *Why:* Shostakovich's late work treats endurance and loss as a kind of incantation; the repetition is the point.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Anything too sudden or climactic (the point is sustained duration, not dramatic eruption)
- Romantic tragedies (too individualized; Fitt II, Scene 3 is about institutional failure, not personal loss)

## FITT 3: Transition—Grendel's Terror to Beowulf's Summoning

### Scene 1: Hrothgar's Helpless Mourning

**Thematic requirement:** The exhaustion of counsel; kingship hollowed by helplessness; sleeplessness and despair.

**Emotional register:** Weariness, aging, the quiet despair of a king who cannot command what he faces.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Grieg (Peer Gynt Suite No. 1, "Åse's Death" — already used in Fitt I but applicable here for different reasons)** — The processional descent, the bare strings, the refusal of the operatic. This is dignified grief, not lamentation. *Why:* Grieg captures the texture of a person aging into despair without losing formal bearing.

- **Sibelius (Symphony No. 4, opening)** — Sparse, minimalist, with a kind of ancient weariness. The harmonies are modal and cold. *Why:* Sibelius's approach to austerity matches Hrothgar's exhaustion—nothing ornamental, just the weight of time.

- **Vaughan Williams (Symphony No. 5, I. Preludio — already used in Fitt I)** — The searching, liminal quality works here too, but in a different register: this is searching that has found no answer. *Why:* Vaughan Williams's readiness can be turned inward, made to suggest the openness of despair.

- **Satie (Gymnopédie No. 1)** — Simple, repetitive, with a kind of resigned beauty. The music does not struggle; it simply continues. *Why:* Satie's minimalism matches the texture of a king too tired to rage, too responsible to surrender.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Anything dramatically tragic (Fitt I already provided tragedy; Fitt III is the valley after tragedy)
- Music that suggests resolution or hope (the resolution comes in Scene 3, not here)

---

### Scene 2: Heathen Vows at Idol-Shrines

**Thematic requirement:** Spiritual nadir; the breach of human order; structure itself failing.

**Emotional register:** Desperation, the acoustic threshold of form being breached, something beyond human control crying out.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Penderecki (Threnody for the Victims of Hiroshima)** — The sound of structure failing. Cluster chords suggesting something beyond human scale. The acoustic breaches threshold. *Why:* Penderecki captures the moment where human counsel gives way to something cosmic and irremediable.

- **Ligeti (Atmosphères or Lux Aeterna)** — Dense, shifting harmonic clouds that suggest presence without form. The sound of something vast and incomprehensible. *Why:* Ligeti's microtonally disturbed approach matches the texture of spiritual collapse.

- **Schnittke (Concerto Grosso No. 1, final movement)** — Dissonant, fractured, with the quality of communication breaking down. Tonality splinters. *Why:* Schnittke treats the breach of order as the loss of a shared language.

- **Messiaen (Et exspecto resurrectionem mortuorum, opening)** — Harsh, dissonant, with the quality of stone and suffering. The orchestration is deliberately difficult. *Why:* Messiaen's treatment of spiritual crisis as something that deforms sound itself.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Anything too aesthetically beautiful (the Danes' desperation is ugly, misplaced)
- Music that suggests divine answer or redemption (redemption comes later, through the hero, not here)

---

### Scene 3: News Reaches Beowulf in Geatland

**Thematic requirement:** The turn; hope rekindled; the opening of possibility; answering the call.

**Emotional register:** Resolution, clarity, the direction of aid. Something that suggests: a need has been heard; a hero answers.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Vaughan Williams (Symphony No. 5, I. Preludio)** — Used at the close of Fitt I, but applicable here too in a different register: this is readiness becoming direction. The searching becomes purposeful. *Why:* Vaughan Williams's ability to shift liminal openness into purposeful anticipation.

- **Nielsen (Symphony No. 5, II. Presto)** — A sudden shift from minor to major, from fragmentation to clarity. The wind instruments cut through the chaos. *Why:* Nielsen's approach to tonal resolution as both sudden and earned.

- **Copland (Fanfare for the Common Man or Rodeo — "Hoe-Down")** — American, direct, with the quality of will being mobilized. Bright, clear brass. *Why:* If the hero is to be understood as a response rather than a mythological inevitability, Copland's democratic clarity captures that.

- **Bruckner (Symphony No. 8, II. Scherzo opening)** — The sudden shift from darkness to a kind of resolute motion. The horns cut through. *Why:* Bruckner's ability to make spiritual awakening audible without sentimentality.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Anything too triumphant (the triumph comes later, after the fight; here is only the opening of hope)
- Music that resolves the despair completely (the despair continues to shadow Fitts 4–5; only the possibility of answer opens here)

## FITT 4: Beowulf's Voyage Across the Whale-Road

### Scene 1: Choosing the Company and Launching

**Thematic requirement:** Martial gathering; will becoming action; the assembly of means for un-summoned aid.

**Emotional register:** Resolve, preparation, camaraderie, the texture of people moving together toward purpose.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Elgar (Marches: Pomp and Circumstance No. 1 or "Imperial March")** — Bright, ceremonial, with the quality of something sovereign assembling. The brass is clear. *Why:* Elgar captures military preparation as dignified will, not mere violence.

- **Holst (The Planets: "Mars" — but selectively; the opening suggests gathering will before the assault)** — Driving, rhythmic, with the quality of order and purpose. *Why:* Holst's approach to martial energy as cosmic order.

- **Copland (Appalachian Spring — opening section)** — Bright, ascending, with the quality of hope and new beginning. American in register. *Why:* If you want the hero's gathering to feel like a democratic answering rather than a feudal obligation.

- **Prokofiev (Scythian Suite, opening)** — Primitive, driving, with the quality of ancient war-bands assembling. The orchestration is bright and harsh. *Why:* Prokofiev captures pre-Christian martial culture without irony.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Anything too symphonically grand (the fitt is about a small band, not an army)
- Anything suggesting conquest (Beowulf comes to serve, not to claim)

---

### Scene 2: The Voyage Over the Whale-Road

**Thematic requirement:** Seafaring theme; the whale-road as threshold-crossing; passage as transformation.

**Emotional register:** Journey, liminal space, forward momentum. The sea as both obstacle and kinship.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Debussy (La Mer)** — The sea as a living, moving presence. The music suggests motion, light on water, the texture of passage. *Why:* Debussy's impressionistic approach captures the sea as something that moves you, not merely as scenery.

- **Britten (Peter Grimes, "Sea Interludes" — particularly "Dawn" or "Passacaglia")** — Maritime, with the texture of both danger and rhythm. The sea is a character, not a backdrop. *Why:* Britten's treatment of the sea in British musical tradition; the passage has both beauty and peril.

- **Vaughan Williams (Symphony No. 1, "Sea Symphony" — movements II or III)** — The sea as spiritual passage. The choir and orchestration suggest vastness and forward motion. *Why:* Vaughan Williams's approach to the sea as threshold between worlds.

- **Ravel (Daphnis et Chloé, "Lever du jour" — if emphasizing the beauty of passage; or the final "Bacchanal" for energy)** — Impressionistic, flowing, rhythmic. *Why:* If the voyage is to be scored as a kind of ecstatic passage rather than mere travel.

- **Rimsky-Korsakov (Scheherazade, "The Voyage of Sinbad")** — Exoticizing and rhythmic, with the sea as adventure. The orchestration is brilliant. *Why:* If the voyage is to feel like a legendary journey rather than a historical one.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Wagner (Tristan Prelude — too interiorized for the physical passage)
- Anything too static (the point is motion, forward momentum)

---

### Scene 3: Sighting Danish Cliffs and Beaching

**Thematic requirement:** Arrival; the journey resolves into place; gratitude for safe passage; the threshold to danger.

**Emotional register:** Relief, arrival, the resolution of passage into presence. Something that suggests: here, at last—though danger waits.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Handel (Music for the Royal Fireworks — Overture, already used in Fitt I)** — Ceremonial, sovereign, genuinely grand. Can be repurposed here to suggest arrival with dignity. *Why:* Handel's brightness and ceremony match the moment of successful landing.

- **Elgar (Pomp and Circumstance — opening, bright fanfare)** — Triumphant but not arrogant. The music suggests arrival and acknowledgment. *Why:* Elgar's ceremonial register for the moment the hero sets foot on imperiled shore.

- **Strauss, Richard (Thus Spake Zarathustra — "Sunrise")** — The famous opening, ascending, bright, with the quality of a new world emerging. Magnificent. *Why:* If the arrival is to feel like a new chapter opening, Strauss's "Sunrise" captures that threshold-crossing.

- **Delius (On Hearing the First Cuckoo in Spring)** — Gentle, arriving, with natural beauty. The orchestra suggests a landscape opening. *Why:* If the Danish shore is to feel like a threshold of peace (before the chaos of Heorot), Delius's pastoral can work.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Anything too ominous (the danger is ahead, in Heorot, not in the arrival)
- Anything that denies the gratitude for safe passage (the poem emphasizes thanks for good voyage)

## FITT 5: Beowulf's Arrival in Denmark & Threshold-Crossing

### Scene 1: The Coast-Guard Challenges Strangers

**Thematic requirement:** Protocol and order at the threshold; law as strength; the formal testing of newcomers.

**Emotional register:** Tension, protocol, the assessment of strangers. Formal but not hostile—law asserting itself.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Janáček (Jenufa, opening scene — formal questioning in music)** — The orchestration is spare and formal. Voices are tested; identity is assessed. *Why:* Janáček's approach to law and order in music—formal without being rigid.

- **Satie (Trois Gymnopédies, No. 1 — recontextualized)** — Spare, protocol-like, with a kind of resigned formality. Could work if the coast-guard's challenge is to feel formal rather than dramatic. *Why:* Satie's minimalism as law.

- **Shostakovich (Symphony No. 8, opening — sparse, formal interrogation)** — The strings move in stark patterns. There is tension but also protocol. *Why:* Shostakovich's approach to formal interrogation as music.

- **Stravinsky (Symphony of Psalms, opening — formal, ceremonial, ritual)** — Formal, archaic, with the texture of ancient protocol. The orchestration is deliberate and sparse. *Why:* Stravinsky's neo-classical approach to law and order.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Anything too confrontational (the coast-guard is not Beowulf's enemy)
- Anything too lyrical (the moment is formal, not emotional)

---

### Scene 2: Beowulf's Reply—Lineage and Errand

**Thematic requirement:** Clear declaration of identity and purpose; supplication in proper forms; the hero honoring the order he enters.

**Emotional register:** Courtesy, clarity, nobility without arrogance. The texture of one speaking to power while offering aid.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Purcell (Dido and Aeneas — Aeneas's declaration, or similar baroque statement-music)** — Baroque formality, clear lines, courtly register. The declaration is made with dignity. *Why:* Purcell's baroque language matches the formal register of ancient court-speech.

- **Handel (Messiah — "Since By Man Came Death" or similar declarative passages)** — Clear, noble, with the quality of statement-making. The voice (or instruments substituting for voice) is direct and dignified. *Why:* Handel's approach to declaration as noble utterance.

- **Mozart (The Magic Flute — Tamino's introduction, or similar moments of noble declaration)** — Formal, clear, with the texture of a young noble introducing himself. The orchestration is spare and direct. *Why:* Mozart's approach to ceremonial introduction in music.

- **Elgar (Cello Concerto, opening — intimate but noble declaration)** — The famous opening, a single voice (the cello) making a statement before the orchestra. Intimate, dignified, clear. *Why:* If the declaration is to feel like a single hero standing before power, Elgar's cello concerto opening captures that.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Anything too submissive (Beowulf offers aid, not mere obedience)
- Anything too bombastic (he speaks to a king, but as a peer, or as an offering)

---

### Scene 3: The Coast-Guard Grants Passage & Becomes Guide

**Thematic requirement:** Hospitality and the opening of the threshold; protocol satisfied; transition from border to hall.

**Emotional register:** Acceptance, welcoming, the border becoming a gateway. Something that suggests the world opening.

**Suggested composer approaches:**

- **Grieg (Peer Gynt Suite No. 2, "The Return of Peer Gynt" or similar moments of homecoming)** — The orchestra opens, the music is welcoming, the threshold is crossed. *Why:* Grieg's ability to suggest arrival and acceptance in musical form.

- **Delius (A Village Romeo and Juliet — "The Walk to the Paradise Garden")** — The famous interlude, a beautiful threshold between scenes. The music suggests passage into a new world. *Why:* Delius captures the moment of threshold-crossing as beautiful and transformative.

- **Elgar (Dream of Gerontius — moments of guidance and transition)** — The guardian figure leading the protagonist forward. The orchestration suggests escort and acceptance. *Why:* If the coast-guard is to be understood as a guide (which the poem makes explicit), Elgar's treatment of guidance and transition works.

- **Vaughan Williams (The Lark Ascending — opening)** — The transition from earth to sky, from one world to another. The music suggests a gateway opening. *Why:* Vaughan Williams's approach to the crossing of thresholds as musical transformation.

**Critical note:** Avoid:
- Anything that diminishes the coast-guard's role (he is a full character, not a functionary)
- Anything too triumphant (the threshold opens, but what waits inside is dangerous)

## Cross-Fitt Continuities: Musical Bridges

### Bridge from Fitt 2 to Fitt 3
The music should carry harmonic memory of Fitt 2's dissonance into Fitt 3. While Fitt 3 introduces new themes (exhaustion, spiritual collapse), the underlying threat (Grendel) should remain audible in the harmonic fabric. Consider a modulation that maintains some elements of Fitt 2's minor/diminished territory while shifting toward Fitt 3's dirge-like register.

### Bridge from Fitt 3 to Fitt 4
The shift from nadir to hope should be gradual but clear. Scene 3 of Fitt 3 already begins to shift toward ascending motion; Scene 1 of Fitt 4 should extend that ascending motion into martial clarity. The music should suggest continuity: the hero has heard; now he acts.

### Bridge from Fitt 4 to Fitt 5
The arrival at Danish cliffs (Scene 3 of Fitt 4) should create a natural transition into the formal challenge of Fitt 5, Scene 1. The triumphant arrival-fanfare should modulate or transform into the more formal, protocol-based music of the coast-guard challenge. The hero moves from action (Fitt 4) into speech and supplication (Fitt 5).

## Practical Notes for Composition

1. **Each scene should be 3–7 minutes of music.** Fitt I used longer tracks; Fitts 2–5 may require shorter, more modular pieces to accommodate the increased number of scenes (4 fitts × 3 scenes = 12 major scene anchors).

2. **Consider reprising elements across fitts.** If Fitt 2, Scene 1 uses a specific dissonant motif, that motif could recur (transformed) in Fitt 3's emotional landscape, reminding the listener that Grendel remains the underlying threat.

3. **The dominant force progression across all four fitts is:** wrath → grief → fate → grief → law → fate → desire → fate → sacrifice → law → kingship → supplication. Music should reflect these shifts without becoming chaotic.

4. **Voice vs. instrument:** Fitt I uses instrumental selections exclusively. Fitts 2–5 could include vocal works if they enhance the thematic register (e.g., Britten's War Requiem for grief passages; Messiaen's texts-based works for spiritual moments). However, avoid vocal selections that individualize the Danes' grief—it is collective, not personal.

5. **Tempo and dynamic range:** Fitt 2 should be darker, more static in harmony. Fitt 3 should be exhausted and sparse. Fitt 4 should be energetic and rhythmic. Fitt 5 should be formal and clear. The overall arc moves from dark to light, but not triumphantly—rather, from darkness through valleys to the opening of a threshold.

## Reference: Fitt I Force Arc (for comparison)

Fitt I: kingship → grief → fate → kingship → kingship → grief → fate → grief → fate → fate

Fitts 2–5: wrath → grief → fate | grief → law → fate → fate | desire → fate → sacrifice → fate | law → kingship → supplication

**Note the shift:** Fitt I is about the building and maintenance of Hrothgar's hall (kingship-centered). Fitts 2–5 are about the testing of both monster and hero (wrath, desire, sacrifice, law, and supplication). The music should reflect this change in the poem's thematic center.
