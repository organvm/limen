# Mahabharata — Drona Parva: The Boy in the Labyrinth, the Vow, and the Teacher Who Could Not Stop

Drona Parva is the book in which the Mahabharata kills its hope. Abhimanyu — sixteen
years old, son of Arjuna, Krishna's nephew, the future of the Pandava line — enters
the Chakravyuha, the wheel formation, and dies because he knows how to enter but not
how to leave. He dies surrounded by six Kaurava warriors who break every rule of war
to kill him. Arjuna, who was away from the field, returns to learn that his son is dead
and vows to kill Jayadratha before sunset the next day or enter the fire. Drona, the
brahmin teacher who commanded both armies at different moments, presides over the
catastrophe and is himself killed by a lie.

The dominant force is **grief** — the specific, personal grief of a father for a son,
a teacher for his own corruption, a war for the innocence it consumed. The Drona Parva
is the first book in the Mahabharata where the war becomes irreversible: Abhimanyu is
not a warrior who died in battle — he is a boy who was murdered by the rules of a game
his elders designed and could not stop.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 5: II. Allegretto**

Drona, the brahmin who taught the art of war to Pandavas and Kauravas alike, becomes
commander of the Kaurava army. He is a guru fighting his own students, bound by oath
to a king whose cause he does not endorse. Shostakovich's scherzo is grotesque: the
strings in their highest register, the woodwinds in a dance that cannot decide whether
it is comic or menacing, the whole thing moving at a velocity that suggests compulsion
rather than joy. Wrath requires rhythmic pressure; Shostakovich's scherzo is the sound
of a man conducting violence at the tempo of a forced march.

**2. Bach — *The Art of Fugue*: Contrapunctus III**

The Chakravyuha is a work of martial architecture: concentric circles of armed men,
each layer feeding the next, the centre containing the trap. Contrapunctus III is a
fugue in contrary motion — the subject going up in one voice while it goes down in
another, a musical wheel within which resolution is perpetually deferred. Law requires
formal clarity and architecture; the Chakravyuha is the law of the labyrinth, a form
that kills not by randomness but by the precision of its design.

**3. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 6 'Pathétique': III. Allegro molto vivace**

Abhimanyu knows the entrance to the Chakravyuha but not the exit. He penetrates the
wheel, cutting through the outer rings, and the Pandava army follows — but the wheel
seals behind him, and the warriors meant to protect his rear are blocked. Tchaikovsky's
scherzo-march is the only major-key movement of the Pathétique — too fast, too bright,
a runner moving at the velocity of his own doom. Fate requires inexorable motif; here
the motif is velocity itself, the young warrior moving deeper into the formation,
faster than caution, faster than help.

**4. Mahler — Symphony No. 9: IV. Adagio (opening)**

Six Kaurava warriors surround Abhimanyu: Drona, Karna, Ashwatthama, Kripa, Shalya,
Dushasana. They break every rule of war — attack a single warrior en masse, cut his
bowstring, kill his horses, strike him when he is weaponless. He dies on a pile of
enemy bodies. Mahler's Ninth Adagio is valediction at the highest register: the strings
unfolding in a melody that knows it is the last thing it will sing. Grief requires slow
tempo and inward line; Mahler's Adagio is the sound of a world that has lost its future,
and Abhimanyu's death is the moment the Mahabharata loses its innocence.

**5. Holst — *The Planets*: 'Mars, the Bringer of War'**

Arjuna returns at sunset and learns his son is dead. He does not weep — he vows: before
the sun sets tomorrow, he will kill Jayadratha, who sealed the Chakravyuha's exit. If
he fails, he will enter the fire. Mars is the canonical wrath-motif of the Mahabharata
corpus — used for Shishupala's beheading (Sabha Parva), Arjuna's duel with Karna (Karna
Parva), and here for the wrath of a father whose son has been killed by the rules of a
war his own teacher designed. The 5/4 pulse is the engine of historical consequence.

**6. Sibelius — Symphony No. 5: II. Andante mosso**

The next day, Arjuna fights through the Kaurava army in a single long push toward
Jayadratha. The sun seems to hang at noon — Krishna's illusion holds it in place —
and the battle stretches beyond its natural limit. Sibelius's second movement is a set
of variations that seem to circle without advancing: the pizzicato strings, the woodwinds
entering fragment by fragment, the theme never quite cohering. Sacrifice requires ritual
weight and processional build; Sibelius is the procession of hours in a day that will
not end.

**7. Beethoven — Symphony No. 7: III. Scherzo**

Krishna causes the sun to set. Jayadratha, believing the day is over, emerges from his
defensive position. Arjuna kills him with a single arrow. The sun rises again. Beethoven's
Scherzo is the most disorienting movement in the symphonic repertoire — the trio section
in 2/4 against the scherzo's 3/4, the whole thing moving at a velocity that seems to
contradict the meter. Fate here is not inexorable march but fate as trick: the sun that
falls and rises, the head that leaves the shoulders, the vow kept by a god's manipulation
of time.

**8. Bach — *St Matthew Passion*: 'Können Tränen meiner Wangen'**

Yudhishthira, at Krishna's direction, tells Drona that Ashwatthama has been killed. The
lie is technically true — an elephant named Ashwatthama has died — and Drona, believing
his son is dead, lays down his arms. Dhrishtadyumna beheads him. 'Können Tränen meiner
Wangen' (Can the tears of my cheeks) is the aria of tears that cannot undo what has been
done. Supplication requires suspended cadence; the weeping figure in the strings is the
sound of Drona's last thought — the student he loved became the instrument of his death.

**9. Verdi — Requiem: 'Dies Irae'**

Drona continues to command even as his army is destroyed around him. He is a teacher
presiding over the deaths of his students, a brahmin who has become the instrument of
kshatriya destruction. The 'Dies Irae' is judgment at full operatic register — the brass
fanfares, the pounding rhythm, the bass voice declaring the day of wrath. Here the judgment
is not divine but internal: Drona judges himself by continuing to do what he knows is
wrong, and the Dies Irae is the soundtrack of his own condemnation.

**10. Wagner — *Götterdämmerung*: 'Siegfried's Funeral March' (opening section)**

The parva closes in darkness. Drona is dead, Abhimanyu is dead, thousands have fallen,
and the war is not yet half over. Wagner's Funeral March appears for the first time in
the Mahabharata corpus — the low strings and bassoons, the darkness of the Rhine flowing
through the orchestra. This is the first appearance of the terminal register, but only
the opening, as the war has only reached its midpoint. Grief requires slow tempo and
inward line; the funeral march is the terminal register finally earned, the sound of
Drona being carried from the field by the weight of his own tradition.

## The Dominant Arc

The Drona Parva's force arc:

**wrath → law → fate → grief → wrath → sacrifice → fate → supplication → sacrifice → grief**

The arc opens with wrath (Drona's command) and law (the Chakravyuha as architecture) and
moves into fate (Abhimanyu entering the trap) and grief (his death). The centre of the
arc is the double sequence of Arjuna's vow (wrath) and the long day's march (sacrifice)
that culminates in Jayadratha's death (fate). The late movement toward Drona's death —
supplication (the lie that kills the teacher), sacrifice (the Dies Irae), and grief (the
funeral march opening) — completes the parva's tragic asymmetry: the son dies first, the
teacher dies after, and the war continues with neither boy nor guide. Grief is the dominant
force because Drona Parva is the book of what cannot be recovered.
