# Mahabharata — Karna Parva: The Son of Surya, the Chariot Wheel, and the Unlived Life

Karna Parva is the story of the man who should have been king. Karna — the eldest
Pandava, the son of Kunti and the sun-god Surya, set adrift in a basket on the Ganges,
raised by a charioteer, crowned king of Anga by Duryodhana's friendship — finally
receives the command he has waited his whole life to hold. He leads the Kaurava army
for two days, fights the battle he was born to fight, and dies when his chariot wheel
sinks into the earth and the god who was his father withholds the light.

The dominant force is **fate** — not the structural fate of the dice game (Sabha Parva)
or the cosmological fate of the wheel of time (Adi Parva) but the intimate, personal
fate of a man whose every achievement was cancelled by the accident of his birth. Karna
is the Mahabharata's most tragic figure because he was never permitted to be who he was:
the eldest brother, the rightful king, the son of the sun. His tragedy is that he knew
himself only as the charioteer's son, and by the time he learned the truth, he had already
chosen the side that would kill him.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Handel — *Music for the Royal Fireworks*: Overture**

Karna becomes commander-in-chief of the Kaurava army. The son of a charioteer, crowned
by Duryodhana's love, now leads the greatest army on earth. Handel's Royal Fireworks
Overture is ceremonial architecture at full imperial register: the dotted rhythms of
the French overture, the brass and timpani marking the arrival of sovereignty. But the
overture is in D major, the key of triumph, and Karna's triumph is the most fragile in
the epic. Kingship requires ceremonial grandeur; Handel gives it, and the grandeur is
shadowed by everything the listener knows is coming.

**2. Prokofiev — *Scythian Suite*: I. The Adoration of Veles and Ala**

Bhima and Karna meet on the field. Their hatred is older than the war — Bhima has never
forgiven Karna for mocking Draupadi, and Karna has never forgiven Bhima for the contempt
of the warrior-born toward the charioteer's son. Prokofiev's Scythian Suite opens with
a primeval roar — the low orchestra in dissonant clusters, the percussion driving beneath,
the sense of a world older than the laws that try to contain it. Wrath requires rhythmic
pressure; the suite's opening is the sound of violence before civilization, the hatred
of two men who share a secret they do not know they share.

**3. Berlioz — *Symphonie fantastique*: IV. 'March to the Scaffold'**

Karna leads the Kaurava army through a day of continuous battle, defeating each Pandava
in turn. The day is his finest military hour. Berlioz's March to the Scaffold — used in
Sabha Parva for Draupadi's dragging into the hall — here finds a new register: the
processional certainty of a man walking toward his own death with full knowledge. Karna
knows about the curse, the vulnerability of his chariot, the protection Krishna grants
Arjuna. The march rhythm is fate as formal procession: Karna moves through the day as
the condemned man moves through the streets, with the same ceremonial dignity and the
same terminus.

**4. Stravinsky — *Petrushka*: Part I, 'The Shrovetide Fair' (opening)**

Karna is wounded by Bhima's mace. His blood is golden — the blood of Surya — and the
Pandavas see it and understand. But the secret changes nothing. Stravinsky's Petrushka
opening — the fairground cacophony, the hurdy-gurdy melodies, the puppet-show — is the
music of someone performing a role assigned in advance. Karna has lived his whole life
as the charioteer's son; the revelation of his divine blood should change the script,
but the script has already been performed to its end.

**5. Holst — *The Planets*: 'Mars, the Bringer of War'**

Karna and Arjuna face each other at last. The duel between them is the war's central
event — the two greatest archers of the age, the eldest Pandava and the third Pandava,
the rejected son and the prince. Mars returns from Sabha Parva (Shishupala) and Drona
Parva (Arjuna's vow) for its most authoritative appearance: the 5/4 pulse, the grinding
brass, the cumulative wrath of the entire war concentrated in two chariots facing each
other on a field of corpses.

**6. Wagner — *Götterdämmerung*: 'Siegfried's Funeral March' (full)**

Karna's chariot wheel sinks into the earth — the result of a curse. He leaps down to
free it and calls on the rules of war: a man without a chariot should not be attacked.
Krishna tells Arjuna: kill him now. Arjuna does. Wagner's Funeral March in full — the
first complete statement in the corpus — is the sound of the hero's life passing in
review: the bassoons and low strings, the great melody in the brass, the sense of a
world mourning something irreplaceable. Divine-intervention here is not theophany but
its opposite: the god who does not intervene, the colour shift from light to darkness.

**7. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5: I. Allegro con brio**

Karna, on the ground, tries to free the wheel. Surya does not intervene. Arjuna's arrow
takes him in the throat. The Fifth's first movement is fate as pure musical structure:
the four-note motif stated, developed, inverted, fragmented, driven into the closing
cadence. The motif was heard in Adi Parva's wheel of time, in Sabha Parva's dice cascade,
in Bhishma Parva's battle rhythm — and here, finally, at the death of the man who should
have been king. Fate requires inexorable motif; Beethoven's four notes are the war's
longest trajectory, arriving at its necessary terminus.

**8. Mahler — Symphony No. 5: IV. Adagietto (closing)**

Duryodhana finds Karna's body on the battlefield. His friend — the only man who believed
in him without calculation — is dead. Duryodhana's grief is the first moment in the epic
that makes him sympathetic: the villain reveals himself capable of love. Mahler's Adagietto
in its closing bars is grief as final recognition — the strings settling into the tonic,
the last chord held into silence. Grief requires slow tempo and inward line; the
Adagietto's ending is the sound of a man who has lost the only person who loved him
without condition.

**9. Bach — *St Matthew Passion*: 'Aus Liebe will mein Heiland sterben'**

Kunti comes to the battlefield at night. Her firstborn son is dead, killed by her third
son, on a field she could have prevented if she had spoken the truth thirty years earlier.
'Aus Liebe will mein Heiland sterben' (Out of love my Saviour is willing to die) is the
most intimate aria in the St Matthew Passion — the soprano alone with a flute and two
oboes, the melody moving through suspensions that never fully resolve. Supplication
requires suspended cadence; Bach's aria is the sound of a mother who knows her love was
not enough to save her son because it came too late.

**10. Wagner — *Götterdämmerung*: 'Siegfried's Funeral March' (closing section)**

After Karna, the war continues — one more commander, one more day, the night attack,
the women's lament — but the centre has been removed. Wagner's Funeral March closes with
the full orchestra playing the great melody in D-flat major, not triumph but the final
acknowledgment of a life that mattered. The march ends, the war continues, and the epic
has crossed into its terminal phase.

## The Dominant Arc

The Karna Parva's force arc:

**kingship → wrath → fate → sacrifice → wrath → divine-intervention → fate → grief → supplication → grief**

The arc opens with the triumph of Karna's command (kingship) and descends immediately into
the hatred with Bhima (wrath). Fate governs the day of Karna's command (track 3) and is
interrupted by sacrifice (his golden blood revealed, track 4). The central sequence —
wrath (the duel), divine-intervention (the wheel sinking), fate (the death) — is the most
concentrated tragic movement in the epic. The closing grief and supplication (Karna's body,
Kunti's lament, the funeral march) complete the arc: the son of Surya has fallen, and the
war continues without its brightest star. Fate is the dominant force because Karna's entire
life was a fate he was not permitted to know until it was too late to change.
