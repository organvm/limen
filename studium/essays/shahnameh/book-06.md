# Shahnameh Cycle 6: Manuchehr — Vengeance, and the Rise of Sam and Zal

This cycle closes one account and opens the heroic age. Its first half answers Cycle 5: Manuchehr, raised by Fereydun, comes of age, wars on Turan, kills Tur and then Salm, and sends their heads to his great-grandfather — the curse for Iraj fulfilled, the blood-debt paid. Fereydun, at last vindicated, crowns Manuchehr and dies in peace, the only one of the early great kings granted a quiet death. But the cycle's deeper gift is in its second half, where the Shahnameh's center of gravity shifts from kings to heroes. The champion Sam fathers a son, Zal, born with white hair and judged an ill omen; ashamed, Sam exposes the infant on Mount Alborz. The Simurgh, the divine bird, finds him and raises him in her nest until Sam, struck by remorse, climbs the mountain to reclaim the son he threw away — and the bird, releasing her foster-child, gives Zal a feather to burn whenever he needs her. The dominant problem of the cycle is the return of right order: vengeance discharged, kingship restored, and a discarded child redeemed into the hero who will father Rostam, the mightiest figure of the poem. It must score the avenging, the old king's death-in-peace, and then the cruelty, the miracle, and the reconciliation of Zal.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Sibelius — Symphony No. 2, IV. Finale**
The avenger Fereydun prayed for has grown. Manuchehr is armed, blessed, and set at the head of Iran's host to answer Iraj's murder; the long-deferred redress finally moves. Sibelius's Second closes with a finale that climbs out of struggle into a broad, repeated hymn of arrival — a theme that returns and returns, each time more massive, until it becomes a nation's affirmation. Return's register is the homeward surge after long absence; this is the wheel that began turning at Iraj's death now coming round with full force, the promised vindicator riding out and the music swelling toward the justice it has waited a generation to deliver.

**2. Prokofiev — *Alexander Nevsky*, "The Battle on the Ice"**
Manuchehr meets Tur — Iraj's murderer — in the field and cuts him down, beginning the reckoning sword-in-hand. Prokofiev's Battle on the Ice is the canon's great medieval clash: a long, grinding build of galloping ostinato and braying brass that erupts into full collision and does not relent until one side breaks. Wrath's register is rhythmic pressure and unresolved violence; the music is two hosts meeting on a frozen plain, and at its center the single death the whole campaign exists to accomplish — the first of the two heads that will go back to Fereydun.

**3. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 5, IV. Finale**
Salm, the elder conspirator, flees to a fortress by the sea; Manuchehr hunts him down and kills him, and the heads of both brothers are carried to Fereydun. The curse is discharged to the letter. Shostakovich's Fifth ends with a finale whose triumph is hammered, forced, relentless — a major-key conclusion driven home by repeated unison D's that feel less like joy than like the completion of something decreed. Fate's register is the inexorable, hammered close; the avenging of Iraj arrives exactly so — not as celebration but as a sentence carried out, the account closed because it could not have stayed open.

**4. R. Strauss — *Tod und Verklärung***
With his sons avenged, the grief that had broken Fereydun is at last answered; he sets the crown on Manuchehr, blesses him, and dies — the only one of the early great kings granted a death in peace. Strauss's Death and Transfiguration is precisely this arc: a dying man's struggle resolving into a radiant final transfiguration theme that rises and broadens into light. Ascent's register is the upward, scope-opening line; Fereydun's death is not a fall but a rising-out — the old king released from the long wound of Iraj into the transfiguration the music names, his account on earth fully settled.

**5. Parry — *I was glad***
Manuchehr ascends the throne of a world set right, the line of Iraj restored to the center it had lost. Parry's I was glad is the English coronation processional itself — a full-throated choral entrance, antiphonal and architecturally grand, written for a sovereign walking into the abbey amid the acclamation of an assembled realm. Kingship's register is ceremonial grandeur and sovereign display; the anthem is legitimate succession given its full public sound, the new king received by a world that has been waiting for the rightful order to take its seat again.

**6. Mahler — Symphony No. 1, III. (the funeral march)**
The champion Sam longs for a son, and one is born — perfect but for hair white as snow. Sam, ashamed, reads the whiteness as a demon's mark and orders the newborn carried to Mount Alborz and abandoned. A living child is cast out for the color of his hair. Mahler's First turns the nursery round "Frère Jacques" into a cold minor-key funeral march, a child's tune made desolate and wrong — innocence set in a frozen, alien landscape. Exile's register is the unhoused, abandoned line; the movement is exactly an infant left to the wild, a familiar tenderness twisted into something orphaned and exposed on a mountainside.

**7. Stravinsky — *The Firebird*, "Berceuse" and Finale**
What the world threw away, heaven adopts. The Simurgh — the great wise bird of Persian myth — hears the infant's cry, lifts him to her nest on the peak, and raises him among her own young. Stravinsky's Firebird is the canon's miraculous bird made audible: the Berceuse a hushed, otherworldly lullaby, the Finale a slow dawn-glow swelling to radiance. Divine-intervention's register is the sudden vertical color-shift, the breaking-in of another order; the Simurgh descending to save the abandoned child is that intervention exactly — the numinous reaching down to gather up a life that men had condemned, and the music rising from lullaby to sunrise as the outcast is kept alive by wings.

**8. Wagner — *Parsifal*, "Good Friday Music"**
Sam dreams of his abandoned son and is overcome with remorse; he climbs to the Simurgh's nest and begs the boy back, and the bird, releasing her foster-child, gives Zal a feather to burn whenever he needs her. A father's cruelty is undone; the broken bond is healed. Wagner's Good Friday Music is the repertoire's purest sound of redemption and reconciliation — a slow, forgiving radiance, the world made gentle again after long suffering. Salvation's register is the lifted, restoring line; Sam's reclaiming of Zal, and the Simurgh's parting gift, have that quality of grace returning to a place that had known only shame — the feather a promise that the divine help will come again when it is called.

**9. R. Strauss — *Ein Heldenleben*, opening**
Restored to his father, raised in the lore of the Simurgh, Zal grows into the foremost champion of the age — the hero who will love Rudabeh and father Rostam, the mightiest figure of the whole poem. With him the Shahnameh's center of gravity shifts from kings to heroes. Strauss's Ein Heldenleben opens by striding upward through the orchestra in a single sweeping theme — a hero announced at full scope, the horizon of a great life thrown open. Ascent's register is the upward, scope-opening line; Zal's coming-of-age is the dawn of the heroic age itself, the music standing up to its full height with the greatest stories of the poem still ahead.

## The Dominant Arc of Cycle 6

```
return → wrath → fate → ascent → kingship → exile → divine-intervention → salvation → ascent
```

The arc is built in two movements that rhyme. The first answers the previous cycle's grief: the avenger rides out (*return*), the murderer falls in battle (*wrath*), the account is closed (*fate*), the vindicated old king dies into light (*ascent*), and a new reign is crowned (*kingship*). The second movement then re-runs the same shape in miniature on a single child: an innocent is cast out (*exile*), heaven reaches down to save him (*divine-intervention*), a broken bond is healed (*salvation*), and a great life opens (*ascent*). The dominant force is **return** — the restoration of right order after the wound of Iraj, vengeance discharged and the rightful line set back on its throne. But the cycle's quieter argument is in its second half, where it discovers that the same logic of redress that governs kings also governs a discarded baby: what is wrongly thrown away can be brought back. The two ascents — Fereydun's transfiguring death and Zal's heroic dawn — bracket the cycle as a closing and an opening at once. One age of kings has paid its debts and gone to its rest; the age of heroes, the age of Zal and Rudabeh and Rostam, is standing up to its full height. The poem's greatest stories begin here.
