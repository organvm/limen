# Shahnameh Cycle 5: The Division of the World — Salm, Tur, and the Murder of Iraj

This is the original wound of Persian history — the fratricide from which the Iran–Turan blood-feud flows for the rest of the poem. Fereydun, grown old, tests his three sons with a dragon (himself in disguise), names them by how they meet it, and divides the world: the West to Salm the eldest, the East — Turan, China — to Tur the middle son, and Iran, the radiant heartland, to Iraj, the youngest and best-beloved. Envy does the rest. Salm and Tur, enraged that the youngest holds the center, demand a redivision and then murder Iraj when he comes to them in peace, offering even to renounce the crown. They fill his severed skull with musk and send it to their father. Fereydun's grief becomes a curse and a prayer for a vindicator, and the wheel of vengeance — which will turn for centuries, burying generations — begins. The dominant problem of the cycle is grief: the first irreparable loss in the poem, and the discovery that some wounds the Shahnameh will never close. It must score the test, the gift, the envy, the gentle prince's doomed peace-offering, the murder, the father's grief, and the first turn of the wheel toward redress.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Sibelius — *Pohjola's Daughter***
Before he divides the world, Fereydun takes the shape of a dragon and confronts his sons on the road. The eldest, Salm, flees ("a wise man does not fight a dragon"); the middle, Tur, attacks blindly; the youngest, Iraj, faces it with measured courage and address. The test discloses three characters — and three fates — at a single stroke. Sibelius's Pohjola's Daughter is a tone-poem of a hero set an impossible trial, the music probing and weighing, restless with the question of what a man will prove to be. Revelation's register is the thing brought suddenly to light; here the light falls on the sons' souls, and what it shows will decide everything that follows.

**2. Holst — *The Planets*, "Jupiter"**
Fereydun apportions the earth: Rum to Salm, Turan to Tur, and to Iraj the heartland — Iran, the seat, the best of the three. It is a king's most generous and most dangerous act, the bestowal of realms. Holst's Jupiter, and above all its broad central hymn, is the canon's great music of magnanimous, large-hearted sovereignty — the open-handed king giving with both hands. Kingship's register is ceremonial grandeur and sovereign display; Jupiter's generosity is the gift itself, radiant and well-meant, with no idea yet that the very fairness of the gift — the youngest given the center — is the spark that will burn the family down.

**3. Verdi — *Otello*, Iago's "Credo"**
Salm, the eldest, cannot bear that the youngest holds the center; he infects Tur with the same poison, and together they send Fereydun an ultimatum demanding Iran. This is desire as envy — the covetous conviction that what another has is owed to oneself. Verdi's Credo is the canon's blackest aria of motiveless malignity: Iago declaring his faith in a cruel god and a cruel self, glorying in destruction for its own sake. Desire's register is the over-reaching want that will not be satisfied; the brothers' envy speaks in exactly Iago's voice — a grievance dressed as a principle, sharpening itself toward murder.

**4. Fauré — *Requiem*, "Pie Jesu"**
Iraj, refusing to make war on his own blood, rides unarmed to Salm and Tur and offers them everything — the throne, Iran, all of it — asking only for peace between brothers. It is the Shahnameh's purest act of supplication: the powerless gesture of a good man kneeling before hatred with open hands. Fauré's Pie Jesu — a single soprano asking, simply and without defense, for rest and mercy — is the gentlest plea in the repertoire, and its very defenselessness is the point. Supplication's register is the exposed, kneeling line; Iraj's peace-offering has the Pie Jesu's terrible vulnerability, and the listener already knows that this kind of gentleness, met with this kind of envy, does not survive.

**5. Mahler — Symphony No. 6, IV. Finale (the hammer-blows)**
The peace-offering fails. Tur kills Iraj where he stands — the best-beloved son murdered by his brothers, the innocent center of the world struck out of it. Mahler's Sixth ends with the hammer-blows of fate: enormous, muffled strokes that fell the symphonic hero mid-stride, each one an irreversible event. Sacrifice's register is the gravity of a life given up to forces larger than itself; here the life is *taken*, not given, and the hammer-blows are the exact sound of a brother's hand ending the one good son — a blow the whole poem will spend centuries trying to answer.

**6. Barber — *Adagio for Strings***
Salm and Tur fill Iraj's skull with musk and send it to their father in a casket. Fereydun opens it, and the king who bound Zahhak and ruled the world in justice is destroyed — he falls to the earth, tears the throne-room, and grief becomes total. Barber's Adagio is the canon's most universal music of mourning: a single line climbing by slow steps to an almost unbearable height, then breaking off into silence. Grief's register is the unconsoled lament; the Adagio's long ascent and its terrible pause are a father's whole being rising into the loss and finding nothing on the far side. The terminal funeral-march register still belongs to the heroes the poem has yet to make us love; this grief is held in pure strings, and it is enough.

**7. Beethoven — *Coriolan Overture***
Out of his grief Fereydun pronounces a curse on his murdering sons and a prayer to heaven for an avenger to rise from Iraj's line — and with that vow the Iran–Turan war is born, a feud that will run for centuries. Beethoven's Coriolan is the music of a fatal resolve: hammered C-minor unisons, a tragic will setting itself against the world and sealing a doom it cannot now revoke. Fate's register is the inexorable, hammered motif; the overture is a vow turning into a destiny — Fereydun's curse becoming the engine that will drive the poem's longest tragedy.

**8. Dvořák — Symphony No. 9, IV. Allegro con fuoco**
The prayer is answered slowly. Iraj left a daughter; she bears a child, and from that line comes Manuchehr, raised by Fereydun to be the redress the old king prayed for. Out of the worst loss, the wheel turns back toward justice. Dvořák's New World finale gathers its earlier themes into a surging, fire-lit drive home — grief transmuted into forward momentum, a destiny assembling itself out of what was nearly destroyed. Return's register is the homeward surge after long absence; the avenger's birth is the first turn of the wheel back toward the light, the promise that Iraj's murder will not be the end of his line. Manuchehr's war of vengeance, and the rise of the house of Sam, is the next cycle.

## The Dominant Arc of Cycle 5

```
revelation → kingship → desire → supplication → sacrifice → grief → fate → return
```

The arc moves from a gift to a wound to the first promise of redress. The test reveals the sons (*revelation*); the world is bestowed (*kingship*); envy curdles the bestowal (*desire*); the gentle prince offers peace (*supplication*) and is murdered for it (*sacrifice*); the father's grief breaks the age (*grief*) and hardens into a curse (*fate*) that will not be answered for a generation (*return*). The dominant force is **grief** — the Shahnameh's first irreparable loss, the death that teaches the poem its own capacity for mourning. Everything in the early cycles until now has been recoverable: Jamshid's golden age fell but Fereydun restored the throne; Zahhak's darkness lasted a thousand years but was bound at last. Iraj's murder is the first event the poem cannot undo, only avenge — and avenging, as the rest of the Shahnameh will show, is not the same as healing. The wheel that begins turning here will roll over Manuchehr, over the wars of Kay Kavus and Afrasiyab, over Siyavash and Sohrab, gathering blood the whole way. Fereydun's grief is where the long tragedy of the poem is born; the generosity of a father dividing the world is what set it loose.
