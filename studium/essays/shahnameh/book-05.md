# Shahnameh Cycle 5: Fereydun's Sons — The Divided Inheritance

Cycle 5 is the Shahnameh's first fully fratricidal story — and because the Shahnameh is a poem about kingship, the fratricide is not a private crime but a political foundation. Fereydun, having vanquished Zahhak and restored the world, divides his realm among his three sons. Iraj receives Iran, the center, the best portion. Salm and Tur receive Rum and Turan. The envy is immediate and rational — not the envy of the wicked but the envy of those who see a younger brother preferred and cannot bear it. They deliberate, they send messengers, they offer Iraj a chance to relinquish, and when he offers them everything — the crown, the treasury, his life if they want it — they kill him anyway and send his head to their father. The dominant force is **grief** — the grief of an old king who has outlived his own justice, the grief of a world that has just learned that the war between Iran and Turan will be its permanent condition.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Elgar — *Enigma Variations*, "Nimrod"** — *kingship*
Fereydun in old age: the king who bound Zahhak, who restored the world's order, who has ruled for more than a human lifetime, must now decide how to distribute the thing he built. The Nimrod variation is the sound of a sovereign who is trying to be just and may not have the wisdom required. The slow build, the orchestral weight that never becomes impatience — it is the gravity of a king performing the last and most consequential act of his reign.

**2. Walton — *Crown Imperial*** — *kingship*
Salm and Tur bow. They accept the thrones of Rum and Turan. The ceremony is correct. But Walton's march, which sounded triumphant in Cycle 2 for Jamshid's inventions and authoritative in Cycle 4 for Fereydun's binding of Zahhak, here sounds different: the brass is too bright, the rhythm too rigid, the pomp hollow. The sons' resentment is not visible in their behavior — it is audible in the music's wrong register, the gap between what the ceremony claims and what the ceremony conceals.

**3. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5, I. Allegro con brio** — *fate*
Salm and Tur decide. The Fifth returns not as cosmic fate but as human decision crystallizing into irreversible consequence. The four-note hammer is the letter being written, the messenger being dispatched, the trajectory of murder being locked into place before a single drop of blood is spilled. Fate here is the structure of a choice that will not be reconsidered.

**4. Brahms — Symphony No. 1, IV. Adagio opening** — *wrath*
Iraj receives his brothers' ultimatum. He could fight, could muster his army, could turn the war that Salm and Tur are threatening into a real war with a real outcome. Instead, he offers them everything: the crown of Iran, the treasury, the title, his own submission — anything to avoid the blood of brothers. Brahms's opening Adagio to the First's finale is the sound of a generous heart that knows its generosity will be met with violence. The strings rise in a grave, almost sacramental ascent; they are not naive. Iraj's wrath is inverted: the force of a man who refuses to hate, which is the most dangerous force in this poem.

**5. Stravinsky — *The Rite of Spring*, "The Augurs of Spring"** — *sacrifice*
The murder is ritual. Salm and Tur do not kill in a frenzy — they have planned this, traveled for it, and they execute it with the cold formality of a sacrifice. Stravinsky's hammering chords are the heartbeat of a world that has just performed its first ritual of fratricide. The irregular accents are the wrongness of what is happening: this is not a sacrifice to Ahriman or a battle-death or a divine punishment. It is a brother killing a brother because he received a smaller portion of the world. The chords do not stop. The sacrifice continues.

**6. Bartók — *Concerto for Orchestra*, IV. Intermezzo interrotto** — *wrath*
The head arrives. Salm and Tur send Iraj's severed head to Fereydun as a message: this is what your division has cost. Bartók's Intermezzo is a music of violated innocence: a folk-like melody that keeps being interrupted by a cynical, grotesque brass intrusion — the song the world wanted to sing interrupted by the sound of what the world actually is. Fereydun holding the head of his youngest son is that interruption made flesh.

**7. Mahler — *Kindertotenlieder*, "Wenn dein Mütterlein"** — *grief*
Fereydun's grief is not the public grief of a king (Cycle 1's Keyumars, who mourned Siamak with the world as witness). It is a father alone with his son's head. Mahler's second Kindertotenlieder song opens with an image of unbearable tenderness — the mother coming through the door, the child's absence where the child should be — set to music that is private and devastating. The funeral-march register would be wrong here because Fereydun's grief is not ceremonial. It is old, intimate, and without any possibility of restoration.

**8. Debussy — *La mer*, "Dialogue du vent et de la mer" (opening)** — *exile*
Iraj's daughter is hidden — sent to the borderlands where Salm and Tur will not find her, carrying the unborn child (Manuchehr) who will avenge Iraj. Debussy's 'Dialogue du vent et de la mer' opens with an unsettled energy: the wind and the sea speaking in a language that is potential, not yet formed into event. The hidden heir is that dialogue — a story waiting to become a story. Exile's requirement is wandering tonality and open intervals; the opening of the Dialogue is as open as music can be.

**9. Sibelius — Symphony No. 2, IV. Allegro moderato (opening)** — *fate*
Fereydun does not curse his sons. He pronounces the consequence: because you have killed Iraj, Iran and Turan will be at war until the world ends. Sibelius's Second finale opens with a theme of wind-swept inevitability — the brass declaring a statement that sounds like a law of geography rather than a human decree. Fate here is not destiny imposed from above but pattern recognized from within: Fereydun sees the structure his own division has created and names it. The Iran-Turan war, which will occupy most of the Shahnameh, is born in this moment.

**10. Sibelius — Symphony No. 4, II. Molto allegro** — *grief*
The cycle does not deliver vengeance. It waits. Iraj's line survives in a border village, growing toward the vengeance that will be Manuchehr. Sibelius's Fourth's second movement is compressed and isolated — fast, flickering, bleak, the music of something that knows it cannot outrun its fate but keeps moving anyway. Grief here is anticipatory: the war is coming, the poem is already writing the blood that will flow, and this quiet moment before the first campaign is the most terrible.

## The Dominant Arc of Cycle 5

```
kingship → kingship → fate → wrath → sacrifice → wrath → grief → exile → fate → grief
```

The arc opens in the divided court (kingship, twice — first the father's justice, then the sons' poisoned acceptance), moves through the decisive act of plotting (fate), the generous refusal of the victim (wrath inverted), the ritual murder (sacrifice), the grotesque aftermath (wrath as violated innocence), the old king's private devastation (grief), the hidden heir sent into the borderlands (exile), the naming of the permanent structure (fate), and the long waiting (grief again). The dominant force is **grief** — not as an emotion but as the structural condition that the poem now inherits. The Shahnameh's longest war, the war between Iran and Turan, begins not with a battle but with a brother's head in a box.

The funeral-march register is held back. Iraj's death does not earn it — he is murdered, not fallen, dismembered, not mourned in public. The funeral march belongs to Sohrab, to Esfandiyar, to Rostam — to the warriors who die in the open, with the world watching. Cycle 5's grief is quieter, older, and in some ways more permanent.
