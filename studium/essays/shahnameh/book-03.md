# Shahnameh Cycle 3: Zahhak — The Serpent King and the Thousand Years of Darkness

Zahhak is a wound the world is forced to wear for a thousand years. Ferdowsi's account of him is the most openly diabolic passage in the poem: Iblis, the devil, seduces the young Arab prince into murdering his own father; then, disguised as a cook who has taught the prince to eat meat, Iblis asks to kiss his shoulders, and from each kiss a black serpent grows — serpents that can be quieted only by being fed two human brains a day. The reign that follows is devouring made into government. Every dawn, two young men are killed for the snakes; righteousness goes into hiding; the world dims and stays dim. The horror is not an event but a duration — the long grinding habituation of a planet to its own consumption. And yet Ferdowsi plants two hinges of rescue inside the darkness: the royal cooks who secretly spare one youth of each pair, smuggling the saved into the mountains (from whom, the poem says, the Kurds descend), and the blacksmith Kaveh, who, when his last son is taken, finally refuses — tears the tyrant's testament of justice to shreds and raises his leather apron on a spear as a banner. The cycle ends not in deliverance, which belongs to Fereydun, but in the banner lifted and a downtrodden people streaming toward a name they have only dreamed.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Liszt — *Mephisto Waltz No. 1***
The serpent-reign begins with a whisper. Iblis comes to the young Zahhak as a flatterer and feeds his appetite for the throne until the prince consents to his father's murder. The lever is desire — the covetous wish for a crown, dressed by the devil as destiny. Liszt's Mephisto Waltz is the canon's definitive music of diabolic seduction: Mephistopheles tuning a fiddle and luring a dance into ever-hotter abandon, sweetness and rot in the same phrase. Desire's register is the over-reaching want that consumes its object; here it is the devil playing on a young man's ambition until that ambition commits a murder. The dance is irresistible, which is precisely the problem.

**2. Bartók — *The Miraculous Mandarin*, Suite**
Iblis, now a cook, asks to kiss the king's shoulders — and where his lips touch, two black serpents spring up, ineradicable and hungry. The sovereign body becomes a monster's body. This is metamorphosis at its most physically horrifying: a man transformed into an organism of pure appetite. Bartók's Mandarin is the repertoire's great study of a body that will not behave, that goes on craving and moving past every limit — lurching, grotesque, unkillable. Metamorphosis's register is the unstable, shape-shifting line; the Mandarin's music is flesh that has stopped obeying its own form, which is exactly what Zahhak has become.

**3. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 11, II. "The Ninth of January"**
The horror of Zahhak's rule is administrative and daily: every dawn, two young men are killed and their brains fed to the snakes. It is slaughter as routine, death as a standing institution. Shostakovich's "Ninth of January" depicts a massacre — a gathering crowd, then the guns, then a silence full of the dead — with a relentlessness that refuses catharsis. Plague's register is the spreading, impersonal, repeating death, and this movement is exactly that: a machinery of killing that does not vary and does not stop. It scores not one death but the principle of death-as-quota that defines the serpent-reign.

**4. Górecki — Symphony No. 3, I. Lento**
Ferdowsi compresses a millennium into a verdict: under Zahhak, virtue hid itself, the wise kept silent, and the world wore mourning. This is exile not of a person but of the good itself — justice driven underground, the rightful order banished from the open air. Górecki's Third opens with a vast, slow canon rising out of the lowest strings like grief becoming geological — a whole world's sorrow held at one unhurried tempo. Exile's register is the long unhoused line; the Sorrowful Songs give the *duration* of the darkness, the sense that it has gone on so long it has become the climate.

**5. Bach — *St Matthew Passion*, "Erbarme dich"**
Inside the slaughterhouse Ferdowsi plants a small, decisive mercy: the two royal cooks conspire to kill only one of each day's pair, substituting a sheep's brain, and they smuggle the spared youths into the mountains — and from those rescued lives, the poem says, the Kurds descend. Salvation in the Shahnameh begins this quietly. Bach's "Erbarme dich" — a single alto voice and a solo violin pleading for mercy over the steady grief of the strings — is the most concentrated music of mercy-in-extremity in the canon. Salvation's register is the lifted, redemptive line emerging from darkness; here it is one act of disobedient kindness keeping a remnant alive inside a thousand years of death.

**6. Sibelius — *The Swan of Tuonela***
The tyrant dreams: three warriors come against him, and the youngest, with a mace shaped like an ox's head, binds him and drags him to a mountain. He wakes screaming; the terrified astrologers finally name the doom — a child not yet grown, Fereydun. This is revelation as dread, the future disclosed against the dreamer's will. Sibelius's Swan glides on the black river that circles the land of the dead, a cor anglais singing over muted, shrouded strings — an omen given form. Revelation's register is the thing suddenly made visible; here the visible thing is the king's own death, floating toward him on dark water, and no astrologer can unsee it.

**7. Wagner — *Das Rheingold*, "Nibelheim"**
Zahhak, to shore up his name, forces his nobles to sign a testament of his justice. The blacksmith Kaveh — who has already lost sons to the serpents — is dragged in to sign, and instead roars his grief into fury, tears the document to pieces, tramples it, and walks out with his last son. The forge rises against the throne. Wagner's "Nibelheim" is built on the sound of anvils — eighteen of them hammering in rhythm, the music of labor and metal and the rage of the worked-upon. Wrath's register is rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony; the anvils give the blacksmith's revolt its literal instrument, the hammer turned from making into refusal.

**8. Sibelius — *Finlandia***
Kaveh lifts his leather smith's apron on a spear — the Derafsh Kaviani, the banner that will lead Persia's armies for a thousand years — and the oppressed of the world gather behind it and march to find Fereydun. The cycle of darkness pivots, in its final image, toward the return of the rightful order. Sibelius wrote Finlandia as a coded hymn of a subjugated nation rising against its oppressor: a brooding, grinding opening that breaks into a hymn so plain and broad it became an anthem. Return's register is the homeward surge after long absence; the apron-banner and the hymn are the same gesture — a downtrodden people discovering they are a host, and turning toward a light they had given up expecting.

## The Dominant Arc of Cycle 3

```
desire → metamorphosis → plague → exile → salvation → revelation → wrath → return
```

The arc descends into the serpent-reign and then climbs out of it. It opens with the devil's seduction (*desire*) and the monstrous transformation it produces (*metamorphosis*), bottoms out in the long machinery of devouring (*plague*) and the world driven into hiding (*exile*), and then — crucially — finds its turning points in two acts of refusal: the cooks' hidden mercy (*salvation*) and, after the tyrant's prophetic nightmare (*revelation*), the blacksmith's revolt (*wrath*) and the banner that gathers a people (*return*). The dominant force is **plague** — evil not as a single villainous deed but as duration, a devouring made routine, a thousand years of quota-death that the world is forced to normalize. The cycle's deepest claim is that such evil is undone not by a hero descending from outside but by the small disobediences inside it: a cook who spares one life, a smith who finally says no. Deliverance itself waits for Cycle 4; what Cycle 3 establishes is that the darkness, however long, is never quite total — and that the refusals hidden inside it are where the light re-enters.
