# Shahnameh Cycle 3: The Serpent King and the Blacksmith's Banner

Cycle 3 covers the darkest period of Iranian mythical history—the thousand-year reign of Zahhak—and the rebellion that ends it. This is not a story of a noble prince organizing an army; it is the story of an institutional terror destabilized by a single grieving father. Kaveh the Blacksmith, whose sons have been fed to Zahhak's serpents, refuses to sign a document attesting to the tyrant's justice. He tears it up, raises his leather apron on a spear, and ignites a revolution. Fereydun, the prophesied savior, is the instrument of divine restoration, but Kaveh's wrath is the engine. The cycle moves from the paranoia of fate to the raw anger of the oppressed, culminating in the ceremonial restoration of legitimate kingship.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 4, I. Andante sostenuto**
Zahhak dreams of three warriors, one of whom strikes him with a mace and drags him to Mount Damavand. Tchaikovsky's fate motif is the sound of a destiny that cannot be avoided, hanging over the tyrant's head like a suspended blade. It introduces fate as a terror to the powerful.

**2. Gluck — *Iphigénie en Aulide*, Overture to Act I**
Told that a boy named Fereydun will destroy him, Zahhak slaughters every child he suspects. Fereydun's father, Abtin, is captured and killed. Gluck's overture carries the grave, formal horror of state-sanctioned slaughter, not as a battlefield casualty, but as a systemic requirement of the regime.

**3. Mahler — Symphony No. 5, IV. Adagietto**
Fereydun's mother hides him in a meadow where he is nursed by the magical cow Barmayeh. When Zahhak's men find the meadow, they kill the cow. The Adagietto captures the intensely private, withdrawn sorrow of a mother trying to keep her child alive in a world built on murder.

**4. Holst — *The Planets*, "Mars, the Bringer of War"**
Kaveh storms the court, demanding justice for his murdered sons, and tears up Zahhak's false decree of benevolence. Holst's 'Mars' provides the asymmetrical, hammering, aggressive pulse of a man who has lost everything and is no longer afraid of the king. It is wrath from below.

**5. Bach — *St. Matthew Passion*, "Erbarme dich"**
Kaveh raises his leather blacksmith's apron on a spear. It becomes the banner of rebellion. The people rally to him, pleading for deliverance. Bach's "Erbarme dich" holds the exposed, desperate plea of a wounded community begging for mercy and justice after a millennium of suffering.

**6. Strauss — *Also sprach Zarathustra*, Sunrise**
Kaveh finds Fereydun, who has an ox-headed mace forged. Guided by the angels Jebrail and Sorush, Fereydun crosses the Arvand river. Strauss's sunrise represents the sudden, blinding return of divine favor to the world after a thousand years of darkness—the cosmos itself re-engaging with human history.

**7. Brahms — Symphony No. 4, I. Allegro non troppo**
Fereydun enters Zahhak's capital, shatters his dark talismans, and frees the captive women of Jamshid's line. Brahms's Fourth is form under pressure—the architecture of the world being forcibly righted, law re-establishing its borders against chaos.

**8. Handel — *Zadok the Priest***
Fereydun strikes Zahhak but an angel commands him not to kill the tyrant, only to bind him in the volcano. Fereydun takes the throne, and the Farr is restored. Handel's coronation anthem provides the absolute, unimpeachable ceremonial majesty of legitimate restoration.

## The Dominant Arc of Cycle 3

```
fate → sacrifice → grief → wrath → supplication → divine-intervention → law → kingship
```

The arc moves from the tyrant's fear of the future (fate) through the suffering of the people (sacrifice, grief) into the ignition of revolt. The turning point is Kaveh's righteous anger (wrath), which brings the people together (supplication) and draws heaven back to earth (divine-intervention). The cycle ends with the structural repair of the world (law) and the re-establishment of the throne (kingship).
