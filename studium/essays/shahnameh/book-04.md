# Shahnameh Cycle 4: Fereydun — The Binding of the Serpent

Fereydun is the deliverer who is forbidden to kill. The overthrow of Zahhak is the Shahnameh's great act of liberation, and Ferdowsi tells it as a full heroic sequence: the hidden heir raised in secret on Mount Alborz, suckled by the wondrous cow Barmayeh; the ox-headed mace forged when Fereydun learns his lineage; Kaveh's banner at the head of the host; the great river crossed; the serpent-king's palace stormed; the captive princesses freed. And then, at the climax, when Fereydun lifts the mace for the killing blow, the angel Sorush stays his hand. Zahhak is not to be slain but *bound* — chained in a cave inside Mount Damavand, hung between earth and sky, suspended there until the end of the world. This refusal is the cycle's strange center. Deliverance, it turns out, does not mean the satisfaction of vengeance; it means the *containment* of an evil that cannot be allowed to die and cannot be allowed to rule. The cycle scores the rescue at full scale and then scores the forbidding mercy that denies it the clean heroic ending — and closes on five hundred years of justice, the one long peace the poem grants.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Vaughan Williams — *Fantasia on a Theme by Thomas Tallis***
The deliverer begins his life as a fugitive. Zahhak, warned by his dream, hunts the newborn; Fereydun's mother hides him with the guardian of the wondrous cow Barmayeh, who suckles him, and when the hunters near, carries him up Mount Alborz to a holy man. The rightful line survives only by hiding. Vaughan Williams's Tallis Fantasia is the sound of something ancient and good kept safe in seclusion — a sixteenth-century hymn enveloped in shimmering, sheltering string-choirs, antique and protected and apart. Exile's register is the unhoused, preserved line; here it is the heir of light raised out of sight, a flame kept covered until it is strong enough to be carried into the open.

**2. Wagner — *Siegfried*, "Forging Song"**
Grown, Fereydun is told the truth: his father murdered by Zahhak, the cow Barmayeh slaughtered, the throne stolen from his blood. The disclosure makes him; he commissions smiths to forge a mace whose head is shaped like an ox, in memory of Barmayeh, so that the weapon and the knowledge arrive together. Wagner's Forging Song is the canon's definitive scene of a hero discovering his destiny by *making* its instrument: Siegfried reforging the shattered sword in a blaze of hammer-strokes, singing his own becoming. Revelation's register is the thing suddenly made plain; here the plain thing is who Fereydun is, and the mace ringing on the anvil is the sound of him learning it.

**3. Smetana — *Má vlast*, "Vltava"**
Kaveh's banner-bearing host meets Fereydun and acclaims him; the army marches on Zahhak's seat, and at the river — refused passage by the ferryman — the host crosses anyway, pouring toward the capital like a stream finding its sea. Return's register is the homeward surge, and Smetana's Vltava is literally a river-journey: a brook gathering tributaries, broadening, sweeping past forest and wedding and moonlit water into one great flowing theme. The march of the dispossessed toward the throne that is theirs by right has the same shape — many waters becoming a single current, moving home.

**4. Prokofiev — *Romeo and Juliet*, "Dance of the Knights"**
Fereydun's force takes Zahhak's palace by storm while the serpent-king is away — the idol-temple of the tyranny entered, its talismans broken, the throne seized. Prokofiev's Dance of the Knights is the heaviest, most implacable advance in the repertoire: a brass-and-timpani tread that does not hurry and cannot be stopped, armored menace as pure forward mass. Wrath's register is rhythmic pressure and harmonic weight bearing down; the music is the gate giving way, the assault arriving as an inevitability the defenders can only watch approach.

**5. Beethoven — *Fidelio*, "O welche Lust"**
Inside the palace Fereydun finds Jamshid's two sisters, Shahrnaz and Arnavaz, held by Zahhak, and frees them — captives of the serpent-age brought back into the light. There is no more exact music for this than Beethoven's Prisoners' Chorus: men who have not seen the sun emerging blinking from the dungeon, voices rising hesitantly toward "O welche Lust" — oh, what joy — half-afraid to believe in air and daylight. Salvation's register is the lifted, redemptive line; the chorus catches liberation at the trembling instant before it dares to become joy, which is exactly the freed princesses' first breath.

**6. Holst — *The Planets*, "Mars"**
Zahhak returns and the two meet; Fereydun lifts the ox-headed mace and strikes him down — the exact blow the tyrant saw a thousand years ago in his dream. The killing stroke was foretold; it arrives as mechanism, not surprise. Holst's Mars is the canon's great engine of inexorable doom: a 5/4 ostinato grinding forward under brutal brass, war as a machine set running that cannot be switched off. Fate's register is the hammered, unstoppable motif; the mace falling on Zahhak is the dream completing itself, the future catching up to the present exactly on schedule.

**7. Wagner — *Lohengrin*, Prelude to Act I**
As Fereydun raises the mace a second time to finish Zahhak, the angel Sorush descends and stays his hand: this evil must not be killed but contained. Heaven overrules the hero at the decisive instant. Wagner's Lohengrin Prelude is the repertoire's purest descent of the otherworldly — high divided strings shimmering down from an impossible height, the Grail's light lowering into the human world and lifting away again. Divine-intervention's register is the sudden vertical color-shift, the brass-and-strings contrast of another order breaking in; Sorush's voice arrives like that light, and the killing blow that fate had readied does not fall.

**8. Wagner — *Die Walküre*, "Magic Fire Music"**
Fereydun drives Zahhak to Mount Damavand and chains him in a deep cave, hung between earth and sky, neither dead nor free, to remain until time ends. It is a sentence, not a slaughter — evil placed under permanent judicial seal. Wagner's close to Die Walküre is the same gesture made music: a god lays his disobedient daughter to enchanted sleep on a rock and rings her with fire by decree, binding her against a destined, far-off waking. Law's register is formal finality, the decreed shape that holds; the Magic Fire Music is a sentence sung — solemn, encircling, irrevocable — the serpent bound by command rather than destroyed by force.

**9. Handel — *Zadok the Priest***
With Zahhak bound, Fereydun takes the throne and rules five hundred years in justice — the longest untroubled peace the Shahnameh allows. The mandate, lost since Jamshid, is restored to a king who earned and did not claim it. Handel's Zadok the Priest is the coronation anthem itself — the slow string crescendo gathering for half a minute and then the choir's blazing "Zadok the priest... and all the people rejoiced" — sovereignty conferred and a whole people consenting to it in one sound. Kingship's register is ceremonial grandeur and sovereign display; Zadok is legitimate accession given its full public voice, the world agreeing, at last, on who its king is.

## The Dominant Arc of Cycle 4

```
exile → revelation → return → wrath → salvation → fate → divine-intervention → law → kingship
```

The arc is a deliverance with a hinge in its tail. It rises cleanly through the standard heroic sequence — the hidden heir (*exile*), the discovery of self and weapon (*revelation*), the homeward march (*return*), the storming (*wrath*), the freeing of captives (*salvation*), the foretold blow (*fate*) — and then, at the moment the genre demands a triumphant kill, it bends. The angel's intervention (*divine-intervention*) and the binding sentence (*law*) refuse vengeance its release and substitute containment, and only then does the cycle resolve into the restored throne (*kingship*). The dominant force is **salvation** — the world delivered from the serpent — but the cycle's distinctive theology is in *how* the salvation is completed. Fereydun is the hero who is told, at the height of his triumph, that some evils must be bound rather than destroyed, kept alive under seal because their death would be its own kind of disorder. Zahhak still hangs in Damavand at the end of the world; the deliverance is real, and it is permanent, and it is incomplete by design. That is the price of the only long peace the Shahnameh ever grants.
