# Shahnameh Cycle 4: The Division of the World and the First Fratricide

Cycle 4 establishes the geographical and political fracture that will drive the rest of the epic. Fereydun, having restored the world, divides it among his three sons: Salm (the West), Tur (the East/Turan), and Iraj (the center/Iran). But this act of law breeds resentment. The older brothers, Salm and Tur, convince themselves they have been slighted. Iraj, the youngest and most noble, refuses to answer their threats with armies; he travels to them unarmed, offering to surrender his crown for peace. They murder him anyway. This is the Shahnameh's original sin—the fratricide that permanently divides Iran and Turan, guaranteeing generations of war. The cycle begins in majestic order and ends in blinding grief.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Walton — *Orb and Sceptre***
Fereydun rules for five hundred years in peace. He seeks wives for his three sons from the King of Yemen. Walton's march captures the established, confident majesty of a reign that has vanquished darkness and believes it will last forever.

**2. Handel — *Music for the Royal Fireworks*, La Réjouissance**
Fereydun divides the world: the West to Salm, the East to Tur, and the center (Iran) to Iraj. Handel's music conveys the formal, architectural clarity of drawing borders and establishing treaties—the law as an instrument of peace and administration.

**3. Elgar — *Enigma Variations*, "Nimrod"**
Over time, the older brothers grow resentful that the youngest received the heart of the empire. They remember their birthright and feel slighted. Elgar's Nimrod is used here not for majesty, but for a nostalgic, heavy brooding on what they believe they are owed.

**4. Scriabin — *The Poem of Ecstasy***
Salm and Tur exchange messages, feeding each other's ambition until it becomes a toxic obsession to take Iran. Scriabin's chromatic longing perfectly embodies this unfulfilled, twisting desire that corrupts the brothers' minds.

**5. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*, Prelude**
Fereydun receives their hostile message and prepares for war, but Iraj refuses to fight. He goes to them with a message of peace. Wagner's prelude acts here as the unresolved tension of the brothers' wrath, waiting for Iraj like a coiled spring.

**6. Sibelius — Symphony No. 4, III. Il tempo largo**
Iraj leaves the safety of Iran and travels into hostile territory, vulnerable and without an army. Sibelius's open, stark intervals evoke the isolation and the terrifying emptiness of stepping outside the protection of the law.

**7. Gluck — *Iphigénie en Aulide*, Overture to Act I**
Despite Iraj's pleas for brotherhood, Tur strikes him with a heavy chair and slashes his throat. It is a sacrifice to ambition, destroying the innocent brother. Gluck's overture carries the grave, terrifying weight of familial slaughter.

**8. Barber — *Adagio for Strings***
Fereydun prepares a feast for his son's return, but instead a rider brings Iraj's head. The king's sorrow is absolute; he weeps until his eyes go blind. Barber's Adagio holds the devastating, suffocating grief of a father burying his favorite son, knowing it means endless war.

## The Dominant Arc of Cycle 4

```
kingship → law → memory → desire → wrath → exile → sacrifice → grief
```

The arc traces the breakdown of political order through personal sin. It begins with the heights of sovereign power (kingship) and the formal structuring of the world (law). But human flaws—brooding resentment (memory) and toxic ambition (desire)—destabilize the peace. The tension of unreleased violence (wrath) forces the innocent brother out of safety (exile) into an unjust death (sacrifice), ending in the permanent sorrow of the king (grief).
