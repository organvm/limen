# Shahnameh Cycle 2: Jamshid — The Golden Age and the Glory that Departed

Jamshid is the king who had everything and forfeited it with a sentence. Ferdowsi gives him the longest reign and the most complete civilization in the poem: seven hundred years in which society is divided into its working classes, metal is worked, cloth is woven, ships are built, medicine is found, wine is discovered, and on the day the king rises into the sky on a demon-borne throne, the festival of Nowruz — the Persian New Year, still kept — is founded. And then Jamshid says the thing no king may say: that there is no lord but himself, that the world's order is his own creation. The *farr* — the visible radiance that makes a Persian king a king, the mandate of heaven made light — lifts off him "like a bird," and it does not return. The realm fractures, the nobles defect, and the greatest king of the age becomes a fugitive who hides for a hundred years before the serpent-king Zahhak catches him and saws him in two. This is not a tragedy of action. It is a tragedy of grammar — the exact moment a king's speech overreaches the order that authorized it. The cycle must score the height first, in full, so that the fall has the altitude it needs.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Mussorgsky — *Night on Bald Mountain***
Before Jamshid comes Tahmuras, "the Div-band," who masters the demons and, as the price of their lives, forces them to surrender the secret of writing — script in thirty tongues. The Shahnameh's literacy is wrung out of darkness, taken by force from things that did not want to give it. Mussorgsky's tone poem is the exact shape of that transaction: a demonic horde at full riot, mastered, and then — at the dawn bell — a cold, clear light that the night has been made to yield. The bell that scatters the witches' sabbath is the alphabet emerging from the subdued dark. Revelation here is not a gift descending; it is knowledge wrested upward.

**2. Walton — *Crown Imperial***
Jamshid takes the throne and the world reorganizes around a single radiant sovereign. This is kingship at its zenith, before any flaw shows — pure ceremonial sun. Walton's Crown Imperial is sovereignty displayed without irony: rank, territory, and light on parade. It is deliberately a different register from Cycle 1's Nimrod, which gave Keyumars' primordial, earned-by-being-first weight; Jamshid's glory is *constructed*, imperial, magnificent — and that magnificence is exactly what will become unbearable to him. The march sounds the full height so the listener has felt the altitude before the king claims to be a god.

**3. Bach — *Brandenburg Concerto No. 3*, I. Allegro**
Jamshid is the supreme civilizer of the poem: he separates priests, warriors, farmers, and artisans into their stations, and teaches the working of metal, the weaving of cloth, the building of baths and ships, the use of perfume and medicine. This is law as architecture — society laid down as a structure with every part in its place. The Brandenburg No. 3, scored for nothing but strings in three choirs of three, is the purest architectural object in the canon: motion generated entirely from the relation of equal parts. Civilization as a machine of perfectly fitted gears is exactly what Jamshid builds, and Bach gives it without a wasted note.

**4. Johann Strauss II — *Die Fledermaus*, Overture**
Ferdowsi tells the wine story as a court comedy: grapes left in a jar ferment, are taken for poison, and a woman in despair drinks the "poison" to die — and instead finds joy, sleep, and health, so that Jamshid proclaims the discovery a royal medicine. It is the Shahnameh's one moment of pure courtly delight, and it asks for the register of intoxication-as-comedy. Johann Strauss's Fledermaus Overture is champagne in orchestral form — waltzing, effervescent, faintly tipsy, entirely without menace. Nothing in the repertoire is more literally about the happy accident of drink.

**5. R. Strauss — *Also sprach Zarathustra*, "Sunrise"**
On the day of his greatest glory Jamshid orders the demons to lift his jeweled throne into the sky; he sits in the air like the sun, and the people, seeing it, name the day Nowruz. This is the literal ascent of the king into the place of light. Strauss's Sunrise was rejected in Cycle 1 as too triumphalist for the cosmogony — and that is precisely why it belongs here. What is wrong for the modest first dawn of creation is right for a king's deliberate, demon-powered self-elevation into the heavens: the C-major monument that Haydn's Chaos withheld, Jamshid erects over his own head. The title carries its own irony — it is the sunrise of Zarathustra's own land — and the very next track is the sentence that brings the sun back down.

**6. Scriabin — *The Poem of Ecstasy***
Here is the sentence that breaks the age. After seven hundred years Jamshid gathers his nobles and declares that the world's order, its crafts, its very life are his own creation — that there is no god but himself. The sin is not lust or greed; it is the desire to be worshipped, the self swelling until it claims the place of heaven. Scriabin's Poem of Ecstasy is the canon's purest music of self-deification: a single ego inflating through wave after wave of harmonic intensification toward a trumpet-crowned climax that announces the self as the creating principle of the universe. The ecstasy is real, and it is the disease. This is "there is no lord but me" rendered as sound.

**7. Sibelius — Symphony No. 4, IV. Allegro**
The instant Jamshid claims godhood, the farr leaves him "like a bird." Nothing strikes him; something simply withdraws, and the world's allegiance goes with it. This is fate not as a hammer but as a draining — the light going out of a thing from inside. Sibelius's Fourth is the bleakest music he wrote: a finale that gathers toward a triumph it never reaches and dies away into the cold, a tonal world losing its center. The inexorable thing here is subtraction. The king is still on his throne, but the necessary radiance is gone, and everyone can feel that it is gone. The wheel has turned, silently.

**8. Schubert — *Winterreise*, "Gute Nacht"**
With the farr gone, the world turns from Jamshid as it once turned toward him. Nobles defect, the realm fragments, and the greatest king of the age becomes a fugitive — wandering, hidden, for a hundred years. Schubert's Winterreise opens with a man setting out alone into the snow, dispossessed, walking away from everything that was his. "Gute Nacht" is the archetype of exile as a single figure trudging into a cold that will not end. The king who rode a throne into the sky now puts one foot in front of the other in the dark, and the music already knows there is no return at the end of this road.

**9. Bartók — *Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta*, III. Adagio**
After a century in hiding Jamshid is caught by Zahhak and sawn in half — the most physically appalling death in the early Shahnameh, the sun-king's body fed to the new age of darkness. This is sacrifice in its terminal, horrific sense: the old sovereign destroyed so the serpent-reign can be complete. Bartók's "night music" Adagio is the sound of a body alone in the dark with something coming for it — eerie celesta, xylophone bones, a glissando like a blade, dread that swells and recedes and swells again. There is no requiem here because no one is left to mourn the king; the world has already turned. The age of Jamshid ends on a saw, and the serpent that did it is the subject of Cycle 3.

## The Dominant Arc of Cycle 2

```
revelation → kingship → law → comedy → ascent → desire → fate → exile → sacrifice
```

The arc is a single rise and a single fall, hinged on one word. It climbs through the gift of writing, the radiant throne, the building of the world, the happy accident of wine, and the literal ascent into the heavens — and then, at *desire*, it turns. The claim of godhood is the fulcrum; everything after it is descent: the silent withdrawal of the mandate (*fate*), the wandering of the dispossessed king (*exile*), and the butchery that ends him (*sacrifice*). The dominant force is **kingship** — not kingship triumphant, as in Cycle 1, but kingship *forfeited*. Jamshid demonstrates the law the whole Shahnameh will keep restating: the throne is held on loan from heaven, the farr can be lost in a sentence, and no greatness is large enough to survive a king forgetting where his greatness came from. The serpent waits in the wings precisely because the king made room for it.
