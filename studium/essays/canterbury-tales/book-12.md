# Canterbury Tales — Tale XII: Shipman's Tale — Music Arc

The Shipman's Tale presents marriage as pure commerce: sex, money, and trust are all fungible commodities in a system where everyone profits through successful fraud. A merchant's wife borrows money from a monk, pays him sexually, and then claims she repaid the debt with sexual favors offered to her husband. The monk lies to the merchant (saying he already repaid the debt). The wife lies to her husband (implying the payment was made through sexual compliance). The merchant, though skeptical, chooses not to investigate too closely. Everyone gets what they wanted, everyone lies perfectly, and the system remains in equilibrium.

The dominant force is **comedy** — understood as the perfect inversion of the social order without anyone bothering to right it. No one is punished; no one is redeemed. The tale simply shows marriage, sexuality, and commerce as a single unified system where deception is the only honest language.

---

## The Dominant Arc

The Shipman's Tale moves from the wife's boredom through the negotiation of the affair to the monk's borrowing to the layered deceptions to the final equilibrium. The dominant force is **comedy** — understood as the triumph of a system of fraud that is so perfectly orchestrated that no individual can be blamed. The tale offers no moral because it presents a world where morality has been replaced by commerce. The music is the sound of a system achieving perfect balance through the coordination of individual lies.
