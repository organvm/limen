# Canterbury Tales — Tale XV: Melibee — Music Arc

Melibee is philosophical rather than narrative: an extended dialogue on the nature of counsel, revenge, and peace. Melibee is robbed and his wife wounded. His first impulse is war and revenge. But his wife Prudence calls a council of wise men and, through sustained dialogue, leads him to the conclusion that peace is preferable to war. The tale is the slow unfolding of wisdom through conversation, where each speaker brings a perspective and the whole is greater than the parts.

The dominant force is **law** — understood not as punishment but as the principle of reasoned order that emerges from the collective wisdom of advisors. The movement is from wrath (the immediate impulse to revenge) through deliberation to covenant (the binding of oneself to a principle through the force of collective testimony) to the achievement of lasting peace.

---

## The Dominant Arc

Melibee moves from the violation and initial wrath through the gathering of a council through the deliberation of advisors to Prudence's final counsel to Melibee's choice of peace to the reconciliation with his enemies. The dominant force is **law** — understood as the architecture of reasoned order. The tale celebrates wisdom not as individual insight but as the product of many voices speaking in harmony. By its end, Melibee has learned that true power is not the ability to destroy but the ability to achieve lasting peace through the application of reason and virtue.
