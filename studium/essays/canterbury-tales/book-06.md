# Canterbury Tales — The Man of Law's Tale
## Law, Exile, and the Refusal of Easy Closure

The Man of Law tells a tale about how institutions classify innocence. Constance is not defeated
by one evil act but by the repeated need to prove her legitimacy before men who claim to
administer justice. Its dominant movement is legal force: each scene either creates, stretches,
or restores a claim to lawful speech.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Handel — Music for the Royal Fireworks: Overture** *(law)*

The tale announces itself as procedural. A legal performance is never merely a private confession.

**2. Brahms — Symphony No. 4: I. Allegro non troppo** *(law)*

Law is still the first instrument on the table even when it is failing the subject it claims to
protect. Brahms gives formal weight to that pressure.

**3. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5: I. Allegro con brio** *(fate)*

When institutions cannot guarantee justice quickly, consequence appears as force. Beethoven's
driving motion reflects events that become unavoidable before being explained.

**4. Sibelius — Finlandia** *(exile)*

The tale's great violence is displacement. Sibelius carries public dignity under loss of place.

**5. Wagner — Das Rheingold: Prelude** *(divine-intervention)*

Providential shifts in the poem work structurally, not sentimentally. Wagner's slow accretion of
force suits this register.

**6. Barber — Adagio for Strings, Op. 11** *(grief)*

Constance endures without theatricalizing her wound. Barber's inward stretch is the clearest
musical match.

**7. Elgar — Enigma Variations: 'Nimrod'** *(law)*

The court remains the place of recognition, even when it misrecognizes. Elgar gives legal process
its own dignified patience.

**8. Stravinsky — The Rite of Spring: Sacrificial Dance** *(fate)*

When accusation becomes system, error becomes ritual. Stravinsky catches that cruel regularity.

**9. Mozart — Don Giovanni: Overture** *(desire)*

Desire here is not romance; it is desire to speak as an acknowledged person in a court full of
assumptions.

**10. Pärt — Spiegel im Spiegel** *(salvation)*

The tale closes by moving from procedural injury to a restored, if scarred, dignity. Pärt's vertical
simplicity gives this transition space.

**11. Messiaen — Quartet for the End of Time: I. Liturgie de cristal** *(law)*

Messiaen's final frame restores order without pretending to erase the tale's repetitions.

## The Dominant Arc of the Tale

```
law -> law -> fate -> exile -> divine-intervention -> grief -> law -> fate -> desire -> salvation -> law
```

The dominant force is law: the tale keeps testing whether justice can hold when institutions are
the first to fail the person they claim to protect.
