# Canterbury Tales — Tale XIV: Sir Thopas — Music Arc

Sir Thopas is Chaucer's self-parody: an attempt to tell a chivalric romance that collapses under its own absurdity. Sir Thopas is a ridiculous knight on a low horse, in love with a fairy queen he has never seen, pursuing increasingly absurd adventures in verse so formulaic it borders on mechanical. Every romantic convention is exaggerated until it becomes grotesque. The Host finally interrupts, telling Chaucer his verse is worthless and he should stop.

The tale is not a failure but a brilliant exposure: the form of romance destroys itself through the force of its own conventions. Chaucer takes the structure seriously but exaggerates it past the point of coherence, revealing that the form itself is the joke.

The dominant force is **comedy** — understood as the deliberate destruction of a literary form through the ruthless application of its own logic. The form persists, accumulating episodes and complications, until it becomes undeniable that the whole structure is ridiculous and must be stopped.

## The Dominant Arc

Sir Thopas moves from the introduction of the ridiculous knight through his pursuit of an impossible beloved through his encounters with increasingly absurd obstacles to the Host's merciful interruption. The dominant force is **comedy** — understood as the self-destruction of a form that has forgotten why it exists. The tale is Chaucer's confession: the romance tradition, carried to its logical conclusion, is not noble but absurd. The music is the sound of a form collapsing under the weight of its own conventions.
