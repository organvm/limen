# Canterbury Tales — Tale VIII: Squire's Tale — Music Arc

The Squire's Tale is not narrative romance but pure ornament and opening — the sound of possibilities branching endlessly without conclusion. The Squire's Tale is not in the General Prologue's world of social type; it is in the world of marvelous romance: King Cambyuskan receives magical gifts — a bronze horse that flies, a mirror that shows the future, a ring that lets the wearer understand animals. Three separate love stories branch from these gifts, none of them resolves. The tale is unfinished, and that unfinishedness is not a failure but its essence. The dominant motion is wonder and metamorphosis: the world is not as it seems, reality opens into layers of possibility, magic intrudes on the rational world and refuses to follow its rules.

The dominant force is **metamorphosis** — understood as the constant shifting of identity and possibility. The Squire's world is fluid: horses become birds, women become water spirits, desire becomes transformation. The tale's power is that it refuses closure: the Host interrupts the Squire before the ending, and the listener is left suspended in a world that is still magical, still opening.

## The Dominant Arc

The Squire's Tale moves from the arrival of magical gifts through the branching of three separate love stories, each incomplete, none resolving. The dominant force is **metamorphosis** — understood as the constant state of becoming rather than being. The tale is structure without closure, opening without conclusion. Every character is in motion: the bronze horse flies across impossible landscapes, the falcon is a transformed woman, desire becomes a vehicle for transformation itself. The tale's greatest power is its incompleteness: the world remains magical precisely because it is unresolved. Magic is always more powerful when it is not explained, and the Squire's tale refuses explanation. It simply opens, and opens, and never closes.
