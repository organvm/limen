# Canterbury Tales — Tale XX: Manciple's Tale — Music Arc

The Manciple's Tale is a warning against gossip and tattling. Phoebus owns a white crow that speaks and sings. The crow reveals to Phoebus that his lover has been unfaithful. Phoebus kills his lover and curses the crow: lose your white feathers, lose your voice, become black. The moral is harsh: the crow's faithfulness in reporting truth is punished because truth, when it should remain silent, becomes a betrayal. The tale teaches that the tongue is a weapon that destroys its wielder as easily as its victim. Silence is wisdom; speech is often betrayal.

The dominant force is **fate** — understood as the inescapable pattern that governs speech and its consequences. The crow's transformation from white to black, from singing to silence, is not a mere curse but the natural outcome of speaking truth at the wrong moment.

---

## The Dominant Arc

The Manciple's Tale moves from Phoebus's divine perfection through the lover's infidelity through the crow's revelation through Phoebus's wrath to the crow's transformation to the memory of former glory to the moral on guarding one's tongue. The dominant force is **fate** — understood as the law that governs speech and silence. The tale offers no escape from this law: those who speak unwisely are destroyed by the truth they reveal. The crow's only sin was faithfulness, yet that faithfulness becomes the grounds for its destruction. The wisdom offered is not moral but strategic: the tongue is dangerous, and safety lies in silence.
