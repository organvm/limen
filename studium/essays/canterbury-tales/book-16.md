# Canterbury Tales — Tale XVI: Monk's Tale — Music Arc

The Monk's Tale is a litany of exemplary falls: Adam, Hercules, Samson, Nebuchadnezzar, Belshazzar, Hadrian, Alexander, Antiochus, and others — each fallen from greatness into misery. The form is repetitive, the pattern inescapable: rise, power, hubris, fall, death. The Monk's theme is Fortune's mutability: worldly power and glory are illusions, and Fortune is the force that brings all things to dust. The tale is not narrative but thematic: each exemplum is a variation on a single truth that cannot be escaped. By the end, the pattern is so overwhelming that the Host interrupts, unable to bear hearing any more tales of destruction.

The dominant force is **fate** — understood as the inexorable working-out of a pattern that is beyond any individual's control or resistance. The music must capture both the grandeur of each individual fall and the accumulating weight of the pattern: fate is not judgment but mathematics.

## The Dominant Arc

The Monk's Tale moves from the announcement of his theme through exemplum after exemplum of falls, each illustrating the same truth, to the final revelation that Fortune rules all estates and the Host's merciful interruption. The dominant force is **fate** — understood as the pattern that no individual can escape. The power of the tale is not in any single exemplum but in the accumulation: by the time the Host interrupts, the listener has internalized the pattern and cannot deny its truth. All rise, all fall. This is fate: not judgment but the law of existence itself.
