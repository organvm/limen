# Canterbury Tales — The Reeve's Tale
## Retaliation as Social Accounting

The Reeve responds to the Miller not with greater vulgarity but with sharper instrumentality. The tale
is built around a household that measures everything: grain, labor, bodies, and humiliation. Where the
Miller used false clerical language to license desire, the Reeve uses household process to license a
reciprocal injury. By the end, the characters have all acted as accountants of appetite.

The dominant motion is retaliation moving through institutions. The form is comic, but the comedy is
strict, procedural, and exacting.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Rossini — Il barbiere di Siviglia: Overture** *(comedy)*

The Reeve doesn't counter a charge with apology; he counter-responds with a form. Rossini's overture
is the right sonic opening for this correction: agile, formal, and already aware of social one-upmanship.

**2. Brahms — Symphony No. 4, I. Allegro non troppo** *(law)*

The mill is law in motion. Brahms supplies architecture with internal pressure — a music for people who
believe in structure while practicing revenge through it.

**3. Monteverdi — L'Orfeo: Sinfonia** *(desire)*

The newcomers arrive as polite laborers but quickly become active players in an erotic economy. Monteverdi
provides theatrical momentum and courtly sparkle without emotional resolution.

**4. Mozart — The Magic Flute: Overture** *(desire)*

Household authority now moves from one gender to another. Mozart's bright opening gives this reversal
clarity: elegance can conceal tactical speed.

**5. Offenbach — Orpheus in the Underworld: Galop infernal** *(comedy)*

John's confidence is the tale's easy target. Offenbach's comic machinery turns self-assurance into
its own catastrophe before catastrophe has occurred.

**6. Beethoven — Symphony No. 9, IV. Finale** *(wrath)*

The theft is not random; it is retaliatory arithmetic. Beethoven's finale-scale force can carry collective
deliberate action rather than solitary impulse.

**7. Schubert — Symphony No. 9 in C major, II: Scherzo** *(comedy)*

The dark room sequence depends on pace and swap. Schubert's scherzo atmosphere fits the comic precision
of people pretending to be other people while never escaping recognition.

**8. Stravinsky — The Rite of Spring: Rite Scene** *(fate)*

The punishment arrives with imperfect identification. Stravinsky's famous ritual energy gives this
misalignment of intention and outcome a non-negotiable force.

**9. Barber — Adagio for Strings** *(grief)*

After the laughter, the aftermath is intimate harm. Barber gives the scene an emotional center
without sentimental excess.

**10. Handel — Royal Fireworks Suite No. 2 in F major** *(law)*

The settlement stage returns speech to public register. Handel recovers ceremonial balance while
acknowledging that balance now contains damage.

**11. Rameau — Platée: Ouverture** *(comedy)*

The final laughter is not innocence; it is the knowledge that every role in the tale has already
counted someone out and counted itself in.

## The Dominant Arc of the Tale

```
comedy -> law -> desire -> desire -> comedy -> wrath -> comedy -> fate -> grief -> law -> comedy
```

The dominant force is comedy.
