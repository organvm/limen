# Canterbury Tales — Tale XIII: Prioress's Tale — Music Arc

The Prioress's Tale is a hagiographic miracle built on anti-Semitic violence. A Christian child, filled with pious devotion to the Virgin Mary, sings an Alma Redemptoris hymn through a Jewish neighborhood. He is murdered; his throat is cut. But his miraculous voice continues singing, leading the people to his murderers. The Jews are exposed and punished. The child becomes a saint.

The tale is beautiful, devotional, luminous — because the Prioress believes absolutely in her piety, and the miracle is absolutely real within the theology of the tale. But the beauty is built on the elimination of the Other: the Jews must be evil so that the Christian child can be innocent; the persecution must be just so that the miracle can be pure.

The dominant force is **salvation** — but it is a salvation that requires a murder and justifies ethnic violence. The music must be reverent and gorgeous, understanding that the reverence is inseparable from the horror.

## The Dominant Arc

The Prioress's Tale moves from the revelation of the child's perfect innocence through his murder to the miraculous continuation of his voice to the exposure and punishment of the Jews to his canonization. The dominant force is **salvation** — understood as the transfiguration of a murdered child into a saint. But the salvation is built on a foundation of eliminated Others. The music must be beautiful and terrible at once, capturing both the Prioress's genuine piety and the terrible cost of that piety expressed through the elimination of a people.
