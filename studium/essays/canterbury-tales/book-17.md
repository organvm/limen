# Canterbury Tales — Tale XVII: Nun's Priest's Tale — Music Arc

The Nun's Priest's Tale is mock-heroic animal fable: the style of epic romance applied to the absolutely trivial. A rooster Chauntecleer and hen Pertelote engage in courtly love; he has a dream of a fox; she dismisses it; the fox appears and captures him through flattery; he escapes through wit. The entire narrative is conducted in the language of high romance, with classical allusions, philosophical debate, learned jokes. A barnyard courtship becomes a profound meditation on flattery, nature, predestination, and the power of rhetoric. The music must exaggerate: elevated orchestration applied to the absolutely ordinary, so that the context reveals the absurdity of the form.

The dominant force is **comedy** — understood as the exposure of form through exaggeration. The tale does not mock the form of romance; it performs the form with absolute seriousness, and the seriousness applied to barnyard fowl reveals the form as inherently ridiculous.

## The Dominant Arc

The Nun's Priest's Tale moves from courtly love in a barnyard through prophetic dream to the fox's approach through capture to the pursuit to Chauntecleer's escape. The dominant force is **comedy** — the comedy of form exceeding its subject, of elevated language applied to the trivial, of the entire romantic tradition being performed for an audience of hens and roosters. The tale celebrates both the form and its absurdity: the Nun's Priest loves the conventions of romance and delights in their exaggeration. The music should capture both the genuine artistry of the language and the ridiculous context in which that artistry is being deployed.
