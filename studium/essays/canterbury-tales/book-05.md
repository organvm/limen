# Canterbury Tales — The Cook's Tale
## Appetite, Vanity, and a Fractured Social Rise

The Cook's Tale is famously unfinished, so its force is not closure but abrupt termination.
The tale begins by presenting social rise as a form of appetite and then lets that appetite
consume the form that sustained it. Every scene points toward the same logic: what is
legible as success in a public room becomes unstable the moment desire has to keep paying
for itself.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Rossini — Il barbiere di Siviglia: Overture** *(comedy)*

The Cook arrives after two increasingly severe social counters. A fast and satirical operatic
opening fits the shift from measured courtliness into improvisational appetite.

**2. Bach — Art of Fugue: Contrapunctus 14** *(law)*

Perkyn is introduced within role, rank, and vocational framing. A canonical contrapuntal
structure is the right way to present this first claim to social legitimacy.

**3. Monteverdi — L'Orfeo: Sinfonia (Act I)** *(desire)*

Monteverdi's early scene-like movement captures the energy of new capacity. Perkyn's rise is
expressive and fast, not yet broken by consequence.

**4. Dvořák — Symphony No. 9, I. Allegro** *(desire)*

The music gives broad, social momentum: the movement from craft to display. Desire is no longer
quietly personal at this point; it has become a public language.

**5. Handel — Water Music Suite No. 1 in F major: Hornpipe** *(law)*

The wedding register brings ceremony into the tale. It also reveals ceremony as a shell:
the form that should secure the household has already become a performance of certainty.

**6. Debussy — Prélude à l'après-midi d'un faune** *(desire)*

Desire in this tale is now less aspiration and more drift. Debussy's harmonic sensuality
tracks the moment where appetite becomes routine rather than spark.

**7. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5: I. Allegro con brio** *(wrath)*

The comic motion hardens into aggression. Social friction now carries force rather than play.

**8. Brahms — Symphony No. 4: I. Allegro non troppo** *(exile)*

Exile here is social removal rather than geographic grandeur. Brahms gives a disciplined
withdrawal that matches Perkyn's loss of place.

**9. Tchaikovsky — Piano Concerto No. 1 in B-flat minor: I** *(fate)*

The tale cuts at a point where narrative machinery outruns its own plot. Tchaikovsky's
propulsive opening mirrors an unfinished design hitting hard edges.

**10. Mahler — Symphony No. 5: IV. Adagietto** *(grief)*

The emotional center is the missing coda. Mahler marks what happens when a tale cannot complete
its own claim.

**11. Handel — Music for the Royal Fireworks: Overture** *(law)*

The host restores frame. Law is not justice here; it is continuity as shared agreement.

## The Dominant Arc of the Tale

```
comedy -> law -> desire -> desire -> law -> desire -> wrath -> exile -> fate -> grief -> law
```

The dominant force is desire, but it is desire under strain: it outruns structure faster than
the room can hold it.
