# Tao Te Ching, Chapter 10: Embracing the One

Chapter 10 is the most concentrated spiritual-practice chapter in the Dao De Jing — a series of six rhetorical questions that together form a complete curriculum of inner cultivation. 'Can you embrace the One and not depart? Can you concentrate your qi and become soft as an infant? Can you cleanse your mystic mirror and leave no blemish? Can you love the people and govern the state without acting? Can you open and close the heavenly gate like the female? Can you be bright and penetrating without knowledge?' These are not separate exercises but the same capacity described from six sides: unity, concentration, clarity, non-action, receptivity, and penetrating intelligence without conceptual grasping. The playlist traces this six-sided jewel, moving through each facet without settling into any single one, and culminating in the mysterious virtue that gives without possessing, acts without expecting, and leads without dominating.

**1. Gregorian chant — Te Deum (Mode III)**

*Bao yi* — embrace the One. The first question of Chapter 10 is the foundation of all the others: before concentration, clarity, or non-action, there must be the One that is embraced. The Gregorian *Te Deum* in Mode III (Phrygian) is a single, unbroken line of praise that holds the same tonic throughout, the community of voices embracing the note that does not change. The revelation force is the One itself — not a concept but a practice, an attention that holds a single point while the entire universe of sound moves around it. To embrace the One is to be held by it.

**2. Pärt — Fratres (violin and piano version)**

*Zhuan qi* — concentrate the breath-energy until the body is as soft as an infant's. An infant is not weak; it is completely yielded to life and therefore completely resilient. Pärt's *Fratres* traces the transformation from tension to softness: the repeating figure hardens and relaxes, hardens and relaxes, each cycle softer than the last, until the sound has the quality of a breathing infant that has never learned to hold its breath. The metamorphosis is from the adult's armored body to the infant's yielding flesh — the concentration that leads not to hardness but to softness.

**3. Chopin — Prelude in A Minor, Op. 28 No. 2**

The mystic mirror (*xuan lan*, 玄覽) is the original clarity of the mind before experience writes on it. *Di chu* (滌除) is the washing-clean, the erasure of the accumulated dust of concepts, judgments, and fixed ideas. Chopin's Prelude in A Minor is a meditation that seems to be remembering something forgotten: the melody hovers over dark, repeated chords, searching for a clarity that it almost touches and then lets go. The memory force is the process of cleaning the mirror — not adding knowledge but removing what covers what was always known. The mirror does not need to be polished; it needs to be un-covered.

**4. Bach — Well-Tempered Clavier I, Prelude and Fugue in D Major (BWV 850)**

To love the people and govern without acting (*wu wei er zhi*, 無為而治) is the political dimension of the inner cultivation — the capacity that, once developed in the self, radiates outward without effort. Bach's D Major Prelude and Fugue is music that governs a complete harmonic universe through the simplest means: the prelude is radiant, the fugue is a model of voices that lead without dominating. The law force is the principle of non-coercive order — the ruler who does not need to rule because the people are aligned with the Tao, governing themselves as naturally as water finds its level.

**5. Hildegard von Bingen — O virtus Sapientiae**

The heavenly gate (*tian men*, 天門) is the opening through which life enters and leaves the body — the breath, the attention, the moment of birth and death. To open and close it like the female (*ci*, 雌) is to allow the coming and going of phenomena without grasping, without fear, without interference. Hildegard's hymn to the power of wisdom sings the gate's opening and closing as a single, natural rhythm — wisdom is not something possessed but something that breathes through the one who is open. The gate opens and closes by itself; the sage is the one who does not interfere.

**6. Debussy — 'Minstrels'**

*Ming da* (明達) — bright and penetrating, the quality of a mind that sees clearly without needing to name what it sees. *Wu zhi* (無知) — not-knowing, the intelligence that operates before conceptual thought intervenes. Debussy's 'Minstrels' is music of pure, playful intelligence: it moves through its gestures with the ease of a performer who knows the music so well that thinking about it would only slow it down. The metamorphosis is from the intelligence that knows things to the intelligence that MOVES among things without the mediation of concepts. The minstrels do not plan their performance; they are the performance.

**7. Tavener — The Lamb**

'Birth it but do not possess it' — the first of the three closing principles that define *xuan de* (玄德), the mysterious virtue. The Tao gives life but does not claim ownership of what it gives; the sage follows this pattern. Tavener's setting of Blake's 'The Lamb' is the sound of pure giving: a melody so simple it seems to have been there all along, offered to the listener without the composer's claim on it. The revelation is that giving without owning is the most natural gesture in the world — the lamb is not possessed by its creator; it is released into the world.

**8. Bach — Cello Suite No. 1 in G Major, Prelude**

'Act but do not expect anything' (*wei er bu shi*, 為而不恃) — the second closing principle. Action that expects reward is already contaminated by the future; action that is complete in itself is the action of the Tao. Bach's Cello Suite Prelude is action without expectation: each note is complete before the next begins, the arc of the phrase is present in its first note, and the music asks for nothing beyond the hearing of it. The law force is the structure of action that is its own reward: the cello does not play for applause; it plays because the note is there to be played.

**9. Pärt — Silouans Song**

'Lead but do not dominate' (*chang er bu zai*, 長而不宰) — the third closing principle. The sage leads the way a melody leads: it goes first, but it does not compel the other voices to follow; they follow because the melody is worthy of being followed. Pärt's *Silouans Song* is a melody that leads without dominating: it rises and falls with the freedom of a voice that trusts the silence around it, and the other voices (the orchestra) join because the melody has created the space for them. The metamorphosis is from leading as control to leading as invitation: the sage is the one who goes first into the unknown, and others follow because they see that the way is safe.

**10. Messiaen — Apparition de l'Église éternelle**

*Xuan de* (玄德) — the mysterious virtue — is the quality that unites the six practices of Chapter 10 and the three closing principles. It is not a virtue one possesses but a depth one inhabits: the place where the One is embraced, the qi is concentrated, the mirror is clean, the people are loved without coercion, the gate opens and closes freely, and the intelligence penetrates without knowing. Messiaen's *Apparition of the Eternal Church* is a single, massive crescendo from near-silence to overwhelming organ sonority and back to silence — the revelation of the eternal that appears in time and then withdraws, leaving the listener in the presence of *xuan de*, the virtue that cannot be named because it is the practice itself. The church appears; the church withdraws; what remains is the mystery that was never built and can never be demolished.

---

The dominant arc of Chapter 10:

```
embracing the One → infant softness → the cleansed mirror → governing without action →
the heavenly gate → intelligence without knowledge → giving without possessing →
acting without expecting → leading without dominating → the mysterious virtue
```

The dominant force is **revelation** — the sustained disclosure of *xuan de* as the quality that is not achieved but uncovered, not possessed but inhabited, the mysterious virtue that appears when the six practices are complete and the three principles are lived, revealing that the cultivation was never about acquiring anything new but about uncovering what was always already there.
