# Tao Te Ching, Chapter 12: The Five Colors Blind the Eye

Chapter 12 is Laozi's critique of sensory addiction — not a blanket condemnation of beauty, sound, or flavor, but a diagnosis of how excess inverts into deprivation. The five colors blind the eye (not color itself, but the proliferation that overwhelms). The five sounds deafen the ear. The five flavors dull the palate. Racing and hunting madden the heart. Rare goods impede right conduct. Each sense, pushed to saturation, collapses into its opposite. The sage's alternative is not ascetic renunciation but reorientation: attend to the belly, not the eye. The belly is the seat of contentless presence — what needs, receives, and rests without grasping. The music below moves from the over-saturated sensory field toward the simplicity of unmediated presence, from the fragmentation of the senses to the integration of the belly.

**1. Wagner — Tristan und Isolde, Prelude**

The five colors blind the eye. The Tristan chord is desire made audible — unresolved, chromatic, aching toward a resolution that never comes. This is the five colors as optical overload: the eye is flooded with such intensity that it can no longer distinguish form from form. Wagner's orchestration is the saturation that blinds by brilliance. The eye that sees everything sees nothing.

**2. Scriabin — Poème de l'extase, Op. 54**

The five sounds deafen the ear. Scriabin's ecstatic poem pushes the orchestra to the threshold of pain — the saturation of sound that deafens by its own voluptuousness. The ear cannot distinguish the individual voices; they merge into a single, overwhelming pressure. Scriabin's synesthesia (he heard colors with sounds) makes him the ideal composer for the entanglement of sensory channels that Chapter 12 diagnoses: the colors blind the ear, the sounds deafen the eye.

**3. Debussy — Prélude à l'après-midi d'un faune**

The five flavors dull the palate. Debussy's faun is the figure of satiated desire — after the dream, after the chase, after the imagined consummation, the palate is dull, the pleasuring body begins to fall back into a different relation to the world. The metamorphosis from sensory grasping to a quieter mode of being begins here. The faun does not renounce — he drifts beyond the need for the next sensation.

**4. Stravinsky — The Rite of Spring, 'The Augurs of Spring'**

Racing and hunting madden the heart. Stravinsky's pounding, irregular rhythm is the racing heart of the hunt: adrenaline, pursuit, capture, and the madness that accompanies the chase. The heart cannot rest; it must always pursue the next quarry. The desire has become systemic, built into the body's rhythm, and the body no longer knows what it is pursuing or why.

**5. Stravinsky — The Rite of Spring, 'The Sacrificial Dance'**

Rare goods impede right conduct. They impede because they focus attention on holding, protecting, displaying. Stravinsky's sacrificial dance is the music of possession as ritual violence: the Chosen One must be possessed, taken, held by the community in the most absolute way possible. The impediment to right conduct is the frenzy of grasping. The dancer dances her own destruction because the community cannot stop taking.

**6. Pärt — Tabula Rasa, Part I (Ludus)**

The belly — the seat of contentless presence. Tabula rasa — the erased slate — is the belly before sensation writes on it. Pärt's Ludus alternates between violent, crowded passages and still, empty spaces, tracing the transformation from sensory overload to the clean slate of receptive presence. The belly does not pursue; it receives. The metamorphosis is from the racing heart to the quiet center.

**7. Sibelius — Symphony No. 4 in A Minor, I. Tempo molto moderato**

The eye takes; the belly receives. The reorientation begins. Sibelius' Fourth Symphony inhabits a cold, spare, spacious world far from sensory saturation. The music seems to have exiled itself from the warm, colorful orchestral tradition — it is the eye learning to stop taking, the attention withdrawing from the riot of the senses. Exile as the necessary condition for reorientation.

**8. Schubert — String Quintet in C Major, II. Adagio**

Return to the root — the belly before the eye. Schubert's Adagio is the return of the body to itself: unhurried, spacious, accepting its own mortality and its own capacity for peace. The return from the five colors to the belly is not a loss of richness but a discovery of a deeper, simpler richness. The music breathes with the patience of the belly, not the hunger of the eye.

**9. Gregorian chant — Veni Creator Spiritus (Mode VIII)**

The sage attends to the belly — revelation of the simple. The sage's practice — attend to the belly, not the eye — is a revelation of what has always been present but was invisible through sensory noise. The chant invokes the spirit's arrival into the emptied vessel of the body. The voice is unadorned, unaccompanied, unpreoccupied — the belly made audible. Mode VIII circles the interval of a fourth, never arriving, always present, always receiving.

**10. Bach — Well-Tempered Clavier I, Prelude in C Major (BWV 846)**

Coda: the law of simplicity. Thirty-five measures of perfect harmonic clarity, no note wasted, no gesture ornamental. The C Major Prelude is the eye that has become the belly — seeing without grasping, receiving without taking. What remains after the five colors, five sounds, and five flavors are released is this — one voice, one harmony, one body, one breath. The sage discards the former and takes the latter, and the latter was always there, hidden by the excess of the former.

---

The dominant arc of Chapter 12:

```
five colors → five sounds → five flavors → racing and hunting → rare goods →
the belly → the eye withdraws → return to the root → the sage's practice →
the simplicity that remains
```

The dominant force is **metamorphosis** — not the sudden change from one state to another but the gradual transformation from sensory grasping to integrated presence, from the distraction of the five colors to the gathered attention of the belly, from taking to receiving, from having to being.
