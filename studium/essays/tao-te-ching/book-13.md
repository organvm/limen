# Tao Te Ching, Chapter 13: Favor and Disgrace Are Alarming

Chapter 13 is Laozi's most penetrating analysis of the self as a source of existential trouble. 'Favor and disgrace are alarming' — not because favor is bad or disgrace is good, but because both imply a self that can be evaluated, elevated, or debased. 'Receiving favor is alarming; losing favor is alarming. That is what is meant by "favor and disgrace are alarming."' The chapter then goes deeper: 'The reason I have great trouble is that I have a self. If I had no self, what trouble would I have?' This is not nihilism — it is the recognition that the self is a construct maintained by anxiety, and that the boundary between self and world is the place where all suffering enters. The music below moves from the experience of social evaluation (favor, disgrace) through the recognition of the self as the wound, to the radical possibility of living without a self to protect — from public drama to private dissolution to liberated presence.

**1. Wagner — Tristan und Isolde, 'Liebestod'**

Favor and disgrace are alarming — the self trembles. Isolde's Liebestod is the music of a self that wants to dissolve, to cease being a self, to merge with the beloved beyond the boundary of the ego. But the desire itself keeps the self in place: longing is the self's most intense registration. Wagner's endless melody, never fully resolving, is the alarming condition of wanting favor and fearing disgrace as the same motion of the heart.

**2. Mahler — Symphony No. 5, IV. Adagietto**

Receiving favor debases the self. Favor received is not pure gift — it places the receiver in debt, in the position of the inferior, the one who owes. The Adagietto's intimate grief is the interior cost of being obligated: the self shrinks under the weight of what it has been given. Mahler's strings sing the private sorrow of a self that cannot accept without losing itself.

**3. Barber — Adagio for Strings**

Disgrace frightens the body — the self exposed. Disgrace is the collapse of the social self — the body exposed, judged, found wanting. Barber's Adagio is the slow architecture of exposure: the melody rises like a body standing trial, quivers at its peak, and descends into the acceptance of judgment. Supplication is the force of the exposed self asking for reprieve from a social world that has already condemned it.

**4. Pärt — Summa**

The self is the source of all alarm — the wound identified. Pärt's Summa sets the Credo text in a single motion — the entire statement of belief unfolded as one continuous, quiet gesture. The metamorphosis here is not from one emotion to another but from the scattered self (favor, disgrace, alarm) to the gathered self (attention, stillness, presence). The self is seen for what it is: a pattern of belief, a credo we recite to ourselves every moment.

**5. Górecki — Symphony No. 3, I. Lento**

Great trouble comes from having a self. Górecki's Symphony of Sorrowful Songs opens with an immense, slow canon — a meditation on the weight of existence. The grief here is not personal but structural: having a self is itself the trouble, not this or that misfortune. The music's relentless slow descent suggests that the trouble is not something that happens to the self — it is the self.

**6. Pärt — Spiegel im Spiegel**

If I had no self, what trouble would I have? The most radical question in the Dao De Jing. If I had no self — not selfless as virtue, but self-less as ontology — what remains? Pärt's mirror piece answers: what remains is the relation itself, the between, the reflection without a reflector. The revelation is that the self was never as solid as it seemed. The music holds the empty space where the self used to be.

**7. Debussy — La mer, 'De l'aube à midi sur la mer'**

What is the self made of? Debussy's sea is a self that cannot be fixed — it is continuously reforming, its boundaries provisional, its identity a function of light, wind, and time. The metamorphosis from a fixed self (defined by favor and disgrace) to a fluid self (present but not graspable) happens across this movement. The self is not a thing but a process, like the sea at dawn, never the same from moment to moment.

**8. Tavener — Song for Athene**

The self dissolved — trouble ceases. Salvation in Chapter 13 is not being saved from something but being freed from the self that needs saving. Tavener's Song for Athene is sung for the dead, for the one who has let go of the self entirely. The voice ascends and rests in intervals that do not resolve, that do not need to resolve — because without a self, there is no one to resolve for.

**9. Hildegard von Bingen — O virtus Sapientiae**

Returning to the world without a self. Without the self as trouble, the world is encountered directly — not through the lens of favor or disgrace but as it is. Hildegard's hymn to the power of wisdom celebrates a knowing that is not personal: wisdom that flows through the one who has stepped aside. The return from the self to the world is not a loss but a recovery of participation.

**10. Messiaen — Quartet for the End of Time, 'Louange à l'Éternité de Jésus'**

Coda: the selfless self — trouble and peace are the same motion. Messiaen's louange (praise) is addressed to eternity from a self that has become transparent. The slow cello melody over pulsing piano chords is the closest music comes to the voice of no-self: present without grasping, praising without needing, alive without a self to keep alive. The revelation is that the end of the self is not annihilation but the beginning of praise.

---

The dominant arc of Chapter 13:

```
favor and disgrace alarm → receiving debases → disgrace exposes →
the wound identified → trouble of self → if no self → what is the self? →
the self dissolves → return to the world without self → the selfless self
```

The dominant force is **revelation** — the progressive disclosure that the self is not a permanent entity but a provisional construction, and that the trouble it causes is the very evidence of its constructedness. The revelation that there is no one to be saved is the salvation itself.
