# Aeneid Book II: The Fall of Troy

Book II is entirely memory. It is a story told by a survivor at a banquet, recounting the annihilation
of his world. The narrative is haunted by the knowledge that all resistance was futile. Aeneas frames
the tale with sorrow (*infandum, regina, iubes renovare dolorem*), establishing that the destruction
of Troy was not merely a military defeat, but a cosmological erasure orchestrated by the gods.

The arc of Book II moves from the deceit of the Wooden Horse to the terrifying realization of divine
will. The dominant force is **fate** — but the arc travels through memory, sacrifice, grief, wrath,
divine machinery, and the luminous revelation of exile.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Rachmaninoff — Symphony No. 2: III. Adagio** — *memory*

Aeneas begins not with action but with the cost of remembering. The violence is already past,
filtered through trauma. Rachmaninoff's Adagio captures the sweeping, nostalgic recall of a lost
world, establishing the entire book as a flashback.

**2. Verdi — *La forza del destino*: Overture** — *fate*

The Trojans are tricked not just by a man, but by destiny. Verdi's hammered fate motif drives
the inescapable momentum of Troy's doom, blind to the Trojans' hope. It is the inexorable
machinery of fate.

**3. Stravinsky — *The Rite of Spring*: 'Sacrificial Dance'** — *sacrifice*

Laocoön is a priest killed at the altar by divine serpents — a terrifying immolation. Stravinsky
provides the ritual weight and asymmetrical violence of a sacrifice demanded by indifferent gods.

**4. Wagner — *Götterdämmerung*: 'Siegfried's Funeral March'** — *grief*

Hector appears to Aeneas mutilated, carrying the death of the city. The terminal, funereal
weight of Wagner's march belongs here, where the greatest hero's ghost confirms that the war
is irrevocably lost.

**5. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 8: III. Allegro non troppo** — *wrath*

Aeneas rushes into a hopeless fight. Shostakovich's relentless, mechanical scherzo embodies
the chaotic, suffocating violence of urban warfare and the desperate rage of doomed defenders.

**6. Mozart — *Requiem*: 'Confutatis'** — *sacrifice*

Priam is slaughtered on his own altar by Pyrrhus. The scene is a grotesque profanation.
The 'Confutatis' opposes the violent flames of the damned with the plea for salvation,
matching the horror of the old king's sacrificial death.

**7. Bruckner — Symphony No. 8: IV. Finale** — *divine-intervention*

Venus lifts the mortal mist from Aeneas' eyes, revealing Neptune, Juno, and Pallas physically
tearing the city apart. Bruckner's massive brass architecture captures this sudden, terrifying
verticality of divine machinery destroying human works.

**8. Vaughan Williams — *Fantasia on a Theme by Thomas Tallis*** — *exile*

Aeneas leaves Troy carrying his father and leading his son. The open intervals and wandering
strings of Vaughan Williams capture the lonely, solemn threshold between the destroyed past
and the unknown future.

**9. Pärt — *Cantus in Memoriam Benjamin Britten*** — *revelation*

Creusa's ghost appears not to mourn, but to prophesy Aeneas' destiny in Hesperia.
Pärt's descending bells and stillness transform personal grief into luminous, quiet revelation.

---

## The dominant arc of Book II

```
memory as framing trauma → fate as inescapable machinery →
sacrifice of the priest → grief at the terminal ghost →
wrath of urban warfare → sacrifice of the king →
divine-intervention tearing the world apart → exile at the threshold →
revelation of destiny
```

Book II is the book of **fate** — the unmaking of a civilization. The arc moves from the
painful, nostalgic recall of the survivor through the horrific, escalating destruction of
the city, culminating in the terrifying vision of the gods themselves acting as demolitionists.
The final movement into exile and revelation sets the stage for the wanderings to come.
