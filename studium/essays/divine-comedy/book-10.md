# Inferno, Canto X: Circle of Heresy

Canto X marks the entrance into the City of Dis proper and introduces the circle of the heretics—those who rejected divine truth through intellectual pride and doctrinal rebellion. Unlike the circles so far, which have contained souls driven by passion or incontinence, Canto X contains those who were damned by their own reasoning, their own refusal to submit the mind to faith.

The canto is also the first where Dante encounters his contemporaries. Farinata degli Uberti, a historical enemy from the Guelph-Ghibelline wars, rises from a burning tomb and speaks. Cavalcante dei Cavalcanti, father of the poet Guido Cavalcanti (whom Dante held dear), asks about his son. For the first time, Hell is populated not by figures from mythology or ancient history but by people Dante knew, people from his own fractious city.

The register shifts from spectacle to something more intimate: historical judgment, personal grief, and the weight of time crystallized into eternal architecture.

## Dominant Force: Fate

Canto X is governed by fate: the fate of the historical factions, the fate of Dante's own exile (which Farinata announces), the fate of the heretics whose pride has sealed them in eternal debate. The music must register both the weight of history and the inexorability of judgment.
