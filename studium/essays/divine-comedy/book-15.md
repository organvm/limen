# Inferno, Canto 15: The Florentine Sodomites

Canto 15 holds one of the poem's sharpest collisions between social memory and infernal
truth. Dante has known some of the souls he now sees, and the canto asks what happens
when public greatness and private damnation are no longer separable.

They are no longer merely figures in a doctrinal schema; they are names Dante has heard
in civic life, now burned and running over the sand. Brunetto Latini's plea for literary
memory is a late secular version of a medieval inheritance request: survive as witness,
even if salvation is denied. The register shifts from terror to ethical ache.

## The Dominant Arc

The force_arc — `grief, grief, grief, supplication, supplication, fate` — tracks the canto:

1. **Grief** introduces the emotional terrain from the opening.
2. **Grief** intensifies as anonymous pain becomes personal.
3. **Grief** continues in Brunetto's recognition.
4. **Supplication** takes form in the request for memory.
5. **Supplication** resolves into Dante's constrained reply.
6. **Fate** reasserts the descent.

---

## Dominant Force: Grief

The canto does not move by spectacle. It moves by the wound of recognition. Grief is therefore
the governing force even when explicit sins differ from that affect: Dante must continue
through Hell and keep memory active.
