# Journey to the West — Chapter 2: The Secret of Immortality and the First Return

> **Principle:** Score the acquisition of supreme power not as heroic montage, but as comic transcendence, followed by the sudden snapping back to physical defense.

## Reasoning

**1. Messiaen — *Quartet for the End of Time*: 'Louange à l'Éternité de Jésus' / Subhuti's instruction**

Monkey cracks Subhuti's riddle, sneaks to his chamber at the third watch, and receives the oral formula for immortality. This is genuine esoteric transmission. Messiaen provides the stillness and verticality required for a secret that arrests the cycle of death. It is not magical action yet; it is profound concentration.

**2. Ravel — *Daphnis et Chloé* Suite No. 2: 'Lever du jour' / Mastering the 72 transformations**

Having learned the base formula, Monkey must now avoid the Three Calamities by mastering transformation. Ravel's fluid, shimmering dawn captures the feeling of a physical form losing its fixed boundaries and learning to flow into any shape.

**3. Offenbach — *Orpheus in the Underworld*: Overture (Galop infernal) / The somersault cloud**

Immortals fly, but Monkey's nature is a macaque: he somersaults. Subhuti invents a custom cloud-soaring technique just for him, allowing him to cross 108,000 li in a single flip. The sheer ridiculousness and kinetic joy of this requires Offenbach's Galop. Supreme spiritual achievement rendered as slapstick momentum.

**4. Strauss — *Metamorphosen*: Opening / Expulsion**

He shows off his new powers to the other disciples by turning into a pine tree. Subhuti catches him, explains that power displayed is power contested, and expels him. The music needs to darken — Strauss's Metamorphosen captures the sudden loss of the safe, sheltered learning environment and the forced return to a dangerous world.

**5. Schubert — String Quartet No. 14 ('Death and the Maiden'): I. Allegro / Return to the Mountain**

He somersaults back to his home, only to find it ruined. The little monkeys are crying, hiding from the Demon King of Confusion who has enslaved them. Schubert's driving, apprehensive D minor captures the shock of homecoming turned sour — the realization that the world did not wait for him.

**6. Holst — *The Planets*: 'Mars, the Bringer of War' / Confronting the Demon King**

Monkey, armed with no weapon but his newly invulnerable body and magic, goes to kill the demon who tormented his "children." This is the first time we see him fight. Holst's Mars gives the aggressive, asymmetrical pulse of real physical violence entering the story.

**7. Mozart — *The Marriage of Figaro*: Overture / The clones of hair**

The Demon King has a huge sword, but Monkey bites off a handful of his own hair, chews it, and spits out hundreds of little monkeys that swarm and overwhelm the demon. It's a completely unorthodox, hilarious, and overwhelming tactic. Mozart's fizzing, hyperactive overture matches the energy of a hundred biting, scratching clones.

**8. Elgar — *Pomp and Circumstance March No. 1* / Establishing martial law**

Monkey returns victorious, bringing the captured weapons. He organizes the monkeys into a militia. The playful apes of Chapter 1 are now an armed force, and Monkey is a true king. Elgar provides the pomp of a sovereign solidifying his realm — but applied to a tribe of macaques brandishing stolen swords.
