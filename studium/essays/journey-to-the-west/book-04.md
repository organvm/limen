# Journey to the West — Chapter 4: Bima Wen and the Great Sage Equal to Heaven

> **Principle:** The absurdity of assigning a chaotic immortal to manage horses, the realization of the insult, and the self-declaration of equal status with Heaven itself.

## Reasoning

**1. Bruckner — Symphony No. 4 ('Romantic'): I. Bewegt, nicht zu schnell / Ascension to Heaven**

Monkey follows the Spirit of Venus up to the celestial gates. Bruckner's expansive, glowing orchestration provides the luminous, rising sequence of entering the true upper world for the first time.

**2. Mozart — *Eine kleine Nachtmusik*: I. Allegro / Appointed 'Protector of the Horses'**

The Jade Emperor appoints him Bima Wen (Protector of the Horses). Monkey takes the job very seriously, organizing the stables perfectly. Mozart's crisp, light, courtly rhythm plays as Monkey energetically applies himself to what is, secretly, a completely insulting and meaningless task.

**3. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5: I. Allegro con brio / Discovering the rank is unclassified**

Monkey learns from the other officials that 'Protector of the Horses' is the lowest possible rank, not even classified. The realization of the insult triggers immediate fury. He kicks over the desk, pulls out his rod, and smashes his way out of the Heavenly Gates. Beethoven's hammered, inexorable insistence is the sound of Monkey's offended dignity becoming a force of nature.

**4. Brahms — Symphony No. 3: I. Allegro con brio / Raising the banner**

He returns to the Mountain of Flowers and Fruit and declares the Heavenly Court a joke. When two horned demon kings suggest he title himself 'Great Sage Equal to Heaven', he immediately agrees and orders a massive banner raised. Brahms provides the sweeping, heroic circular form of returning home and claiming one's true identity.

**5. Elgar — *Enigma Variations*: 'E.D.U.' (Variation XIV) / Defeating the Heavenly Vanguard**

Heaven sends Mighty Miracle God and Prince Nezha to arrest him. Monkey defeats them both easily, mocking their youth and weakness. Elgar's 'E.D.U.' is a triumphant, self-assured declaration of sovereignty — Monkey proving his self-proclaimed title through absolute martial superiority.

**6. Wagner — *Die Meistersinger von Nürnberg*: Prelude to Act I / Official recognition**

Rather than risk further humiliation, the Jade Emperor listens to Venus again: give him the empty title 'Great Sage Equal to Heaven' to keep him quiet. Venus descends again. Wagner's pompous, majestic prelude is Heaven rearranging its own hierarchy to accommodate the anomaly it cannot defeat, cloaking a surrender in the language of a promotion.
