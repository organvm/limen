# Iliad Book XXII: The Death of Hector
## A Classical Playlist for the Book Where the Funeral Register Opens

Book XXII is the moment where epic becomes tragedy. Everything that came before — ten years of war, the quarrel, the plague, Patroclus burning on the pyre — exists to make this one scene possible: Hector alone on the plain, the city at his back with the gates still open, and Achilles coming. Homer takes the most decisive event of the Trojan War and gives it the grammar of a slow collapse. Nerve. Flight. Chase. Betrayal. Duel. Death. Refusal. Defilement. And then, from a tower, a woman heating water for a bath her husband will never take.

The playlist does not score the Trojan War. It scores *this* book's specific movement — its five turns, its register shift, its architecture of grief building from private parental supplication through public communal lamentation to Andromache's intimate catastrophe at the tower. The funeral-march register, held in reserve since Book I, unlocks here. Wagner's Siegfried's Funeral March has been waiting since the Adagietto was chosen for Book I's Achilles-and-Thetis scene precisely because that terminal-monumental-public grief belonged to *this* death, not an earlier wound. It arrives on track 8. Everything before it is approach; everything after is aftermath.

## The Reasoning, Track by Track

**1. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5, I. Allegro con brio** *(fate: inexorable motif, hammered insistence)*

Hector is standing outside the Scaean Gate. The gates are open. The city is inside. Everyone who loves him has already begged, and he has not moved. It is not stubbornness; it is something closer to the paralysis of a man who understands that every available option leads to the same place.

Beethoven's four-note opening is the correct grammar for this moment because it does not describe doom — it *installs* it as an axiom. The famous motif is not a warning or a prophecy; it is a statement of structural fact repeated until the listener stops expecting it to resolve. Hector standing at the gate is fate as a held position, not yet as motion. The first movement gives that stillness an engine.

---

**2. Purcell — Dido and Aeneas, "When I am laid in earth" (Dido's Lament)** *(grief: slow tempo, inward line)*

Priam tears his hair and names the sons already lost to Achilles. Hecuba bares her breast — the one that nursed Hector — and holds it up and begs. This is the oldest grief in Western literature, and Homer gives it full weight: two parents watching through the walls as what they made chooses not to come home.

Purcell's lament is built on a descending chromatic ground bass, a four-bar figure that repeats eleven times without variation while the vocal line above it searches for some way out and cannot find one. The repetition is not a compositional limitation — it is the structural truth of the scene. Priam and Hecuba have no new argument to make. They repeat themselves because there is nothing else to do. The ground bass repeats because grief at this scale has no development, only recurrence. This is the private register: wounded inwardness, not yet public ceremony. The funeral register has not opened. This is a mother and father watching.

---

**3. Brahms — Symphony No. 1, I. Un poco sostenuto — Allegro** *(fate: inexorable motif, hammered insistence)*

Homer gives Hector a soliloquy. He runs through the argument: go inside (Priam is right, Hecuba is right, there is no dishonor in surviving this); face Achilles now (honor demands it, I staked Troy's defense on my own plan, I cannot now hide); negotiate (offer Helen back, offer the treasures, split the wealth of Troy). He reaches the negotiation option and knows immediately that Achilles will not accept it, that no words will be possible, that Achilles has moved past the register of argument.

Brahms took twenty years to write his First Symphony because he understood what it cost to commit to forward motion when you know the terrain. The introduction is formally suppressed — enormous tension held under a slow harmonic weight — before the Allegro begins its sustained march into the argument. Hector's soliloquy is Brahms' introduction: a man thinking clearly toward his own death, eliminating each possibility with the precision of someone who is not in denial. The Allegro that follows is the moment he decides to stand. Architecture under pressure. Form that costs something.

---

**4. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 7 'Leningrad', I. Allegretto (invasion episode)** *(wrath: rhythmic pressure, unresolved harmony)*

Achilles rounds the corner. The armor blazes — Homer gives us the simile of the Dog Star rising, a star whose beauty signals disease and catastrophe. Hector looks at Achilles and runs.

Homer does not make this ambiguous or explain it away. The great protector of Troy turns and runs and Achilles gives chase. The Shostakovich invasion episode — the mechanical, accelerating build from a distant snare-drum figure to overwhelming orchestral mass — is the only piece of music that gives the correct account of what Achilles is in this moment. He is not a man expressing anger. He is a force bearing down without variation or mercy, operating below the register of emotion, mechanically forward and unanswerable. Wrath here is not a feeling. It is velocity. It is what happens when grief curdles into something that has left the human behind.

---

**5. Ravel — Bolero** *(wrath: rhythmic pressure, unresolved harmony)*

Three times around the walls of Troy. Homer stretches the chase to mythological duration: the gods watch from Olympus; Zeus takes out his golden scales; time slows while two men run the same circuit again and again. Homer's repetition is formal and deliberate. He is not rushing past it. He wants you inside the repetition.

Bolero is the only piece in the classical repertory that is built entirely on formal, mechanical, insistent repetition — the same melody, the same unvarying rhythm, the same accumulating harmonic pressure through identical circuits until the one moment of deviation at the end. It has been misused as sensual background music; here it is doing structural work. The three circuits of Troy *are* Bolero: the relentlessness is the content. Wrath as inexorable structure, not as explosive event.

---

**6. Verdi — La forza del destino, Overture** *(fate: inexorable motif, hammered insistence)*

Zeus takes the golden scales and places a fate on each side. Hector's side sinks. This is not a decision Zeus makes; it is a weighing. Apollo, who has protected Hector throughout the war, withdraws his protection and leaves. Athena descends, takes the shape of Deiphobus — Hector's brother, the brother who would have handed him a second spear — and tells him to stop, to stand, to fight. Hector stops. He turns. He believes his brother is beside him.

Verdi's Overture opens with the three-note fate motif — three brass notes of absolute declaration — and builds to the catastrophe that the opera's title promises. What is formally extraordinary about it is the gap between the announcement and the working-out: you know where you are going; the Overture is the mechanism of arrival, not the arrival itself. Zeus' scales are that mechanism. Fate here is not a theme or a symbol; it is a cosmic instrument, calibrated and executing. The disguise of Athena is fate's cruelty perfected: she tricks Hector into position by taking the face of someone who loves him.

---

**7. Schubert — String Quartet No. 14 in D minor 'Death and the Maiden', II. Andante con moto** *(grief: slow tempo, inward line)*

Hector turns to call Deiphobus for a second spear. There is no one there. The plain is empty behind him. He understands — not in a flash of anger, not in denial, but in a moment of absolutely lucid grief — that the gods have tricked him, that Athena arranged this, that there has never been an escape.

Schubert's Andante takes the theme from his song "Death and the Maiden" — death speaking softly to a frightened girl, telling her not to be afraid — and works it into a series of variations that are patient, even gentle, because death in this register is not violent. It is a recognition. Hector draws his sword, which is the only thing left to him, and walks toward Achilles. The grief here is not yet public; it is interior, perfectly calm, a man choosing the form of his ending after the ending was already chosen for him. Schubert understood that the approach to death has its own quiet music.

---

**8. Wagner — Götterdämmerung, "Siegfried's Funeral March"** *(grief: slow tempo, inward line) — THE REGISTER OPENS HERE*

This track has been held since Book I, when the Mahler Adagietto was chosen for Achilles and Thetis precisely because the Funeral March belonged somewhere else, to a death that had not happened yet. It belongs here.

Achilles' spear finds the gap in the armor at Hector's throat — the gap Achilles knows because the armor was his, stripped from Patroclus' body. Hector falls. He asks, dying, that his body be returned for burial, that the Greeks not feed him to the dogs. Achilles refuses. He calls him a dog. He says the vultures will eat him before his father can ransom his body back. Hector curses Achilles as he dies. He is right about what will happen at the Scaean Gate.

Wagner's Funeral March is built on Siegfried's leitmotifs cycling through their own annihilation — themes associated with the hero's joy, his strength, his love, played now as elegy, each fragment surfacing and then sinking. It is terminal-monumental grief: not inward, not private, but world-historical. It is the public announcement that the hero is gone and the world that required him cannot continue in its old form. This is the register that has been waiting. Hector's death is not one death; it is the death that makes Troy's fall inevitable, that shifts the poem's entire moral weight, that makes possible Priam's coming to Achilles in Book XXIV. The Funeral March announces the scale of what has ended.

---

**9. Bartók — Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta, III. Adagio** *(wrath: rhythmic pressure, unresolved harmony)*

Achilles pierces Hector's ankles, threads the reins through the wounds, lashes the body behind his chariot, and drags it around the walls. Priam watches from above. This is the defilement of the body, which Homer presents as a violation of cosmic order — the dead are owed their burial, their passage, their dignity. Achilles is committing an act that the gods will eventually punish him for, that Priam will eventually cross the lines to beg him to stop.

Bartók's Adagio is string writing that is beautiful and wrong simultaneously: harmonics, muted passages, a night-music quality that is not peaceful but suspended in wrongness, grief and violence occupying the same sonic space without resolving into either. Wrath here has moved past anger into something pathological. The dragging of Hector's body is not a military act or even an emotional act. It is ritual, compulsive, an attempt to exceed the violence that was done to Patroclus. Bartók gives the music for an act that has no correct musical register, because the act itself has exceeded what is permitted.

---

**10. Górecki — Symphony No. 3 'Symphony of Sorrowful Songs', I. Lento — Sostenuto tranquillo ma cantabile** *(grief: slow tempo, inward line)*

The walls of Troy fill with lamentation. The women tear their hair, beat their breasts, cry out — the formal ancient threnos, the ritual of communal grief that Homer gives full and explicit attention. This is not a private moment. This is an entire city watching its protector dragged in the dust, the walls that Hector defended now the frame for his humiliation.

Górecki built his Symphony of Sorrowful Songs from actual cries of mourning: a mother's lament over a lost child, an inscription scratched on a Gestapo cell wall by a girl who would not survive it, a folk song of a mother searching for her son killed in battle. The opening movement's slow string ascent — building from a single cello line to full orchestra over fifteen minutes — is communal grief at the scale of catastrophe, grief that has no individual face because it belongs to everyone who is watching. The inward line, the slow tempo, the weight of something that has not finished falling: this is the Trojan women on the walls.

---

**11. Mahler — Symphony No. 9, IV. Adagio** *(grief: slow tempo, inward line)*

Andromache does not know yet. She is inside the palace, at her loom, weaving. She has called the servants to heat water for Hector's bath when he comes home. She hears the cry from the walls and does not understand it. She runs to the tower and sees the body being dragged behind Achilles' chariot. She faints.

When she recovers she gives the fullest lament in the book — not for Hector's glory, not for Troy's fall, but for their son Astyanax. He will be an orphan. He will sit at another man's feast and be pushed away when the wine is poured. He will be mocked by other boys. The men who survived will take their fathers' hands; Astyanax has no father to take his hand. Andromache is not grieving Troy's fall as a political event. She is grieving the specific texture of what her son's life will now be without his father.

Mahler's Ninth Adagio was the last thing he completed. Written in the knowledge of his own approaching death, it is not a lament so much as a farewell held with perfect formal clarity — grief that knows what it is, that does not sentimentalize itself, that sustains its inward line until it simply ceases. There is no apotheosis, no transfiguration, no resolution into light. The music dissolves into silence because that is what endings do. Andromache's lament is the most human thing in Book XXII. It ends there, at the tower, with her holding the fact of what has happened.

## The Dominant Arc of Book XXII

```
fate (waiting) → grief (supplication) → fate (deliberation) → wrath (flight / chase) → fate (divine betrayal) → grief (recognition) → grief (the death / the register opens) → wrath (defilement) → grief (communal lamentation) → grief (Andromache at the tower)
```

This is why the playlist moves Beethoven → Purcell → Brahms → Shostakovich → Ravel → Verdi → Schubert → Wagner → Bartók → Górecki → Mahler.

Fate builds the architecture; wrath provides the mechanism; grief is what survives when both are finished. Book XXII is where the *Iliad* stops being a poem about the pride of men and becomes a poem about what pride costs the people who love those men. The funeral register opens at track 8, and it does not close.
