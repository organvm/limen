# Iliad Book IX: The Embassy, the Refusal

Book IX is the moment where the poem tests whether Achilles can be bought back. The answer is no — and that refusal, delivered with courtesy, is more devastating than any rage. Three men go to him: Odysseus with the lawyer's argument, Phoenix with the father's myth, Ajax with the soldier's claim. He receives them with wine and song, pours for them, listens to all of it, and declines. He does not storm. He has settled into his wrath the way a man settles into a philosophy. The grief at the end of the book belongs to the Greeks walking back across the beach with nothing, and to Achilles alone — suspended between two deaths he does not yet know are coming.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Bach — *St Matthew Passion*: "Erbarme dich, mein Gott"**

The embassy begins in supplication even though it cannot quite bring itself to say so. Agamemnon is humbled but cannot fully confess his humbling — he sends Odysseus to do the rhetorical work, Phoenix for the emotional work, Ajax for the blunt appeal. Behind all three of them is the king who wronged Achilles, reduced from the man who commanded the greatest army in the world to a man who needs something he gave away. Bach's aria is the archetype of that posture: an exposed melodic line reaching across a void with no certainty of answer, the oboe sustaining a plea that has not yet been received. There is no guarantee it will be. The supplicant goes anyway. That is Agamemnon's position: enormous in rank, small in agency, needing a man he wronged to save him.

**2. Bach — *The Art of Fugue*: Contrapunctus I (BWV 1080)**

Odysseus' speech is the most lawyerly oration in the poem. He enumerates the gifts: seven tripods, ten talents of gold, twenty cauldrons, twelve horses, seven Lesbian women skilled in craft, and Briseis herself, unviolated — he swears it. Clause by clause. The argument is structurally correct: the offense was real, the compensation is proportionate, the honor will be restored, the Trojans will be defeated. Nothing is false. Nothing works. The Art of Fugue's opening Contrapunctus is pure architecture: a subject introduced, answered in the dominant, restated in inversion, stacked into four voices that together constitute an argument from which no note is missing. As persuasion it is flawless. As a strategy for reaching Achilles, it is wrong in kind. He is not arguing about gifts. He is arguing about whether gifts mean anything.

**3. Handel — *Solomon*: "The Arrival of the Queen of Sheba"**

Phoenix's contribution is the most formally elaborate: embedded myth as precedent, moral instruction as story. He tells the tale of Meleager — another hero, another quarrel, another withdrawal, another plea from family and comrades and city. Meleager waited too long. He fought without gifts in the end, and the honor he was promised was never given. Phoenix is offering Achilles the tradition's own accumulated wisdom: this is how it goes, this is the pattern, you can choose the better version. Handel's entry of the Queen of Sheba carries the register of that offer — brilliant, ceremonially generous, full of the formal intelligence of high civilization brought to bear on a difficult situation. The music shines. It offers everything. In the poem, Achilles thanks Phoenix with warmth and declines the precedent entirely.

**4. Brahms — String Sextet No. 1 in B-flat major, I. Allegro ma non troppo (Op. 18)**

Ajax does not make a rhetorical case. His speech is the shortest: accept the ransom; be moved by us; a man can accept payment even for a murdered brother; your stubbornness injures not Agamemnon but your comrades who love you. It is the argument from the body, from standing in the line together, from the simple loyalty of soldiers. Brahms Op. 18 carries that weight — chamber textures of genuine gravity, masculine warmth without decoration, seriousness without grandeur. It is not monumental; it is the thing Brahms does when he refuses to be theatrical and means it anyway. Ajax cannot imagine that Achilles' grievance has become philosophical. He thinks this is still about compensation. He is wrong, but he is not wrong to try.

**5. Beethoven — String Quartet No. 14 in C-sharp minor, I. Adagio quasi una fantasia (Op. 131)**

The refusal speech is one of the most extraordinary things in Homer. Achilles does not refuse because he is still furious. He refuses because he has thought his way through his fury to a new position: the heroic economy is a lie. The brave man and the coward get the same death. The man who fights earns the same prize as the man who stays home. Why should he risk his life for a gift that can be taken? He is not refusing Agamemnon's compensation; he is refusing Agamemnon's world. Beethoven's Op. 131 opens with a slow fugue that is also a lament — organized, inward, austere, and bereft all at once. It is the right texture for wrath-become-philosophy: not eruption, not protest, but a mind that has arrived at a settled position through suffering and will not be dislodged by rhetoric. The music is rigorous and it is also grief-stricken. So is Achilles.

**6. Shostakovich — String Quartet No. 8 in C minor, I. Largo (Op. 110)**

Achilles names his two fates: a long life without glory at home in Phthia, or a short life and eternal kleos at Troy. He has chosen. This is not an announcement; it is a disclosure. He is telling them what he has already decided, alone, before they arrived. The Shostakovich Eighth Quartet was written in three days; the composer inscribed his own musical monogram — D–S–C–H — into the opening subject as a private signature on what he believed might be his last work. That is the register: a man encoding himself into a piece that might outlast him, facing his own extinction with terrible clarity and a kind of internal stillness. Wrath in Book IX has become acceptance of death. That is why no argument can reach it.

**7. Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel***

After all three arguments are refused, Phoenix does not leave. He weeps. He says: I will stay here; I will sleep here; I cannot go back to Agamemnon without you. The supplication continues past argument and past reason into bare exposed love. Phoenix raised Achilles; he held him on his knee; he chewed his food for him when he was a baby. None of that is an argument. It is just there. Pärt's 'Spiegel im Spiegel' — mirror in the mirror — is suspended cadence made permanent: a melodic line over slow piano shimmer, repeating, unresolved, continuing past any formal closure. It does not demand. It continues. Phoenix's posture is the same: I am staying because I have nowhere else to be. The music is not weeping; it is simply open, and it does not close.

**8. Schubert — String Quintet in C major, II. Adagio (D. 956)**

The strangest register in Book IX, and the one that most marks the book as unlike any other: Achilles is genuinely warm. When the embassy arrives he is singing to himself, accompanying himself on a lyre, singing of the famous deeds of men. He sees them coming and he is glad. He pours wine. He tells Patroclus to mix a stronger bowl. He is a good host, and he will not move. Schubert's Quintet Adagio holds two things in suspension that should not coexist: profound inward peace and inconsolable grief. The second movement is simultaneously the most serene and the most heartbroken music in the chamber repertoire — it is neither simple happiness nor simple sorrow but both together in a way that cannot be explained, only heard. Achilles' warmth and his intransigence are not contradictions. They are two surfaces of the same settled soul. Schubert understood that.

**9. Mahler — Symphony No. 9, IV. Adagio**

The ambassadors cross the beach in silence back to the Greek camp. They have nothing. Diomedes, in a few lines, will say: let him stay, we will win without him — but that is bravado to cover the catastrophe. The real feeling is in the walk itself: the recognition that the embassy failed, that the greatest soldier in the army has closed himself off, that the Greeks are now exposed to whatever happens next. Mahler's Ninth Symphony Adagio is the composer's farewell to the world — not heroic, not weeping, but releasing: the texture thins, phrases trail into silence, the line continues until it doesn't. This is the grief register appropriate to Book IX: private, inward, not yet funeral-monumental. The funeral register belongs to Books XXII and XXIV. This is earlier, quieter — the grief of an opening that closed.

**10. Fauré — *Requiem*: "In Paradisum" (Op. 48)**

Book IX ends on Achilles, not on the Greeks. He has made his choice. He will sleep; he will not go to Troy in the morning; he is going home when the wind is right. He is at rest in his refusal. He does not know that Patroclus will die. He does not know that his own death is near, closer now than ever, because of what he said tonight. Fauré's 'In Paradisum' is the most radiant piece of non-funereal grief in the repertoire: it is not mourning the dead, it is a voice released into a stillness that is neither triumph nor defeat — a radiance that holds sorrow inside it without being overcome by it. Achilles in Book IX is suspended between two deaths he cannot see yet. The Fauré holds that suspension exactly: still, inward, at rest in its own unresolvable condition.

## The dominant arc of Book IX

```
supplication → law → law → law → wrath → wrath → supplication → wrath → grief → grief
```

That is why the playlist moves Bach/Handel/Brahms (three modes of appeal, three textures of law and persuasion) into Beethoven/Shostakovich (the two faces of wrath-as-philosophy), through Pärt (love that continues past argument), into Schubert (warmth and intransigence as one thing), and closes with Mahler/Fauré (two modes of private grief — the Greeks' and Achilles' own). The dominant force is wrath — but it is wrath that has become so settled it no longer looks like rage. That is what makes Book IX the most intellectually terrifying book in the poem.
