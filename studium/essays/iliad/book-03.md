# Iliad Book III: Desire, the Walls, the Duel, Aphrodite

Book III is the moment where the war remembers what it is about — and discovers that what it is about cannot be resolved by war. Paris steps forward to fight and then flinches. Hector shames him back into it. A formal duel is agreed, oaths are sworn, lambs are sacrificed, the gods are summoned as witnesses. Helen stands on the walls of Troy and names the men who crossed the sea for her, to a Trojan king who has been her surrogate father. Menelaus is winning — he has Paris by the helmet, seconds from killing him, and the war ending on the terms it began — and then Aphrodite reaches down and removes Paris from the battlefield. The duel is cancelled. Helen is compelled to the bedchamber. The war continues for no military reason except that the divine engine that started it is still running.

The playlist does not score the Trojan War. It scores this book's specific movement: desire → shame → law → memory → force → divine snatching → desire reinstated. The dominant force is desire — not wrath, not fate, not martial spectacle — because desire is the grammar underneath every scene, including the ones that look like law or battle.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Scriabin — Piano Sonata No. 4, Op. 30: I. Andante** — *Paris advances, then quails*

Paris walks out between the armies in his panther-skin and then, the moment he sees Menelaus, flinches back into the ranks like a man who has spotted a snake. This is the war's founding irony: the man it is about is a coward. The correct music is not martial — it is desire without nerve. Scriabin's Fourth Sonata opens in suspended, chromatic longing, trembling and iridescent, beautiful and irresolute. It does not advance; it hovers. That is Paris exactly — a man constituted by wanting, not by will. The force is desire, not wrath, because Paris is not an aggressor in this moment. He is a beautiful, frightened object who started a war he cannot fight.

**2. Bartók — Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta: III. Adagio** — *Hector's rebuke*

Hector's speech to Paris is one of the coldest things in Homer: "Paris, you disaster, you beautiful seducer, you should never have been born or should have died unmarried." It is not hot anger; it is clinical contempt from a soldier who loves his brother and is disgusted by him simultaneously. Bartók's Adagio from the Music for Strings operates at exactly this temperature — a descending chromatic line in the strings, dark and precise, percussive punctuation underneath, pressure without explosion. Wrath here is not Mars; it is Hector: measured, devastating, internal. The scene ends when Paris agrees to fight, partly out of shame, partly because Hector is still more frightening than Menelaus.

**3. Bach — Mass in B minor: "Et in unum Dominum"** — *The truce / formal oath*

The agreement of single combat is a legal ceremony: wine mixed, lambs slaughtered, prayers offered to Zeus and Helios and Gaia and the rivers and dead men who punish oath-breakers underground. Both sides lower their weapons. Agamemnon speaks a prayer that is really a contract. Bach's duet from the Credo has the architecture of binding agreement — two voices in formal counterpoint, moving in and out of unison, ceremonial gravity without solemnity. The force is law: not military law but sacred law, the grammar of witnessed oaths. When the oath is later broken (in Book IV), Bach's counterpoint will sound like the thing that was violated.

**4. Elgar — Enigma Variations: IX. "Nimrod"** — *Teichoscopia I: Helen names the Achaean kings*

The teichoscopia — Helen standing on the walls of Troy naming the Achaean heroes to Priam — is one of the most elegiac passages in the Iliad, and one of the strangest. She identifies Ajax, Odysseus, Agamemnon, Idomeneus, and she does it with eerie calm, the calm of someone who has watched this war from both sides and carries the knowledge in her body. Priam calls her "dear child" and does not blame her. The dominant force is memory — not grief, not exile — because Helen names with authority, not with tears. Elgar's "Nimrod" is the music of a gaze backward: stately, inward, luminous with loss that has been lived with long enough to become something else. It is the only piece at the right altitude: commemoration without breakdown.

**5. Fauré — Pavane, Op. 50** — *Teichoscopia II: Priam asks about Odysseus*

Antenor's description of Odysseus mid-speech — hunched over at first, seeming stupid, then suddenly overwhelming, words falling like winter snow — is a portrait, a memory of a man seen at a distance. The Pavane is not grief; it is suspended recognition, the quality of watching something vast from a wall and naming it accurately. Fauré's Pavane is a measured, elevated processional, hovering above the scene rather than in it. It holds the teichoscopia in its second phase: still on the walls, still looking down at the plain, still naming the men who will eventually break the gates.

**6. Prokofiev — Romeo and Juliet, Op. 64: "The Death of Tybalt"** — *The duel: Paris vs. Menelaus*

The duel is not a battle — it is two men, the personal violence the war was always about, distilled to its source. Paris throws his spear; it bends on Menelaus' shield. Menelaus throws his; it punches through Paris' corselet and Paris dodges. Then Menelaus draws his sword, strikes Paris' helmet, and the sword shatters. This is the scale of Prokofiev's duel music: not an army, not a war, but a grinding, asymmetrical violence between specific named men. "The Death of Tybalt" is wrath made intimate and personal — a furious rhythmic escalation that terminates in a hard stop. It is exactly the right register for two bodies in a field trying to kill each other because a woman is in a bedroom behind the walls: small-scale, urgent, and without epic distance.

**7. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5: I. Allegro con brio** — *Menelaus seizes Paris — the killing is seconds away*

The hammer-motif of Beethoven's Fifth is fate announcing itself. Menelaus has Paris by the crested helmet, is dragging him toward the Greek lines; the leather chinstrap is choking him; the war is about to end correctly. The poem is three seconds from justice. That is the only register the fate-motif fits: not the beginning of fate but fate at the moment of arrival, the moment before delivery. The more violent the interruption that follows, the more important it is that the music here be inexorable — so that when Aphrodite breaks it, the rupture is absolute.

**8. Messiaen — Quartet for the End of Time: V. "Louange à l'Éternité de Jésus"** — *Aphrodite snatches Paris from the field*

Aphrodite breaks the Beethoven by operating from an entirely different order of reality. She wraps Paris in mist and lifts him from the field, deposits him in his own bedchamber fragrant with cedar, and the duel is simply over. There is no explosion, no counter-force, no military explanation. Messiaen's "Praise to the Eternity of Jesus" is the correct music for this: a cello melody over utterly static piano chords, slow, uncanny, from somewhere else entirely. This is not the divine-intervention of Das Rheingold's cosmic machinery — it is intimate, local, domestic-scale divinity. Aphrodite does not move armies; she moves one man to one bed. Messiaen gives the supernatural not as spectacle but as sudden, absolute stillness.

**9. Wagner — Tristan und Isolde: "Liebestod"** — *Aphrodite compels Helen to the bedchamber*

This is the book's most uncomfortable scene, and Homer does not flinch from it: Aphrodite, disguised as an old wool-worker, grabs Helen by the dress and threatens her. Helen recognizes the goddess, refuses, rebukes her — and then goes anyway, silent and ashamed, to Paris' bed. The Liebestod is the only music at this register: chromatic longing that overwhelms the will, deferred resolution that feels simultaneously like annihilation and ecstasy, desire as something that happens to you rather than something you choose. The war began with desire running this way — Helen taken, or going, or both at once. Aphrodite reinstates it. Homer is not romantic about the scene. Neither is Wagner. The Liebestod is not about happiness; it is about the irresistibility of the force.

**10. Szymanowski — Violin Concerto No. 1, Op. 35: opening** — *Paris in the bed / the war's engine still running*

The book closes with Paris in bed, Helen reluctant beside him, and Menelaus hunting the battlefield for a man who no longer exists on it. The war will continue tomorrow. Nothing was resolved. Szymanowski's First Violin Concerto opens in iridescent, post-Wagnerian chromatic lines that spiral upward without landing — sensual, uncanny, hovering. It is not tragic, not heroic, not martial. It is purely, strangely erotic: the quality of being inside a force that has no resolution and no end. The war's engine is desire, and Aphrodite has just confirmed that the engine is immune to military outcome. Book III ends where it began: in desire, still running, untouched.

## Removed / Demoted

**Wagner — Götterdämmerung: "Siegfried's Funeral March"** — not this book; save for XXII / XXIV. Book III contains no death. The grief here is Helen's private shame and Priam's distant sorrow — both are the memory register, not the funeral-monumental register. The funeral march belongs to Hector's death and what follows it.

**Strauss — Also sprach Zarathustra: opening** — demoted; too cosmogonic for Aphrodite's specific action. Aphrodite's intervention is intimate and domestic-scale, not world-creating. Messiaen operates at the right altitude.

**Verdi — La forza del destino: Overture** — demoted; too operatically spectacular for the duel's intimacy. Beethoven's Fifth captures the same inexorability at human scale.

**Holst — The Planets: "Mars, the Bringer of War"** — demoted from Book III; already used in Book I (track 5) for wrath. Mars belongs to the general war register; Book I has prior claim. Prokofiev's duel music works at the intimate scale Book III actually needs: two men, not two armies.

## The dominant arc of Book III

```
desire → shame/wrath → law → memory → memory → wrath → fate → divine-intervention → desire → desire
```

That is why the playlist moves Scriabin → Bartók → Bach → Elgar → Fauré → Prokofiev → Beethoven → Messiaen → Wagner → Szymanowski.

Book III is the poem's first proof that the war cannot end by military means. The force that started it is a goddess, and she is still in the room.
