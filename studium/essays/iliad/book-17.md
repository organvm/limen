# Iliad Book XVII: The Fight over Patroclus' Body

Book XVII is the moment where grief becomes a military problem. Patroclus is already dead — Book XVI closed on that — and now the question is whether the body can be recovered or lost. What Homer builds across 761 lines is not a battle in the familiar sense. It is a sustained, grinding refusal: Menelaus and Ajax planting themselves over a corpse and holding it, step by step, against a Trojan press led by Hector wearing divine stolen armor. Nothing is decided. No rout, no rally, no climax. The body moves back and forth. Men die holding ground. Achilles' immortal horses weep beside a stopped chariot. The messenger runs. And at the end, the Achaeans are walking backward toward the ships, carrying what they came for.

That is the emotional structure this playlist scores: grief(the contested body) → wrath(grief-fury of the defenders) → grief(the weeping horses) → fate(loyalty under doom). The dominant force is grief — not public-funeral grief, not the Wagnerian terminal register, but the private, pressed, inward grief of men fighting over something irreversible.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Schubert — String Quintet in C, D. 956: II. Adagio** (grief)

The book's opening image is Menelaus alone over the body, before help arrives — a single man holding ground against the knowledge that what he is holding is already gone. Schubert's Adagio is the most inward, sustained grief in the chamber repertoire: not theatrical, not weeping, but a temperature drop. Two cellos. A suspended descent that keeps threatening to resolve and doesn't. The physical scene — Menelaus leaning over, spear in hand, nobody else yet present — demands music that is quiet, not grand. This is grief before it becomes wrath; grief in its first cold form.

**2. Bartók — Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta: III. Adagio** (wrath)

Hector stripping Achilles' divine armor from Patroclus' body is an act of violent appropriation that crosses several thresholds at once: taking god-forged objects from a human corpse, claiming a god's identity by wearing his armor, committing the hubris that Zeus, watching above, immediately marks as his doom. Bartók's third movement builds from nocturnal shadow through accumulating percussive weight without fully discharging. The xylophone-and-strings texture is dry, slightly supernatural, cold. Wrath here is not fanfare — it is deliberate seizure, the chill of someone taking what isn't his from the wrong body.

**3. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5: I. Allegro con brio** (fate)

When Hector puts on the divine armor, Zeus looks down and grieves. The god knows this man has claimed what will not save him — that doom is now inscribed in the wearing. The Fifth Symphony's famous motif is about fate not as shock but as recurrence: it comes back, comes back, comes back. This is not the terror of something unknown arriving; it is the weight of something confirmed. Zeus' sorrow over Hector is not surprise. It is recognition. The hammered insistence of the Beethoven opening is exactly that: fate recognizing itself in a mortal's confident mistake.

**4. Prokofiev — Romeo and Juliet, Op. 64: "Montagues and Capulets"** (wrath)

Menelaus cannot hold alone. He calls Ajax, and the Achaean defense begins to coalesce around the corpse. Prokofiev's 'Montagues and Capulets' is formation-music under siege: its hammered minor chords do not charge forward but press and hold, the rhythmic weight asymmetric and grinding without catharsis. Men not seeking glory but holding a line against a force that outnumbers them. "Mars" belongs to Book I's quarrel register — political ego and verbal war. This scene is different: it is defense as a form of loyalty, and Prokofiev's martial block, refusing all relief, is the right sound for a line that holds at enormous cost and without triumph.

**5. Gluck — Iphigénie en Tauride: "O malheureuse Iphigénie"** (sacrifice)

Euphorbus, who first speared Patroclus before Hector finished the kill, now steps forward to challenge Menelaus for the body. Menelaus kills him. The exchange follows the strict Homeric grammar of ritual combat: wound, boast, counter-kill, the loser becoming the price that confirms the transaction. Gluck's aria is stately and darkly ceremonial — not the grandeur of sacrifice at its largest but its medium register, the cost that must be paid to keep the sequence moving. The scene is about irreversibility, duty, and the price of a body that keeps requiring payment.

**6. Mahler — Symphony No. 9: I. Andante comodo** (grief)

The most startling moment in Book XVII: Achilles' immortal horses, standing apart from the fighting, weep for Patroclus. Homer gives them grief as vast as any human's. They will not move, will not eat; they stand and mourn. The Mahler Ninth's first movement is built around an irregular pulse — the stumbling heartbeat that Mahler scholars read as the sound of a man who knows he is dying — and it dissolves repeatedly into melodic lines that cannot sustain themselves, figures that start and fall away. This is not the Adagietto's wounded private inwardness (Book I, Achilles and Thetis). This is grief at the edge of cosmic incomprehension: immortal creatures encountering mortality for the first time, with no framework to process it. The music must be both vast and private. The Ninth's first movement is the only thing in the repertoire that is both.

**7. Messiaen — Quatuor pour la fin du Temps: V. "Louange à l'Éternité de Jésus"** (divine-intervention)

Zeus looks down at the weeping horses and pities them. This is one of the rare moments in the Iliad where a god's intervention is not strategic but tender — genuine compassion for creatures whose suffering has no heroic function. He cannot restore Patroclus; he can only witness the horses' grief and be moved by it. Messiaen's Louange is the stillest sustained luminosity in the modern chamber repertoire: a cello melody of vast, slow breath over harmonics that seem to come from beyond the instrument, the piano nearly motionless beneath. The divine-intervention force usually carries sudden color-shift and brass-shock; here the shift is toward silence and light. Zeus is moved, and the music needs to hold that.

**8. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 8: II. Allegretto** (wrath)

The central duration of Book XVII is attrition. No side routs, no decisive blow, the body dragged back and forth, men dying to hold a line. This is the siege inside the siege. Shostakovich wrote the Eighth Symphony under one; the Allegretto second movement is not triumphant war music but the sound of machinery grinding against human flesh — sardonic, relentless, rhythmically propulsive without payoff. The wrath registered here is the wrath of sustained effort under overwhelming pressure: not fury as emotion but fury as the only operational mode available when you have no better option.

**9. Verdi — Otello: "Esultate!"** (wrath)

When Hector temporarily retreats, Glaucus turns on him publicly — shaming him for abandoning the body, the armor, the Trojan dead. Public shame in Homer is a mechanism for reactivating courage: the honor-wound drives the wounded man back into danger. "Esultate!" — Otello's entrance aria — is pure masculine assertion, a single voice claiming the field after triumph, daring anyone to challenge it. Glaucus does not sing triumph; but the force that drives Hector back to the front operates on the same vocal register: this is what a man sounds like when he is publicly challenged and must respond by action.

**10. Barber — Adagio for Strings** (grief)

At the book's close, Menelaus makes a decision: someone must tell Achilles. He sends Antilochus — the closest to Achilles in age, the man he trusts to manage the news. The Adagio for Strings is the exact sound of a man walking toward someone to break the worst news: sustained, aching, directional, inevitable. This is not yet Achilles' grief (that is Book XVIII, where it explodes). This is the approach — the errand of mourning, the ground between the death and the knowing. The Adagio's famous quality is precisely that it moves toward something without ever arriving in triumph or release. Antilochus is running toward a tent where nothing will ever be the same again.

**11. Sibelius — Symphony No. 7 in C major** (fate)

The book ends with the Achaeans walking backward toward the ships, Ajax holding the rear line, carrying Patroclus' body. They hold — not because they win but because loyalty is its own force, and they refuse to leave. The Sibelius Seventh is a single-movement arc, compressed and inexorable, that reaches its great trombone climax and then resolves into something not triumphant but affirmed: a thing completed under compression, through endurance rather than victory. The wandering tonality of fate, resolving not with a fanfare but with a walking-on, is exactly what the Achaean rear-guard earns at the end of this book. They did not win. They held. That is a different thing.

## The dominant arc of Book XVII

```
grief(contested body) → wrath(defenders' fury) → grief(weeping horses) → fate(loyalty under doom)
```

That is why the playlist moves Schubert → Bartók → Beethoven → Prokofiev → Gluck → Mahler → Messiaen → Shostakovich → Verdi → Barber → Sibelius: from private inward grief, through cold violent wrath, through the most astonishing moment of animal mourning in Western literature, through divine tenderness, through the machinery of siege, through the messenger's running, and finally into the sustained, compressed, unrewarded loyalty of men carrying their dead home.

Book XVII does not end with a funeral. The funeral register — the Wagnerian terminal gravity, the public weight of "Siegfried's Funeral March" — does not open here. It opens in Book XXII when Hector falls, and fully consumes everything in Book XXIV. Here the grief is still contested. The body is still moving. The score must remain pressed, inward, enduring — and it does.
