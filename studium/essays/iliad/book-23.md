# Iliad Book XXIII: The Ghost, the Pyre, the Games

Book XXIII is the Iliad's argument that grief cannot be permanent — not because it heals, but because the living cannot afford it. The ghost of Patroclus appears at night, asks for burial, and vanishes when Achilles reaches for him. The great pyre burns. The mound is raised. And then Achilles stands before the army and announces prizes: horses, cauldrons, women, iron, cattle. The funeral games begin. Five men race chariots. Ajax and Odysseus wrestle to a draw. Someone slips in dung. An old man is given a prize and remembers his youth. The community comes alive inside its grief and is, temporarily, more alive for it. At the end, Achilles is still bereaved. The games did not fix anything. But the community has reasserted itself — around its rituals, its competitions, its laughter, its adjudicated disputes — and that reassertion is what Book XXIII is actually about.

Do not score this book as generic funeral music. The funeral-march register opened in Book XXII with Hector's death; it closes in Book XXIV with Priam and Achilles. Book XXIII sits between them as something stranger: a community in grief that must also compete, dispute prize-rulings, laugh at a boxer's boast, and listen to an old man's memories. The dominant arc is grief → law → return → comedy → grief — private wound to shared arena and back.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Schubert — String Quartet No. 14 ('Death and the Maiden'), II. Andante con moto**

The ghost comes in the dark while the army sleeps. He has Patroclus' voice and his eyes and his height, but he is the wrong temperature. He asks for burial so his shade may pass the river. He says: *we will never again sit apart from our companions and take counsel.* Then he fades, and Achilles wakes with his hands empty.

Schubert's slow movement from the 'Death and the Maiden' quartet is the right register: intimate, not monumental; the theme stated with infinite tenderness, then varied into something that presses harder than comfort can answer. This is grief as private nocturne — wounded inwardness before it becomes public rite. The funeral-march register belongs to Book XXII, where Hector fell. Patroclus' ghost in Book XXIII is the earlier wound, still raw, still unburied. Schubert holds that distinction perfectly.

**2. Fauré — Requiem, Op. 48: 'In Paradisum'**

Patroclus asks for burial so his shade may pass. He is half-already gone — he has crossed the threshold but cannot cross the river without the rites. Achilles reaches for him and grasps air. The ghost recedes.

Fauré's 'In Paradisum' is the only music that holds both departure and suspension at once. It is serene and irreversible and unbearably gentle. It does not mourn the way a funeral march mourns; it opens a gate. That is precisely the ghost's grammar in Book XXIII: not requiem for the dead man, but passage music for a soul still waiting on the wrong side of crossing.

**3. Gluck — Alceste: Ouverture**

At dawn, the Myrmidons mourn in their armor. Then the timber is felled, the trees piled high. Animals are slaughtered. Honey and oil poured. The bodies of twelve Trojan youths are laid alongside. This is one of the most elaborate sacrificial rites in the poem, performed correctly, at full scale, by men who know the grammar of it.

Gluck's Alceste Overture opens directly into the underworld register — solemn, processional, genuinely dark, without the camp theatricality of later funeral music. It captures the moment when grief becomes ritual: still terrible, but now structured, formal, proceeding according to a logic older than any individual loss.

**4. Berlioz — Symphonie fantastique, V. 'Songe d'une nuit du sabbat'**

Achilles pours libations and calls upon Boreas and Zephyrus — the North Wind and the West Wind — to fan the flames. They hear him. The pyre burns all night.

Berlioz's 'Witches' Sabbath' is the right fever for this: not military, but elemental — fire summoned by a human voice, a world lit from within by forces older than any army. The Dies Irae quotation inside the movement catches the ritual's underworld weight without becoming sentimental. Achilles has called on the winds and the winds have answered. The world is burning because he asked it to.

**5. Bach — Mass in B minor, BWV 232: 'Agnus Dei'**

The pyre burns down to embers. The bones of Patroclus are gathered, wrapped in fat, placed in a golden urn. Wine is poured to quench the remaining fire. Posts are staked to mark the mound. The rite is concluded.

Bach's 'Agnus Dei' from the B minor Mass is architecture under grief: the alto aria descends slowly, formally, with an inwardness that never collapses into display. This is law in the deep sense — not state law, but the ritual grammar that structures how a civilization handles its dead. The rite has been performed correctly. That correctness is its own solace: not comfort, but completion.

**6. Handel — Zadok the Priest, HWV 258**

Achilles stands before the army and announces the games. He has prizes: a woman skilled in fine work; a cauldron; a horse; iron; gold. He distributes them by contest category. He is performing kingship — the reconstitution of communal order through the distribution of honor under competitive rules.

Handel's 'Zadok the Priest' is ceremonial-sovereign, the music of a community re-anointing its structure after rupture. It is the hinge of Book XXIII: the moment where private grief becomes public law, where Achilles stops being only a bereaved man and becomes again the arbiter of his community's honor economy.

**7. Beethoven — Symphony No. 7 in A major, Op. 92: IV. Allegro con brio**

Five men race chariots: Diomedes with Aeneas' horses; Eumelus with the best horses in the army; Antilochus, cunning at the turn; Menelaus, proud; Meriones, steady. The crowd watches. The horses run.

Beethoven's Seventh finale is irresistible forward motion — not aggressive like Mars, but exhilarated, almost dance-drunk, the energy of a community that has remembered how to move together. It catches the chariot race's specific quality: people who have been grieving are suddenly, for the duration of the race, purely alive. The crowd is alive. The horses are alive. For a few minutes, nothing else matters.

**8. Stravinsky — The Rite of Spring: 'The Sage'**

Apollo dashes the whip from Diomedes' hands. Athena restores it and then breaks the yoke of Eumelus, sending his horses wide. The divine interferences are quick, violent, asymmetrical — not the great sustained wrath of Achilles, but the capricious micro-wrath of gods who cannot resist meddling in human sport.

Stravinsky's 'The Sage' is brief and hieratic, something ritualized between two world-orders. It interrupts the race's momentum the way the gods interrupt it: without warning, on its own authority, completely. Then the race continues. The gods withdraw. The music releases.

**9. Haydn — Symphony No. 104 ('London'), IV. Spiritoso**

The race ends in dispute. Antilochus cut off Menelaus at the narrow pass; Menelaus demands satisfaction; Antilochus is formally accused; he offers the horse rather than take the oath; Menelaus is moved, almost amused. He accepts the horse and gives back the mare. The quarrel dissolves in generosity.

This is Homeric comedy in its purest form: social lubrication, honor economy working as designed, a dispute that could have become a wound becoming instead a demonstration of how noblemen negotiate. Haydn's London finale has exactly this register — brisk, good-natured, a little aggressive, ending in reconciliation. The quarrel was real. It is now over. Everyone is fine.

**10. Rossini — La gazza ladra: Overture**

Epeius steps up for the boxing and announces, without embarrassment, that he is the best and will win. He does. He knocks Euryalus cold, then catches him before he falls and helps him stand. The crowd laughs warmly.

Rossini's overture has exactly this quality: quick, physical, a little absurd, with a snare-drum military energy that resolves into pleasure rather than violence. The boxing match in Book XXIII is not tragic; it is not even tense. Epeius told everyone he would win, and he won, and he was gracious about it, and everybody knew it was going to happen that way. That is comedy in the Greek sense: the world working as it should, which is funnier than anyone expects.

**11. Mozart — Sinfonie Concertante in E-flat major, K. 364: III. Presto**

The remaining contests move quickly. Ajax and Odysseus wrestle to a draw — neither can throw the other. The foot-race: Odysseus prays to Athena mid-sprint; Ajax slips in the dung left from Achilles' sacrifice and finishes with a mouthful of it; everyone laughs, including Ajax. Armed combat is stopped before blood is drawn. Discus. Spear.

Mozart's Sinfonie Concertante finale captures what this sequence actually is: two instruments in constantly shifting, playful, competitive dialogue — now one leads, now the other — the ensemble warm and supportive behind them. The community is the instrument here. These are people who know each other, who have fought alongside each other, who are competing within a structure of shared understanding. The Sinfonie Concertante's finale has that warmth: competitive, but never cold.

**12. Elgar — Enigma Variations, Op. 36: IX. 'Nimrod'**

After the games, Achilles calls Nestor aside. He gives him a prize — a double-handled cauldron — because Nestor is too old to compete. Nestor takes it and remembers: the Funeral Games of Amarynceus, when he was young, when he won the boxing and the wrestling and the javelin and the foot-race, when only Castor beat him in the chariot race because the Dioscuri were favored by the gods. He was the best. That was long ago.

Elgar's 'Nimrod' is exact for this. It is memory rendered as music: not nostalgic in the weak sense, but deep, specific, honoring something that was real and is now past. Achilles honoring Nestor is the most tender moment in Book XXIII, and 'Nimrod' holds tenderness without sentimentality — it knows what it is honoring and honors it cleanly.

**13. Mahler — Symphony No. 9 in D major: I. Andante comodo (opening)**

The games end. The community disperses to eat and sleep. Achilles is alone.

Book XXIII does not end in triumph. The games were real — the community was genuinely alive in them — but they were also a form of covering. The covering is done. Patroclus is still dead. The burial is complete, the mound is raised, the bones are wrapped in gold, and none of it fills the absence. Achilles returns to his grief.

Mahler's Ninth opens with a broken pulse — a heartbeat rhythm that falters, something that cannot quite sustain its own continuity. It is not the Adagietto's suspended tenderness from Book I; it is older, more fractured, more alone. It is what remains after a man has done everything correctly and is still bereaved. The games were real. The grief is also real. Book XXIII holds both without resolving either.

## The dominant arc of Book XXIII

```
grief (the ghost) → grief (the ghost fades) → sacrifice (the pyre built) → sacrifice (the pyre lit) → law (bones gathered, mound raised) → law (games announced) → return (the race) → divine-intervention (gods interfere) → comedy (the dispute, resolved) → comedy (the boxing) → comedy (the games at play) → memory (Nestor honored, the old games) → grief (Achilles alone)
```

Book XXIII is the poem's argument that life reasserts itself through grief, not despite it — that the community must compete and laugh and dispute prize-rulings and remember the dead, and that all of it is how the living stay alive. Achilles knows this. He organizes the games himself. He still goes to sleep alone.
