# Heike Book II: Court Ascendancy, Rising Unease

After the Heiji Uprising, Kiyomori stands at the apex of military power. The Minamoto are scattered or dead. The imperial court has accepted Taira dominion as the new condition of existence. And yet Book II does not celebrate this triumph — it documents the corrosion that absolute victory brings to the soul of the victor. The book is the portrait of a man and a clan at the moment when they have won everything and are beginning, without knowing it, to lose the only things that would have made the winning meaningful: legitimacy that derives from anything other than force, the affection or even genuine respect of those they rule, and the ability to imagine a future other than their own perpetual dominance.

The arc moves from triumph through consolidation into unease and finally into a kind of creeping dread — not yet the dread of falling, but the dread of the isolated wielder of power who suddenly cannot hear anything but the echo of his own command. Kiyomori is now so large that the court itself has become a thing he must dominate rather than participate in. In winning the court, he has lost access to it.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Berlioz — Rákóczy March from La damnation de Faust** — *kingship*

The Heiji Uprising ends. Kiyomori has defeated his enemies and stands alone. The text does not dwell on the victory itself — it moves quickly to its aftermath: the enumeration of the vanquished, the installation of Taira men into every office that matters. Berlioz's Rákóczy March is triumph, but it is the triumph of a composer who understood that military victory is a beginning, not a conclusion. The march is ceremonial, grand, unambiguous — and yet it carries a kind of melancholy in its splendor. Kingship in its most militant form, yet already tinged with the knowledge that what has been won must now be held, and holding is harder than taking. The requirement — ceremonial grandeur, sovereign display — is met with a particular coloration: this grandeur is not without shadow.

**2. Fauré — Pelléas et Mélisande — Prelude** — *memory*

The text catalogs the Taira appointments with what seems like dry administrative precision: thirty-seven clan members in high office, nephews and sons placed in the great ministries, daughters married to imperial princes. But the recitation has a quality of memorial — this is being recorded as if it must be preserved because it will not last. The court that was is no longer. The court that is, is no longer quite itself. Fauré's prelude is not celebration but a kind of veiling — the music suggests atmosphere rather than action, presence rather than event. It introduces a permanent mist into the palace: these men in these offices are wearing the robes of court, but they do not quite inhabit the court the way their predecessors did. Something has been subtracted. Memory requires the quality of nostalgic recall, faded recapitulation; Fauré gives it the further quality of a world seen through water — nothing is quite clear anymore.

**3. Wagner — Tristan und Isolde: Act II, "O sink' hernieder'"** — *desire*

Go-Shirakawa brought Kiyomori into power as an instrument, a military tool to consolidate authority against his rivals. But Kiyomori has begun to see the relationship differently. He no longer serves the retired Emperor — he uses him. The text does not dramatize this as explicit betrayal; it suggests it through action and consequence: when Go-Shirakawa wants something, Kiyomori considers whether it serves Taira interests. When Kiyomori wants something, it is obtained. The text shows the moment when the instrument discovers it no longer requires the hand that wielded it. Wagner's duet is exactly this — two figures who appear to move in concert but who are actually in competition for the same space. The music is sensuously beautiful and poisoned with the knowledge that the union being sung will not hold. Desire requires chromatic longing and deferred resolution; the resolution here is not harmony but rupture.

**4. Szymanowski — Symphony No. 3, II. Andante doloroso** — *desire*

Against this political maneuver runs a more intimate one: the Taira marry into the imperial family. Taira daughters become imperial wives. Imperial sons are born to Taira mothers. This is not unique to the Taira — every great house uses marriage alliance — but the scale is new: the entire future of the imperial succession is being woven into Taira bloodlines. The text treats this with a kind of ritualized formality that masks the violence beneath it: women and men are being coupled according to state policy, and the children they produce will bind the imperial family to the Taira forever. Szymanowski's second movement is sensual and withdrawn — it gives the register of desire that is also instrumentality, passion that is also calculation. The beauty is real; the use to which it is put is problematic. The music does not judge; it simply suggests that love and strategy have become indistinguishable.

**5. Penderecki — Threnody for the Victims of Hiroshima** — *wrath*

The book documents brutal episodes: monks who speak against the Taira are seized; temples that resist are burned; the warrior-monk general Saitō Sanemori is killed and his head paraded. These are not scenes of warfare between armed men but of state violence applied to unarmed religious communities. The Penderecki is relentless and impersonal — it is not the passion of anger but the machinery of terror operating at full capacity. It is wrath as system rather than emotion, the sound of power that has become pure technique, applied without hesitation or mercy. The monks represent a form of authority Kiyomori cannot grasp: legitimacy that does not flow from military force. His response is the only response available to him — destruction. The Penderecki's requirement for rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony is met in its most modernist, least melodic form: the music is almost abstract in its violence, untethered to any human expression.

**6. Arvo Pärt — Cantus in Memoriam Benjamin Britten** — *sacrifice*

Yet against this violence runs an invisible current. The text introduces a motif that will grow through the remaining books: the deliberate withdrawal from the world Kiyomori is building. Women and men of all classes begin to renounce position, beauty, status, everything the court prizes, and take vows in remote monasteries. They are not fleeing in fear; they are choosing a different world. The Pärt is not mournful but resolute — it is the sound of a choice made with full lucidity. The verticality and stillness of the music match the sacrifice requirement exactly: these are souls turning toward what does not pass, away from the impermanence the bells announced in Book I. Sacrifice here is not victimhood but deliberate refusal to participate further in the machinery of power.

**7. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5, IV. Allegro** — *fate*

Kiyomori controls the emperor. He has placed the child under constant supervision, ensured that the child knows no authority but Taira authority, arranged his education in Taira ways. The child is not imprisoned; he is simply remade. This is not conscious malice but the inexorable logic of total victory: Kiyomori must ensure that the next generation continues what he has begun, and the only way to do that is to make the child unable to imagine any other world. Beethoven's fate motif is no longer external doom but internalized constraint — the emperor's future is written already by the fact of his birth into captivity. The requirements of fate — inexorable motif, hammered insistence — are met not through obvious repetition but through the sense that certain choices have been removed from possibility. This is what fate is when it operates through love and care rather than violence.

**8. Vivaldi — Gloria, I. Gloria in excelsis Deo** — *kingship*

The book shows the Taira at their most magnificent: ceremonies, festivals, elegant entertainments fill the capital. The clan displays itself through beauty rather than through force — and the beauty is genuine. Vivaldi's Gloria is unabashed celebration, the sound of a court dressed in magnificence. And this is the text's essential claim: the Taira are not frauds. They are magnificent. The unease comes not from the magnificence but from the fact that such beauty is being marshaled as an instrument of power. Kingship in its most radiant form, yet radiating something rotten at the center — not visible yet, but felt. The ceremony performs its splendor without irony; the irony lies in what the splendor conceals.

**9. Ravel — Daphnis et Chloé, Sunrise / Final Bacchanal** — *metamorphosis*

Under Taira rule, the entire social world is being rearranged. Old hierarchies dissolve; warrior clans rise; the court itself is being transformed from a place where scholars and ritualists held sway into a place where military power is the only logic. The text does not narrate this change as discrete events but as a continuous process of world-dissolution and reformation. Ravel's bacchanal is fluidity and transformation at a grand scale — colors shifting, patterns dissolving and re-forming, a world in perpetual metamorphosis. The changing orchestral color and fluid harmony match the requirement exactly. What was is no longer; what will be is unstable. Kiyomori has unleashed forces he cannot contain — the society itself is being remade in real time.

**10. Barber — Adagio for Strings** — *supplication*

A Buddhist elder, seeing the direction of events, sends a letter to Kiyomori, begging him to change course, to return to the dharma, to remember that all power is impermanent and that his clan's dominance already contains its own destruction. The letter is not angry — it is desperate, precise, and measured. Kiyomori does not answer it. The Barber is exactly this tone: profound supplication suspended in the knowledge that it will not be heard. The exposed melodic line carries both the monk's clarity and the futility of his appeal. He is right. He will be ignored. And both things are true at once. Supplication in the form of a prophecy that cannot save because it cannot be heard.

**11. Shostakovich — String Quartet No. 8, I. Largo** — *grief*

Book II closes not with an external event but with an inward moment. Kiyomori, alone at night, seized by something like unease that he cannot name. He has everything. He has won. And something is wrong. The text does not elaborate; it leaves the moment suspended. Shostakovich's quartet opens in a grief that is not yet mourning but premonition — the slow, inexorable realization that victory has been hollow, that the apparatus of power isolates its wielder from everything that makes life intelligible. This is not yet the fall; it is the moment when the fall becomes inevitable. The music suggests that Kiyomori is already, without knowing it, beginning to drift away from the world he has conquered.

## The Dominant Arc of Book II

```
kingship (triumph) → memory (fading) → desire (alliance corrupted) →
desire (reproduction as policy) → wrath (systematic violence) →
sacrifice (spiritual refusal) → fate (captive succession) →
kingship (magnificent display) → metamorphosis (world rearranged) →
supplication (unheeded warning) → grief (premonition without form)
```

Book II is scored as the moment when triumph becomes isolation. The arc does not rise and fall; it circles, turning inward. The Taira begin at the apex and end in a kind of private despair — not yet aware of the forces gathering against them, but already beginning to feel, in the darkness of the night, that something has shifted. The dominant force is **kingship** — the book is about the exercise and paradoxes of absolute power — but kingship here is not triumphant. It is a crown that isolates its wearer, a dominion that requires the subjugation of everything that would have made dominion meaningful.
