# Heike Book XII: Dan-no-ura — The Drowning of the Clan

Book XII is the Heike's final canto, the conclusion that the whole work has been moving toward since the opening bells of Gion Shoja announced impermanence. The Taira fleet arrives at Dan-no-ura, a narrow strait in the Inland Sea, and there encounters the Genji in the final, massive sea battle of the Gempei War. The book is not primarily about military maneuvers — though the text records them — but about what happens when an entire people, who have held the imperial throne, who ruled for generations, who built cities and commanded armies, accept that they are about to cease to exist.

The text is ruthlessly specific: the child Emperor, unwilling to face capture, is told by his grandmother that the Buddha-lands lie to the west; she takes him in her arms and walks into the sea. Taira no Tomomori, bound to his former Emperor's body, walks into the sea. Lady Nii and her women walk into the sea. Those who do not walk are killed in the final slaughter. The text records their deaths not with heroic language but with a kind of terrible clarity — names, ages, family relations, last words. The book is a meditation on total annihilation and the strange grace that can exist even in that.

The dominant force is **fate** brought to its ultimate realization: the work that began with "mujo" (impermanence) ends with its final manifestation. Nothing remains; nothing can remain; and in that finality there is a kind of completeness, a shape made whole by its own ending.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Verdi — La forza del destino, the final scena** — *fate*

The Taira fleet and the Genji fleet meet in the narrow waters of Dan-no-ura. There is no surprise, no sudden revelation — everything about this moment was inevitable from the first book. The Genji force is overwhelming; the outcome is certain; and yet the battle must still be fought, must still be recorded, must still occur in history before it becomes legend. Verdi's final scena from La forza is the moment when the relentless three-note fate-motif is finally resolved, when the inexorable force that has driven the whole opera reaches its ending. But the resolution is not triumph; it is completion. Both fleets know that this moment is where the story had to arrive.

**2. Berlioz — Symphonie fantastique, V. Rêve d'une nuit du Sabbat** — *wrath*

The Genji attack, and for a time the Taira fight back with the skill of seasoned warriors. Men fall on both sides; ships collide and lock; archers rain arrows into the massed troops. The battle is furious, chaotic, and ultimately hopeless — the Taira numbers are too small, the Genji too many. But in this hopelessness there is a kind of purity: the Taira are no longer fighting to win but to maintain the only thing left — their dignity in the act of falling. Berlioz's infernal rout is wrath as chaos and beauty at once, the orchestra throwing itself at the listener with no regard for resolution or comfort. This is the final expenditure of what remains.

**3. Stravinsky — Le Sacre du Printemps, The Sacrificial Dance** — *sacrifice*

As the Taira fleet begins to break and sink, the survivors make their choice. They will not be taken prisoner, will not see the child Emperor humiliated, will not permit the sacred regalia to be possessed by the Genji. They will walk into the sea. The text names them as they go: Lady Nii takes the child Emperor into her arms and steps from the deck into the water. Tomomori binds himself to an ancient enemy and descends together. The warriors who are left walk, dive, jump, choosing the manner of their ending. The sacrifice force reaches its apotheosis here — ritual weight and processional immolation-build are no longer musical metaphors but the literal action of a people ritualizing their own ending. The body of the Taira clan, all at once, becomes the offering.

**4. Mahler — Symphony No. 9, IV. Adagio** — *grief*

In the chaos of the final drowning, Kumagai Naozane — the man who killed Atsumori at Ichi-no-tani and became a monk for it — finds himself in the water, dying like everyone else. He encounters Taira no Tomoakira, the father of the boy he killed years ago. The two men recognize each other across the gulf that separates them. There is no time for speech, but in that recognition all the grief of the whole epic crystallizes: not the abstract fall of a clan, but the particular person who was loved and is now dead, and the particular person who killed him and is now also about to die. Mahler's Adagio is the longest, deepest meditation on farewell — letting go of someone who has lived a full life and now faces the absolute end. But here it is applied to a moment of violent dying, and the juxtaposition makes the grief complete.

**5. Tavener — The Beautiful Names, Al-Aziz** — *revelation*

The child Emperor, in Lady Nii's arms as they descend into the water, experiences a moment of clarity. The text is understated about this — there is no ecstatic vision, no supernatural manifestation — but the reader understands that in this moment, the distinction between life and death, between the realm of suffering and the realm of the Buddha-lands, is dissolved. The fear falls away. The child is no longer afraid. This is not triumph or compensation for loss — it is not offered as making the loss acceptable — but it is a moment of absolute recognition that what is happening is complete in itself.

**6. Scriabin — Vers la Flamme Op. 72** — *desire*

The Genji have won. The Taira are gone. The imperial capital is returned to Genji control. The regalia are recovered — or most of it; some sank with the clan. Everything Yoshitsune fought for has been achieved. But the moment of victory is strangely hollow. The sea is full of bodies; the Inland Sea is stained with blood; and what the Genji have won is a nation already half-empty of the people who filled it. The desire has been fulfilled — the Taira have been destroyed, the threat is ended — but fulfillment brings not triumph but a kind of terrible emptiness. Scriabin's ecstatic climax and the void that follows capture this precisely: the reaching toward what is wanted, the sudden arrival at the wanted thing, and the recognition that arrival brings no satisfaction.

**7. Pärt — Cantus in Memoriam Benjamin Britten** — *ascent*

A handful of Taira survive the sea — some by accident, some by deliberate choice not to die, some pulled aboard Genji ships and preserved as prisoners. They rise from the water into the light, dripping, stunned, alive when everyone expected them to be dead. This is not a redemptive moment — the survivors will mostly die in captivity or choose to follow their lord into the Buddha-lands — but it is a moment of strange ascent. The living rising from the water becomes the opening of the next part of the story, the part where the Taira, though defeated, begin their transformation into the ghosts and legends that will haunt the narrative forever. Pärt's rising line is luminous orchestration, ascending sequence, the sound of something moving upward through weight and darkness.

**8. Bach — The Musical Offering, Ricercar a 6** — *law*

In the aftermath of Dan-no-ura, the realm must be ordered again. Yoshitsune takes the titles he has earned; Yoritomo consolidates power; the surviving Taira are paraded, questioned, imprisoned. The machinery of law moves forward — inquiries are held, lineages are declared forfeit, the machinery of the state reasserts itself over the chaos of the sea. Bach's six-part ricercar is the supreme musical statement of formal order: six independent voices winding through each other in perfect mathematical relationship. But this order is not the same as the order that existed before. It is order built on the graves of the Taira, order secured by their annihilation. The perfection of Bach's fugue structure is pristine and cold.

**9. Messiaen — Chronochromie, I. Introduction** — *divine-intervention*

In the nights following Dan-no-ura, the Genji soldiers report visions: the drowned Taira appear, not vengeful but mournful, asking for prayers and for remembrance. Yoshitsune orders monks to chant for the dead; offerings are made; the living begin the work of caring for those who can no longer care for themselves. This is divine-intervention in a new register: not as the sudden reversal of outcome but as the breaking-through of the boundary between the living and the dead, the immediate appearance of the transcendent in the form of ghosts who are also prayers. Messiaen's passage is sudden color shift, the supernatural made manifest as a change in the texture of ordinary reality. The dead do not depart but remain, attached to the living by need and by love.

**10. Ravel — Daphnis et Chloé, sunrise and daybreak** — *metamorphosis*

The book's tenth movement is not a scene from the text but the understanding that emerges in the aftermath: the Taira have been transformed. They are no longer a political force or a military threat or even, strictly speaking, a clan. They have become the Taira of legend — the glorious defeated, the clan that was proud and fell, the figures who move through the tale of the Heike as reminders of impermanence and beauty and loss. The transformation is not one they chose or one that brings them compensation, but it is complete and irreversible. Ravel's sunrise passage is changing orchestral color, fluid harmony, the world undergoes metamorphosis without stopping to ask permission — the light gradually replacing the dark, form emerging from formlessness, but never stopping, never settling into a final state.

**11. Fauré — Requiem, In Paradisum** — *salvation*

The final moment of the Tale of the Heike is not in the text itself but in the fact that the text exists, that it is sung, that it is remembered, that it moves from voice to voice and time to time. The Taira were drowned at Dan-no-ura — all of them, effectively, killed by the waters and by the choice to enter the waters. But they are saved from total annihilation by the act of being remembered. The Heike Monogatari exists because a succession of singers and writers believed that the story was worth preserving, worth passing on, worth saving from the forgetting that claims all things. The salvation is not rescue from death — that was never offered — but salvation from being forgotten, from having been wholly without meaning. Fauré's In Paradisum is the requiem's word of peace: the reaching toward rest, the acceptance that even the departed have somewhere to dwell, even in memory. The Taira are dead, and they are also alive in every voice that says their names.

## The Dominant Arc of Book XII

```
fate (the fleets meet) → wrath (the battle) → sacrifice (the drowning) →
grief (Kumagai and Tomoakira) → revelation (the child Emperor's last moment) →
desire (the victory that yields nothing) → ascent (the survivors rise from the water) →
law (the realm is ordered) → divine-intervention (the ghosts appear) →
metamorphosis (the Taira become legend) → salvation (the singing that preserves)
```

Book XII is the Heike's final statement on its central theme: impermanence is not punishment; it is the nature of things. The dominant force is **fate** — not as something to fight against, but as the shape that all things take when time is allowed to complete its work. The Taira, who began as the proud and magnificent founders of a dynasty, end in the waters of Dan-no-ura — not as tragedy (which would require them to be wronged), but as the completion of a pattern that was always there, promised in the first bells of Gion Shoja.

Yet the book does not end in despair. It ends in the recognition that even annihilation is not absolute. The Taira drown, yes; they are erased from the world of power, yes; they cease to exist as a political force, yes. But they are preserved in memory, in song, in the act of telling their story across a thousand years. And that preservation — not of their lives, but of their story — is itself a kind of salvation: the knowledge that what we make, what we make of grief and beauty and the courage to face ending, matters; is worth keeping; is worth singing again and again into the dark. The bells of impermanence that opened the Tale of the Heike have tolled the clan into the sea, but the Tale itself — the telling itself — begins where the drowning ends, and the Taira, though dead, live in every voice that says their names.
