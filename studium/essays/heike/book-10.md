# Heike Book X: Yashima — The Fortress of Memory

Book X opens with the Taira settled at Yashima, a fortified island position where they have gathered the survivors of Ichi-no-tani. The clan is diminished — carrying the child Emperor, their treasures, and the ghosts of the dead (Atsumori and Tadanori and the thousands who fell on the beach) — but not yet broken. The book is neither triumph nor collapse but suspension: a clan that has lost its homeland and is trying, desperately, to hold what remains.

Here occur some of the Heike's most famous episodes: the fan-throwing contest, where a young Taira warrior shoots down a fan held aloft by a Genji archer in a moment of pure martial skill and beauty that stands entirely apart from any outcome; the religious ceremonies the court maintains to sanctify the child Emperor's refuge; the rumors and messengers that arrive daily with news that the Minamoto are drawing closer. Yashima is a book of suspended time — neither victory nor defeat, but a people holding their breath before the tide comes in.

The dominant force is **desire** — the desire to endure, to preserve something from the wreckage, to maintain beauty and ceremony and the child's royal person even as everything else falls away. It is the desire that makes the inevitable ending something more than mere catastrophe, and it is desire that gives the fortress its strange, poignant grace.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Dvořák — Symphony No. 9 'From the New World', II. Largo** — *return*

The Taira have fled Ichi-no-tani and crossed the Inland Sea to Yashima, an island fortress that will be their last stronghold before Dan-no-ura. They bring with them the child Emperor, the regalia, the remnants of their court. The journey itself is a return to the sea — the element that has carried them from the beginning, now become both womb and tomb. Dvořák's Largo is the circular form, the recognition motif — the lonely English horn that speaks of wandering made home, journey made temporary refuge. The Taira settle into what they hope will be a place of holding, an island where time might stop. But the Largo carries a deeper sorrow: it is a return that is also exile, a homecoming that is a dispersal.

**2. Szymanowski — Symphony No. 3 'Song of the Night', I. Adagio** — *desire*

Yashima is a religious site as well as a military one. The Taira maintain daily ceremonies to sanctify the child Emperor's refuge, invoking Buddhist protection and Shinto favor. There is an intensity to these observances — not the casual piety of peacetime, but the desperate reaching toward divine protection by a people who have lost everything else. Szymanowski's opening is chromatic longing, deferred resolution — a soprano voice rising over strings in a prayer that wants something it knows it cannot have. The Taira sing the prayers not because they believe the gods will answer, but because singing is the last act left to them — the maintenance of beauty and form against the breaking wave.

**3. Elgar — Cello Concerto in E Minor, I. Adagio** — *memory*

News arrives at Yashima that the boy warrior Atsumori, killed at Ichi-no-tani, has become legendary. The enemy — Kumagai Naozane — has renounced his arms and become a monk, praying daily for the youth's soul. The Taira understand that Atsumori, though dead, has entered the realm of memory where he cannot be touched by further loss. The Elgar Cello Concerto is nostalgic recall, faded recapitulation — the instrument itself speaking for those who no longer can. Atsumori's flute, Tadanori's poem, the beauty they possessed in life now radiating backward, untouchable, purified by their own passing.

**4. Saint-Saëns — Danse Macabre, the fanfare** — *ascent*

One of the most famous episodes in all the Heike occurs now: a Genji archer holds aloft a painted fan as a target for a Taira warrior. The young Taira warrior — identified by tradition as Nasu no Yoichi — draws his bow and shoots the fan from the air at a distance that seems impossible. The fan spins off, and both armies cheer. It is a moment of pure martial beauty, entirely divorced from outcome. Saint-Saëns' fanfare is the apotheosis of the gesture, the music rising into the light as the arrow rises toward the fan. This moment, more than any victory, is what will survive — not the outcome, but the beauty of the attempt itself.

**5. Messiaen — Turangalîla-Symphonie, VII. Turangalîla I** — *divine-intervention*

The fan falls, and in the moment of Taira triumph, the Genji commander does not retreat or despair. He does something unexpected: he draws his bow and shoots an arrow that passes so close to the young archer that both armies fall silent. It is a gesture — a response that is neither defeat nor victory, but a statement from one warrior to another across the line of battle. Messiaen's passage is sudden color shift, the brass and strings in unanticipated contrast — a moment where something from outside the expected order appears. This reply makes the fan episode a conversation, a shared moment of chivalry that transcends the war itself.

**6. Scriabin — Piano Concerto Op. 20, I. Allegro agitato** — *desire*

Shigehira, one of Kiyomori's sons and a military commander of considerable talents, still harbors hopes of restoration. The Taira fleet at Yashima is substantial; the child Emperor is safe; if the fortifications hold and the Genji hesitate, might not the war turn again? Scriabin's opening is chromatic longing with an edge of ecstasy — the piano line reaching insistently upward, seeking resolution that keeps receding. Shigehira believes, or wants to believe, or wants to want to believe, that the impossible might yet be undone. The music gives that striving its exact pitch — not despair, but the energy of grasping at what has been lost.

**7. Tavener — Song for Athene, the chant** — *revelation*

A Buddhist priest, in meditation at Yashima, has a vision of the child Emperor's fate. The text does not elaborate, and we are not told what he sees, but the vision troubles him in ways he cannot speak. He knows something that the court does not — something about what waits at Dan-no-ura. Tavener's Song for Athene is chant-like, vertical rather than horizontal, the voices rising in lament for something already lost though not yet taken. Something is seen, something is known, something that cannot be unseen or unknown, hanging over the island like a veil.

**8. Bach — The Art of Fugue, Contrapunctus XIV (unfinished)** — *law*

The Taira court at Yashima is a study in formal defiance. Despite everything lost, the court maintains its protocols, its hierarchies, its ceremonial order. Courtiers serve the child Emperor with exact propriety; seating is formal; the imperial standards are kept. There is no desperation visible in the forms — they are enacted with the precision of a ritual that must be performed correctly or not at all. Bach's unfinished fugue is form under the deepest pressure, a mathematical architecture that continues even as the composer's hand falters and stops. The court's refusal to abandon its forms becomes a kind of heroic defiance, the attempt to maintain an order that has become obsolete.

**9. Debussy — Pelléas et Mélisande, the opening** — *metamorphosis*

Messengers arrive daily now with news: the Genji are moving by sea; the eastern Minamoto cavalry is being concentrated; Yoshitsune's fleet is growing. The Taira do not flee — they cannot; the child Emperor and the regalia cannot be moved so constantly — but the news transforms the fortress from refuge into cage. The walls that were protection become limitation. Debussy's opening is changing orchestral color, fluid harmony, a world undergoing metamorphosis without stopping to announce it. Sanctuary becomes prison, hope becomes waiting, defiance becomes endurance.

**10. Stravinsky — Le Sacre du Printemps, The Sacrificial Dance** — *sacrifice*

At some point in the Yashima episodes, the decision crystallizes: the Taira will not surrender the child Emperor to the Genji. They will hold Yashima as long as possible, and if it falls, they will take him with them into the sea rather than see him captive. This is the moment when the book's suspended time ends and the path to the ending becomes fixed. Stravinsky's Sacrificial Dance is ritual weight, processional build, the music's most relentless immolation sequence — rhythms hammering, driving toward a conclusion that cannot be stopped. The Taira are choosing their ending, not the gift-like self-offering of Imai Kanehira in Book IX, but the acceptance that everything will be spent, all will be lost, and the losing itself will be made into an act of honor.

**11. Sibelius — Tapiola, the forest awakens** — *exile*

Book X closes on the island as evening comes. The Taira are not refugees anymore; they are exiles from everywhere — from the capital, from victory, from the future. They have a fortress, a child Emperor, a fleet, ceremonies, order, but they have nowhere to return to and no path forward that does not lead through loss. Sibelius' Tapiola is wandering tonality, open intervals, the sound of a people displaced into a place that does not welcome them. The Taira at Yashima are not waiting to go home. They are learning what it is to have no home left to go to — and in that learning, they are becoming a different people, exiled not from a place but from time itself.

## The Dominant Arc of Book X

```
return (refuge found) → desire (prayer and ceremony) → memory (Atsumori honored) →
ascent (the fan shot down) → divine-intervention (the reply) → desire (hope for restoration) →
revelation (the monk's vision) → law (the court maintains order) → metamorphosis (the noose tightens) →
sacrifice (the choice to sail toward the sea) → exile (the fortress as prison)
```

Book X is the Heike's meditation on suspension — a moment between climax and ending where a doomed people try to hold what they have left. The dominant force is **desire**: the desire to endure, to preserve something, to maintain beauty and ceremony even as the tide rises. Three moments stand out: the return to Yashima as a refuge made temporary by the knowledge that it cannot hold; the fan-throwing contest, where a young warrior achieves a moment of pure grace in the middle of catastrophe; and the underlying current of knowledge, carried by the monk's vision and the circling Genji fleet, that what is coming is unavoidable. But desire gives it all weight — not the striving to escape (which would be wrath or rebellion), but the reaching toward beauty and order and the preservation of what cannot be preserved. In this reaching, the Taira, already broken, become something more: not noble (the text offers no such comfort), but human in the fullness of that term — reaching, hoping, holding on, knowing it will not be enough.
