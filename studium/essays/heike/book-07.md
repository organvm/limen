# Heike Book VII: The Flight from the Capital

For six books the Tale of the Heike has watched the Taira rise, hold, and harden — a power that bent the imperial court, bought the realm with its wealth, and silenced resistance with its violence. Book VII is where all of that becomes weight, and the weight begins to fall. The reversal comes from the north, where Kiso Yoshinaka, a Minamoto cousin raised in the mountains, has gathered an army and now comes down on the capital like weather. At Kurikara Pass he destroys the main Taira host in a single night by the famous stratagem of oxen with torches lashed to their horns, stampeding the great army over the cliff into a ravine where thousands are crushed and drowned in the dark. The news reaches Kyoto, and the unthinkable becomes a plan: the Taira cannot hold the capital.

So they leave it. They burn their own mansions at Rokuhara rather than yield them to the enemy; they gather the child-Emperor Antoku and the three sacred regalia and carry them westward; and overnight they become what they had made of so many others — exiles, refugees, people of no fixed place. The book is built out of partings. Koremori leaves his wife and small children behind in the doomed city, not knowing if he will return. The poet Tadanori turns his horse around in the middle of the flight and rides back through the burning streets to entrust his life's verses to his teacher Shunzei, asking only that one poem survive in the imperial anthology, even if it must appear under "author unknown." He wants not his name but his art to outlast him.

The dominant force is **exile** — wandering tonality, open intervals, the home dissolving behind the travelers. Impermanence, which has been the bells' philosophy since the opening of the work, stops being an idea in Book VII and becomes a road, walked west under the autumn sky. What rose in Book I is, in Book VII, on the road.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Bruckner — Symphony No. 8, IV. Finale** — *ascent*

The book opens far from the capital, in the northern provinces, where Yoshinaka has been gathering men. The Heike describes the Minamoto resurgence not as a single event but as an accumulation, a pressure building in the country the Taira assumed was pacified. Bruckner's Eighth finale is exactly this register: a rising sequence that gathers mass as it climbs, brass piling on brass until the orchestra seems to lift the world. But ascent in the Heike is never innocent — what rises here rises to throw down what rose before it. The mountain wind is gathering, and it is blowing toward Kyoto.

**2. Holst — The Planets, I. Mars, the Bringer of War** — *wrath*

At Kurikara, Yoshinaka pins the Taira host against the head of a ravine and at nightfall looses a herd of oxen with lighted torches bound to their horns, driving them into the camp; in the dark and the panic the great army stampedes over the cliff. Holst's Mars is the perfect engine of it — a relentless 5/4 ostinato, mechanical and impersonal, a war-machine that cannot be reasoned with. The requirement, rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony, is met as pure momentum: a mass of bodies and animals carried past the point of control toward the edge.

**3. Penderecki — Threnody to the Victims of Hiroshima** — *plague*

The text turns from the stratagem to its result: the ravine choked with men and horses, the river dammed with bodies. Penderecki's Threnody is the only adequate sound — fifty-two strings sliding into a single mass of dissonance that is neither melody nor chord but the texture of disaster itself. The plague requirement, descending motion and dark orchestration, becomes something below music, a glissando into the pit. The Taira are not defeated in the ordinary sense; a piece of their world simply falls into a hole in the earth.

**4. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 4, I** — *fate*

Word of Kurikara reaches the capital, and with it a recognition no one in the Taira council can avoid: the heart of their army is gone and Kyoto is indefensible. Tchaikovsky's Fourth opens with the brass fanfare the composer himself called Fate — the implacable announcement that hangs over the whole symphony and cannot be argued away. The Taira do not decide to lose the capital; they discover that they already have, and the only remaining decision is how to leave.

**5. Ravel — La Valse** — *metamorphosis*

Rather than leave their palaces to the enemy, the Taira burn Rokuhara — the seat of their power, the mansions from which they ruled. Ravel's La Valse is the exact image: a waltz, the music of an aristocratic order at its most opulent, that swells and spins and then disintegrates from within, the elegant figures dissolving into a roar. Metamorphosis here is solid into smoke, the world of fixed mansions becoming a fluid of ash. The Taira do not merely leave Rokuhara; they unmake it with their own hands.

**6. Mahler — Symphony No. 9, IV. Adagio** — *grief*

The flight forces an impossible choice on Koremori: the road west is no place for women and children, and so he must leave his wife and small son and daughter behind in the doomed city. The text gives the parting in full — the children clinging, the wife's collapse, Koremori tearing himself away because the clan is moving and will not wait. Mahler's Ninth Adagio is the music of last farewells, long descending lines that keep trying to resolve and keep failing. This is the most private wound inside the public catastrophe: one father's hand pulled out of his children's hands.

**7. Elgar — Enigma Variations, IX. Nimrod** — *memory*

The book's most quietly famous scene: Tadanori, warrior and poet, turns back through the burning city to the house of Shunzei, the anthologist compiling an imperial collection, and asks one thing — that a poem of his be included, even under "author unknown." He wants not his name but his art to survive. Elgar's Nimrod is the sound of exactly this devotion, a slow nobly building elegy for everything worth preserving. The poem survives; the name is lost; Tadanori chose correctly.

**8. Walton — Crown Imperial** — *kingship*

The Taira carry the one thing that gives their flight any legitimacy: the child-Emperor Antoku and the three sacred regalia. As long as they hold the Emperor, they can argue the court travels with them and the Minamoto in Kyoto are mere rebels. Walton's Crown Imperial is ceremonial sovereignty in full pomp — but scored here for a coronation in reverse, majesty carried out the back gate of its own capital. The grandeur is real and the regalia genuine, and they are being loaded onto boats to flee. Majesty in motion is still majesty, but it has no throne left to sit on.

**9. Sibelius — Symphony No. 4, I** — *exile*

This is the book's title and center: the Taira on the road, leaving the capital their ancestors won, becoming exiles in their own country. The text follows them west past places named in old poems, each name a reminder of the world they are losing. Sibelius's Fourth is the bleakest, most wandering music he wrote — open intervals that refuse to settle, a tonality that drifts without finding home, vast cold spaces between the notes. This is the dominant force of the whole book made audible: the proud clan that bent the realm to its will is now a line of travelers, with no fixed place and no certainty of arrival.

**10. Arvo Pärt — Spiegel im Spiegel** — *supplication*

Stripped of their fortresses and their army, the Taira have little left but prayer — invocations to the Buddha, to the gods of the shrines they pass, to whatever power might still intervene for a house that has lost the Mandate. Pärt's Spiegel im Spiegel is the most exposed possible supplication: a single line over the slow rocking of a piano, nothing hidden, nothing defended. There is no demand left in the Taira voice now, only petition: spare us, receive us, let something of us survive.

**11. Górecki — Symphony No. 3, I. Lento** — *grief*

The book closes by turning back to look at the empty city — the capital open and masterless, its great houses burned, the autumn wind in the gardens, the temple bells sounding over streets the clan has vanished from. Górecki's Third, with its long canon rising out of silence and its setting of a mother's lament, is the grief of a whole place mourning at once: not one death but the death of an order. The bells of Gion Shoja that opened the work toll here for a fulfilled prophecy — the proud did not last; they were as a dream on a spring night; and the city they left is the silence after the dream.

## The Dominant Arc of Book VII

```
ascent (the north rises) → wrath (Kurikara, the fire-oxen) →
plague (the ravine of the dead) → fate (the capital cannot be held) →
metamorphosis (Rokuhara burns) → grief (Koremori's parting) →
memory (Tadanori's poem) → kingship (the regalia in flight) →
exile (the road west) → supplication (prayers on the road) →
grief (bells over the empty capital)
```

Book VII is the great inversion of the whole work. For six books the Taira were the center of gravity; here they become the thing that falls. The dominant force is **exile** — the proud clan turned overnight into a procession of wanderers, carrying their child-Emperor and their grief west under the autumn sky. The book's genius is to refuse the easy satisfaction of an enemy's defeat. It does not gloat over Kurikara; it grieves Koremori's children and honors Tadanori's poem and tolls the bells over the city the Taira left behind. Impermanence here is no longer a thesis. It is a road, and the Taira are on it, and there is no way back to the capital — only the sea ahead, and the books that will close over them there.
