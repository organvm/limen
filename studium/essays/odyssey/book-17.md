# Odyssey Book XVII: Argos, the Footstool, and the Invisible King
## A Classical Playlist for the Book Where Grief Wears a Beggar's Rags

Book XVII is the Odyssey's most sustained experiment in what we might call grief as disguise. Odysseus walks into his own kingdom in rags, on purpose, by plan — the cleverest man alive performing helplessness so precisely that no one in the hall can see who he is. He has to be invisible. The plan requires it. He walks past his own dog, past his own servants, past his own wife's herald carrying bread to him as charity, and must not flinch. The dominant force of the book is not the suitors' wrath or the cunning of the disguise — it is grief: a sustained, inward, swallowed grief that Odysseus carries through every scene without being permitted to set it down.

The crack in the armor is Argos. Homer gives the dog ten lines — one of the most compressed elegies in Western literature — and then moves on, because the poem cannot afford to stop there. Argos was raised and trained by Odysseus, hunted with him, was a great dog once, and now lies on the dung-heap by the gate, flea-ridden, too old to stand. He lifts his head. He wags his tail once. He drops it. He dies. Odysseus wipes his eye and walks into the hall. The playlist cannot afford to move on as quickly as the poem does. Track 6 — Górecki's second movement — holds the scene the text cannot hold without breaking.

After Argos, the book becomes an intelligence operation and a slow escalation of contempt. Odysseus begs his way around the hall, reading every suitor. Antinous holds back his bread and makes a speech about it. Then he throws a footstool. The wrath tracks near the end of the arc are grief's other face — the grief of a man who has been patient for twenty years and is beginning, under the surface, to measure the distance between now and the moment he can stop pretending.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Sibelius — Symphony No. 2, II. Andante e quasi adagio** *(exile: wandering tonality, open intervals)*

The walk from Eumaeus's farm to the town is exile at ground level. Odysseus is moving through his own land as though it belongs to someone else, and it effectively does: the paths are the same, the hills are the same, but the man walking them has no claim to anything he sees. He is a stranger by design.

Sibelius's second symphony slow movement is the darkest and most rootless piece in his orchestral catalogue — pizzicato strings marking a landscape without orientation, the melodic line searching for a tonal center it never secures. This is not the oceanic exile of 'The Oceanides' or the visionary wandering of Vaughan Williams; it is exile at a smaller, colder scale, a man on foot in a familiar country that will not acknowledge him. The open intervals hold the wrongness of the scene without dramatizing it.

---

**2. Schubert — Winterreise, 'Der Lindenbaum'** *(memory: nostalgic recall, faded recapitulation)*

Eumaeus walks beside the disguised king and speaks of the household: Penelope's faithfulness, the suitors' destruction, what Ithaca used to be. He is narrating Odysseus's own history to Odysseus, a cruelty the swineherd cannot know he is inflicting. Every sentence that comes out of Eumaeus's mouth is a wound that Odysseus must not flinch from.

Schubert's 'Lindenbaum' is the Winterreise cycle's great song of nostalgic recall: a wanderer passing the linden tree where he once carved his name and felt at home, hearing the branches whisper that he could rest there. The major-key recollection and the minor-key reality coexist in the song's harmonic structure — the past and present occupying the same musical phrase. Memory's requirement is nostalgic recall and faded recapitulation; the 'Lindenbaum' locates this in the body of a traveler who knows he cannot stop and will not turn back.

---

**3. Handel — Messiah, 'Hallelujah' chorus** *(divine-intervention: sudden color shift, brass-strings contrast)*

The fountain of the Nymphs stands at the edge of town, built by Ithacans in an earlier age. It is a sacred threshold, and Odysseus is crossing from the pastoral world of the farm into the world of his hall. The gods have arranged this return systematically since Book I — Athena's interventions, the divine councils, the long machinery of the plan — and this fountain marks where that machinery is now delivering its result.

Handel's 'Hallelujah' chorus meets the divine-intervention requirement of sudden color shift and brass-strings contrast with an authority nothing else can match: the full orchestra and chorus enter at once, sovereign and declaratory, announcing a power in motion. The choice is not ironic. The gods are not mocking Odysseus in his rags; they are fulfilling a plan that has twenty years of momentum behind it. The return is beginning. The brass announces the arrival without softening it into something tentative.

---

**4. Prokofiev — Scythian Suite, II. 'The Enemy God and the Dance of the Dark Spirits'** *(wrath: rhythmic pressure, unresolved harmony)*

Melanthius the goatherd is the book's first act of humiliation. He taunts Eumaeus for bringing a worthless beggar to the hall, prophesies that the stranger will find nothing but abuse, and kicks Odysseus in the hip as he passes. Odysseus grips his hands into fists, considers killing him, and holds back.

Prokofiev's second movement from the Scythian Suite is the darkest piece in his early orchestral output: bass figures lurching in asymmetric rhythms, brass writing of a dissonant sullenness that never resolves, a grinding malevolence without the explosive release of Bartók or the mechanical accumulation of Shostakovich. Wrath's requirement is rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony; the dark-spirits movement delivers both at a register of mean, strutting contempt — not heroic villainy but the self-satisfied cruelty of a small man with temporary advantage. Melanthius kicks Odysseus because he can and because he has forgotten to imagine that anything could change.

---

**5. Pärt — Für Alina** *(memory: nostalgic recall, faded recapitulation)*

Homer introduces Argos in the past tense. Once Odysseus raised him, hunted with him, ran him on the plains of Ithaca while the dog was young and great. Now he lies on the dung-heap, neglected since his master left, full of fleas, too old to stand. The introduction is a recollection before the recognition: what the dog was, and what twenty years without care have made of him. The scene is not written as grief — it is written as memory, a before and after held in the same frame, the gap between them too large to measure.

Pärt's Für Alina is one of the most tender and compressed pieces in the twentieth-century piano repertoire: a single tintinnabuli melody, each note placed with absolute deliberateness, the silences between notes as weighted as the notes themselves. It is the sonic form of something very old and very still, attending. Memory's requirement is nostalgic recall and faded recapitulation; Für Alina supplies both without sentimentality — its harmonic economy mirrors the economy of the scene directly. Homer gives Argos ten lines. Pärt gives the piece barely a minute's material. Both achieve compression rather than reduction: not less than the subject deserves, but exactly as much as it can bear. What is recalled in this track is not a grand career but a particular fidelity — a dog on a dung-heap recognizing his master across twenty years of abandonment — and Für Alina is the most tender small death in the repertoire, music for the moment just before something very faithful stops.

---

**6. Górecki — Symphony No. 3 'Symphony of Sorrowful Songs', Op. 36, II. Lento e largo** *(grief: slow tempo, inward line)*

Ten lines in the Greek. Argos lifts his head. He drops his ears and wags his tail — the only unambiguous gesture of welcome Odysseus will receive until the bow is strung — and dies. Odysseus turns his head away. He wipes his eye. He walks into the hall.

Homer does not stop here because the poem cannot afford to stop here and remain a poem about a cunning man making a plan. The playlist can afford to stop. Górecki's second movement of the Symphony of Sorrowful Songs sets a text scratched on the wall of a Gestapo cell by an eighteen-year-old girl: 'O Mamo nie płacz — nie — Niebios Przeczysta Królowo — Ty zawsze wspieraj mnie' (Oh Mother, do not weep — no — Immaculate Queen of Heaven — always support me). A completely exposed soprano line over trembling strings, a message left where no one expected to find it, grief that has no audience and does not require one. Grief's requirement is slow tempo and inward line; this movement is both: intimate, exposed, a gesture of fidelity performed in private by someone who cannot stop and knows it. Argos wags his tail into an absence. The soprano sings into an absence. Both are held in the same register.

---

**7. Schubert — Symphony No. 8 in B minor ('Unfinished'), II. Andante con moto** *(return: circular form, recognition motif)*

Odysseus steps across the threshold into his own hall. The suitors are there, eating and drinking, occupying his tables. He knows the hall — the same pillars, the same fire, the same threshold he crossed every day for years before Troy — and none of it belongs to him.

This is the strangest and most painful return in Western literature, and Schubert's second movement of the Unfinished captures it with precision. Where the first movement (used in this cycle for Odysseus's departure-and-exile in Book I) is driven and forward-reaching, the second is interior and suspended — a theme that circles back without resolving, recognition without arrival, the familiar made wrong. Return's requirement is circular form and recognition motif; the second movement provides both in the most ambiguous register possible. You are home. Home does not know you are home. The music holds this gap in formal suspension, neither grieving nor celebrating, simply turning in the key of a place that used to fit.

---

**8. Handel — Samson, 'Total Eclipse' (Act I, aria)** *(supplication: suspended cadence, exposed melodic line)*

Telemachus cannot acknowledge his father in public and cannot watch him starve. He instructs the herald Medon quietly to carry bread and meat across the hall to the stranger. This is an act of charity that is secretly an act of fidelity — a son feeding his father without being able to say so.

Handel wrote 'Total Eclipse' for Samson, the blinded hero standing captive in the hall of his enemies, receiving care from those who must pretend not to know him. 'Total eclipse! No sun, no moon, all dark amidst the blaze of noon.' Supplication's requirement is suspended cadence and exposed melodic line; the aria delivers both in the register of a great man reduced, his power gone, his identity concealed, receiving sustenance that cannot name what it knows. The thematic parallel is not ornamental: Samson and Odysseus are both heroes in abjection, both sustained by fidelity that cannot speak itself, both waiting for the moment when the strength they are concealing can finally be used.

---

**9. Bach — Mass in B minor, BWV 232, 'Qui tollis' (chorus, Credo)** *(revelation: stillness, chant, verticality)*

Odysseus moves around the hall, begging from man to man, bowl extended. He is not humiliated — he is working. Each suitor he approaches gives him information: the ones who give, the ones who refuse, the ones who look at him with something like unease, the ones who laugh. He is building a census of who will die and in what order.

Bach's 'Qui tollis' from the Mass in B minor is the most formally vertical piece in the choral canon: a chorus of absolute liturgical weight, the harmonic movement glacial and authoritative, each voice entry arriving with the patience of something that does not need to hurry because it knows how this ends. Revelation's requirement is stillness, chant, and verticality. Odysseus begging the circuit of the hall is revelation enacted as sustained, methodical attention — not a flash of divine knowledge but the slow accumulation of evidence, note by note, man by man, until the full architecture of judgment is complete.

**10. Nielsen — Symphony No. 5, I. Tempo giusto (snare-drum episode)** *(wrath: rhythmic pressure, unresolved harmony)*

Antinous refuses the bowl. Where other suitors have given — grudgingly, some of them, but given — Antinous makes a speech about it: the beggar is a social problem, Eumaeus should not have brought him here, strangers are an imposition. Odysseus answers him with controlled precision, naming the injustice without losing the beggar's mask, and the hall watches.

Nielsen's Fifth Symphony first movement contains the most formally startling episode of unresolved wrath in the symphonic canon: a snare drummer is instructed to play a disruptive, improvised counter-rhythm against the orchestra, resisting absorption, refusing to be silenced, maintaining its rhythmic pressure outside the harmonic language of everything surrounding it. Wrath's requirement is rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony; Nielsen's episode is wrath as contention — not explosion but sustained refusal, a force that will not be integrated. This is Antinous across the table: the man who has decided the rules do not apply to him, whose contempt operates at a frequency the hall cannot absorb.

---

**11. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 4 in C minor, Op. 43, III. Presto** *(wrath: rhythmic pressure, unresolved harmony)*

Antinous picks up the footstool and throws it. It strikes Odysseus on the right shoulder. He does not move. He does not cry out. He stands there with the bowl and takes it. Even some suitors go quiet; there is a recognition in the hall that something has been done that should not have been done.

Shostakovich's Fourth Symphony finale is the most brutal closing movement he ever wrote — a Presto of mechanized, systematic force, rhythmic patterns of grotesque regularity, harmonic dissonance that never offers relief. This is not the passionate wrath of a Beethoven outburst or the heroic wrath of a battle piece; it is the wrath of men who have been permitted to do what they like for so long that the doing has become reflexive. Antinous throws the footstool the way a man swats a fly: casually, without passion, because nothing has taught him yet that there are consequences. Shostakovich understood the grammar of institutional cruelty — violence as habit, harm as background noise.

---

**12. Sibelius — Symphony No. 5 in E-flat major, Op. 82, III. Allegro molto (hammer-blow finale)** *(fate: inexorable motif, hammered insistence)*

Penelope hears through the herald what has been done in her hall. She is furious, but more than that: she wants to see the stranger. She sends word asking him to come to her. She suspects he may carry news of Odysseus. The summons ends the book.

This summons is not a dramatic climax — it is a structural click, a mechanism engaging. The meeting between Penelope and the disguised Odysseus, which will extend across Books XVIII and XIX into the great recognition, has now been set in motion. Sibelius's Fifth Symphony finale ends not with resolution but with six isolated hammer-blows — chords separated by silence, each arrival deliberate, the rhythm of something completing itself. Fate's requirement is inexorable motif and hammered insistence; the hammer-blows are the most precisely formal enactment of that requirement in the symphonic repertoire. The gods have been running this plan for twenty years. Penelope's summons is the mechanism clicking into the next position. The blows fall, one by one, into silence.

## The Dominant Arc of Book XVII

```
exile (the road) → memory (the swineherd speaks) → divine-intervention (the threshold) → wrath (Melanthius) → memory (Argos, who he was) → grief (Argos, who he is / who dies) → return (the hall, wrong) → supplication (bread from his own son) → revelation (the census of the suitors) → wrath (refusal) → wrath (the footstool) → fate (the summons closes)
```

This is why the playlist moves Sibelius → Schubert → Handel → Prokofiev → Pärt → Górecki → Schubert → Handel → Bach → Nielsen → Shostakovich → Sibelius.

The arc begins in exile and ends in fate, but the center — tracks 5 and 6, Pärt and Górecki — is what everything else is organized around. The book's genius is that Homer does not pause at the center. He gives Argos his ten lines and moves to the next scene. The music pauses there because pausing is what music can do that Homer, at the speed of oral epic, cannot afford. The grief that Odysseus carries through the rest of the book — the begging circuit, the footstool, the composed acceptance of the blow — is the grief of someone who has already passed track 6 and is still walking.
