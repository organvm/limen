# Odyssey Book XXIV: The Second Nekyia, the Orchard, and the Covenant of Peace
## A Classical Playlist for the Book Where the Poem Earns Its Stillness

Book XXIV is not a coda. It is the poem's second descent — and its hardest earned close. Homer does not end the Odyssey with Odysseus restored to the throne, feasting in the hall, Penelope beside him. He ends it with an old man in an orchard, dressed in rags, who has been tending trees for twenty years because tending trees is the only act that still connects him to the son he believes is dead. The recognition that unlocks the book's emotional center comes not through the scar, not through the bow, but through a child's memory of a walk through an orchard with his father: thirteen pear trees, ten apple trees, forty fig trees, the rows of vines. That is the Odyssey's final and most intimate proof — an inheritance of planted things, a father's hand in a boy's, the names of what grows.

The book holds two registers simultaneously. In Hades, the suitors' shades arrive and Agamemnon, who has been in the underworld since before the poem began, hears the full story of Penelope's fidelity and cannot stop comparing it to his own wife's treachery. In Ithaca, Odysseus goes to his father before anyone else, and the meeting is marked by a strange prolonged cruelty: Odysseus gives Laertes a false name, tells him a false story, watches him grieve for news of the son standing in front of him, and only then crosses the scene and speaks. Both registers are grief — the grief of the permanently separated and the grief of a man watching his father perform grief for him — and the arc from that doubled grief through the brief violence of the kinsmen's revenge to Athena's covenant of peace and the poem's final stillness is the arc the playlist scores.

The dominant force is grief: not catastrophic, not public, not the funeral-march register that Book XXII unlocked for Hector, but the slow accumulating weight of what time costs the people who are left behind. The playlist moves that grief through memory (two tracks for the dead comparing marriages in Hades) into the orchard's double grief, and then through the revelatory moment when the orchard names unlock Laertes, and forward through wrath and divine-intervention into the formal covenant of law that allows the final track — the one the whole cycle has been working toward — to arrive in a resolving radiance that is not triumph but peace honestly earned.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Gluck — Orfeo ed Euridice, 'Che farò senza Euridice'** *(grief: slow tempo, inward line)*

The suitors arrive in Hades as bats knocked from a roost — Homer's simile, not an interpretation. They are not tormented; they are diminished, the faded copies of men who had weight in the world. Hermes leads them past Oceanus, past the White Rock, past the Gate of the Sun and the People of Dreams, to the meadow of asphodel where the shades mill and drift. Homer describes their arrival with clinical efficiency. He does not mourn them.

But the underworld's threshold requires music, and the threshold's music is Gluck's: 'What shall I do without Euridice? Where shall I go without my darling?' — the aria Orfeo sings when he has lost her a second time, at the boundary between the living and the dead, with nothing left to bargain with. The piece is grief as pure melodic recursion, the phrase returning on itself because grief at this scale has no development, only recurrence. It gives the meadow of asphodel its correct register: not horror, not punishment, but the quiet permanent diminishment of souls deprived of light.

---

**2. Schubert — Winterreise, 'Der Lindenbaum' (No. 5)** *(memory: nostalgic recall, faded recapitulation)*

Agamemnon, shade among shades, listens to Amphimedon recount everything: the web woven and unwoven, the bow contest, the slaughter in the hall. He receives it the way the dead receive news of the living — with a wondering distance, not quite present to the weight of the events, because the dead have crossed past the place where things can still be changed. He is remembering a world he left before the suitors even began their siege.

Schubert's 'Der Lindenbaum' is the song of a wanderer who passes the linden tree where he had once carved his name and whispered his hopes into the branches. The tree seems to call him back: here you would have found rest. The song is faded recapitulation at its most exact — a sweetness at the border of despair, the mind reaching back toward something it cannot recover, the memory of a place that was once alive with meaning now heard from a cold distance. Agamemnon receiving the story of the house he will never see again inhabits exactly this register.

---

**3. Brahms — Horn Trio in E-flat major, Op. 40, I. Andante** *(memory: nostalgic recall, faded recapitulation)*

Agamemnon praises Penelope's faithfulness — she will be a song of virtue forever — and then places her directly against Clytemnestra, whose treachery made his own name hateful. Two marriages, one war, twenty years: the same absence, two different choices. The dead man sees both outcomes whole, from a vantage point that the living don't get. He is not bitter; he is beyond bitterness, in the register of understanding arrived at too late to use.

Brahms specified the natural horn for this trio precisely because the natural horn carries the overtone color of things remembered rather than things present — hunting, distance, the sound of something receding into the trees. The Andante moves in the faded-recapitulation mode that is memory's sonic requirement: warm, autumnal, turning over its material with the unhurried attention of a mind that has time now, that is no longer pressed by any immediate need, that can hold two things side by side and simply regard them. Agamemnon in the meadow of asphodel comparing Penelope and Clytemnestra is exactly this: contemplation at permanent remove.

---

**4. Mahler — Kindertotenlieder, No. 1 'Nun will die Sonn' so hell aufgeh'n'** *(grief: slow tempo, inward line)*

Homer's description of Laertes is among the most quietly devastating passages in either epic: the father of Odysseus, dressed in filthy patched clothing, bent over the soil of his own orchard, grieving outwardly in the way the destitute and the bereft do because there is nothing left to disguise it. He has been tending the trees because tending the trees is the only act that still connects him to the son who once tended them beside him. Odysseus watches from a distance and his own heart tears.

Rückert wrote the Kindertotenlieder poems after losing two of his children; the first song opens with the observation that the sun rises this morning as bright as ever, as if nothing had ended, as if a child had not died — and that observation is the whole bitterness of grief. Mahler's orchestral writing is grief made cellular: the voice carries the thought, and the orchestra beneath it is the weight that the thought has to carry. Grief requires slow tempo and inward line; this movement is the canonical form of grief at the wrongness of a world continuing in its ordinary courses when something essential is missing. Laertes in the orchard is that image.

---

**5. Elgar — Cello Concerto in E minor, Op. 85, I. Adagio — Moderato** *(grief: slow tempo, inward line)*

Odysseus gives his father a false name. He tells him Odysseus is gone. He watches Laertes pour dirt on his gray head and weep openly in the orchard for news of the son standing before him in disguise. This is the Odyssey's strangest scene and its most prolonged act of deliberate cruelty, and neither Homer nor the commentators have ever satisfactorily explained why Odysseus does it. What is clear is that he stands there one last moment in the role of witness before he becomes the son — and that it costs him to watch.

Elgar wrote his Cello Concerto after the First World War had ended the England he had spent his whole career composing for. The opening Adagio is an inward-turned soliloquy: the cello speaks to itself at a private register that is not performance but personal reckoning, the sound of a consciousness that has lived past the world it was shaped by. It is grief as private soliloquy, slow and inward, not demonstrative, not reaching outward for consolation. This is the music of a man who cannot yet cross the scene but must stand at its edge and watch. The cello's slow arc holds the false name in the air and does not yet speak the true one.

---

**6. Messiaen — Éclairs sur l'Au-delà, X. 'Le chemin de l'invisible'** *(revelation: stillness, chant, verticality)*

Odysseus cannot bear it. He crosses the scene. He takes his father by the hands and names himself. But the recognition reaches its fullest form not in the embrace but in what follows: Odysseus recites the trees his father gave him as a boy, walking this same orchard, naming each row — thirteen pear trees, ten apple trees, forty fig trees, fifty vine rows — the inheritance of a father's hand laid into the earth for a child who would inherit it. Laertes' knees give out. The proof is in the names of planted things.

Messiaen titled this movement 'The Path of the Invisible' — and that is the grammar of this recognition: the invisible thing that has been walking the orchard for twenty years suddenly made visible, not through a dramatic gesture but through the naming of what was given and remembered and kept. Revelation requires stillness, chant, and verticality; Messiaen builds his harmonic columns the way recognition builds — the preparation is long and interior, the arrival is a vertical fact that does not argue for itself. The orchard trees are the path of the invisible made suddenly present. Messiaen understood that divine disclosure and a child's memory of his father's gift can require exactly the same register.

---

**7. Dvořák — Symphony No. 8 in G major, Op. 88, IV. Allegro ma non troppo** *(return: circular form, recognition motif)*

Athena does something Homer rarely does for anyone in the poem: she gives Laertes stature back, the bloom of age returning to his limbs and face. He arms himself and stands beside his son and grandson — three generations of Ithaca's line in the same orchard where the recognition just happened — and when Eupeithes leads the kinsmen toward them, Laertes throws his spear and kills him and shouts with joy. The old man has come back.

Dvořák's Eighth Symphony finale is joyful in the specific register that return requires: not triumphant, not martial, but abundantly warm, its theme-and-variation form circling back to the opening fanfare again and again in a kind of exuberant recognition, the melody rediscovering itself with each new variation as though arriving home after a long absence. The movement is the musical form of something being restored to its right relationship with the world. Laertes restored is exactly this: not youth recovered, but the right order briefly and improbably re-established, the father standing with the son in the orchard they both know.

---

**8. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 11 in G minor 'The Year 1905', II. Allegretto ('The Ninth of January')** *(wrath: rhythmic pressure, unresolved harmony)*

Eupeithes, father of Antinous, the suitor Homer singles out for the first killing, arms the kinsmen of the dead and marches them toward Odysseus's estate. They are not wrong by Homer's logic: the law of blood-debt was binding, and Odysseus killed two hundred men. But they are too late, and the gods have already decided against them, and they are walking into a battle they will not be allowed to finish.

Shostakovich's second movement depicts the massacre of peaceful demonstrators in St. Petersburg's Palace Square in January 1905, but its musical effect is the mechanism of collective violence: a march-rhythm that accumulates mass steadily and without individual will, the dissonances underneath the surface never fully resolving, the rhythmic pressure not the expression of any particular anger but the momentum of an obligation pressing forward. Wrath here is not passion but structure — institutional forward motion toward an inevitable collision. The kinsmen march because blood-debt is the only law they can still invoke.

---

**9. Walton — Belshazzar's Feast, 'Praise ye the God of Gold' (opening invocation and first section)** *(wrath: rhythmic pressure, unresolved harmony)*

The battle is brief. Odysseus and Telemachus fight; Laertes kills Eupeithes; the fighting is real and close before Athena intervenes. This is the Odyssey's final expenditure of martial energy — not the Iliad's mythological-scale combat but the intimate, urgent violence of men fighting in an orchard over the right to govern a small island. It burns hard and ends fast.

Walton's Belshazzar's Feast opens with a baritone proclamation that gathers mass like a tide: the brass layer upon choral layer, the rhythmic engine building without resolution, collective violence rendered as polyphonic mechanism. It is the right wrath for the Odyssey's ending: not Mars-scale, not cosmic, but the sound of many voices pressing together in the same urgent direction, the harmony never settling because the event is not settled yet, the pressure sustaining itself until the moment the god intervenes. After this track, the music stops being the fighters' music and becomes the gods'.

---

**10. Handel — Messiah, HWV 56, 'Hallelujah' Chorus** *(divine-intervention: sudden color shift, brass/strings contrast)*

Athena shouts in the voice of Mentor. Zeus fires a thunderbolt between the forces. It lands in the earth between them and the fighters freeze. This is not a request or a suggestion. Olympian intervention at this scale is sovereign command — the word of Zeus delivered through brass and fire — and it terminates human activity not by argument but by vertical authority landing from outside the scene entirely.

The Hallelujah Chorus arrives with exactly this quality. There is no preparation — the opening cry descends without preamble, the brass and strings split immediately into contrasting masses, the choral declaration 'Hallelujah' does not argue its case because it does not need to. Divine-intervention requires sudden color shift and brass/strings contrast; Handel gives both in the opening bar, and the sustained repetition that follows is not doubt but the formal iteration of a fact already established. 'The kingdom of this world is become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ' — structurally identical to what Zeus's thunderbolt asserts: that the order of human events has just been superseded by the order of divine authority, and there is no appeal.

---

**11. Bach — Mass in B minor, BWV 232, 'Et in terra pax' (Gloria, chorus)** *(law: formal clarity, repetition, architecture)*

Athena commands the covenants of peace and both sides swear. The families of the slaughtered suitors and the household of Odysseus enter a legal settlement, enforced by divine authority, that will govern Ithaca going forward. This is not reconciliation in the modern emotional sense — it is law: binding, formal, witnessed by the divine presence that has structured the poem's action from its beginning. The fighting does not end because everyone feels better. It ends because a covenant has been sworn.

Bach's 'Et in terra pax' from the Gloria is the musical form of a covenant. The choral voices enter in succession, each adding its layer to the contrapuntal architecture, the text declaring 'and on earth peace, goodwill toward men' in a texture of perfect formal order — repetition, architecture, the kind of formal clarity that law requires. The movement is brief and structurally inevitable, a close that knows exactly what it is doing and does it without ornament or sentimentality. The covenant of peace in Homer is not sentimental either: it is a legal act imposed on parties with legitimate grievances by a god with the authority to enforce it. Bach understood what that structure sounds like.

---

**12. Beethoven — Missa Solemnis, Op. 123, 'Agnus Dei: Dona nobis pacem'** *(salvation: resolving radiance, chorale apotheosis)*

Homer's last line: Athena urges the peace; both sides agree; the poem ends. No feast, no ceremony, no elaborate closure — the Odyssey closes with a breath. After twenty years of war and wandering, after the slaughter and the recognition scenes and the false names and the orchard, after the long patience of Penelope and the wasted years of Laertes and the suitors eating the cattle and Telemachus growing up without his father and Odysseus lashed to a mast or held on an island or navigating between monsters, the poem simply stops in a stillness that is not silence but rest.

Beethoven marked a passage in the 'Dona nobis pacem' 'a plea for inward and outward peace.' The movement earns what it arrives at: it begins in anxiety, passes through martial interruptions that recall the violence that makes peace necessary, and reaches its final settlement not as a triumph over conflict but as a reconciliation with what conflict costs. The peace is real, but it has been earned through the full weight of what came before. Salvation requires resolving radiance and chorale apotheosis; Beethoven gives both, but through the harder path — through the anxious plea, through the military episode, through the sustained effort of a movement that knows peace is not the absence of what came before but the presence of something that can hold it. That is the Odyssey's peace exactly.

## The Dominant Arc of Book XXIV

```
grief (shades descend) → memory (dead man hearing) → memory (two marriages compared) → grief (father in the orchard) → grief (false tale, father weeps) → revelation (the orchard names unlock) → return (Laertes restored) → wrath (kinsmen march) → wrath (battle) → divine-intervention (the thunderbolt) → law (the covenant sworn) → salvation (stillness)
```

This is why the playlist moves Gluck → Schubert → Brahms → Mahler → Elgar → Messiaen → Dvořák → Shostakovich → Walton → Handel → Bach → Beethoven.

Grief builds the foundation; memory keeps the dead in the conversation; revelation unlocks the orchard; return restores the father; wrath burns briefly and hard; divine authority lands vertically and stops everything; law formalizes the peace; salvation completes it. Book XXIV is where the Odyssey stops being a poem about homecoming and becomes a poem about what home costs the people who kept it while the hero was away. The final stillness is not Odysseus's alone. It belongs to Penelope, to Laertes, to Telemachus, to the orchard rows that waited in the earth.
