# Odyssey Book X: The Bag of Winds, the Cannibal Giants, and the Enchantress
## A Classical Playlist for the Book Where Every State of Being Is Unstable

Book X is the Odyssey's great metamorphic engine. In two hundred and seventy lines, a fleet of twelve ships becomes one; a scouting party becomes a herd of swine; an enchantress becomes a lover; a year of feasting becomes a year that must be abandoned; and the forward path to Ithaca is revealed as leading, first, through the land of the dead. Every scene in the book enacts a transformation of state — not metaphorically but literally, materially, in the bodies of men who grow bristles and snouts and then, when the ointment is applied, recover themselves and are taller and more beautiful than before. Homer does not moralize the transformations. He watches them happen with the same impassive precision he brings to the catalogues of the dead.

The playlist begins with divine provision at its most theatrical — Aeolus's bag of winds, nine days of blessed sailing, Ithaca visible on the horizon — and it ends with divine instruction at its most terrible: go to Hades, speak with the dead, learn the path from a man who has been blind since before Odysseus was born. Between those two moments, the dominant force is metamorphosis: every track works with material in the process of becoming something else, every harmonic surface refuses to stay fixed, every formal resolution is a setup for the next transformation. The exception is the eighth track — Bach's Goldberg Aria for the scene where Odysseus masters Circe with a sword and an oath — because the pact that formalizes their relationship is the book's single moment of lawful stability, the one transaction that does not dissolve into something other than what it was.

The year of forgetting is the book's buried moral center. After the men are restored, after the feasting and the union and the abundance of the island, a year passes in which no one tries to go home. When the men finally remind Odysseus, he weeps. He has been the one to forget longest. The playlist does not score this as weakness. It scores it as the correct human response to an island where the hospitality is genuine, the danger is past, and the sea that waits outside the harbor is unforgivingly cold.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Mendelssohn — A Midsummer Night's Dream, Overture, Op. 21** *(divine-intervention: sudden color shift, brass/strings contrast)*

Aeolus, king of the winds, gives Odysseus a sealed bag of oxhide — every hostile gale imprisoned, a westerly to carry the fleet straight home. For nine days the ships sail in flawless order. Ithaca begins to appear on the horizon. It is the poem's single moment of unambiguous divine gift, and it is working.

Mendelssohn's Overture opens with four wind chords in the quiet of string harmonics — a sound that comes from nowhere and then dissolves into an orchestral world governed by invisible forces operating at perfect lightness. The famous string pianissimo opening does not suggest menace; it suggests a world where enchantment is real and currently arranged in your favor. Brass arrive like organized gusts. The whole piece enacts passage into a zone where the rules have been suspended by a benevolent authority.

Divine-intervention requires sudden color shift and brass/strings contrast, and the Overture provides both with a comic-magical buoyancy that exactly matches the nine days before the catastrophe. This is not ironic scoring. Those nine days were genuinely good. The bag held. The westerly blew. For nine days, the gods kept their word.

---

**2. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 4 in F minor, Op. 36, IV. Allegro con fuoco** *(fate: inexorable motif, hammered insistence)*

Odysseus falls asleep within sight of home. The crew have been watching the bag and muttering — he must be carrying home treasures from Aeolus's island, gold and silver, why should he have it all? They open it. Every wind in the world erupts. The storm drives the fleet back past everything they have crossed, back to Aeolia, farther from home than they have been since Troy.

Tchaikovsky built his Fourth Symphony around a fate motif announced in the brass opening of the first movement — a figure that promises the thing you fear most will come back. The finale brings it back at the moment the folk-dance celebrations are in full swing: the hammer arrives in the middle of festivity, not as announcement but as resumption, the thing that was never actually resolved. The Allegro con fuoco is fate as the return of what was only ever temporarily suppressed. The crew opened a bag; what came out was always going to come out.

Fate requires inexorable motif and hammered insistence, and the finale provides both without the explicit four-note grammar of Beethoven's Fifth — which belongs to the Iliad. Tchaikovsky's fate motif is a longer, more narrative figure, the shape of a thing that rises and falls and rises again on its own schedule, indifferent to what the man in its path happens to be doing when it returns.

**3. Sibelius — Tapiola, Op. 112** *(exile: wandering tonality, open intervals)*

Odysseus returns to Aeolia and begs. Aeolus looks at him and says: go away. A man the gods hate cannot be helped; helping him would only anger the gods at him. He sends the fleet away without provisions, without wind, without a word of sorrow. The men must row. Ithaca, which was visible on the horizon nine days ago, is now across an open sea with no favorable wind.

Sibelius wrote Tapiola as a portrait of the great northern forest and the god who inhabits it — a single theme dispersed into endless permutations, the tonality never arriving anywhere, the texture expanding outward without perimeter or center. It is the most rigorous sustained wandering tonality in the orchestral literature: not a storm (which at least has energy and direction) but the condition of a world that is simply without destination.

Exile requires wandering tonality and open intervals, and Tapiola provides them in their purest form. The fleet that rows away from Aeolia is not fleeing anything or sailing toward anything. It is simply in the condition of being turned away — which is the oldest definition of exile.

**4. Janáček — Sinfonietta, I. Fanfare (Allegretto)** *(wrath: rhythmic pressure, unresolved harmony)*

The Laestrygonians are giants who live where day and night barely alternate, where the paths of herdsmen going out and coming in with their flocks almost cross. The harbor is a perfect trap: cliffs on every side, a narrow entrance, calm water within. Eleven ships sail in. The Laestrygonian king's daughter leads the Greeks to her father. He eats one on the spot. Then the giants swarm the cliffs and hurl boulders, and the ships shatter, and they spear the crews like fish and carry them home to eat.

Janáček's Fanfare is rhythmic assault conducted as ceremony — the brass writing is asymmetric, physically pressurized, a sound of force operating at a register beyond argument or negotiation. What makes it the right piece is not its volume but its formal indifference: it does not build to release, it does not resolve, it sustains its hammered rhythmic cells as if they are self-sufficient, as if this is simply what happens and the question of why does not arise.

Wrath requires rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony, but the Laestrygonians are not wrathful in any moral sense. They are hungry and enormous. Janáček's Fanfare catches the impersonal violence of scale — the sound of a world where the proportions are catastrophically wrong and there is no one to appeal to about it.

**5. Górecki — Symphony No. 3 'Symphony of Sorrowful Songs', II. Lento e largo** *(grief: slow tempo, inward line)*

Odysseus cuts the mooring rope from the outside of the harbor and escapes. His men row. Homer says they rowed with grief in their hearts, happy to be alive, grieving for their lost companions. The fleet is one ship. The silence in that ship is the poem's grief even when the text moves past it at the speed of the surviving ship's oars.

Górecki's second movement is built from the text scratched into the wall of a Gestapo cell by an eighteen-year-old girl: 'O Mamo nie płacz — nie.' The accompaniment is an ostinato of absolute simplicity, a quiet iteration in piano and strings beneath a soprano line that does not melodramatize what it carries. The movement is grief at the scale of loss that cannot yet be spoken — because you are still moving, still rowing, and the grief would stop the oars.

Grief requires slow tempo and an inward line. This movement has both, and it has the specific quality of grief held under the necessity of forward motion — the grief of people who survived by luck and have not yet been given permission to stop and register what happened.

**6. Ravel — Gaspard de la Nuit, I. 'Ondine'** *(metamorphosis: changing orchestral color, fluid harmony)*

The scouts approach Circe's palace through a forest of wild animals — lions and wolves who fawn on the men like dogs, enchanted men from earlier visits, still in their altered states. Inside, Circe sings. They knock. She welcomes them, feeds them, drugs the food, touches each with her wand. Homer says: they had the heads and voice and bristles and body of swine, but their minds were unchanged as before.

Ravel's 'Ondine' is the most seductive metamorphic surface in the keyboard literature — the water-spirit beckoning from her harmonically unstable world, every apparent arrival immediately dissolving into the next shimmering texture, the form itself refusing to crystallize. The opening is water at rest; within seconds it is water in motion; it never stops being in motion. There is no violence in the transformation; there is only the beautiful, inexorable liquefaction of the solid into something more interesting.

Metamorphosis requires changing orchestral color and fluid harmony. Ravel provides both in the register the scene demands — not the tragic metamorphosis of Polyphemus's blinding (book IX's Strauss) but the seductive, recoverable, beautiful-in-its-menace metamorphosis of a goddess who does not hate her victims. She simply prefers them as swine.

**7. Weber — Oberon, Overture** *(divine-intervention: sudden color shift, brass/strings contrast)*

Odysseus sets off alone to find his men. Hermes meets him in the forest — a young man with the first down on his lip, carrying the golden staff. He gives Odysseus the moly: a plant with a black root and a milk-white flower, hard for mortal men to dig but the gods can do anything. He tells Odysseus exactly what to do when Circe tries the drug and the wand: draw your sword, rush at her as if to kill her, and she will invite you to bed, and you must make her swear the oath.

Weber's Overture opens with a solo horn call of crystalline isolation — the fairy-king's summons across an empty space — and then produces one of the most formally complete sudden-color-shift sequences in romantic opera: the horn dissolves into shimmering strings, which give way to an Allegro of irresistible, organized forward motion. The movement from mystical isolation to purposeful action, achieved through an instantaneous change of sonic color and register, is the overture's structural signature.

Divine-intervention requires sudden color shift and brass/strings contrast. The Oberon Overture provides them in the register of beneficent magical assistance — the gift from a god who is genuinely on your side, who arrives with exact instructions and a herb that costs him nothing but changes everything, who hands you the counter-charm with the lightness of someone who has always known how this particular story goes.

**8. Bach — Goldberg Variations, BWV 988 — Aria** *(law: formal clarity, repetition, architecture)*

Circe gives Odysseus the drugged food. It does not work. He draws his sword. She falls to her knees. She says: who are you, what man are you, from what city and what parents? No man has ever resisted my drugs before. You must be Odysseus; Hermes told me you would come. Then she invites him to bed. And Odysseus says: how can I trust you, who just turned my men to swine? Swear me an oath. She swears, by the name of the gods, that she will not harm him.

Bach's Goldberg Aria is a ground bass of fourteen harmonies repeated in perfect regularity, over which a melody of great formal elaboration moves without ever departing from what the ground makes possible. Nothing in the Aria is arbitrary. Every note is derivable from the governing architecture. The beauty is not in the departure from structure but in the discovery of how much can be accomplished within it.

Law requires formal clarity, repetition, and architecture. Circe's oath is a legal instrument — mutual obligation between a mortal and an immortal, witnessed by the gods, with exact terms. The Aria is the sonic form of a legal instrument: not cold, not coercive, but binding in the way that beautiful formal structures are binding — because their regularity is a kind of guarantee, a promise encoded in the grammar of the thing itself.

**9. Ravel — Shéhérazade, III. 'L'Indifférent'** *(desire: chromatic longing, deferred resolution)*

After the oath, Circe bathes Odysseus, anoints him, gives him a beautiful tunic and cloak. The handmaidens bring food. He cannot eat. He sits in silence. She says: why do you sit like a man who has lost his voice? Are you afraid of me still? And he says: how could any decent man eat and drink in your house when his companions are still swine in a sty? She goes to the sty and restores them. Then they feast. Then she and Odysseus go to bed together. The year begins.

Ravel's 'L'Indifférent' is the most erotically complex of the Shéhérazade songs — a youth passes the singer's window without stopping, and the watching becomes desire, and the desire is inseparable from the youth's indifference, and the chromatic harmony defers its resolution through the entire song as if the arrival of the beloved and the resolution of the chord are the same impossible event. The charged space between the watcher and the watched is the song's subject.

Desire requires chromatic longing and deferred resolution. The island of Aiaia provides desire of a complicated kind — Odysseus is genuinely held by Circe, but the holding is also a kind of forgetting, and the feasting is also a kind of suspension, and the year will have to end. Ravel's 'L'Indifférent' gives the scene its correct tonality: not consummation but proximity, not possession but the live current between two people in the same house who have declared a truce and discovered they prefer each other.

**10. Debussy — Nocturnes, III. 'Sirènes'** *(metamorphosis: changing orchestral color, fluid harmony)*

Circe goes to the sty with the ointment and anoints each man's face. The bristles fall. The snouts retract. They are men again. Homer says: they were younger than before, and more beautiful, and taller. The transformation has passed through its worst point and emerged not at the baseline but above it. The scouts weep; Odysseus weeps; even Circe pities them. The island has been transformed: what was a trap is now an enchanted abundance.

Debussy's 'Sirènes' is the only major choral-orchestral nocturne that renders enchantment as a sustained change of sonic state. The wordless women's voices dissolve into the orchestral texture and re-emerge without separation — you cannot tell where the instruments end and the voices begin. The harmonic surface shifts continuously, the music inhabiting a register between the natural and the supernatural without resolving into either. It is the sound of a world that has been made permeable.

Metamorphosis requires changing orchestral color and fluid harmony. 'Sirènes' provides them in the register of restoration rather than dissolution — not the dark seductive liquefaction of track 6 but the luminous, improbable clarification of men who came back from something. The reverse transformation is the book's quiet miracle, and Debussy's nocturne gives it its correct impossible radiance: things that should not be possible are, briefly, the condition of the ordinary.

**11. Schubert — Winterreise, D. 911, 'Der Lindenbaum' (No. 5)** *(memory: nostalgic recall, faded recapitulation)*

A year passes. The men eat well and drink well and sleep in Circe's house. One day they come to Odysseus and say: remember Ithaca? Remember home? He weeps. He does not know how long they have been here. He asks Circe to let them go. She says yes — but first, you must go to the underworld and speak with Tiresias.

Schubert's 'Der Lindenbaum' is the Winterreise song about the linden tree at the town gate where the wanderer once carved his sweetheart's names in the bark, where he sat and dreamed in good times and bad times, where the branches called to him in the cold night as he passed: come here, come find your peace here. He did not stop. The wind cut through his hat. And now, far away, he still hears those branches, still feels as if he should have turned aside there.

Memory requires nostalgic recall and faded recapitulation. The year in Circe's house is exactly the linden tree — a place that offered rest, that was genuinely good, that the men are now being asked to leave for a road that passes through the dead. Schubert's song does not sentimentalize the memory; it gives it its correct weight: the thing you did not take when it was offered, or the thing you took too long and can no longer afford, the tree whose branches you can still hear in the wind when you are far from everything that was comfortable.

**12. Mahler — Symphony No. 2 in C minor 'Resurrection', V. Finale: 'Aufersteh'n' (opening chorus)** *(revelation: stillness, chant, verticality)*

Circe tells Odysseus what he must do. She describes the path to the land of the dead — the route to the river Oceanus, the grove of Persephone, the instructions for the trench and the sacrifice and the blood the shades will drink. She tells him who will come: Tiresias the blind prophet, who alone among the dead retains his understanding. She says: he will tell you your road and how long it is.

Odysseus sits in the dark all night without sleeping. At dawn he rouses his men. One of them, Elpenor, the youngest, wakes from his sleep on the roof and falls and breaks his neck. They leave him there unburied — there is no time — and sail out at Circe's direction to the place she has described.

Mahler's 'Aufersteh'n' chorus opens in a register of absolute stillness — the vast orchestra reduced to the minimum, the chorus entering with the Klopstock text as if from an enormous distance, the voices organized in pure vertical harmony above a bass line that is the floor of the world. What makes it the right piece is not its eventual overwhelmingness but its opening: the sound of something vast being stated quietly, as fact, in the grammar of revelation rather than announcement.

Revelation requires stillness, chant, and verticality. Circe's instructions are not a vision or a prophecy — they are exact logistical information delivered by a goddess who knows the route. But the instruction to descend to the dead is revelation in the structural sense: it discloses the necessary path, the shape of what must happen before anything else can. Odysseus sits all night in the fact of that disclosure. The chorus begins in that stillness.

## The Dominant Arc of Book X

```
divine-intervention (the gift of wind) → fate (the bag opened) → exile (turned away) →
wrath (the giants) → grief (the remnant) → metamorphosis (men as swine) →
divine-intervention (the moly) → law (the oath) → desire (the year) →
metamorphosis (men restored) → memory (a year forgotten) → revelation (go to the dead)
```

Book X is constructed as a double arc: the first half (tracks 1–5) is destruction, the systematic removal of everything Odysseus had assembled — the gift of the winds, the proximity of home, the fleet, the bulk of his men. The second half (tracks 6–12) is transformation, the process by which an enchantress becomes an ally, enemies become lovers, swine become men, and a year of forgetting becomes the necessary prologue to the one journey that cannot be avoided.

The dominant force is metamorphosis because both arcs are transformations of state — the first involuntary and catastrophic, the second voluntary and ambiguous. Every stable identity in the book is subject to revision. The winds were favorable until they were not. The men were men until they were not. The year was restful until it was over. Circe was an enemy until she was not. The path to Ithaca went forward until it went down.

The book ends with Odysseus sailing at dawn toward the underworld, one of his men already dead and unburied on the roof of the enchantress's house, carrying instructions from a goddess who knows how this works because she has seen it before. What has changed is not the destination — it was always Ithaca — but the knowledge of what must be passed through first.
