# Bhagavad Gita Chapter 5: True Renunciation and the Equal Vision

Chapter 5 resolves the apparent contradiction between action (karma yoga) and renunciation (sannyasa). It should not be scored as a dry ethical debate, but as a profound structural shift in how reality is perceived. The drama is the transformation of action itself: from binding labor to liberating offering. We move from the tension of Arjuna's confusion (track 1) to the dissolution of the action/inaction binary (track 2). The arc then reaches the purity of the untouched actor (track 3), the revelation of the equal vision (track 4), the luminous inner ascent of the sage (track 5), and the cyclical return to the world with the peace of Brahman (track 6).

The dominant force is **metamorphosis**.

**1. Bach — *The Art of Fugue, BWV 1080 — Contrapunctus I*** — *law*

Arjuna is still trapped in formal categories—seeking the 'better' rule to follow. Bach's Art of Fugue begins with the strictest formal clarity, exposing the subject in its pure architectural form. Law requires formal clarity and architecture; Contrapunctus I represents the mind trying to structure reality through rigid rules, right before Krishna breaks the binary.

**2. Richard Strauss — *Metamorphosen*** — *metamorphosis*

Krishna explains that the wise see no difference between the two paths. Action is transformed into non-action when the ego is removed. Strauss's Metamorphosen—a study for 23 solo strings—constantly shifts and weaves, dissolving themes into one another. Metamorphosis requires fluid harmony and changing color; this music perfectly captures the state where the rigid lines of 'doing' and 'not doing' melt into a single continuum of being.

**3. Mozart — *Ave verum corpus, K. 618*** — *salvation*

The image of the lotus leaf—resting on the water but never wet—is the Gita's symbol of absolute purity amidst the world. Mozart's Ave verum corpus is a brief, breathtakingly pure motet that moves with absolute grace and lack of friction. Salvation requires resolving radiance; the untroubled, luminous choral harmonies reflect the soul that has crossed beyond the taint of action.

**4. Messiaen — *Quartet for the End of Time — V. Louange à l'Éternité de Jésus*** — *revelation*

This is the true sight: seeing past the temporary forms of the world to the eternal reality within all beings. Messiaen's 'Louange' for cello and piano is excruciatingly slow, asking time to stand still. Revelation requires stillness and verticality; the cello's long, ecstatic lines against the piano's pulsing chords embody a gaze that has finally pierced through the illusion of difference to see the eternal everywhere.

**5. Richard Strauss — *Tod und Verklärung (Death and Transfiguration) — Transfiguration motif*** — *ascent*

The soul turns entirely inward, finding a source of joy that does not depend on external contact. Strauss's transfiguration theme builds in a rising sequence that lifts the entire orchestra into a blindingly bright major key. Ascent requires luminous orchestration and rising sequence; the music swells not with worldly triumph, but with the sudden, overwhelming discovery of the infinite inner space.

**6. Beethoven — *Symphony No. 6 'Pastoral' — V. Shepherd's Song*** — *return*

The final state of Chapter 5 is not an escape from the world, but a peaceful dwelling within it. The sage has attained 'Brahman-nirvana' but continues to live. Beethoven's Pastoral finale is the ultimate music of return and gratitude after the storm. Return requires a circular form and recognition; the gentle, rolling 6/8 rhythm and the repeated themes of thanksgiving perfectly score the state of the sage who walks the earth in perfect peace.

---

The dominant arc of Chapter 5:

```
law →
metamorphosis →
salvation →
revelation →
ascent →
return
```
