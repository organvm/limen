# Bhagavad Gita Chapter 6: The Restless Mind and the Yoga of Meditation

Chapter 6 introduces the mechanics of meditation (Dhyana Yoga), but its emotional core is the struggle with the unyielding mind. It should not be scored as a peaceful, ambient soundscape. The drama is the wrestling match with human nature: the formal instructions of practice (track 1), the concentrated effort of sitting (track 2), Arjuna's desperate realization of the mind's turbulence (track 3), the slow, patient bringing back of the wandering attention (track 4), the existential fear of failing the path completely (track 5), and Krishna's grand promise that no effort is ever lost (track 6).

The dominant force is **exile**.

**1. Handel — *Keyboard Suite in D minor, HWV 437 — III. Sarabande*** — *law*

Krishna begins by laying down the strict definition of what makes a yogi, stripping away outward asceticism for inner discipline. Handel's D minor Sarabande is severe, stately, and unbending. Law requires architecture and formal clarity; the heavy, repeated chordal structure of the Sarabande represents the foundational discipline required before meditation can even begin.

**2. Stravinsky — *Symphony of Psalms — III. Alleluia*** — *sacrifice*

Meditation in the Gita is an intense internal ritual, a sacrifice of the wandering senses into the fire of concentration. Stravinsky's Symphony of Psalms uses driving ostinatos and ritualistic chanting to create an atmosphere of severe devotion. Sacrifice requires ritual weight; the opening of the third movement establishes a massive, repetitive, chanting focus, mimicking the sheer will required to hold the mind steady.

**3. Dvořák — *Symphony No. 9 'From the New World' — II. Largo*** — *exile*

This is the most relatable moment in the Gita: Arjuna despairs at his own nature. The mind wanders, exiled from its own center, lost in the winds of distraction. Dvořák's Largo, with its famous English horn solo, is the ultimate sound of longing and displacement. Exile requires open intervals and wandering tonality; the music aches with the realization of how far the mind has drifted from the peace it seeks.

**4. Schubert — *Piano Sonata in B-flat major, D. 960 — I. Molto moderato*** — *return*

Krishna does not offer a magic trick, only the slow, patient work of bringing the mind back every time it wanders. Schubert's final sonata is a vast, patient landscape where the main theme continually returns, often interrupted by dark trills in the bass. Return requires circular form; the repetitive, hypnotic returning to the gentle main theme captures the gentle but firm discipline of the meditator repeatedly coming back to the point of focus.

**5. Tchaikovsky — *Symphony No. 4 — I. Andante sostenuto - Moderato con anima*** — *fate*

Arjuna voices a terrifying existential dread: if you abandon the material world but fail to reach the spiritual goal, do you just perish in the void? Tchaikovsky's Fourth Symphony opens with the inexorable fanfare of Fate, plunging into a restless, anxious first movement. Fate requires a hammered motif and inexorable pressure; this scores the paralyzing fear of being ground to dust between the gears of cosmic time, having achieved nothing.

**6. Elgar — *Enigma Variations — IX. Nimrod*** — *memory*

Krishna's answer is a breathtaking reassurance: the cosmos remembers. The effort of this life carries over to the next; nothing is lost. Elgar's 'Nimrod' is the quintessential music of noble, nostalgic recall, building slowly from a whisper to a monumental climax. Memory requires a faded recapitulation and nostalgic recall; Elgar provides the profound, tear-inducing relief that the universe is not indifferent, and the soul's progress is held in memory across lifetimes.
