# Bhagavad Gita Chapter 7: The Lower and Higher Natures, the Thread of the World

Chapter 7 moves from ethics and practice to cosmology and theology. Krishna begins to reveal his nature as the absolute reality behind the manifest world. The arc must sound vast and elemental. We begin with the shifting forms of material nature (track 1), then freeze in awe as Krishna reveals himself as the thread holding all forms together (track 2). The human response is varied longing (track 3), leading to the tragic wandering of those caught in illusion (track 4), the eventual surrender that crosses the illusion (track 5), and the final triumphant realization that 'Vasudeva is all' (track 6).

The dominant force is **revelation**.

**1. Debussy — *La Mer — I. De l'aube à midi sur la mer*** — *metamorphosis*

Krishna describes his eightfold lower nature (the material elements and mind) animated by his higher nature. It is the world in constant flux. Debussy's La Mer is not just about the ocean; it is about matter continually shifting form under the play of light. Metamorphosis requires changing color and fluid harmony; Debussy scores the very essence of Prakriti (nature) as a beautiful, restless, ever-changing surface.

**2. John Tavener — *The Protecting Veil*** — *revelation*

This verse is a structural pivot of the Gita: the multiplicity of the world is held together by a single, invisible divine thread. Tavener's The Protecting Veil features a soaring, ecstatic solo cello suspended over a bed of slow-moving strings. Revelation requires stillness and verticality; the cello acts as that divine thread, an unbroken line of luminous presence holding together the vast, slow-breathing cosmos beneath it.

**3. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde — Prelude to Act I*** — *desire*

Krishna says that people turn to the divine for different reasons, but all are driven by a fundamental longing. The Tristan Prelude is the absolute peak of chromatic longing in Western music, endlessly delaying resolution. Desire requires deferred resolution and chromaticism; the music captures the ache of the human condition, reaching blindly in the dark for something that will fulfill it.

**4. Vaughan Williams — *Fantasia on a Theme by Thomas Tallis*** — *exile*

The tragedy of the world is that it is mesmerized by the play of the gunas and cannot see the reality behind them. Vaughan Williams uses divided string orchestras to create a sound of vast, echoing distance, a landscape of mist and shadow. Exile requires wandering tonality and open intervals; the Fantasia feels like wandering through a beautiful but impenetrable fog, perfectly scoring the state of being lost in divine illusion.

**5. Bach — *Mass in B minor, BWV 232 — Dona nobis pacem*** — *salvation*

The only way out of the labyrinth of Maya is total surrender to its creator. Bach's 'Dona nobis pacem' builds an immense, overwhelming structure of vocal polyphony that resolves all the tension of the Mass into a single, massive plea for peace that is granted in the singing of it. Salvation requires chorale apotheosis and resolving radiance; this is the sound of the soul finally breaking through the illusion and finding solid ground.

**6. Bruckner — *Symphony No. 4 'Romantic' — I. Bewegt, nicht zu schnell*** — *ascent*

The highest knowledge is the realization that the divine is not just the creator, but the substance of everything. Bruckner's Fourth Symphony begins with a shimmering tremolo in the strings out of which a solitary, majestic horn call rises, expanding eventually to the full brass. Ascent requires luminous orchestration and a rising sequence; the music scores the dawn of cosmic consciousness, the awe of seeing the universe as a single, living divine body.

The dominant arc of Chapter 7:

```
metamorphosis →

desire →
exile →
salvation →
```
