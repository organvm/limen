# Bhagavad Gita Chapter 2: The Eternal Self, Karma Yoga, the Steady-Minded Sage

Chapter 2 is where the Gita's answer begins — but it does not begin with comfort. It begins with correction. Krishna looks at the weeping man in the chariot and says, in effect: you are grieving for the wrong reason, for the wrong thing, and at the wrong scale. The self you believe is about to die cannot die. The bodies you mourn as future corpses are garments the soul has already begun to shed.

What follows is the most compressed philosophical sequence in the Gita: the ontology of the eternal self (never born, never dying, not slain when the body is slain), the epistemology of action (you have a right to the act but not its fruit), and the phenomenology of the completed human being (the sthitaprajna, the steady-minded one who has finished wanting). Chapter 2 is not one teaching but four, layered so quickly that a reader might not notice the shifts. The music below respects each shift as a separate movement.

The dominant force is **revelation** — not because the chapter lacks law, metamorphosis, ascent, or salvation, but because its central act is the disclosure of the soul's true nature. Everything else in the chapter is the preparation for or consequence of that disclosure.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Bach — Mass in B minor: "Kyrie eleison"** — *law*

Krishna opens with a question that is also a judgment: "Whence this defilement in the hour of crisis?" The first thirty verses of Chapter 2 are the most architectonic passage in the Gita — a logical proof of the soul's immortality delivered as rebuke. The Kyrie of Bach's B minor Mass is the right music for this because it is the sound of supplication governed by form. The voices enter in fugal order, the plea for mercy is structured as a formal procedure, the dissonances are resolved within the architecture of the movement itself. Krishna does not advise Arjuna; he corrects him, and the correction is delivered with the formal clarity of a demonstration. Law's requirement — formal clarity, repetition, architecture — maps directly onto Bach's treatment of the oldest Christian prayer. The Kyrie is mercy as structure, just as Krishna's opening speech is compassion as correction.

**2. Tavener — Song for Athene** — *revelation*

The transition from law to revelation occurs at verse 11: "You grieve for those who should not be grieved for — the wise do not grieve for the dead or the living." What follows is the Gita's central teaching — the soul is never born, never dies, is not slain when the body is slain. The language is insistently negative (never, never, not, not, never) but what it describes is not absence but presence: the self that precedes all predicates. Tavener's Song for Athene — written for a young woman killed in a car accident, performed at Princess Diana's funeral — is not sad. It is ecstatic. The vocal lines rise through repetition, accumulating alleluias, creating a sense of vertical space within which the claims of Chapter 2 can be heard as statements of fact rather than hope. Revelation requires stillness and verticality; Tavener composes the sound of something that has always been true and is only now being heard.

**3. Ravel — Le tombeau de Couperin: I. Prélude** — *metamorphosis*

At verse 22, the Gita offers its most vivid simile: "As a man casts off worn-out clothes and puts on new ones, so the soul casts off worn-out bodies and enters new ones." The image is domestic, ordinary, and radical — death is not more dramatic than changing clothes. Ravel's Prélude is the first movement of a tombeau (memorial) composed for friends lost in World War I, but its most striking quality is its refusal of grief. The flute line moves through the old baroque forms of the dance suite and transforms them into something fluid, color-shifting, and light. The orchestration never settles; the harmonies shift from whole-tone to modal to diatonic and back. That is exactly the musical correlate of the garment-simile: the body is the old harmonic system, the soul is the melody that moves through it unharmed, and death is just a modulation into a new key.

**4. Handel — Water Music: Suite in D, Alla Hornpipe** — *law*

Verse 47: "You have a right to action alone, never to its fruits. Let not the fruits of action be your motive, nor be you attached to inaction." This is the most famous single teaching in the Gita, and it is structured as a legal proposition. The sentence has four clauses, each modifying the last, building a framework within which action can be performed without the anxiety of outcome. The Alla Hornpipe from Handel's Water Music is the emblem of law at its most beautiful: ceremonial, bright, the dotted rhythms giving it the character of a civic announcement. The melody is stated clearly, then embellished, then restated — the form holds, the obligation is visible. Handel wrote this to be heard on the Thames during a royal procession. It is public music, spacious, designed to be heard by many people at once. That is the register of the karma yoga teaching: a principle that restructures action itself, announced in the open, for everyone.

**5. Bruckner — Symphony No. 7: II. Adagio (opening)** — *ascent*

From verse 54 onward, Arjuna asks Krishna to describe the steady-minded sage: "How does one of steady wisdom speak? How does he sit? How does he walk?" The answer is the Gita's portrait of human completion — the one who has cast off desire, is satisfied in the Self alone, and moves among the senses with senses controlled. Bruckner's Seventh Adagio opens in the cellos and basses, a theme so slow and so low that it seems to rise from the ground itself. It ascends through the orchestra without apparent effort, accumulating mass and radiance as it climbs, reaching its peak at a moment that feels not like climax but like arrival. The sthitaprajna passage in Chapter 2 is not about becoming better. It is about completing the work of wanting so that the soul can rest in what it is. Bruckner's Adagio is the sound of that completion: not a striving ascent but an ascension that happens because the weight is gone.

**6. Fauré — Requiem: "In Paradisum"** — *salvation*

Chapter 2 closes with a description of the state of Brahman: "One who is free from desire, self-controlled, and attains peace — this is the state of Brahman. Attaining this, one is never deluded again. And abiding in this state even at the hour of death, one attains the peace of Brahman." Fauré's In Paradisum is the most serene music in the funerary repertoire: the choir ascends through modal harmony — D Dorian, the old church mode — into a radiance that is not meteorological but ontological. The organ holds the harmony; the voices move as if they are walking into light that has always been there. The Gita does not promise the steady-minded sage a future reward. It says he attains Brahman now, while living, while walking the earth. Fauré's Paradisum is not a vision of heaven after death. It is the present condition of the one who has understood. The closing cadence — the voices arriving at the tonic and simply staying there — is the sound of the soul settling into itself and recognizing that it has always been home.

## The dominant arc of Chapter 2

```
law (correction as architecture) → revelation (the eternal self disclosed) →
metamorphosis (the body understood as garment) →
law (karma yoga as legal proposition) →
ascent (the steady-minded sage rising by having ceased to strive) →
salvation (abiding in Brahman as present peace)
```

Chapter 2 is the book of **revelation** — the disclosure of the self's indestructible nature. But the arc does not simply move from ignorance to knowledge. It moves through four distinct modes: Krishna rebukes the confusion (law), discloses the soul (revelation), reframes the body (metamorphosis), restructures action (law again), portrays human completion (ascent), and settles finally into the peace of Brahman (salvation). The two law movements bracket the revelation; the ascent and salvation follow from it. The chiasm is intentional: law first as external correction, law second as internalized principle; revelation first as ontology, revelation finally as experience. Chapter 2 is the architecture of the Gita's entire teaching compressed into a single chapter — and the music above follows that architecture note by note.
