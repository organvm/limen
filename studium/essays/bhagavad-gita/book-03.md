# Bhagavad Gita Chapter 3: The Wheel of Action

In Chapter 2, Krishna laid out a staggering metaphysical proposition: the soul is immortal, and true wisdom lies in detachment. In Chapter 3, Arjuna responds with a perfectly reasonable question: "If knowledge is superior to action, why do you drive me to this terrible deed of war?"

Arjuna wants to withdraw. He wants the peace of the hermit. But Krishna explains that inaction is biologically and cosmetically impossible. Even to maintain the body requires action. The universe itself is a continuous, churning wheel of interdependent action—a cycle of sacrifice (Yajna) where every element nourishes the next. To refuse to participate is not holiness; it is a violation of the cosmic order (Lokasangraha). The music for this chapter shifts away from the abstract logic of Chapter 2 into something rhythmic, cyclical, processional, and heavily grounded in ritual weight.

**1. Stravinsky — The Rite of Spring, "Introduction"** — *the complexity of the manifest world*

Arjuna is bewildered by what he perceives as contradictory advice. He wants to step outside the machinery of the war, to renounce action entirely. But Stravinsky's 'Introduction' begins with a reawakening of primal forces—a complicated, swarming web of overlapping woodwinds that suggests the inescapable, churning complexity of life. It is the sound of a world that cannot simply be paused or opted out of; it pulls the listener back into the dense undergrowth of existence.

**2. Glass — Koyaanisqatsi, "Vessels"** — *the perpetual motion machine*

"No one can remain even for a moment without acting." Krishna establishes a fundamental law of physics and biology: existence is motion. Philip Glass's 'Vessels', with its relentless, cyclical, overlapping choral rhythms, captures this perfectly. It is a perpetual motion machine. It isn't agitated or chaotic; it is simply continuous, a driving engine that underpins all reality, proving that the dream of absolute physical stillness is an illusion.

**3. Wagner — Parsifal, Prelude to Act I** — *the cosmic cycle of nourishment*

Krishna describes the wheel of existence: beings are nourished by food, food is produced by rain, rain comes from sacrifice, and sacrifice is born of prescribed action. It is a holy, interdependent cycle. The Parsifal Prelude is the ultimate music of sacrament. Its brass chorales and shimmering strings move with a slow, processional grace, evoking the profound sanctity of this mutual nourishment. It treats action not as a burden, but as a liturgy.

**4. Elgar — Enigma Variations, "Nimrod"** — *the burden of the standard-bearer*

"Whatever a great man does, that very thing other men do." Krishna tells Arjuna that even if he himself has nothing to gain, he must fight for the sake of world-maintenance (*Lokasangraha*). The masses will follow his example; if he abandons his post, society will collapse into confusion. Elgar's 'Nimrod' provides the exact gravitas for this immense social responsibility. It is noble, weighty, and deeply serious, the music of someone accepting a burden for the sake of those who look up to him.

**5. Beethoven — Symphony No. 7, II. Allegretto** — *the Gunas in motion*

The unawakened man, deluded by ego, thinks "I am the doer." The wise man knows that the qualities of nature (the *Gunas*) are simply acting upon the qualities of nature. Beethoven's Allegretto is the perfect expression of inexorable, structural momentum. The underlying ostinato rhythm drives forward steadily, regardless of the melodic variations playing above it. It mirrors how the fundamental forces of nature propel the world, irrespective of human vanity.

**6. Mahler — Symphony No. 2, V. Finale (Chorale: "Aufersteh'n")** — *the surrender of the fruits*

"Dedicating all actions to Me, with your mind fixed on the Supreme, free from hope and egoism, fight." Krishna offers the mechanism of liberation: action without psychological ownership. Mahler's Resurrection chorale begins quietly, a profound, hushed arrival of grace that lifts the crushing weight of the ego. It is a shift from the grinding wheel of karma into a luminous space of surrender, where the action remains but the anxiety disappears.

**7. Shostakovich — String Quartet No. 8, II. Allegro molto** — *the insatiable fire of desire*

Arjuna asks what force compels a man to sin, even against his will. Krishna's answer is immediate and terrifying: "It is desire (*Kama*), it is wrath (*Krodha*)... the all-consuming, all-polluting enemy." He describes it as an insatiable fire that covers wisdom like smoke covers a flame. Shostakovich's furious, slashing Allegro molto captures this destructive, dark energy. It is relentless and violent—the internal parasite that hijacks the senses and destroys the mind, the true enemy on the battlefield.
