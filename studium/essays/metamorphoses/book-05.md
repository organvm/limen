# Metamorphoses Book V: Medusa's Feast, The Muses' Song

Book V begins where IV ended — in the middle of Perseus' story — and then opens into something much stranger: a song within a song within a song, the poem nesting transformations inside each other like boxes. Phineus attacks the wedding feast, Perseus petrifies him with Medusa's head, and then, without transition, we are on Helicon with the Muses, listening to Calliope sing of Proserpina, Ceres, and Arethusa. The frame is a contest — the Pierides challenged the Muses and lost — but the thematic center is not competition but the power of song to hold and release transformation. The dominant force is **metamorphosis**, which here operates not only as bodies changing form but as stories changing into other stories, the narrative itself refusing to hold a single shape.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Handel — *Music for the Royal Fireworks*: Overture** — *kingship*

Cepheus holds a wedding feast for Perseus and Andromeda. The court of Ethiopia is in full ceremonial display: tables, wine, songs, the hero who saved the princess seated in honor, the kingdom restored. Kingship here is not Zeus on Olympus but the public pageantry of a mortal court celebrating victory. The Royal Fireworks Overture is the 18th century's most unambiguous ceremonial music: brass, timpani, sovereign grandeur, the sound of order restored and festivity declared.

**2. Mussorgsky — *Night on Bald Mountain*** — *wrath*

Phineus bursts in. He was betrothed to Andromeda before Perseus arrived; his right to her was established, his claim was prior. He does not ask. He comes armed, with armed men, and turns the wedding hall into a battlefield. The demonic energy of *Night on Bald Mountain* — its howling strings, its brass eruptions, its wild acceleration — is the sound of a civilized space invaded by the irrational. Mussorgsky's witches' sabbath has the rhythmic pressure and harmonic violence of wrath as mob force.

**3. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 5: IV. Finale** — *fate*

The battle is long. Perseus fights from a position of disadvantage, surrounded, outnumbered. He kills dozens — the poem gives them names, lineages, the way they fell. Fate here is not destiny but the momentum of combat, the way a fight, once it becomes general, cannot be stopped by any individual will. Tchaikovsky's Fifth Finale takes the fate motif from the symphony's opening and transforms it into a march of steadily increasing pressure — the inexorable motif hammered into escalation, the sound of violence that will not cease until no one is left to fight.

**4. Wagner — *Das Rheingold*: Entrance of the Gods into Valhalla** — *divine-intervention*

Perseus can no longer hold the hall. He calls to his friends: turn away. Then he lifts Medusa's head. Phineus' men, mid-strike, freeze into stone — each in the posture of attack, their weapons raised, their bodies hardening into statues that will stand in that hall forever. The Entrance of the Gods into Valhalla is Wagner's most overwhelming flash of cosmic authority: the brass climbs, the strings swell, the rainbow bridge descends, and a new order replaces the old mid-phrase. Perseus' use of Medusa's head is exactly this: a sudden, total shift of the battlefield's grammar, the arrival of a rule that the fight cannot answer.

**5. Bach — Brandenburg Concerto No. 3: I. Allegro** — *law*

The Muses on Helicon sing for Minerva. They have just won a contest: the Pierides, nine mortal women, challenged them to a song-match and lost. The song Calliope sings — the long tale of Proserpina — is the poem's most sustained meditation on the power of song to encode the world's transformations. The Brandenburg No. 3 is Bach at his most purely architectural: three violin parts, three viola, three cello, all interlocking in a structure so clear and so joyful that form becomes its own argument. Law requires formal clarity and architecture; this is the sound of song as the highest form of order.

**6. Tchaikovsky — *Romeo and Juliet*: Overture-Fantasy** — *desire*

Dis looks at Proserpina gathering flowers — lilies, violets, crocuses, hyacinths — and decides, in the instant, that she will be his. The earth opens; his chariot rises; he takes her; her flowers fall. Tchaikovsky's *Romeo and Juliet* is desire as catastrophe: the love theme is the most achingly chromatic melody in the 19th-century repertoire, and the violence of the feud music that surrounds it is the world answering desire with destruction. The abduction of Proserpina has this exact shape — the sweetness of the flower field, the violence of the chariot, the descent into darkness, the mother's grief that follows.

**7. Debussy — *Nocturnes*: I. Nuages** — *metamorphosis*

Cyane springs from her pool to stop Dis. He drives his chariot through her — or rather, through the water of her body, and the water does not know how to hold him. She tries to call to Ceres but cannot find the words. Lying in her pool, she dissolves: her bones become liquid, her hair becomes flowing water, her voice becomes the sound of water over stones. She is a body melting into its element. Nuages is clouds: chords that shift without harmonic gravity, orchestral colors that dissolve into each other, music that moves without narrative direction. It is Cyane's dissolution — the body becoming what it came from, the impossibility of telling what you have seen, the water that cannot speak.

**8. Dvořák — Symphony No. 9 'From the New World': II. Largo** — *exile*

Ceres searches. She carries torches through the night, calls her daughter's name across continents. The earth, which she has made fertile, withholds its fruit in sympathy; Ceres curses the land that cannot return her daughter. The Largo — the English horn melody that is the world's most recognizable sound of homesickness — is exile as wandering grief, the open intervals of a landscape that has become unbearable because the person who should be in it is not there. Exile here is not geographical but maternal: a mother displaced from her own love, walking through a world that has lost its meaning because it no longer contains her child.

**9. Ravel — *Jeux d'eau*** — *metamorphosis*

Arethusa emerges from the Alph and tells Ceres her story: she was a huntress, bathing in a clear stream, when the river god Alpheus saw her and pursued. She fled — over mountains, through fields, across whole regions — until she was so exhausted she called to Diana for release. Diana wrapped her in mist and poured her into a spring. But Alpheus, who is a river, flows underground and finds her even there, mingling his fresh water with hers so that even in escape, she is joined to the desire she fled. Ravel's *Jeux d'eau* is water as the primary genre of transformation: cascading arpeggios that form momentary shapes and then dissolve, splashing figuration that sparkles and is gone, a texture that is always in motion because it is made of the thing that does not hold still. Arethusa's voice — the body that becomes water and speaks from inside its own dissolution — is this piece.

**10. Fauré — *Élégie*** — *grief*

Ceres finds Proserpina. But the girl has eaten seven pomegranate seeds in the underworld, and the Fates decree: she who has tasted the food of the dead must remain among the dead. A compromise: half the year with Dis, half with Ceres. The mother's grief is double: she has found her daughter and lost her in the same negotiation, and the loss will recur with every spring's return. Fauré's *Élégie* is cello music of private grief — slow, inward, without rage, the sound of a sorrow that has no remedy because the terms of the loss are built into the structure of the world.

## The dominant arc of Book V

```
kingship (wedding feast / public pageantry) →
wrath (Phineus' invasion / violence) →
fate (battle's momentum / inexorable killing) →
divine-intervention (Medusa's head / petrified stillness) →
law (the Muses' song / song as structure) →
desire (Dis abducts Proserpina / fatal beauty) →
metamorphosis (Cyane dissolves / water reclaims body) →
exile (Ceres searches / maternal wandering) →
metamorphosis (Arethusa becomes spring / pursuit inside transformation) →
grief (the pomegranate seed / recurring loss)
```

Book V argues that **metamorphosis** is the medium in which narrative itself operates. The book is a series of nested frames — Perseus' story, the Muses' story, Calliope's song, Arethusa's story — each containing transformations that are also transitions between frames. Cyane cannot speak what she saw because her body is already dissolving. Arethusa tells her story from inside her own transformation, a voice that is also a spring. Even Ceres' grief — the half-year loss — is a transformation that recurs, the earth dying and reviving with the daughter's return. The force that makes all this possible is the capacity of form to change and of song to hold that change. Book V is the *Metamorphoses* reflecting on its own method: the world is made of stories that are made of changes, and the poem exists because the Muses won the contest on Helicon and continue to sing.
