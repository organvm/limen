# Metamorphoses Book VI: The Loom, The Stone, The Tongue

Book VI is the book of wrath — and it is the most uncompromising book of the poem so far. Arachne is destroyed for being too talented; Niobe loses all fourteen children for being too proud; Marsyas is flayed alive for losing a contest; and the story of Tereus, Procne, and Philomela is the most sustained horror in Ovid's work — rape, mutilation, child-murder, cannibalism, and the gods' final intervention not as justice but as escape. The dominant force is **wrath** — not the wrath of a single god or hero, but wrath as the structure of a world in which the powerful cannot tolerate being equaled, in which divine dignity is enforced by massacre, in which the only response to the unpunishable crime is the vengeance that makes the avenger a criminal too. Book VI is not about rage; it is about what anger does when it has no limit.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Bach — *The Well-Tempered Clavier*, Book I: Prelude and Fugue No. 1 in C major** — *law*

The Prelude in C is the sound of pure structure: the left hand and right hand tracing the same arpeggio pattern, ascending and descending through every chord in the key, the harmonic motion so natural that it sounds inevitable. This is the music of craft — not inspiration but the patient discovery of what the keyboard can do when fingers move with perfect order. Arachne's weaving has this quality: she does not need to be inspired; she has simply mastered her material so completely that the wool does what she asks. Her fame is the fame of technique at the edge of the miraculous. Law requires formal clarity and architecture; Bach in C major is the sound of architecture discovering itself.

**2. Prokofiev — Symphony No. 5: I. Andante** — *wrath*

Minerva comes as an old woman and warns Arachne to ask forgiveness for her impiety. Arachne, young and talented and furious, tells the goddess she has no grandmother and no advice for her — let Minerva come herself if she has a complaint. The contest is joined. Minerva's tapestry shows the gods in their majesty; Arachne's shows the gods in their crimes — Europa, Leda, Danaë, all the mortals the gods destroyed by loving. The tapestries are perfect. Minerva cannot bear it — she tears Arachne's weaving, strikes her forehead. Prokofiev's Fifth opens with a majestic, solemn theme that sounds like the highest authority making a formal statement. But the development is not courtly; it is massive, percussive, increasingly violent. The Andante has the structure of wrath that begins as law and ends as assault — exactly Minerva's trajectory from disguised warning to open attack.

**3. Ligeti — *Atmosphères*** — *metamorphosis*

Arachne, shamed, loops a rope around her neck. Minerva catches her — you will not die, you will hang forever at your own thread. She sprinkles her with aconite; Arachne's head shrinks, her nose and ears disappear, her hair falls out, her body contracts into a small dark abdomen with legs that spin thread. She becomes the spider, the thing that weaves by its nature, not by its art. *Atmosphères* is music that has surrendered every familiar structural category — no melody, no harmony, no beat — and become pure timbre, orchestral color as texture so dense it is physical. The transformation of Arachne is this dissolution: the girl who was famous for her weaving becomes the act of weaving itself, her body reduced to the function she performed. The music does not narrate; it *is* the state of being-reduced.

**4. Elgar — *Pomp and Circumstance* March No. 1** — *kingship*

Niobe is queen of Thebes, mother of seven sons and seven daughters, rich, powerful, beloved. She tells the women of Thebes to stop their worship of Latona — a goddess with only two children — and to worship her instead. The sovereignty she claims is not just political: it is existential. She believes her fertility, her kingdom, her beauty, are arguments against the divine. Elgar's March is the most public ceremonial music in the English repertoire — the melody that crowns kings, the tune Britons know by heart. It is the sound of authority that believes in its own eternity, which is exactly the belief that will destroy Niobe. Kingship here is not the dignity of rule but the arrogance of it.

**5. Beethoven — Symphony No. 7: I. Poco sostenuto — Vivace** — *fate*

Latona, insulted, calls on Apollo and Diana. Apollo goes to the fields where Niobe's seven sons are training and begins to shoot. One by one they fall — each named, each with a way of dying, the arrows finding them in the throat or the chest or the back as they run. Ilioneus, the youngest, prays — but the arrow is already released. Fate here is the hunt, and the hunt is the mechanism of divine enforcement. The slow introduction of Beethoven's Seventh is the goddess deciding; the Vivace that erupts from it is the hunt itself — the famous dotted rhythm that drives forward without stopping, the sound of something that has started and will not be stopped until the target is reached.

**6. Mahler — *Kindertotenlieder*: No. 1, 'Nun will die Sonn' so hell aufgehn'** — *grief*

Niobe's daughters are killed at the altar. The youngest, hiding in her mother's robes, is found by the arrow. Niobe stands among her fourteen dead children. She cannot move — not because she chooses stillness but because the grief is already turning her body into something that cannot move. 'Nun will die Sonn' so hell aufgehn' — Now the sun will rise so brightly — is a song about the world continuing after a child's death, and how the continuation is the most unbearable part. The opening horn call is a question; the voice enters with an answer that cannot be borne. Niobe in this moment is not yet stone, but she is already harder than stone: grief at this scale is the body preparing to become what it can no longer feel.

**7. Sibelius — Symphony No. 4: I. Tempo molto moderato** — *exile*

Niobe is carried by a whirlwind to Mount Sipylus, where she stands alone. She is stone, but her eyes still weep. She cannot leave; she cannot die; she has been exiled into a single posture on a single mountain, frozen in the moment after she has lost everything. Sibelius's Fourth Symphony is the bleakest orchestral landscape in the Northern repertoire — open fourths and tritones, a harmonic language that refuses comfort, a tempo so slow it feels like the music is contemplating its own end. The opening movement is the sound of a place so cold that nothing can live there — which is where Niobe lives now, forever.

**8. Penderecki — *Threnody to the Victims of Hiroshima*** — *sacrifice*

Marsyas finds Athena's discarded flute, learns to play it, and challenges Apollo to a musical contest. The Muses judge: Apollo wins. His punishment of Marsyas is not a quick death but a performance — he strips the skin from Marsyas' body, slowly, while the satyr screams. The poem notes that Marsyas is a single wound, that you can count his muscles and his veins. Penderecki's *Threnody* is written for 52 string instruments but produces no conventional string sound — only clusters, shrieks, massed glissandi that sound like human tissue under stress. Sacrifice requires ritual weight and immolation; this is the ritual of a body being unmade by the power of the god whose laws it violated, and the music does not flinch from what that sounds like.

**9. Berg — *Three Orchestral Pieces*, Op. 6: I. Präludium** — *desire*

Tereus marries Procne, daughter of Pandion of Athens. Years later, Procne asks Tereus to bring her sister Philomela from Athens for a visit. Tereus sees Philomela and desires her immediately — not as love but as a force that overrides every other consideration. He rapes her in a hunting lodge. When she threatens to tell, he cuts out her tongue. The tongue writhes on the ground, still trying to form words. Berg's Präludium is slow, chromatic, oppressive — sliding harmonies that feel like something approaching through fog, dark orchestral colors that thicken as they move. Desire here is the force that destroys the capacity for testimony: not lovemaking but the silencing of the voice that could speak the crime.

**10. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 10: II. Scherzo** — *wrath*

Philomela, unable to speak, weaves her story into a robe and sends it to Procne. Procne understands. She waits for the festival of Bacchus, dresses as a maenad, and frees her sister. Then Procne kills her own son, Itys — her hand trembles, she turns away, then she turns back and does it. She boils his flesh and serves him to Tereus. When Tereus calls for his son, Procne tells the truth: you are eating him. The sisters flee; Tereus pursues with an axe; the gods turn all three into birds — Procne the nightingale who sings Itys forever, Philomela the silent swallow, Tereus the hoopoe who chases them. Shostakovich's Tenth Scherzo is a two-minute explosion of manic violence — the strings play in frantic octaves, the brass and percussion attack, the rhythm drives with a ferocity that sounds less like music than like murder. Wrath here is the anger of a woman who loved her sister so much that she killed her own child to avenge her. The music is the chase, the act, the aftermath, all compressed into the time it takes a mother to walk from her kitchen to her husband's table.

## The dominant arc of Book VI

```
law (craft as order, Arachne's weaving) →
wrath (Minerva's violence / the destroyed tapestry) →
metamorphosis (Arachne becomes spider / form reduced to function) →
kingship (Niobe's pride / sovereignty as hubris) →
fate (Apollo hunts Niobe's sons / divine mechanism) →
grief (the fourteen dead / Niobe's stillness) →
exile (Niobe on Sipylus / stone that weeps) →
sacrifice (Marsyas flayed / the body as performance of punishment) →
desire (Tereus rapes Philomela / predation as silencing) →
wrath (Procne kills Itys / vengeance that destroys the avenger)
```

Book VI argues that **wrath** is the force that most directly opposes metamorphosis. Each transformation in this book is not an opening into new form but a closing — Arachne reduced to spider, Niobe to stone, Marsyas to a wound, Philomela to silence, Procne to a bird that sings only one name forever. The transformations in earlier books were often ambiguous (Daphne becoming a laurel is both death and survival); in Book VI, every transformation is a diminishment. The poem's argument seems to be that anger, when it becomes total, does not generate new form but collapses it. Arachne the weaver becomes a thing that weaves but cannot create; Niobe the mother becomes a stone that weeps but cannot hold; Procne the mother becomes a nightingale that sings the name of the son she killed — forever, without rest, without forgiveness.
