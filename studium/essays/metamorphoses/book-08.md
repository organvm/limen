# Metamorphoses Book VIII: The Labyrinth, The Wing, The Boar

Book VIII is the most densely packed book of the poem so far — Scylla's betrayal and transformation, the Minotaur, Daedalus and Icarus, the Calydonian boar hunt, Meleager's death, Philemon and Baucis, Erysichthon's hunger. Each story is a complete narrative that could stand alone, but Ovid weaves them through a common thread: every act in this book produces consequences that cannot be contained or reversed. Scylla loves the wrong man, and both she and her father become birds hunting each other forever. Daedalus builds wings for escape, and his son falls into the sea. Meleager kills a boar and gives its hide to a woman, and his mother kills him. Erysichthon cuts down a tree and eats himself. The only exception is Philemon and Baucis — and their reward is to become trees, joined in death, which is also a transformation they did not choose. The dominant force is **fate** — not the personified Fates of Greek myth but the structure of irreversible consequence that governs the world of this book.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Strauss — *Salome*: 'Dance of the Seven Veils'** — *desire*

Scylla sees Minos from the walls of her father's city and is undone. She watches him on horseback, in his helmet, in his purple robe; she imagines his hand on his spear; she wishes she could fly down to him. The 'Dance of the Seven Veils' is the most explicit musical rendering of desire as performance: the opening is a seduction conducted in a register that is both intimate and public, the chromatic slides and exotic percussion creating a sound world in which the normal rules of conduct have been suspended. Scylla in this state is not making a decision; she is being carried by a force that feels like love and acts like possession. She will cut her father's purple lock — the talisman that protects his kingdom — and give Minos the city, because not to give it is more unbearable than to betray everything she has known.

**2. Messiaen — *Réveil des oiseaux*** — *metamorphosis*

Minos takes the city but rejects Scylla. He will not be the trophy of a traitor — even a traitor who loves him. She throws herself at his ship, clawing at the hull, and as she clings, her father Nisus — who has become a sea-eagle — swoops to tear at her. She lets go, falls, and the air holds her: she has become a shearer, a bird that skims the sea, and Nisus the sea-eagle will hunt her over the waves forever. Messiaen's *Réveil des oiseaux* — the Awakening of the Birds — is a dawn ceremony of avian transformation: the piano and orchestra build from a single bird call into a whole aviary of layered voices, each species singing its own song, the texture rising into light. Scylla's transformation into a bird of prey is not a punishment; it is the literalization of what she already was — a woman who abandoned her human loyalties for the object of her sight, and the shape of that abandonment is now permanent and visible from the shore.

**3. Dvořák — Symphony No. 7: I. Allegro maestoso** — *fate*

The Minotaur is in the labyrinth. Daedalus built it — passage coiling into passage, door leading to door that leads nowhere, a darkness in which the half-bull half-man walks forever, eating the young Athenians sent every year as tribute. Fate here is the architecture of consequence: Pasiphaë's desire for the bull produced the monster; Minos' shame hid it; Theseus will kill it; Ariadne will help; Ariadne will be abandoned. The labyrinth is the physical shape of fate: you enter it and cannot find the way out because the structure was designed to ensure you never do. Dvořák's Seventh opens in the cellos with a theme that descends through the harmonic series — dark, brooding, inevitable — and the movement's long architectural arc is music built like a labyrinth: every passage leads to another passage, the climax arrives only after you have walked through every corridor of the development.

**4. Messiaen — *L'Ascension*: IV. Prière du Christ montant vers son Père** — *ascent*

Daedalus gathers feathers, arranges them by size — small to large, like panpipes — and binds them with thread and wax. He fits the wings to his son's shoulders and teaches him: fly between the water and the sun. The flight is the first time a human leaves the earth not by climbing but by flying. Ascent requires rising sequence and luminous orchestration; Messiaen's 'Prière du Christ montant vers son Père' — Prayer of Christ Ascending to His Father — is music that rises through a whole series of upward modulations, the strings climbing toward a register that sounds like light entering a space that had been dark. Icarus rises, and the music rises with him, and the listener knows — even if the boy does not — that the ascent is only possible because it ends.

**5. Schubert — String Quintet in C: II. Adagio** — *grief*

Icarus, exhilarated by the flight, rises toward the sun. The wax melts. He calls for his father — Daedalus, Icarus, Daedalus — and falls. The sea receives him. Daedalus circles, calls his son's name, finds only feathers on the waves. The Adagio of Schubert's String Quintet is the most sustained piece of mourning in the chamber repertoire: the second cello adds a weight to the harmony that makes every chord feel like someone holding a body, the first violin hovers above like a bird that cannot descend. The music is slow — unbearably slow — because the father cannot stop circling, because the boy is falling through an air that does not feel like air, because the sea that receives him will hold him forever and give no sign.

**6. Janáček — *Sinfonietta*: I. Allegretto** — *sacrifice*

King Oeneus of Calydon thanks all the gods for the harvest except Diana. She sends a boar — a boar with tusks like an elephant's, a hide that turns spears, a breath that blights crops. The boar tears the fields, uproots vines, kills cattle and men. Meleager summons the heroes of Greece for the hunt: Theseus, Peleus, Telamon, Nestor, Jason — the generation of the Argo, the heroes who are the ancestors of the Trojan War. Janáček's *Sinfonietta* opens with a brass fanfare that sounds like the waking of the world: the horns call the hunt from every corner of the Czech landscape, the rhythm is martial and urgent, the whole piece feels like a ritual that is about to begin. Sacrifice requires ritual weight; the opening fanfare is the moment before the spears are thrown, the gods watching, the boar waiting in the thicket, the heroes standing in the dawn with their weapons.

**7. Brahms — Symphony No. 4: I. Allegro non troppo** — *fate*

Meleager kills the boar. He gives its hide to Atalanta, the huntress he loves. His uncles, offended that a woman received the prize, take it from her; Meleager kills them. His mother Althaea, hearing that her brothers are dead by her son's hand, goes to a chest and takes out the brand that the Fates placed in the fire at Meleager's birth — its burning and his life are one. She lights it, watches it catch, and Meleager burns from within, far away. Brahms's Fourth opens with falling thirds — a five-note theme that descends, climbs, and descends again, each attempt at escape pulled back by the harmonic gravity that governs the movement. Fate here is the melody that cannot escape itself, the brand that burns in the mother's hand while the son dies in another room, the structure from which no character can exit because they are already inside it.

**8. Allegri — *Miserere mei, Deus*** — *revelation*

Jupiter and Mercury, disguised as mortals, walk through the hill country of Phrygia. A thousand doors are shut in their faces. At the last hut — a poor roof of thatch and reeds — Philemon and Baucis welcome them. The old couple give them what little they have: a fire, a cabbage, bacon, the wine bowl that refills itself. The gods reveal themselves: the roof rises into columns, the floor shines with marble, the hut becomes a temple. The *Miserere* of Allegri is the revelation of mercy in sound: the nine voices plead for forgiveness, and at the highest point of the piece, a single soprano ascends to a high C that sounds like light breaking through a roof of thatch — the stranger you fed is a god, and the poor hut you offered him is now a temple that will stand forever.

**9. Debussy — *Préludes*, Book I: No. 10, 'La cathédrale engloutie'** — *metamorphosis*

Philemon and Baucis ask the gods for one gift: to be priests of the temple and to die together, so that neither will have to bury the other. As they stand before the altar, Philemon becomes an oak, Baucis a linden — branches weaving together, two trees growing from one root, joined as they were joined in life. Debussy's 'La cathédrale engloutie' is a submerged cathedral rising from the sea: the opening chords emerge from the lowest register of the piano, building through parallel harmonies that sound like stone walls assembling themselves from water. The piece is the sound of something becoming what it was always meant to be — the old couple were always trees, were always rooted in this place, were always going to outlast the human world by becoming the sacred landscape they served. Metamorphosis here is the slow emergence of the eternal from the temporal, the underwater cathedral breaking the surface.

**10. Nielsen — Symphony No. 5: I. Tempo giusto** — *fate*

Erysichthon cuts down Ceres' sacred oak with an axe. The tree trembles, bleeds, the dryads grieve. Ceres sends Famine to him — Famine who cannot be fed, who wraps herself around him in his sleep and breathes emptiness into his throat. He wakes hungry and cannot be filled. He sells his possessions for food, sells his daughter, and finally, when everything and everyone is gone, he turns on himself — devouring his own body, consuming the self that consumed the sacred tree. Nielsen's Fifth is a symphony at war with itself: the first movement's pastoral opening is invaded by a snare drum that plays at its own rhythm, trying to disrupt and destroy the orchestral pulse. The orchestra fights to maintain order; the drum attacks again. Erysichthon's hunger is the snare drum — the appetite that has no organic relation to the body it inhabits, that will destroy everything that might satisfy it, that ends only when the self has been entirely consumed by the force it thought it could control.

## The dominant arc of Book VIII

```
desire (Scylla loves Minos / longing as betrayal) →
metamorphosis (Scylla becomes shearer / the body of the pursuit) →
fate (the Minotaur in the labyrinth / consequence as architecture) →
ascent (Daedalus and Icarus fly / the miracle of craft) →
grief (Icarus falls / feathers on the sea) →
sacrifice (the Calydonian boar hunt / the ritual of the beast) →
fate (Meleager's brand / the mother's fire) →
revelation (Philemon and Baucis / the gods at the table) →
metamorphosis (the old couple become trees / the temple's roots) →
fate (Erysichthon's hunger / the self devouring)
```

Book VIII argues that **fate** is not a personified power in this poem but the name for the fact that actions have consequences that cannot be controlled by the actor. Scylla's desire is real, but she cannot control what that desire will cost; Daedalus' craft is real, but he cannot control what his son will do with it; Meleager's victory is real, but he cannot control the anger of his uncles or the grief of his mother; Erysichthon's hunger is the most extreme case — he becomes the consequence of his own act, the body eating the self that offended the god. Only Philemon and Baucis escape the pattern: their fate is not punishment but reward, their transformation not dissolution but union. They become trees, and the trees are joined. The book begins with a woman who becomes a bird fleeing her father and ends with a man who eats himself — between these two fates, the poem surveys the full range of what irreversible consequence looks like.
