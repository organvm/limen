# Metamorphoses Book VII: Medea, Magic, Myrmidons

Book VII is defined by the terrifying power of witchcraft and the extreme lengths to which mortals will go to secure their desires. Medea’s dark magic dominates the first half, from saving Jason to boiling King Pelias. The second half contrasts this chaotic eastern sorcery with the rigid, organized tragedy of Cephalus and Procris in Athens. The dominant force is fate—the inexorable doom brought on by Medea's poisons and the inescapable tragic misunderstanding between husband and wife.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde — Prelude*** — *desire*

Medea, princess of Colchis, is struck by a sudden, consuming passion for the foreign hero Jason. She knows it is madness and treason, yet she cannot resist. The deep, unresolved chromaticism of the Tristan chord is the heavy, subterranean awakening of a desire that will destroy her life.

**2. Strauss — *Also sprach Zarathustra — 'Sunrise'*** — *divine-intervention*

Protected by Medea's magic herbs, Jason casually approaches the massive, fire-breathing bronze bulls and yokes them, stunning the Colchians. A sudden color shift and brass fanfare reflect the shocking, superhuman feat made possible by divine (or at least supernatural) intervention.

**3. Ravel — *Daphnis et Chloé — Suite No. 2, 'Lever du jour'*** — *metamorphosis*

Under a full moon, Medea gathers herbs, slices Aeson's throat to drain his old blood, and pours in her boiling magical brew. His gray hair turns black, and his wrinkles vanish. The sweeping, colorful orchestration captures the fluid, miraculous transformation from death's door back to youth.

**4. Shostakovich — *Symphony No. 5 — I. Moderato (climax)*** — *wrath*

Pretending to offer the same rejuvenation to King Pelias, Medea tricks his daughters into stabbing him, then throws his bleeding body into a boiling pot of powerless water. Relentless rhythmic pressure and harsh dissonance capture the brutal, deceptive violence of her vengeance.

**5. Berlioz — *Symphonie fantastique — IV. 'March to the Scaffold'*** — *fate*

Her crimes complete, Medea escapes through the air on a chariot drawn by winged dragons, looking down on the lands below. The inexorable, hammered rhythm captures her flight not as heroic ascent, but as the grim departure of a monstrous, fateful force moving to her next target.

**6. Bach — *The Art of Fugue — Contrapunctus I*** — *law*

King Minos of Crete rallies a massive fleet to avenge the death of his son Androgeos, sending envoys to gather allies across the Aegean. The formal clarity and repetition of the fugue capture the structured, logistical preparation for a widespread, legally justified military campaign.

**7. Penderecki — *Threnody to the Victims of Hiroshima*** — *plague*

King Aeacus recounts how Juno sent a devastating plague that killed nearly all the animals and people of his island. The air turned thick, and bodies piled up in the temples. The descending motion, shrieking strings, and dark orchestration perfectly embody the agonizing spread of mass death.

**8. Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel*** — *metamorphosis*

In response to Aeacus's prayer, Jupiter transforms an army of ants on a sacred oak tree into a new race of men: the Myrmidons, fiercely loyal and disciplined. Emergence and verticality capture the quiet, miraculous birth of a new society rising from the tiny insects of the earth.

**9. Rossini — *William Tell — Overture (Finale)*** — *comedy*

Cephalus releases his magical dog Laelaps, who always catches his prey, to hunt a magical fox that can never be caught. The two race endlessly in circles until the gods turn them both to stone. The fast, bouncing rhythm highlights the absurdity and paradox of this impossible pursuit.

**10. Mahler — *Symphony No. 5 — IV. Adagietto*** — *grief*

Suspecting her husband Cephalus of infidelity, Procris hides in the bushes. Hearing a rustle, Cephalus hurls his never-missing javelin and fatally strikes his own wife. She dies in his arms. The slow, inward line captures the absolute heartbreak of a tragedy born entirely of misunderstanding.
