# Metamorphoses Book VII: Medea, Plague, Myrmidons

Book VII belongs to Medea — the sorceress who can reverse age, who kills a king by his own daughters' hands, who murders her own children, who flies across the world on a chariot of dragons. She is the poem's most concentrated figure of metamorphosis as power: she does not undergo change but causes it, and each transformation she performs — the rejuvenation of Aeson, the destruction of Pelias, the poison cup for Theseus — is also an act of violence against the natural order. The book then opens outward into inset stories — the plague at Aegina, the Myrmidons, Cephalus and Procris — as if the poem cannot contain Medea's energy in a single frame and must disperse it into other narratives. The dominant force is **metamorphosis**, not as the experience of change (as Daphne or Narcissus underwent it) but as the exercise of change upon others — the power that crosses boundaries between youth and age, insect and human, love and death, and leaves the boundary-crosser alone in the center of her own terrible competence.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Dukas — *The Sorcerer's Apprentice*** — *metamorphosis*

Medea fills a bronze cauldron with herbs gathered from every corner of the known world — from the Caucasus, from the Emathian hills, from the shores of the Euboean sea. She stirs the mixture with a branch that turns green as it touches the liquid. Aeson's veins fill with new blood; his white hair darkens; his wrinkles vanish; he stands forty years younger, amazed at his own strength. Dukas' scherzo is the most famous piece of musical magic ever written — the sense of a spell operating, of forces beyond the visible at work, of the world obeying a will that should not have the power it is exercising. And the piece carries its own warning: the apprentice who sets forces in motion cannot stop them. Medea can make Aeson young, but she cannot make the consequences of that act — the daughters of Pelias' trust, the killing of the king — unhappen. Metamorphosis here is competence that has outpaced wisdom.

**2. Bartók — *The Miraculous Mandarin*: Suite** — *sacrifice*

Medea tells the daughters of Pelias that she can do for their father what she did for Aeson. She demonstrates on a ram: boils it to the bone, then draws a young lamb from the cauldron. The daughters believe. They take a sword and stab their father — again and again, to drain his old blood. Pelias screams, crawls, dies on the floor. Medea's demonstration was real; her promise was a lie. The Miraculous Mandarin is Bartók's most physically violent score — music of urban predation, chromatic menace, rhythmic brutality. The opening builds a thick, sweaty texture of desire and violence, and the chase that follows is a man being hunted through the streets. Sacrifice here is inverted: the ritual is a fraud, the victim is a king who trusted his daughters, and the daughters are left holding a sword they cannot unhold.

**3. Cherubini — *Medea*: Overture** — *grief*

Medea kills her children. The poem does not dwell on this; but the tradition does, and the weight of the myth hangs over every moment of Medea's story in Ovid. She is the mother who became the murderer of her own blood, the woman who loved Jason enough to betray her father and kill her brother, and who hated him enough to destroy what they made together. Cherubini's overture is the classical music's most concentrated expression of what Medea carries: the storm, the darkness, the theme that rises like a woman who has decided. Grief in this register is not the grief of the bereaved but the grief of the bereaver — the woman who will survive what she has done and live inside it.

**4. Sibelius — *Pohjola's Daughter*** — *exile*

Medea flies from Iolcus in her dragon-drawn chariot. Below her, the avengers of Pelias fall behind; cities pass; the sea opens. She lands in Athens and marries Aegeus, but Athens is not her home. She is always the foreigner — the Colchian, the sorceress, the woman who came in a chariot of dragons and will leave the same way. *Pohjola's Daughter* is Sibelius' tone poem about the daughter of the North, the woman who lives in a cold land that no one from the south can understand. The music opens with open intervals that sound like wind across a northern plain, and the strings carry a melody that is beautiful and unreachable. Exile here is the condition of the woman who is always at the threshold — never inside, never at rest, always holding her powers like a weapon.

**5. Pärt — *Fratres*** — *revelation*

Theseus arrives in Athens, bearing his father's sword but not yet known to his father. Medea sees a threat to her position and persuades Aegeus to poison the young stranger at the feast. Theseus, to carve the meat, draws his father's sword — the old sword that Aegeus left under a rock in Troezen, the sign he gave Aethra so that his son would one day be known by it. Aegeus sees the sword and knocks the cup from Theseus' hand. The poison spills; the truth is known. *Fratres* — brothers — is built on a repeating chord progression that each time rises to a higher register, like something emerging from darkness into light. Revelation requires stillness and verticality; the recognition scene has both: the moment of seeing, the cup falling in slow motion, the son who was about to die recognized in time.

**6. Rachmaninoff — *The Isle of the Dead*** — *plague*

Minos besieges Athens. Aegeus sends to Aegina for allies, but Aegina is a dead city — plague has swept through, the houses are full of corpses, the fields are untended, the streets are silent except for the dogs. The 5/8 rhythm of Rachmaninoff's tone poem is the oars of the boat approaching the island of the dead; the descending phrases are the darkness of a place where no life remains. Every timbre is weighted, every harmony dark, every gesture toward ascent pulled back down into the orchestral mass. Plague requires descending motion and dark orchestration; *The Isle of the Dead* is music that stays in the register of irrevocable loss from its first note to its last.

**7. Ravel — *Alborada del gracioso*** — *metamorphosis*

Aeacus prays to Jupiter. He dreams of ants — the sacred ants of Aegina, long columns of them carrying grain — and when he wakes, his people have returned. The ants have become men, each ant a citizen of the renewed city. The Myrmidons, the poem tells us, take their name from the Greek for ant, and they are known for their industry, their endurance, their capacity to work without ceasing. Ravel's *Alborada del gracioso* — the jester's dawn song — is built on rapid repeated notes, skittering figuration, Spanish dance rhythms that sound like movement at the scale of insects: busy, purposeful, alive. The transformation here is collective — not a single body but a population — and the music captures the quality of energy that belongs to a people who do not know they were once something smaller.

**8. Rachmaninoff — Symphony No. 2: III. Adagio** — *desire*

Cephalus tells his story: he married Procris, loved her with a love that was total. Aurora, the dawn, tried to seduce him; he refused. In revenge, Aurora whispered to Procris that Cephalus was unfaithful. The story is told after Procris is dead, and the beauty of the love remembered is inseparable from the loss that frames it. Rachmaninoff's Adagio — the clarinet theme that melts into the strings, the slow climb of the melody through registers, the heat of the orchestral climax — is the most sustained Romantic melody of the 20th century. Desire here is retrospective, memory-shaped, the love that remains as absence.

**9. Sibelius — Symphony No. 5: I. Tempo molto moderato — Allegro moderato** — *fate*

Procris cannot shake her suspicion. She hides in the bushes where Cephalus hunts, waiting to see the woman he calls to. Cephalus hears a noise in the brush; his javelin, given by Procris herself, never misses. He throws. The cry is not a woman's name but a woman's death. Fate here is the tragic structure of irony: the gift that kills the giver, the weapon that finds the one person it should never have found, the lover who dies because she loved too much to trust. Sibelius's Fifth opens with a horn call that sounds like a question asked of the air — and the movement that follows is the slow, inexorable assembly of that question into something like certainty. The fate motif is not hammered but emergent: it arrives as the shape of a thing that was always going to happen.

**10. Fauré — *Requiem*: 'In Paradisum'** — *grief*

Procris dies in Cephalus' arms, forgiving him with her last breath. He carries her home, buries her in the grove where she died, and hangs his javelin in the temple. The 'In Paradisum' from Fauré's Requiem is the gentlest close in the choral repertoire — the soprano floats over organ and strings, asking the angels to receive the soul, and the music does not descend into the darkness of grief but rises into the light of what survives. Grief here is not the storm of the early movements of this book but the calm after: a man has lost the woman he loved, and the only adequate response is to release her into a place he cannot follow, holding the forgiveness she gave him as the last warmth he will ever have.

## The dominant arc of Book VII

```
metamorphosis (Medea's cauldron / age reversed) →
sacrifice (Pelias killed by his daughters / ritual as fraud) →
grief (Medea's children / the mother who destroys) →
exile (Medea in Athens / always the foreigner) →
revelation (Theseus recognized / the sword that saves) →
plague (Aegina dead / the city as tomb) →
metamorphosis (ants become Myrmidons / population reborn) →
desire (Cephalus remembers Procris / love as memory) →
fate (the javelin / the gift that kills the giver) →
grief (Procris dies forgiven / release into the light)
```

Book VII argues that **metamorphosis** is not a natural process but a power — and that the person who holds it is the loneliest figure in the poem. Medea can change the shape of the world, but she cannot keep a husband or hold a city or protect her own children. The Myrmidons are reborn from ants, but they do not know what they were; the transformation is invisible to them. Cephalus and Procris show the opposite: the change that matters most is the one that cannot be undone — Procris' death, which no magic can reverse. The book opens with the most powerful magician in the tradition and closes with a man burying his wife in a grove. The arc of the power to change things is the arc of loneliness: the more you can transform, the less you can hold.
