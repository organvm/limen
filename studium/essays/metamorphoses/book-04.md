# Metamorphoses Book IV: Weavers, Lovers, Monsters

Book IV is the book of closed spaces — the interiors where transformation happens away from the city, away from the gods, away from every public register of meaning. The daughters of Minyas stay indoors weaving while Thebes worships Bacchus, and they tell each other stories of private love: Pyramus and Thisbe whispering through a wall, Mars and Venus caught in a net, Salmacis and Hermaphroditus melted together in a pool. But the closed space cannot hold: Bacchus breaks in, turns the weavers into bats, and the book opens into the widest spaces yet — Perseus flying across the Mediterranean, killing monsters, turning seaweed into coral. The dominant force is **memory** — not as nostalgia but as the capacity of narrative to hold what has been lost, to keep the dead alive inside the weave of the story. The Minyades tell stories to pass the time; Ovid tells stories to keep the dead from vanishing. The book is the poem's most sustained meditation on what storytelling is for.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Bach — *The Art of Fugue*: Contrapunctus I** — *law*

The daughters of Minyas — Alcithoe, Leucippe, Arsippe — are the only women in Thebes who refuse the festival of Bacchus. While the rest of the city pours into the streets in ecstatic worship, they stay at home, serving Minerva, working their looms. The loom is the instrument of law: thread, tension, pattern, the geometry of the weave, the craft that requires patience and rule-following and the refusal of improvisation. Bach's *Art of Fugue* is the most purely architectural music ever written — a single theme subjected to every contrapuntal transformation the mind can devise, the entire edifice built from the opening four bars. The Contrapunctus I is the first working-out of the subject, innocent of the complexities to come, the simplest form of a form that will generate an entire universe. Law requires formal clarity and architecture; the Minyades' looms and Bach's fugue are the same activity — the discovery of order through the patient repetition of a pattern that reveals itself as it is followed.

**2. Elgar — *Enigma Variations*: 'Nimrod'** — *grief*

Pyramus and Thisbe love each other through a wall. They are neighbors in Babylon, their families enemies, but they have found a crack in the shared wall — a narrow fissure, invisible to others — and through it they whisper, breathe, exchange words that are also kisses. They arrange to meet at Ninus's tomb under the mulberry tree. Thisbe arrives first; a lioness fresh from the kill, her mouth bloody, finds Thisbe's veil and tears it. Thisbe flees. Pyramus arrives, finds the torn veil, and assumes Thisbe is dead. He draws his sword and falls on it. Thisbe returns, finds Pyramus dying, and joins him under the tree whose white berries she stains red with her blood. Elgar's 'Nimrod' — the slowest, most inward of the *Enigma Variations* — is a melody that builds from a single rising interval through a span that seems impossibly wide, the strings climbing toward a nobility that the melody earns note by note. Grief requires slow tempo and inward line; the story of Pyramus and Thisbe is the first love story in the poem that is not about gods and mortals but about two people whose only crime was loving each other through a crack in a wall.

**3. Mozart — *Così fan tutte*: Overture** — *comedy*

The Sun sees Venus in bed with Mars and tells Vulcan. The smith-god, the lame craftsman, forges a net of bronze so fine it is transparent, and suspends it over the bed where his wife and the war-god meet. He catches them in the act and calls the gods to witness: come and see how the beautiful and the strong look when they are caught. Comedy here is the exposure of dignity's failure: the god of war and the goddess of love tangled in a net forged by the god of craft, the gods laughing at the spectacle of their own kind caught in the act they all commit. Mozart's *Così fan tutte* overture is the most elegant music ever written about infidelity — the orchestra dances through the themes that will later be sung about sister-swapping and betrayal, and the music is so beautiful it makes the cruelty of the plot seem like a game. Comedy requires dance rhythm and courtly exaggeration; the net of Vulcan is the figure of comedy itself — the invisible snare that catches the powerful and holds them up for laughter.

**4. Bach — *Matthäus-Passion*: 'Erbarme dich'** — *supplication*

Clytie loved the Sun god — Apollo, or Helios, the figure who drives the chariot across the sky. He left her for Leucothoe, a Persian princess. Clytie, in jealousy, revealed the affair to Leucothoe's father, who buried his daughter alive. The Sun, enraged, abandoned Clytie completely. She sat on the bare ground for nine days, refusing food, turning her face to follow the sun across the sky, her tears watering the earth. On the ninth day she had taken root — she had become a heliotrope, a sunflower, forever turning her face toward the god who left her. The 'Erbarme dich' — Have mercy — from Bach's *St. Matthew Passion* is a violin aria of suspended grief: the solo violin weaves a line around the voice that pleads for comfort, and the plea is never answered, because this is the Passion and there is no comfort for the betrayed. Supplication requires a suspended cadence and an exposed melodic line; Clytie's nine days on the bare ground are the same structure — the long petition that does not change the beloved's mind but changes the petitioner's body into a monument to the asking.

**5. Schoenberg — *Verklärte Nacht*** — *metamorphosis*

Hermaphroditus, the son of Mercury and Venus, is fifteen years old when he comes to the pool of Salmacis in Caria. The nymph Salmacis — who does not hunt, who does not run, who combs her hair and looks at her reflection — sees him and burns. She offers herself; he rejects her. She pretends to leave, but she is watching from behind a bush. Hermaphroditus strips and dives into the pool. Salmacis, still watching, tears off her clothes and dives in after him. She wraps herself around him, her body coiling around his, and prays to the gods that they never be separated. The prayer is answered: their bodies melt together, becoming one form that is both male and female — neither fully one nor the other. Schoenberg's *Verklärte Nacht* — Transfigured Night — is a string sextet based on a poem by Richard Dehmel: a woman confesses to her lover that she is pregnant by another man, and the lover forgives her, and the night is transfigured. The music is late-Romantic chromaticism at its most fluid: themes dissolve into each other, the harmony never settles into a stable key because the boundaries that define key — like the boundaries that define gender — are being dissolved. It is the sound of two bodies becoming one texture, and the texture is water, longing, and the confusion of form that is the origin of desire.

**6. Strauss — *Elektra*: Agamemnon motif** — *divine-intervention*

The Minyades are still weaving, still telling stories, still refusing the god. The looms turn to vines; the room fills with the cries of phantom beasts; oil drips from the rafters and sticks to their skin. Their arms become webbed membranes; their voices become thin squeaking; they shrink, cluster, and fly into the rafters. They are bats now, the creatures that shun the light, exiled into the perpetual darkness they chose when they refused the god of ecstasy. The Agamemnon motif from Strauss's *Elektra* — a single chord that arrives with the force of a murder — is the sound of the decision: the brass and percussion and strings coalescing into a noise that is less music than verdict. Divine-intervention requires sudden color shift; the transformation of the Minyades into bats is the punishment not of Bacchus's anger but of Bacchus's absence — the god they refused has refused them back, and they become the shape of negation, the blank wing in the dark.

**7. Berlioz — *Symphonie fantastique*: V. 'Dream of a Witches' Sabbath'** — *wrath*

Juno, implacable in her hatred of the house of Thebes, descends to the underworld and hires Tisiphone, the Fury who carries a torch and a whip of snakes. The Fury enters the house of Ino and Athamas and hurls poison at them: madness, the blindness that makes a husband see his wife as a lioness and his son as a cub. Athamas catches Learchus — his son — and throws him against a wall. Ino, possessed, runs with her surviving son Melicertes and leaps from a cliff into the sea. They become sea deities: Leucothea and Palaemon, the helpers of sailors in distress. The Furies' work is done. Berlioz's 'Dream of a Witches' Sabbath' is the most grotesque orchestral movement of the 19th century — the Dies irae played as a dance, the strings playing col legno (with the wood of the bow, a skeletal percussion), the shrillness of the piccolo and the weight of the brass creating a texture of infernal carnival. Wrath requires rhythmic pressure and grotesque energy; Tisiphone's work is the explosive violence that destroys a family in the time it takes Juno to remember a grudge.

**8. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 6 ('Pathétique'): IV. Finale** — *memory*

Cadmus and Harmonia, old and broken by everything they have survived and everything they have lost, walk out of Thebes. Semele is ash; Pentheus is headless; Ino is a sea-goddess; the city they founded is still standing but the family that founded it is gone. They walk into the Illyrian hills, Cadmus remembering the serpent he killed, the voice that cursed him, the teeth he sowed. He feels his skin hardening. He is becoming what the serpent was — becoming a serpent himself. Harmonia, who cannot bear to be human without him, prays to be changed too. They become snakes: gentle, slow, coiling together in the grass, remembering what it was to walk upright. Tchaikovsky's Pathétique finale is the most heartbroken music in the symphonic repertoire — a slow movement of descending phrases that sound like memories falling through water, each phrase lower than the last, the strings singing a melody that returns to its beginning because there is nowhere else to go. Memory requires faded recapitulation; Cadmus and Harmonia become the thing they killed, and they remember being human as they slide into the grass that will cover them.

**9. Strauss — *Ein Heldenleben*: 'The Hero'** — *ascent*

The book shifts. From the closed rooms of Thebes we rise into the open air of the Mediterranean. Perseus, the son of Jupiter and Danaë, straps on the winged sandals Mercury gave him, slings the sword on his hip, and lifts into the air above the known world. Below him the deserts of Libya unroll; the blood dripping from Medusa's head becomes the snakes of the Sahara. He flies. Strauss's *Ein Heldenleben* — A Hero's Life — opens with the hero theme: a rising sequence in the horns and strings that sounds like someone climbing into a realm that does not belong to him and claiming it by force of will. Ascent requires rising sequence and luminous orchestration; Perseus has just killed Medusa and is flying across the world with the head in a bag, and the music of the hero's life is the sound of a mortal who moves through the air as if it were his element, even though it is not.

**10. Messiaen — *Quatuor pour la fin du temps*: VIII. 'Louange à l'Immortalité de Jésus'** — *revelation*

Perseus sees Andromeda chained to the rock, saves her from the sea monster, wins her as his wife. After the battle, he lays Medusa's head on a bed of seaweed. The seaweed, touched by the Gorgon's gaze, hardens into coral — the first coral the world has ever seen, branching from the ocean floor, a reef born from the dead face. The 'Louange à l'Immortalité de Jésus' from Messiaen's *Quartet for the End of Time* — written in a prisoner-of-war camp, premiered for an audience of prisoners and guards — is the composer's most ecstatic meditation on what survives: the cello plays a long, singing line over the piano's slow harmonies, rising through registers until it reaches a pitch that sounds like light entering a place that has never known light. Revelation requires stillness and verticality; the coral is the sea becoming stone, the soft becoming hard, the dead face of a monster creating the first reef. Perseus hangs in the air above the new coral, and for a moment the poem is silent, letting the ocean harden into a monument that will last longer than any of its heroes.

## The dominant arc of Book IV

```
law (the Minyades weave / craft as resistance to ecstasy) →
grief (Pyramus and Thisbe / the first human love in the poem) →
comedy (Mars and Venus caught / the net of the betrayed craftsman) →
supplication (Clytie rooted / the nine days of turning) →
metamorphosis (Salmacis and Hermaphroditus / the boundaryless pool) →
divine-intervention (Minyades as bats / the refusal of the god) →
wrath (Tisiphone in the house / the fury that breaks a family) →
memory (Cadmus and Harmonia / becoming what you killed) →
ascent (Perseus flies / the hero above the world) →
revelation (coral / the sea becoming stone)
```

Book IV argues that **memory** is the medium of transformation in the *Metamorphoses* — not the past remembered but the dead held inside the living through the act of telling. The Minyades tell stories to pass the time they are refusing the god; the stories they tell are stories of lovers, craftsmen, nymphs, gods — the whole inventory of desire and punishment that constitutes the poem itself. When their room dissolves into bats, the stories do not disappear; they remain, woven into the poem, carried forward by Ovid's telling. Cadmus and Harmonia become snakes and remember being human; Perseus creates coral and the ocean remembers the dead face that gave it form. Book IV is the poem's argument that transformation is not the opposite of memory but its instrument: you change because the thing you carry from the past is too heavy to stay the same shape, and the shape you become is the shape of what you remember.
