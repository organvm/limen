# Metamorphoses Book II: The Chariot, The Bear, The Bull

Book II is governed by the catastrophic consequences of approaching the gods too closely, or of the gods approaching mortals too closely. Phaethon demands to drive the sun and burns the world; Callisto is raped by Jupiter and banished; Coronis is killed by Apollo; Europa is abducted. The dominant force is desire—not as romance, but as a gravitation that destroys the boundary between the divine and the mortal.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Walton — *Crown Imperial*** — *kingship*

The Palace of the Sun is described as an architectural marvel of gold, bronze, and ivory. Phoebus sits in state surrounded by the Days, Months, Years, and Ages. This requires ceremonial grandeur and sovereign display—music of absolute courtly authority.

**2. Scriabin — *The Poem of Ecstasy*** — *desire*

Phaethon asks for a day driving the sun's chariot. Phoebus warns him it is certain death, but the boy's longing for glory overpowers his reason. Scriabin provides chromatic longing and a soaring, perilous ambition.

**3. Tchaikovsky — *Symphony No. 4 — I. Andante sostenuto*** — *fate*

Phaethon loses the reins. The horses leave the track, and the world begins to burn. Fate here is not a sudden judgment but a hammered insistence of disaster unfolding, a mechanism set in motion that cannot be stopped.

**4. Barber — *Adagio for Strings*** — *supplication*

The Earth itself, burning and cracking, raises a voice to Jupiter begging for an end to the torment. Supplication requires a suspended cadence and an exposed, agonizing melodic line.

**5. Verdi — *Requiem — 'Dies irae'*** — *divine-intervention*

Jupiter intervenes not with mercy but with overwhelming force, striking Phaethon from the chariot with a thunderbolt. The Dies irae provides the sudden color shift and massive brass/strings contrast of a cosmic execution.

**6. Strauss — *Metamorphosen*** — *metamorphosis*

Phaethon's sisters weep on his grave until their bodies harden into bark and their tears become amber. Metamorphosis here is an elegy, changing orchestral color and fluid harmony capturing the slow descent of flesh into wood.

**7. Mozart — *Le nozze di Figaro — Overture*** — *comedy*

Jupiter disguises himself as Diana to seduce Callisto. The scene operates on the theatrical logic of farce—a god wearing another god's face to bypass a nymph's defenses—requiring courtly exaggeration and rapid, plotting energy.

**8. Vaughan Williams — *Symphony No. 3 (Pastoral) — II. Lento moderato*** — *exile*

Banished by Diana and turned into a bear by Juno, Callisto wanders the forests she once hunted in, terrified of the other beasts. Exile requires wandering tonality and open intervals, the sound of a vast, empty landscape.

**9. Shostakovich — *String Quartet No. 8 — II. Allegro molto*** — *wrath*

The raven tells Apollo of Coronis' infidelity. In a flash of rage, Apollo shoots her, immediately regretting it but unable to call back the arrow. Wrath here requires frantic, rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony.

**10. Szymanowski — *Violin Concerto No. 1*** — *desire*

Jupiter turns himself into a beautiful, gentle white bull to lure Europa into the sea. The abduction is smooth, almost dreamlike. Szymanowski's concerto provides the shimmering, chromatic longing of deferred resolution.

## The dominant arc of Book II

```
kingship → desire → fate → supplication → divine-intervention → metamorphosis →
comedy → exile → wrath → desire
```

Book II argues that **desire** is the fundamental driver of these transformations. Each event traces this force through different domains, showing how mortal limits break under its pressure.
