# Metamorphoses Book II: Phaethon, Callisto, Europa

Book II is dominated by the catastrophic consequences of proximity to the divine. If Book I was about the ontology of change—about matter failing to hold its form—Book II is about the specific danger of intersection between the mortal and the immortal. The book opens with Phaethon, a mortal boy, climbing to the Palace of the Sun to demand proof of his divine parentage; he gets the proof, and the world nearly burns to ash as a result. Every major episode that follows is a variation on this theme: Callisto catches Jupiter's eye and is destroyed by Juno's revenge; Coronis is killed by Apollo's jealousy; Aglauros is turned to stone for obstructing Mercury; Europa is carried off across the sea. The dominant force is **desire**, but it is desire operating with the asymmetrical power of gods among humans, where every divine impulse—whether erotic, jealous, or simply careless—leaves a wake of scorched earth, exiled beasts, and broken bodies.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Wagner — *Lohengrin*: Prelude to Act III** — *ascent*

Phaethon walks into the blinding, gold-and-jewel Palace of the Sun. He wants one thing: proof that he belongs here, that the Sun is his father. The Sun swears by the Styx to grant him any wish, and Phaethon demands to drive the sun-chariot for a day. The launch into the sky is an adrenaline rush of absolute, tragic confidence. Wagner's Prelude is pure, radiant brass and driving rhythm—the sound of a triumphant launch that cannot possibly sustain its own momentum without burning up. Ascent requires a luminous, rising sequence; this is a vertical climb powered by pure hubris.

**2. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 10: II. Allegro** — *fate*

The horses realize immediately that the driver is too light, that the reins are slack. They leave the path. They plunge too close to the earth, and the world catches fire. Rivers boil dry, cities are turned to ash, the earth cracks open and lets light into the underworld. The disaster is not malicious; it is a failure of control at a cosmic scale, a physical mechanism grinding the world down. Shostakovich's scherzo is four minutes of relentless, terrifying momentum—unstoppable, destructive energy moving too fast to be steered. Fate here is not a spoken decree, but the inexorable consequence of mass times velocity.

**3. Verdi — *Requiem*: 'Dies irae'** — *divine-intervention*

Mother Earth herself, choking on smoke, begs Jupiter to put an end to the destruction. Jupiter, who has no rainclouds left to send, hurls a thunderbolt. Phaethon is blasted from the chariot and falls like a shooting star. This is divine-intervention as instantaneous, absolute violence—the emergency brake pulled on the cosmos. The Verdi *Dies irae* delivers four massive, percussive blows from the orchestra and chorus that smash the music into pieces. It has the exact sudden color shift and arbitrary-seeming totality that a divine strike requires.

**4. Barber — *Adagio for Strings*** — *grief*

Phaethon's mother Clymene searches the ruined earth for his bones. His sisters, the Heliades, weep at his tomb for four months. Ovid slows the poem down here; the frenetic destruction of the world gives way to an exhausted, absolute, paralyzed mourning. Barber's *Adagio* is a sustained, unresolving arc of inward sorrow. It perfectly matches the posture of the sisters—frozen in their grief, repeating their laments until the repetition itself begins to calcify their bodies.

**5. Strauss — *Metamorphosen*: closing section** — *metamorphosis*

As the sisters weep, bark encloses their legs. Their mother tries to tear the wood away, but she only breaks twigs that bleed. Their cousin Cygnus, watching the horror, turns into a swan out of pure sorrow, his voice narrowing into a hiss, his arms becoming wings. Strauss's piece is an unbroken meditation on form slowly giving way under the pressure of loss. The fluid harmony and changing orchestral color enact the very process Ovid describes: sorrow calcifying into a new biological reality.

**6. Debussy — *Pelléas et Mélisande*: Act III, Scene 1 (opening)** — *desire*

Jupiter, patrolling the earth to restore the damaged forests, sees the nymph Callisto. She is a follower of Diana, sworn to virginity. He disguises himself as Diana to get close to her, and then rapes her. Debussy's music gives the shifting, shadowed, ambiguous atmosphere of a desire that approaches under a false shape. The chromatic longing is entirely deceptive—a creeping tension that relies on disguise to cross the boundary. 

**7. Bartók — *The Miraculous Mandarin*: Suite (excerpt)** — *metamorphosis*

When Diana discovers Callisto's pregnancy, she banishes her. But the true punishment comes after Callisto gives birth: Juno, enraged by her husband's infidelity, grabs Callisto by the hair and turns her into a bear. The transformation is violent and degrading—Callisto loses her human hands, her human voice becomes a terrifying growl, and she is forced to live in terror of the very beasts she used to hunt. Bartók's music is brutally physical, harsh, and frightening, matching the visceral stripping of her humanity.

**8. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5: I. Allegro con brio** — *wrath*

A raven flies to Apollo to tell him that his lover Coronis has been unfaithful. In a flash of divine jealousy, Apollo reaches for his bow and shoots Coronis through the chest. As she dies, she tells him he has killed his own unborn child. Apollo immediately repents, but it is too late; he can only rip the child (Aesculapius) from her womb on the funeral pyre. Beethoven's hammered motif gives the exact quality of sudden, irreversible, destructive action taken in anger. It is the sound of a decision made too quickly, before regret can catch up.

**9. Scriabin — *The Poem of Ecstasy*** — *metamorphosis*

The centaur Chiron's daughter, Ocyrhoe, is gripped by prophetic frenzy. She foretells the fates of the baby Aesculapius and of her father Chiron, but the divine knowledge overloads her human form. As she speaks, her voice changes into a whinny; her face lengthens; she turns into a mare. Scriabin's music captures the overwhelming, mind-dissolving pressure of ecstatic revelation that quite literally breaks the vessel trying to contain it. The fluid harmony here is the sound of a mind expanding until the body fails.

**10. Mozart — *The Marriage of Figaro*: Overture** — *comedy*

Mercury steals Apollo's cattle. An old man, Battus, sees him do it. Mercury bribes him to stay quiet, then leaves, disguises himself, returns, and offers a bigger bribe for information about the stolen cows. Battus betrays the secret immediately, and Mercury turns him into a touchstone. The sequence is purely comedic—a fast-paced, cynical farce about human greed and divine trickery. Mozart's overture gives it the speed, lightness, and theatrical wit that the episode demands.

**11. Ravel — *La Valse*** — *desire*

Mercury flies over Athens and sees a girl named Herse. He wants her. Her sister, Aglauros, demands gold for access. Minerva, hating Aglauros, sends Envy to poison her mind. Aglauros becomes so sick with jealousy that she sits in the doorway and refuses to move, telling Mercury he cannot pass. He turns her into a black statue. Ravel's waltz begins elegantly and degrades into a mechanical, obsessive, self-destructive swirl. It is the perfect musical equivalent for the descent of Aglauros's mind into paralyzing, stony envy.

**12. Debussy — *L'Isle joyeuse*** — *desire*

Jupiter transforms himself into a beautiful, gentle white bull to approach the princess Europa as she plays on the beach. She garlands his horns; she climbs onto his back. He walks slowly into the sea and swims away with her, looking back as she holds his horn with one hand and her dress with the other. The book ends on an image of abduction disguised as shimmering romance. Debussy's piano piece is virtuosic, glittering, and oceanic—the perfect cover for a divine kidnapping that looks, from the outside, like ecstasy.

## The dominant arc of Book II

```
ascent (Phaethon's flight) → fate (chariot out of control) →
divine-intervention (Phaethon struck down) → grief (Clymene's mourning) →
metamorphosis (sisters turn to trees) → desire (Jupiter targets Callisto) →
metamorphosis (Callisto becomes a bear) → wrath (Apollo kills Coronis) →
metamorphosis (Ocyrhoe broken by prophecy) → comedy (Mercury tricks Battus) →
desire (Envy ruins Aglauros) → desire (Jupiter abducts Europa)
```

Book II begins with the sun crashing into the earth and ends with a god swimming across the sea with a stolen girl on his back. Between these two images of divine irresponsibility, the book establishes its pattern: the gods act on their impulses, and mortals pay the price. The mortal who asks too much of a god (Phaethon) is killed; the mortal who is loved by a god (Callisto, Coronis) is destroyed; the mortal who interferes with a god (Aglauros, Battus) is turned to stone. The closing image of Europa on the bull is technically an abduction, but Ovid paints it with such golden, maritime beauty that the violence is sublimated into art. That sublimation is the *Metamorphoses* in a nutshell: horror converted into aesthetic perfection.
