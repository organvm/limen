# Metamorphoses Book II: The Chariot, The Bear, The Bull

Book II is the book of proximity — what happens when the boundary between mortal and divine breaks down, not through punishment but through desire. Phaethon wants to be the sun; Callisto is wanted by Jupiter; Coronis is wanted by Apollo; Europa is wanted. The book's structure is not a single narrative but a cascade of episodes linked by the same logic: the approach of a god to a mortal, or a mortal to a god, results in catastrophe. The dominant force is **desire** — not as eros alone but as the gravitational attraction that draws bodies of different orders together until the weaker body is destroyed or transformed. Every story in Book II is the same thermodynamic event at a different temperature: the heat transfers, the boundary burns, and what survives is something that remembers being something else.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Walton — *Crown Imperial*** — *kingship*

The Palace of the Sun is the first space of Book II, and it is not a home but a throne room. Phoebus sits in state surrounded by the Days, the Months, the Years, the Ages — time itself arranged as a court around his seat. The palace gleams with gold, bronze, and ivory, its doors silver, its decoration the work of the divine craftsman Mulciber. Ovid's description is a display of cosmic sovereignty: the sun is not a natural object but a king whose palace is the architecture of temporality. Walton's *Crown Imperial* was written for the coronation of George VI — ceremonial grandeur in the English tradition of sovereign display, brass and strings and timpani that sound like the authority of a throne that assumes its right without question. The Palace of the Sun requires this register: not wonder at beauty but the recognition of absolute power seated at the center of time.

**2. Scriabin — *The Poem of Ecstasy*** — *desire*

Phaethon comes to the palace to demand proof of his parentage. Phoebus swears by the Styx to grant whatever he asks — the oath no god can break — and Phaethon asks for the chariot. The desire that drives him is not ambition for power but a longing for identity: he wants to be known as the son of the Sun, and he thinks driving the sun is how you prove it. Phoebus warns him — the horses are fire, the track is tilted, the journey requires strength no mortal has — but Phaethon hears the warning as a test of his courage. The desire is real, and it is fatal. Scriabin's *Poem of Ecstasy* is desire as a metaphysical event: the harmony reaches upward through chromatic sequences that never settle, the brass and strings climb toward a climax that promises release but delivers only more longing. The piece is what Phaethon feels when he wraps his arms around his father and says give me the reins — the ecstasy of a will that has outstripped its ability to judge what it wants.

**3. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 4: I. Andante sostenuto** — *fate*

The horses feel the weight of an unfamiliar hand. They bolt. The chariot leaves the track. Mountains burn — Atlas's peak, the Caucasus, the Alps — then the cities, then the rivers dry up, then the sea shrinks. The world is burning because a boy wanted to prove he was his father's son, and the mechanism has no off-switch. Tchaikovsky's Fourth opens with the fate motif: a brass fanfare that states the theme before the movement's main material begins, a three-note phrase that sounds like a door closing. The Andante sostenuto that follows is the working out of that motif — the theme returns, transformed, insistent, the orchestra building toward climaxes that are always the same climax because the fate is always the same fate. Fate here is the mechanical law of consequence: once the chariot is released from the track, it cannot be steered back. The horses do not know the boy who holds the reins; they know only the fire they are made of.

**4. Barber — *Adagio for Strings*** — *supplication*

The Earth herself raises a voice. She burns, she cracks, her cities are ash and her seas boil, and she speaks to Jupiter — not as a goddess (Tellus is older than the Olympians, and she does not beg) but as the oldest thing that has been wounded by the youngest thing. She asks for mercy for what remains of the world. Barber's Adagio for Strings begins with a B flat on the violins, held, exposed, the melody climbing note by note through a span that seems impossibly wide. The line breaks, rebuilds, and breaks again — the Earth's voice speaking through strings, asking not for justice but for intervention, for the one god who can stop the fire to decide that the world is worth saving. Supplication requires a suspended cadence and an exposed melodic line; the Adagio is a single continuous arc of plea.

**5. Verdi — *Requiem*: 'Dies irae'** — *divine-intervention*

Jupiter looks at the burning world and acts. He throws a thunderbolt — not at the chariot but through the charioteer. Phaethon falls from the sky trailing fire, his hair burning, his body smoking, a bolt of something that was a boy. He falls into the river Eridanus, and his sisters find his bones on the bank. The 'Dies irae' from Verdi's Requiem arrives with the force of something that does not negotiate: the brass blast, the timpani, the choir shouting the day of wrath in octaves. Divine-intervention requires sudden color shift and brass/strings contrast; Verdi gives the thunderbolt its sound — the moment when the cosmos says enough, and the fire stops because the driver is dead.

**6. Strauss — *Metamorphosen*** — *metamorphosis*

Phaethon's sisters, the Heliades, find his bones. They will not leave the grave. Day after day they sit on the riverbank and weep. Their feet take root; their arms lengthen into branches; their skin hardens into bark; their tears become amber that hardens in the sun and will be gathered forever. Strauss's *Metamorphosen* was written in 1945, after the destruction of the German opera houses he had built his career in, and the title is both a musical term (thematic transformation) and a statement about what suffering does to form. Themes dissolve into other themes; the orchestral texture splits and reforms; the music cannot hold a single shape because the grief that drives it is changing the substance of the music as it moves. Metamorphosis requires changing orchestral color and fluid harmony; *Metamorphosen* is the piece that knows that transformation is what happens to a form that cannot survive its own feeling.

**7. Mozart — *Le nozze di Figaro*: Overture** — *comedy*

Jupiter, not satisfied with the destruction of the world, pursues the nymph Callisto. She serves Diana, the virgin goddess of the hunt, and Jupiter — unable to approach her directly — transforms himself into Diana. He enters Callisto's company in the goddess's form, embraces her, and when she resists he reveals himself through the disguise by force. The scene is disturbing and theatrical: a god wearing another god's face to rape a nymph who trusts that face. Mozart's *Figaro* Overture is the quickest, most plotting music in the operatic repertoire — it moves at a speed that suggests a conspiracy already in motion, characters running through corridors, doors opening and closing. Comedy requires dance rhythm and courtly exaggeration; the register here is the comedy of divine predation, the gods treating mortal bodies as the material of their entertainment.

**8. Vaughan Williams — Symphony No. 3 (Pastoral): II. Lento moderato** — *exile*

Juno discovers the crime. She cannot punish Jupiter, so she punishes Callisto: she transforms her into a bear. Callisto, who ran through the forests as a huntress, now haunts them as prey. She is terrified of the wolves, of the hunters, of her own reflection in the water — she is a bear, and her face is no longer the face that men desired. When her son Arcas, now a young hunter, nearly kills her in the woods, Jupiter lifts them both into the sky as constellations — the Great Bear and the Bear Watcher — but Callisto never speaks again. Vaughan Williams's Pastoral Symphony is the sound of a landscape that has outlived its inhabitants: the Lento moderato opens with open intervals, a wandering tonality that sounds like wind across a field where something terrible has happened. Exile requires the quality of a world that no longer recognizes you, and Callisto in her bear-body is the shape of that recognition lost.

**9. Shostakovich — String Quartet No. 8: II. Allegro molto** — *wrath*

The raven, whose feathers were once white, was set by Apollo to watch his beloved Coronis while he traveled. The raven sees her with a young man and flies to Delphi to tell Apollo. Apollo, in a flash of rage, shoots Coronis with an arrow — and immediately regrets it, but the arrow cannot be called back. She dies; Apollo saves the unborn child from her body (Aesculapius, who will become the god of medicine). Shostakovich's Eighth Quartet was written in Dresden, in ruins, as a memorial to the victims of fascism and war. The Allegro molto is frantic, rhythmic pressure that accelerates toward an event that cannot be stopped: the strings repeat a motif that sounds like a pulse racing, the harmony unresolved because the decision has already been made and cannot be unmade. Wrath requires rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony; here the pressure is the speed of a god's anger acting before his love can intervene.

**10. Szymanowski — Violin Concerto No. 1** — *desire*

Jupiter, still driven by desire, sees Europa playing on the beach at Tyre. She is gathering flowers with her companions, and the sight of her stirs the same force that burned the world and broke Callisto. He transforms into a bull — a white bull with horns like jewels, breath smelling of wild thyme — and walks gently among the herd. Europa is charmed, decorates his horns with garlands, climbs on his back. He walks into the sea and carries her away. She clings to his horns, looking back at the shore, her hand holding his back, her gown billowing on the water. Szymanowski's Violin Concerto No. 1 opens with a violin line that hovers above the orchestra in a register that sounds like light on moving water: chromatic, iridescent, unresolved. The piece is the sound of a girl being carried across the sea on the back of a god who was a bull who was a lover who was a predator, and the music does not tell us whether she is afraid or ecstatic or both. Book II ends on the water, the shore receding, Europa holding the bull's horn, the poem drifting toward Book III and the next set of boundaries to be crossed.

## The dominant arc of Book II

```
kingship (the cosmic court / authority of the sun) →
desire (Phaethon's ambition / the fatal wish) →
fate (the chariot uncontrolled / mechanical disaster) →
supplication (Earth begs for mercy) →
divine-intervention (Jupiter's thunderbolt) →
metamorphosis (the Heliades become trees / grief becoming substance) →
comedy (Jupiter and Callisto / divine predation as farce) →
exile (Callisto the bear / the body that cannot speak) →
wrath (Apollo kills Coronis / reflex before regret) →
desire (Europa and the Bull / carried across the sea)
```

Book II argues that **desire** is the thermodynamic force of the *Metamorphoses* — not love, not longing, but the heat that transfers from one body to another when the boundary between mortal and divine breaks down. Phaethon desires to be his father and burns the world; Jupiter desires Callisto and turns her into a bear; Apollo desires Coronis and kills her; Jupiter desires Europa and carries her into the sea. The book ends with a woman on a bull's back, looking back at a shore she will never stand on again — the image of desire as distance, the thing you wanted receding even as you are taken by it. The next book will take place in the city Cadmus founds, and the doom that will destroy it was set in motion before its first stone was laid.
