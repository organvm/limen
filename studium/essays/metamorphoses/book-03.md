# Metamorphoses Book III: Thebes, Dogs, Reflections

Book III is the tragedy of looking. Its episodes form a catalogue of vision — who looks, at what, with what consequence. Actaeon sees Diana naked and is devoured by his own hunting dogs; Semele demands to see Jupiter's divine form and is incinerated; Tiresias sees two snakes coupling in the forest and is transformed into a woman, then back, then blinded; Narcissus sees his own reflection and wastes away; Pentheus sees the Bacchic rites and is dismembered by his own mother. The gaze is the wound. The dominant force is **fate** — not as an external agent (the Fates do not appear in this book) but as the inexorable structure of consequence that governs the house of Cadmus from its foundation. Cadmus kills the dragon of Mars, sows its teeth, and hears a voice: you too will one day be a serpent. Everything that follows — every gaze, every punishment, every transformation — is the working out of that sentence.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Verdi — *La forza del destino*: Overture** — *fate*

Cadmus follows the heifer to the site of Thebes. He sends his men to draw water from a grove sacred to Mars. The dragon of Mars — a serpent with a crest of gold, triple rows of teeth, a body that coils through the cave — kills them. Cadmus destroys the dragon, and a voice from heaven tells him: you, who have slain the serpent of Mars, will yourself become a serpent — and your wife, and your descendants, will be serpents, and the city you found will be a monument to the impiety that founded it. The house of Thebes is cursed before it has walls. Verdi's overture to *La forza del destino* — The Force of Destiny — is the most concentrated statement of the fate motif in Italian opera: the brass intone a phrase that will return throughout the opera, inescapable, the sound of a destiny that cannot be outrun. It is the first stone of Thebes being placed in a foundation of blood.

**2. Prokofiev — *Romeo and Juliet*: 'Dance of the Knights'** — *wrath*

Cadmus sows the dragon's teeth. From the furrows spring armed men — Spartoi, the sown ones — who rise from the soil in full armor and immediately attack each other. They are brothers born of the same earth, and they kill each other with the fraternal violence of those who share an origin and cannot share a world. Only five survive. Thebes is built by the survivors over the bodies of their kin. Prokofiev's 'Dance of the Knights' — from *Romeo and Juliet*, the music of the Capulet ball where violence is ceremonial — is held in the lower register, heavy, processional, the chords falling like feet on stone. Wrath here is not individual passion but the fury that arises from the earth itself when the dragon's teeth are sown, the anger of the serpent's lineage expressing itself as civil war before the city has a name.

**3. Debussy — *Jeux*** — *metamorphosis*

Actaeon, the grandson of Cadmus, is hunting on Mount Cithaeron at midday. The hunt is over; his companions have gathered for the feast; Actaeon wanders alone through the forest and comes to a valley where a spring feeds a grotto. Diana, the goddess of the hunt, is bathing with her nymphs. He parts the branches and sees her — naked, water streaming from her hair, her body exposed. She sees him. She cannot reach her bow; she splashes water at his face, and the antlers begin to branch from his head before she has finished speaking. Debussy's *Jeux* — the last ballet he wrote, commissioned by Diaghilev, a game of tennis that becomes a dream — is music of constant transformation: the harmony shifts by the smallest intervals, the orchestral colors dissolve into each other, the themes never return to the same place twice. It is the sound of a body that is being rewritten without pain, without warning, and without the possibility of appeal. The antlers grow; the boy becomes the stag; the metamorphosis is silent and complete.

**4. Stravinsky — *The Rite of Spring*: 'Sacrificial Dance'** — *sacrifice*

Actaeon tries to speak — his own dogs will recognize his voice — but the sound that comes out is a groan. The dogs hear him running. They catch his scent: Blackfoot, Tracker, Mountain-eater, Storm-dog — the poem names them all, the dogs he raised from puppies, the dogs he fed from his own hand. They pull him down. He wants to call to them, to say it is me, Actaeon, your master — but he has no words. His friends, hearing the commotion, call for Actaeon to come and see the magnificent stag the dogs have caught. Stravinsky's 'Sacrificial Dance' — the final section of *The Rite of Spring*, the Chosen One dancing herself to death — is the most brutal piece of ritual music ever composed: the irregular accents, the hammering ostinato, the sense of a body being offered to a force that does not hear appeals. No ritual, no god, no meaning — just the tearing of flesh by creatures who loved you. Actaeon dies because he looked at a goddess, and the dogs are the instrument of her punishment.

**5. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*: 'Liebestod'** — *desire*

Semele, the daughter of Cadmus, is beloved by Jupiter. Juno, jealous, disguises herself as an old nurse and tells Semele: your lover is not a god, or if he is, he does not love you enough to show himself as he truly is. Make him swear on the Styx to grant your wish, then ask him to come to you as he comes to Juno. Semele does. Jupiter swears, hears her request, and knows she is dead. Desire here is a suicide pact: Semele wants to see the god she has been sleeping with because she cannot bear the gap between his mortal form and his divine reality, and the desire to see is the desire to die into the sight. Wagner's 'Liebestod' — Isolde's transfiguration over Tristan's body — is desire as dissolution: the harmony climbs through a sequence that has no resolution except the final release into death. Semele climbs toward Jupiter the way Isolde climbs toward Tristan: the reach is the death.

**6. Messiaen — *Turangalîla-Symphonie*: V. 'Joie du sang des étoiles'** — *divine-intervention*

Jupiter comes to Semele in his full divine form. The text says: she asked for a god and received a thunderbolt. Her mortal body cannot withstand the presence of the divine; she catches fire, dissolves into light, is reduced to ash. But from her womb, Jupiter snatches the unborn Bacchus and sews him into his own thigh, where the god will gestate until term. The 'Joy of the Blood of Stars' from Messiaen's *Turangalîla-Symphonie* is the most ecstatic and terrifying movement in the 20th-century orchestral repertoire: the ondes Martenot shrieks at the edge of hearing, the piano and percussion create a texture of overwhelming radiance, the orchestra moves at a speed that feels like panic inside joy. Divine-intervention here is the arrival of a god in his true nature — not a visitation but an exposure, the revelation that Semele's mortal body was never going to survive the full reality of the being she had been sleeping with.

**7. Pärt — *Cantus in Memoriam Benjamin Britten*** — *revelation*

Tiresias, the Theban prophet, is the figure who sees what others cannot because he has lived what others have not. As a young man he struck two mating snakes with his staff, was transformed into a woman, lived as a woman for seven years, and then was transformed back. When Jupiter and Juno asked him who enjoyed sex more, his answer displeased Juno, who struck him blind; Jupiter gave him prophecy in compensation. He knows what it is to be both genders, to be blind and to see the future, to be outside every category and therefore inside all of them. Pärt's *Cantus in Memoriam Benjamin Britten* is a descending scale in A minor, repeated by the strings over the tolling of a bell, each repetition entering before the last has finished, building a texture of overlapping descents that sound like a single line of time falling in on itself. Revelation requires stillness and verticality; Tiresias's sight is not the sight of the eyes but the sight of the structure, and the Cantus sounds like that structure becoming visible.

**8. Fauré — *Pavane*** — *memory*

Narcissus, the son of the river god Cephisus and the nymph Liriope, was told by Tiresias that he would live a long life if he never knew himself. At sixteen he is the most beautiful young man in Boeotia, and he rejects every lover — the nymph Echo pines for him until her body is only voice, and others pray to Nemesis: let him love and not possess. He is thirsty from the hunt, leans over a clear pool, and sees his own reflection for the first time. He falls in love with the image. He cannot touch it; he cannot kiss it; he lies on the bank and stares into the water, slowly wasting away. Fauré's *Pavane* is a slow dance in F-sharp minor that circles its theme — a melody that returns to its beginning having gone nowhere, the sound of a desire that has found its perfect object and found that object unreachable. Memory requires faded recapitulation; Narcissus's love is the memory of an encounter that is happening in the present, the self remembering its own image as something already lost.

**9. Vaughan Williams — *The Lark Ascending*** — *exile*

Echo, the nymph who cannot initiate speech but only repeat the last words of others, hides in caves and mountain valleys. Her body wastes away from grief until only her voice remains — thin, disembodied, the sound that responds but does not begin. She is the figure of exile as the condition of pure response: she has no origin, no body, no first word; she can only answer what has already been said. Vaughan Williams's *The Lark Ascending* is a romance for violin and orchestra in which the violin rises through the orchestral texture like a bird climbing into the upper air: the melody is exposed, solitary, drifting upward until it is barely audible. Exile requires wandering tonality and open intervals; Echo's voice, detached from her body, is the lark's song — the sound of something that has left the ground and floats in a medium that cannot hold it.

**10. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5: I. Allegro con brio** — *fate*

Pentheus, the grandson of Cadmus and the new king of Thebes, refuses to acknowledge Bacchus as a god. He imprisons the Bacchic followers, mocks the rituals, and when the god — in disguise — tells him he cannot fight a god, Pentheus orders his chains. The god escapes, and Pentheus — now obsessed — is convinced to dress as a woman and spy on the Bacchic rites on Mount Cithaeron. The women, mad with wine and divine possession, see him in the trees. They pull him down. His mother Agave, possessed, sees him as a boar and tears off his head. She carries it back to Thebes on a thyrsus, calling for Pentheus to come and see what she has caught. The house of Cadmus ends in a mother carrying her son's head through the streets. Beethoven's Fifth opens with the most famous four notes in Western music — the fate motif, the hammered insistence of something that arrives before the first phrase is finished. The entire first movement is the working out of that opening gesture: once stated, the movement can only develop what has already been decided. Pentheus walks up the mountain, and from the moment he refuses Bacchus, he is walking toward his mother's hands. Fate is the structure of the Fifth — the motif that governs every measure, the theme that returns because it can never leave.

## The dominant arc of Book III

```
fate (Cadmus kills the dragon / the curse of Thebes) →
wrath (the sown men / fratricide as foundation) →
metamorphosis (Actaeon sees Diana / the body rewritten without warning) →
sacrifice (the dogs / the hunter becomes the hunted) →
desire (Semele demands the god / the wish that kills) →
divine-intervention (Jupiter in his glory / incineration) →
revelation (Tiresias blind / the sight that sees the structure) →
memory (Narcissus at the pool / the self falling in love with itself) →
exile (Echo as only voice / the body reduced to response) →
fate (Pentheus on the mountain / the mother who does not know)
```

Book III argues that **fate** is not an external force in the *Metamorphoses* but the structure of consequence that every act of looking sets in motion. Cadmus kills a dragon and hears his own doom; Actaeon looks at a goddess and becomes the prey of his own dogs; Semele looks at her lover and burns; Narcissus looks at himself and wastes away; Pentheus looks at the rites and loses his head. The book that opens with the founding of Thebes closes with the destruction of its king by his own mother. Thebes will continue — the city will survive, but the house of Cadmus is finished — and the snake that Cadmus will become in Book IV is already moving under the earth, waiting for him to come home.
