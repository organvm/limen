# Metamorphoses Book III: Cadmus, Actaeon, Narcissus, Pentheus

Book III shifts the poem's focus to a single geographical space: the founding and early history of Thebes. If Book II was about the danger of crossing paths with a god, Book III is about the specific tragedy of *looking*. Almost every disaster in the Theban cycle comes from seeing something forbidden, or seeing oneself too clearly. Cadmus sees the serpent; Actaeon accidentally sees the virgin goddess Diana naked; Semele demands to see Jupiter in his true, unshielded form; Tiresias sees the mating snakes and is blinded; Narcissus sees his own reflection and starves to death; Pentheus insists on watching the secret rites of Bacchus. The dominant force here is **revelation**—but not revelation as salvation. It is a lethal unmasking, a lifting of the veil that destroys the observer. To look upon the divine, or to look too closely at the self, is to invite annihilation.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Brahms — Symphony No. 1: I. Un poco sostenuto - Allegro** — *fate*

Cadmus, exiled by his father until he can find his abducted sister Europa, consults the oracle of Apollo. He is told to forget his sister, follow a cow that has never borne the yoke, and build a city where she lies down. Brahms's opening, with its pounding, inescapable timpani heartbeat and chromatic ascent, gives the heavy weight of a destiny being forged. It is the sound of a foundational myth taking its first slow, deliberate steps under the pressure of history.

**2. Holst — *The Planets*: 'Mars, the Bringer of War'** — *wrath*

Cadmus sends his men to find water. In an ancient, untouched forest, they find a spring hidden in a cave—guarded by a massive serpent with a golden crest and a triple row of teeth, sacred to Mars. The beast slaughters the men. Holst's 'Mars' is not just military; it is fundamentally hostile. The brutal, asymmetric 5/4 rhythm and aggressive brass capture the sheer territorial violence of a primordial monster protecting the water source of a future city.

**3. Stravinsky — *The Rite of Spring*: 'The Augurs of Spring'** — *metamorphosis*

Cadmus slays the serpent and, instructed by Minerva, sows its teeth in the plowed earth. The clods begin to move. Spear points emerge, then painted helmets, then shoulders, then a full crop of armed men who immediately turn on each other and fight to the death until only five remain to help build Thebes. The hammering, rhythmic violence of Stravinsky's 'Augurs' is the exact sound of an army growing out of the dirt like a violent, mechanical crop. It is life generated for the sole purpose of immediate war.

**4. Messiaen — *Quartet for the End of Time*: I. Liturgie de cristal** — *revelation*

Cadmus's grandson Actaeon, resting from the hunt, wanders into a cool, shadowed grotto and accidentally sees the virgin goddess Diana bathing nude with her nymphs. It is a fatal revelation. Messiaen's music, with its crystalline piano chords, violin harmonics, and blackbird calls, gives the eerie, frozen stillness of stepping out of ordinary time into a sacred, untouchable space. It is beautiful, but entirely alien and utterly uncompromising. 

**5. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 4: I. Andante sostenuto** — *fate*

Diana, enraged that a mortal has seen her naked, throws water in Actaeon's face. He grows horns, hooves, and a pelt. He runs, sees his reflection in a stream, tries to groan, and is tracked down by his own hunting dogs. They tear him apart while he tries to scream his own name. Tchaikovsky's relentless, blasting "fate" fanfare and the agonizing, driven descent of the movement mirror the terrifying realization of his doom and the unstoppable, breathless pursuit by the hounds.

**6. Strauss — *Also sprach Zarathustra*: 'Sunrise'** — *divine-intervention*

Juno, hating the house of Cadmus, tricks Semele (who is pregnant with Jupiter's child) into demanding that Jupiter make love to her in his full divine majesty. Jupiter, having sworn an unbreakable oath by the river Styx, arrives with his thunderbolts. Semele's mortal body is instantly incinerated by the glory. Strauss's 'Sunrise' is the ultimate musical representation of overwhelming, blinding radiance. Here, the brass/strings contrast does not signify triumph; it signifies an ontological weight that simply obliterates the scale of the human.

**7. Pärt — *Cantus in Memoriam Benjamin Britten*** — *metamorphosis*

Tiresias is asked to settle a bet between Jupiter and Juno about who enjoys sex more. Having lived as both a man and a woman (a transformation triggered by striking mating snakes), he sides with Jupiter. Juno instantly strikes him blind; Jupiter, in compensation, grants him the gift of prophecy. Pärt's descending bells and strings capture the solemnity of lost sight, replaced by the cold, clear, inexorable vision of the future. The music descends like a veil falling over the eyes, leaving behind only an inward resonance.

**8. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*: Prelude** — *desire*

Narcissus bends to drink from a perfectly still pool and sees his own reflection. Unaware that it is himself, he falls deeply in love with an image that cannot hold him, cannot speak, and vanishes when he touches the water. He starves to death on the bank, weeping. Wagner's Prelude is the quintessential music of unresolved, impossible longing. It is a desire that constantly reaches for a resolution it can never attain—a perfect mirror for a boy dying of thirst while staring at the water.

**9. Vaughan Williams — *The Lark Ascending*** — *metamorphosis*

Echo, the nymph whose voice was cursed by Juno to only repeat the last words spoken to her, falls in love with Narcissus. When he rejects her, she wastes away in the caves. Her bones turn to stone, and all that is left of her is her voice, bouncing back the ends of his phrases. The soaring, lonely violin of Vaughan Williams, climbing higher and higher until it disappears into the ether, captures the attenuation of a body becoming nothing but an acoustic phenomenon. 

**10. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 5: IV. Allegro non troppo** — *wrath*

King Pentheus of Thebes fiercely rejects the new cult of Bacchus. Blind Tiresias warns him he will be torn apart, but Pentheus doubles down. He orders the god arrested, mocks his effeminate followers, and tries to enforce martial, rational authority against the chaotic frenzy of the cult. Shostakovich's finale, with its hard, forced, militaristic march, represents the brittle rigidity of a tyrant trying to hammer a shifting, ecstatic world into submission. It is order asserting itself right before it breaks.

**11. Stravinsky — *The Rite of Spring*: 'Sacrificial Dance'** — *sacrifice*

Pentheus goes to Mount Cithaeron to spy on the secret Bacchic rites. He is spotted. His own mother, Agave, blinded by religious ecstasy, mistakes him for a wild boar. She and her sisters swarm him, tear off his arms, and rip his head from his body. Stravinsky's 'Sacrificial Dance' is the definitive score for a mob possessed by a rhythm it cannot control. The erratic, violent accents and relentless percussive drive enact a ritual murder with terrifying, mechanical precision.

## The dominant arc of Book III

```
fate (Cadmus founds Thebes) → wrath (the serpent kills the men) →
metamorphosis (soldiers grow from teeth) → revelation (Actaeon sees Diana) →
fate (Actaeon torn apart) → divine-intervention (Semele incinerated) →
metamorphosis (Tiresias blinded/prophetic) → desire (Narcissus and his image) →
metamorphosis (Echo fades to stone) → wrath (Pentheus demands order) →
sacrifice (Pentheus torn apart by Maenads)
```

Book III traces the life cycle of a city cursed by its encounters with the divine. Thebes is built on the bones of a sacred serpent, and the violence of its origin haunts its generations. The tragedy of Actaeon, the immolation of Semele, the starvation of Narcissus, and the dismemberment of Pentheus are all variations on a single error: a mortal comes into contact with a power—or a truth—that they were not designed to accommodate. The book ends not with a peaceful transformation into a tree or a bird, but with a mother holding her son's torn-off head, proving that when the rational mind attempts to suppress the divine frenzy, the frenzy will simply tear the mind apart.
