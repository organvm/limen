# Analects Book V: The Character of Others, the Limit of Judgment

## 公冶長 Gongye Chang — "Gongye Chang" — a music arc

---

Book V is a portrait gallery. It opens with a question of marriage: Gongye Chang was in prison, but Confucius gives him his daughter anyway — a judgment that sees past circumstance to substance. The book proceeds through a series of evaluations of the disciples: Yan Hui who knows ten things from one, Zi-lu who is fearless but incomplete, Zai Yu who sleeps during the day and provokes the Master's sharpest rebuke, Zi-gong who is a sacred vessel but still a vessel.

The evaluations are not grades; they are acts of discernment. Confucius measures each disciple's quality and limitation in the same breath, and the cumulative effect is a portrait of the Master as a reader of character — a man who can see the thread of virtue running through a person's life even when it is tangled in circumstance or deficit. The book's epistemological heart is the revision of method: "formerly I heard a man's words and trusted his actions; now I hear his words and watch his actions." This is learning as the correction of error, the modification of judgment by experience.

The closing chapters are the most subtle: Confucius refuses to claim benevolence or wisdom for himself, insists he has "never seen a man of real integrity," and commits only to the practice of teaching without weariness. The book's terminal note is not certainty but generosity — the three wishes: peace to the aged, trust among friends, care for the young. After all the careful discriminations, judgment ends in goodwill.

The dominant force is **memory** — not of the past but of character, the capacity to read the accumulated evidence of a person's life and discern where the thread of virtue runs through it.

## Track-by-Track Reasoning

**1. Mozart — Eine kleine Nachtmusik: II. Romance** — *memory*

Gongye Chang, the prisoner, is worthy of alliance. Mozart's Romance is measured, gracious, quietly decisive — the sound of a person who can read character choosing correctly.

**2. Haydn — String Quartet Op. 76 No. 2 ('Fifths'): I. Allegro** — *memory*

Nan Yung repeated the White Jade poem until it mastered him. Haydn's Fifths Quartet opens with a falling interval that sounds like a question repeated until the answer becomes inevitable.

**3. Schubert — Symphony No. 5: II. Andante con moto** — *memory*

Zi-jian the superior man is known by the company he keeps. Schubert's Andante is open-hearted composure, the quality of a person who has chosen his neighbors well.

**4. Beethoven — Piano Sonata Op. 109: II. Prestissimo** — *law*

"女器也" — you are a vessel — the highest praise, and still: a gentleman is not a vessel. Beethoven's compressed, brilliant scherzo is the sound of a sacred vessel in the temple: magnificent, and still only one thing.

**5. Brahms — Piano Concerto No. 2: II. Allegro appassionato** — *memory*

Yan Hui knows ten from one. Brahms's scherzo derives every gesture from the opening — the many in the one.

**6. Stravinsky — The Rite of Spring: 'Ritual of the Rival Tribes'** — *sacrifice*

"朽木不可雕也" — rotten wood cannot be carved. Stravinsky's percussive, blunt violence is judgment harsh but not personal — the sacrifice of false expectation on the altar of reality.

**7. Elgar — Falstaff, Op. 68: opening** — *memory*

"未見剛者" — I have never seen a man of real integrity. Elgar's Falstaff swaggers through the opening bars, desire at the wheel, knowing his own limits.

**8. Schumann — Humoreske, Op. 20: 'Einfach'** — *memory*

"聽其言而觀其行" — hear the words, watch the actions. Schumann's shifting registers trace the movement from naive trust to knowing observation.

**9. Rossini — William Tell Overture: Finale** — *return*

"從我者，其由與？" — Zi-lu will follow the Master out to sea. Rossini's galloping charge is fearless action, the man who will always be there.

**10. Mozart — Le nozze di Figaro: 'Non più andrai'** — *memory*

Each disciple's wish, and the Master's amendment: peace for the aged, trust among friends, care for the young. Figaro's aria is a farewell to the old order — comic on the surface, serious underneath.

**11. Fauré — Requiem: III. Sanctus** — *memory*

Yan Hui never disagreed with the Master and was delighted by everything. Fauré's Sanctus is the sound of perfect understanding — not submission but recognition.

**12. Bach — Violin Sonata No. 1 in G minor: I. Adagio** — *grief*

Yan Hui's heart does not depart from benevolence for three months. Bach's Adagio is a single unaccompanied line that sustains intensity through its own inner gravity.

**13. Mozart — String Quintet in G minor, K. 516: I. Allegro** — *memory*

"眾惡之，必察焉；眾好之，必察焉" — investigate both. Mozart's G-minor Quintet searches through every chromatic corner before arriving at judgment.

**14. Brahms — Clarinet Sonata No. 2 in E-flat: I. Allegro amabile** — *memory*

The man who is ashamed of fine words and insinuating appearance. Brahms's late sonata is music that has no interest in being impressive — virtue that does not advertise itself.

**15. Haydn — Symphony No. 82 ('The Bear'): IV. Finale** — *comedy*

In any village of ten houses, someone as loyal as me — but none as fond of learning. Haydn's boisterous finale is the sound of a man who laughs at himself while naming his one genuine excellence.

**16. Bach — Cello Suite No. 5 in C minor: Sarabande** — *memory*

"若聖與仁，則吾豈敢？" — as for sage and benevolent, how dare I claim them? Bach's C-minor Sarabande is the most inward dance of the suites: a single line carrying the weight of a life's practice.

**17. Schubert — String Quartet No. 15 in G, D. 887: II. Andante un poco moto** — *return*

"君子道者三，我無能焉" — the Master says he has none of the three gentlemanly qualities. Schubert's Andante is profound stillness that moves through dark and luminous harmonies, assessing itself against an invisible standard.

**18. Beethoven — Piano Sonata Op. 110: III. Adagio ma non troppo** — *return*

"由也好勇過我" — Zi-lu surpasses me in courage. Beethoven's late recitative is the sound of a man who has survived his own courage and is trying to find the way back to form.

**19. Elgar — Enigma Variations: I. (C.A.E.)** — *memory*

The man entrusted with orphan and state. Elgar's variation on his wife is the portrait of absolute trustworthiness — simple, devoted, adequate.

**20. Ravel — String Quartet in F: II. Assez vif, très rythmé** — *desire*

"今吾聞其言而觀其行" — now I watch what they do. Ravel's pizzicato scherzo is all surface brilliance, but the harmonies underneath are darker — the distinction between speech and action made audible.

**21. Bach — Partita No. 2 in D minor: Allemande** — *memory*

"君子欲訥於言而敏於行" — slow to speak, swift to act. Bach's Allemande is the dancer in motion — no time for words, the motion is the meaning.

**22. Telemann — Concerto for Two Flutes in E minor: I. Largo** — *law*

Virtue has neighbors. Telemann's two flutes intertwine, neither dominating — the shape of a life that draws companionship because its form is generous.

**23. Mozart — Clarinet Concerto in A, K. 622: II. Adagio** — *memory*

"我不欲人之加諸我也" — what I do not want done to me, I do not do to others. Mozart's Adagio knows its own limits — the phrase never reaches for more than it can hold.

**24. Haydn — Symphony No. 104 ('London'): I. Adagio — Allegro** — *law*

"吾道一以貫之" — threaded on one principle. Haydn's London Symphony unfolds a vast structure from a single opening kernel.

**25. Brahms — Symphony No. 3: III. Poco allegretto** — *memory*

"吾無隱乎爾" — I conceal nothing. Brahms's cello theme pours out as though it had always been there, waiting to be heard — the teaching that is available to anyone who listens.

**26. Dvořák — String Quartet No. 12 ('American'): I. Allegro ma non troppo** — *memory*

Six virtues, six obscurations — every virtue needs the corrective of learning. Dvořák's fresh, open Quartet is a virtue not yet obscured.

**27. Mahler — Symphony No. 5: I. Trauermarsch** — *grief*

The teacher's grief for the disciple who sleeps. Mahler's trumpet solo sounds like a question no one can answer: why do some learn and others sleep?

**28. Fauré — Pavane, Op. 50** — *memory*

"老者安之，朋友信之，少者懷之" — peace for the aged, trust among friends, care for the young. Fauré's slow procession through a beautiful form is the sound of a generous wish moving through the world.

## The dominant arc of Book V

```
memory → memory → memory → law → memory → sacrifice → memory → memory → return → memory → memory → grief → memory → memory → comedy → memory → return → return → memory → desire → memory → law → memory → law → memory → memory → grief → memory
```

The arc of Book V is not narrative. It is the repeated act of looking at another person and trying to see clearly — the accumulated practice of discernment that a teacher develops across a lifetime of watching students become themselves.
