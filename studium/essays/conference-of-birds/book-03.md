# Conference of the Birds, Section 3: The Parrot and the Cage

The parrot's objection is different from the nightingale's, and Attar is careful to make this
distinction clear. The nightingale refuses to leave its beloved. The parrot refuses to leave its
familiar world — not because the world is loved (as the rose is loved) but because the world is
known, is safe, is continuous. The parrot wants immortality. It wants the guarantee that it will
not be annihilated, that its existence will not be interrupted or transformed. It wants forever.

This is one of the deepest longings in the human and animal heart: the wish to persist, to
continue, to not become nothing. The parrot speaks for every being that has ever feared death,
dissolution, the loss of the self. Attar does not dismiss this fear. He takes it seriously. But
his reply is paradoxical: the immortality the parrot seeks — an endless continuation of the same
self, in the same world, with the same certainties — is itself a kind of death. It is a stasis so
complete that it becomes a cage.

The hoopoe's teaching on the parrot is about accepting mortality, not as loss but as the
necessary condition for true life. The soul that refuses to change, that clings to its form, that
insists on continuity at all costs, has already died. It has chosen a cage over the uncertainty
of flight. True immortality — the immortality of the spirit that has learned to die before death
— comes only through letting go.

The dominant force is **desire**, but desire now understood as the ego's grip on continuation,
its terror of transformation. The journey requires the parrot to accept that it will not be the
same on the other side, and that loss of self is the necessary precondition for the discovery of
the true self.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Rachmaninoff — Piano Concerto No. 2: I. Moderato** — *desire*

The parrot's longing for immortality is not abstract. It is visceral, embodied, desperate. The
parrot does not want to die. Not as doctrine or principle, but as the simple, overwhelming desire
to continue, to persist, to not become nothing. Rachmaninoff's Second Concerto opens with a
melody that reaches and reaches, that climbs toward something it cannot hold. The trembling
beneath the melody, the romantic excess, the forward-pulled energy — this is the parrot's state.
The music does not mock this desire. It validates it, honors it, while also revealing it to be
impossible. The melody cannot be held. The ascent always falls back. This is the texture of the
parrot's condition: endless reaching for something that cannot be grasped.

**2. Elgar — *Nimrod* (from Enigma Variations)** — *memory*

The parrot's refusal to leave is not simply fear of death; it is also attachment to memory.
Paradise — whether conceived as a geographical place (the garden where the parrot once lived) or
as a state (the time before exile, before change, before the knowledge of mortality) — is a
memory. The parrot remembers being safe, being whole, being protected. This memory is not false.
But it has become a cage: it keeps the parrot looking backward, using the past as a reason not to
move forward.

Elgar's 'Nimrod' is often interpreted as a meditation on nobility and loss. The melody is dignified,
not bitter, but it is haunted by distance and yearning. The listener hears something of great worth
being remembered, being honored, but also lost. The parrot, listening to 'Nimrod', recognizes
itself: a being of worth, a being who once belonged somewhere, but now exiled, now separated from
that origin. The memory is both a wound and a comfort.

**3. Barber — *Knoxville: Summer of 1915*** — *supplication*

The parrot's refusal is not a philosophical argument. It is a plea: Let me stay. Let me keep what
I have. The parrot does not frame the cage as a prison; it frames it as a home. The routine is not
oppressive; it is order, it is predictability, it is the guarantee that tomorrow will resemble
today. The parrot is afraid. Not of the hoopoe, but of change, of the unknown, of the
discontinuity that the journey will bring.

Barber's setting of James Agee's prose poem is the music of longing to hold time still. The
narrator remembers a summer evening with his family, and the deepest longing beneath the memory
is: Let this not pass. Let this moment be eternal. The voice is gentle, almost childlike, pleading
for the day never to end. This is the parrot's supplication: not an argument against the journey,
but a wordless plea for permission to remain in the known world.

**4. Tavener — *The Protecting Veil*** — *revelation*

The hoopoe's reply to the parrot is perhaps his most radical teaching. The parrot, he says, is
already immortal. Not the immortality of endless identical days, but the immortality of the soul
that has annihilated its claim on continuation. The parrot fears that on the journey, it will die.
It will cease to exist. But the truth is stranger: the being that is afraid to die has already
died. It is living a death disguised as life.

True immortality comes through *fana* — the annihilation of the self that clings to its own
existence. The parrot must surrender its need to persist as the same being. In that surrender —
which feels like the ultimate death — the parrot will discover that it is immortal, that the self
that can die is not the true self.

Tavener's opening to 'The Protecting Veil' has a vertical simplicity that seems to open onto
infinity. A single note is held; then gradually the space around it opens. The revelation here is
not that the parrot must achieve immortality through effort; the revelation is that immortality is
already the parrot's nature, once the parrot stops clinging to the cage that seems to guarantee
continuation.

**5. Ravel — *Daphnis et Chloé*: 'Lever du jour'** — *metamorphosis*

The parrot cannot move forward and remain the same. This is the hard truth that the hoopoe's
teaching implies. The journey requires transformation. The parrot must change. It must lose the
certainty it has built its existence around. The fixed form, the familiar patterns, the continuity
of routine — all must yield.

But change is not destruction. This is what Ravel's 'Lever du jour' demonstrates: transformation
can be beautiful. The dawn comes gradually; the world emerges from darkness into light; new forms
appear; the landscape is revealed. Nothing is destroyed, but everything is altered by the coming
of light. The parrot will not lose its essential nature, but it will lose its fixity, its
certainty about what it is. It will become something it cannot yet imagine. Ravel's patient,
luminous orchestration shows that this transformation, while unavoidable, is not violent or
destructive. It is natural. It is beautiful. It is the way of the world.

**6. Mahler — Symphony No. 5: IV. Adagietto** — *grief*

Before the parrot can leave, it must grieve. The cage was not only a prison; it was also a home.
The routine was not only oppressive; it was also comfortable. The parrot must acknowledge this
without either denying the loss or using it as a reason to stay. This is genuine grief: the
recognition that something good is being left behind, even if the thing left behind is also a
hindrance.

Mahler's Adagietto is wound and tenderness at once. The melody searches and cannot find rest. The
harmony is poignant. But the piece is not bitter or angry; it is gentle with grief. The parrot's
sadness here is not weakness. It is the acknowledgment that immortality requires loss, that
transformation requires letting go, that the new self cannot be born until the old self has been
mourned.

**7. Sibelius — Symphony No. 2: III. Scherzo (finale passage)** — *ascent*

The turning point: the parrot begins to understand something that seems impossible but is true:
the fear of death is the thing that keeps it from life. To live — really live, not merely persist
— requires risk. To grow requires change. To journey is to accept that the being who arrives will
not be the being who departed.

This is the ascent of the parrot: not a triumph but a awakening. The parrot recognizes that it
has been half-alive, preserved in the cage, kept safe but also kept from vitality. Sibelius's
passage — driving, insistent, rhythmically alive with a kind of nervous energy — captures this
awakening. The parrot is now ready to move, not because it has conquered its fear but because it
has understood that the fear itself is the problem, not the solution.

**8. Pärt — *Tabula Rasa*** — *salvation*

The section ends with the parrot's commitment: I will leave. I will accept change. I will be
unmade so that I can be remade. The parrot has emptied itself of the need to persist as the same
being. It is now a blank slate — *tabula rasa* — ready to receive a new form, a new identity, a
new understanding of what immortality means.

Pärt's 'Tabula Rasa' is music that begins again from stillness. The opening is bare, simple,
almost austere. The parrot has released its grip on continuation, and in that release finds an
opening into something larger. It is not ecstatic joy (as Scriabin might have scored it), but
something quieter and more profound: the strange peace that comes from having let go.
