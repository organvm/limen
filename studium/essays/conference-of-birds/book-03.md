# Conference of the Birds, Section 3: The Peacock's Lament / The Gate of Paradise

## The Problem of Having Been Chosen

The peacock steps forward and the quality of the assembly changes. This is not a bird with
an ordinary attachment — the peacock was Adam's companion in paradise, a creature who walked
among the angels, who saw the garden from inside. His objection is the most poignant in the
poem because it is grounded in genuine experience: he does not doubt the Simurgh's existence;
he has been closer to the divine than any bird present, and he was cast out. His longing is
not for something he imagines but for something he remembers.

The hoopoe's answer is the poem's first major theological pivot. The paradise the peacock
longs for is not the destination — the Simurgh is not Eden regained. The search for the
Simurgh does not retrace the path of the fall; it goes through the fall and past it, into a
territory the peacock has never seen. His memory of paradise is both real and dangerous: it
makes him look backward when the journey requires forward motion. The peacock must learn
that the gate opens both ways, and that the journey is the gate.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Elgar — Symphony No. 2, I. Allegro vivace e nobilmente (opening theme)** — *memory*

The peacock's entrance is the most visually magnificent moment in the assembly. His tail is
a field of eyes — each one a witness to the paradise he carries in his body. Elgar's Second
Symphony opening is noble and shadowed: the theme rises with full orchestral dignity, but the
harmonies beneath are unsettled. This is the peacock's exact register — a creature whose
beauty is itself a memory of something lost, whose magnificence is laced with the shame of
exile.

**2. Mahler — Symphony No. 5, IV. Adagietto** — *grief*

The peacock tells his story: he was there, in the garden, when the world was new. He walked
with Adam. He saw the four rivers. Then — the serpent, the disobedience, the expulsion, the
angel with the flaming sword at the gate. Mahler's Adagietto is grief that has become pure
gesture: the strings alone, the melody arching over a pulse that is barely felt, the
sensibility of a loss so total that the one who suffered it no longer has words for it. The
peacock's grief is not for paradise alone — it is for the self he was in paradise, the bird
he no longer is.

**3. Pärt — Fratres** — *revelation*

The hoopoe's tale of the gate of paradise: it opens in both directions. Adam walked through
into the world and found that the world was also a gate — that every place after Eden is a
threshold to something else. Pärt's Fratres is a pattern that repeats and deepens: the same
chord sequence, the same percussion figure, each iteration revealing something that was
hidden in the previous one. The gate the peacock remembers closing behind him is also the
gate the hoopoe is asking him to walk through. It was never locked. It was waiting.

**4. Wagner — Lohengrin Prelude to Act I** — *desire*

The peacock interrupts: you do not understand. I do not want an opportunity — I want the
garden. The trees, the river, the actual place. Wagner's Lohengrin Prelude is desire that
has become so refined it has left the body behind: the violins ascending in octaves, the
harmony moving through an extended suspension that resolves into A major with the quality of
light entering a closed room. The peacock's longing for paradise is this pure, and this
specific. He does not want a metaphor for the garden; he wants the garden itself.

**5. Handel — Zadok the Priest** — *kingship*

The hoopoe's answer is Attar's theological pivot: paradise is a garden, but the Simurgh is
the king of the garden. To seek the garden without seeking the king is to mistake the house
for the householder. Handel's coronation anthem — the slow orchestral gathering, then the
explosion of 'God save the King' from the full choir — is sovereignty in its most absolute
musical form. The hoopoe is not diminishing the peacock's paradise; he is naming the reality
that paradise was created to serve.

**6. Barber — Adagio for Strings, Op. 11** — *supplication*

The peacock's deepest attachment is to his own election. He is not just a former resident of
paradise; he was the most beautiful bird there, Adam's companion. His supplication is mixed
with a pride he cannot separate from his prayer. Barber's Adagio is the sound of a single
line of feeling stretched to its breaking point — the strings moving through a single long
crescendo and diminuendo, the texture thinning as the emotional weight increases. The
peacock's prayer has this quality: he is crying out, but the cry carries the dignity of one
who believes he deserves to be heard.

**7. Strauss — Also sprach Zarathustra, 'Das Grablied' (The Song of the Grave)** — *divine-intervention*

The hoopoe's second tale — of the angel (Iblis) who loved his own light so perfectly that he
could not look away from it — is a story about pride that looks like devotion. Strauss's
'Das Grablied' (The Song of the Grave) is the darkest movement of Also sprach Zarathustra:
the low strings and brass in slow, funereal motion, the harmony descending, the light
draining out of the register. The peacock sees himself in the angel: the beauty that became
a cage. The recognition is devastating because it is true.

**8. Vaughan Williams — The Lark Ascending (opening)** — *memory*

The tale shifts something. The peacock's memory of paradise begins to change — no longer the
garden as a place with walls, but the garden as a quality of presence. Vaughan Williams's
The Lark Ascending opens with a violin line that seems to rise without effort, the orchestra
beneath it a field of open fifths and pastoral light. The peacock's new memory is not
nostalgic — it is the trace of presence that survives the loss of the place, the scent of
the garden that the exile carries in his feathers.

**9. Mahler — Symphony No. 4, IV. 'Sehr behaglich' (Das himmlische Leben)** — *ascent*

The peacock's attention shifts. He does not declare he will go. But the direction of his
looking changes — from the garden behind to the possibility ahead. Mahler's Fourth Symphony
finale — a child's vision of heaven — is ascent without terror: the child's voice describes
the celestial banquet, the saints, the lamb, with the simple matter-of-factness of someone
who has never doubted. The peacock's shift has this quality: a release into the trust that
the garden he remembers and the destination he cannot yet see are continuous in a way he
could not see when he was looking backward.

**10. Dvořák — Symphony No. 9 ('From the New World'), II. Largo** — *return*

The section closes in suspension. The assembly is held between the peacock's remembered
paradise and the Simurgh's unknown one. Dvořák's Largo — the English horn melody over held
chords — is homesickness for a home that exists only as longing. The birds have not moved,
but they have been moved. The music holds them in the space between: not in paradise, not
on the road, but in the newly felt awareness that there is somewhere to go, and that the
going will require leaving even the memory of where they have been.
