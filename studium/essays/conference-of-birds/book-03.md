# Conference of the Birds, Section 3: The Peacock's Excuse

## The Trap of Nostalgia

In the third section, the peacock presents his case. His is the tragedy of a glorious past. He
once lived in paradise but was expelled because he consorted with the serpent. His entire spiritual
life is now a backward-looking project: he wants to go back. He wants the garden. The hoopoe's
response is one of Attar's most brilliant theological corrections: to seek the King's garden rather
than the King himself is a failure of ambition. The created thing, even if it is paradise, is an
idol if it replaces the Creator.

The music of this arc traces the peacock's journey from majestic nostalgia, through the cold shock
of structural correction, to a redirection of his desire — trading a backward gaze for an upward
ascent.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Handel — Zadok the Priest**
The peacock arrives with the memory of royalty. He considers himself the king of birds by
right of his former residence. Handel's coronation anthem provides the overwhelming ceremonial
grandeur that the peacock projects — it is magnificent, but it is a worldly, courtly
magnificence. It is the music of someone whose self-worth is entirely tied to their status.

**2. Elgar — Enigma Variations, IX. Nimrod**
The peacock speaks of paradise, a place he lost and cannot stop remembering. Elgar's Nimrod
is the quintessential music of noble, aching nostalgia. It swells with the warmth of what
was, capturing the peacock's complete absorption in his own backward-looking longing. It is
beautiful, but dangerously comfortable.

**3. Barber — Adagio for Strings**
The peacock remembers his expulsion. The stain of the serpent is on him. Barber's Adagio is
music that slowly, inescapably descends into the depths of sorrow. It is the sound of a
creature realizing the gates are locked behind it, feeling the full weight of permanent exile
from the only home it ever wanted.

**4. Stravinsky — Symphony of Psalms, I. Exaudi orationem meam**
The hoopoe points out the fatal flaw: the peacock is settling for the King's real estate
instead of the King. Stravinsky's neoclassical architecture in the Symphony of Psalms is
austere and objective. It cuts through the peacock's sentimentality with cold, rigorous truth.
The chorus does not weep; it states the law.

**5. Messiaen — Quartet for the End of Time, V. Louange à l'Éternité de Jésus**
Attar's sub-tale illustrates that proximity to the King is worth more than all the treasures
of the kingdom. Messiaen's endlessly slow, suspended cello line in the Louange is the sound
of stepping outside time and encountering the divine essence directly, bypassing all
intermediaries and gardens. It reveals what the peacock has been missing.

**6. Strauss — Also sprach Zarathustra, Introduction**
The hoopoe reveals that the Simurgh is vastly greater than paradise. Paradise is a mere
shadow of the Simurgh's reality. The brass and timpani of Strauss's sunrise provide this
sudden, blinding shift in scale — the sudden realization of a vastly larger theological
framework that shatters the peacock's small, garden-sized dreams.

**7. Beethoven — Symphony No. 7, II. Allegretto**
The peacock realizes that true return is not going back to Eden, but moving forward to the
source. Beethoven's Allegretto, with its inexorable walking pace and circular variations,
embodies this truer, deeper march. It is not nostalgia; it is the rhythm of homecoming,
steady and inevitable.

**8. Mahler — Symphony No. 2 ('Resurrection'), V. Im Tempo des Scherzos (opening)**
The peacock redirects his gaze from the past to the future, from the garden to the King. The
opening of Mahler's resurrection finale — before the choir — is music that pushes upward from
the ashes, full of terror but definitively ascending. The bird leaves his memories behind and
joins the brutal, necessary journey.

---

## The Dominant Arc

```
false kingship → nostalgia → the grief of the fall → theological correction → the vision of the center →
the shattering of scale → the new march → the upward drive
```

The peacock must unlearn his history. The music moves from the heavy sentiment of the past into the
rigorous, terrifying demands of the present ascent.
