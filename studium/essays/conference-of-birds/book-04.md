# Conference of the Birds, Section 4: The Duck's Purity / The Water That Drowns

## The Problem of Mistaking the Means for the End

The duck's objection is the most gentle and the most difficult to answer. The duck does not
want immortality (the parrot) or paradise regained (the peacock). The duck wants water —
simple, everyday, necessary water. He is attached to the element that sustains him, the
medium of his life. His objection is not a refusal of the journey but a practical question:
how can I leave the thing that keeps me alive?

The hoopoe's answer goes to the heart of Attar's teaching about means and ends. Water is
holy — no Muslim, no Sufi, could deny that water is the element of purification, the
substance of wudu, the gift of rain. But the duck's error is the most refined form of
attachment because it looks like piety. He does not love water idolatrously; he loves water
as the place where the sacred touches the world. The hoopoe must show him that the sacred
that touches the world through water is not water itself — and that the journey to the
Simurgh passes through a landscape where water must be left behind, not as renunciation but
as the precondition of finding the source of all water.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Debussy — La Mer, I. 'De l'aube à midi sur la mer' (opening)** — *desire*

The duck's opening description of water is one of the most beautiful passages in the poem.
He speaks not as a theologian but as a creature who lives in the element: the feel of it,
the taste, the sound, the way light enters it differently at dawn. Debussy's La Mer opens
with a sense of water not yet seen but felt — the harp harmonics, the low strings, the cello
melody rising from depth. The duck's desire for water is not greed; it is the desire of the
element for itself, the creature's recognition that it was made from what it loves.

**2. Ravel — Gaspard de la nuit, 'Ondine'** — *revelation*

The hoopoe's tale of the fish who never left the sea: the fish was so devoted to the water
that it refused any other dimension, and when the drought came and the water vanished, the
fish died faithful to an element that did not know it existed. Ravel's 'Ondine' is water
music at the edge of the inhuman: the piano's cascading figures never settle into melody,
the harmonies shift as water shifts, the sprite's song is seductive and without compassion.
The duck hears, for the first time, that his beloved water might not love him back.

**3. Offenbach — Orpheus in the Underworld Overture (can-can)** — *comedy*

The duck's piety tips into the comic: he describes his daily ablutions, his meticulous
cleansing rituals, his horror at the thought of a day without water. The other birds begin
to laugh — not unkindly, but with the recognition that the duck's holiness has become a
compulsion. Offenbach's can-can is comedy as body music: irresistible, irrepressible,
physical. The duck's rituals are beautiful and ridiculous, and the laughter is the poem's
way of releasing the duck (and the reader) from the prison of literal piety.

**4. Stravinsky — The Rite of Spring, I. 'The Augurs of Spring (Dances of the Young Girls)'** — *sacrifice*

The hoopoe's second tale is harsher: a hermit who purified himself so constantly that he
washed away everything he was, arriving at a purity so absolute that he became incapable of
feeling, of loving, of journeying. Stravinsky's 'Augurs of Spring' — the famous pounding
repeated chord in 7/8 — is ritual violence without cause: the young girl dances herself to
death, the rhythm grinds without release, the sacrifice consumes the sacrificer. The duck's
purity, taken to its extreme, becomes this: the element that was supposed to cleanse becomes
the thing that drowns.

**5. Fauré — Requiem, 'Agnus Dei'** — *supplication*

The duck's prayer is the simplest in the poem: I only want to be clean. Fauré's 'Agnus Dei'
is supplication that has stopped bargaining — the lamb of God who takes away the sins of the
world, the 'Dona eis requiem' (Grant them rest) repeated in a lilting 3/4, the asking itself
the only rest available. The duck's prayer is not for anything new; it is for what he has
always asked for. The difference is that now he is asking in front of witnesses, in the
presence of a teaching that has begun to unsettle him.

**6. Smetana — Má vlast, 'Vltava' (opening)** — *metamorphosis*

The hoopoe softens. He tells a third tale — shorter, gentler — of a river that flowed
through every kind of terrain and eventually reached a sea so vast that the river could not
tell where it ended and the sea began. The river did not die; it changed its name. Smetana's
Vltava begins as two thin streams, flute and clarinet, that merge into the river theme —
water music not as element but as movement, not as stasis but as transformation. The duck
hears the tale differently: the river did not leave water; it became more water than it had
been.

**7. Sibelius — The Oceanides, Op. 73** — *ascent*

Something shifts in the duck. He begins to sense that water was never the destination —
water was the carrier, the thing that moves toward what it cannot contain. Sibelius's The
Oceanides is water becoming sky: the orchestra builds from low strings through harp to a
radiant climax, the waves no longer separate from the light above them. The duck's first
glimpse of understanding is this: the water he loved was always moving toward something
beyond itself.

**8. Ravel — Jeux d'eau** — *memory*

The duck remembers: every stream he has known, every pond, every spring, every drop of
morning dew on a leaf. These memories are not false — the water was truly holy, each body
of water a place where the sacred touched the world. Ravel's Jeux d'eau is memory as liquid
piano texture: the glissandi like light on moving water, the harmonies shifting as surfaces
shift, the whole piece a prayer of gratitude for water that was not preparation for something
else but water itself, given and received.

**9. Schubert — String Quintet in C Major, D. 956, II. Adagio (opening)** — *desire*

The duck's final question is the most touching in the entire assembly: can I bring water on
the journey? The hoopoe answers: you are water. Schubert's Adagio opens with a texture of
almost unbearable tenderness: the cello and viola holding a long chord while the first violin
moves above in a line that seems to have no weight. The duck's question is this line — the
desire to keep what is good while moving toward what is better, the terror that the good and
the better might not be the same thing.

**10. Vaughan Williams — Fantasia on a Theme by Thomas Tallis** — *salvation*

The section does not resolve. It opens. Vaughan Williams's Fantasia divides the strings into
two choirs, the ancient Tallis hymn melody moving between them like water between two banks.
The duck will travel; he will miss the water; the missing will be part of the journey. But
the water is not leaving him — it is being transfigured into the larger element of grace
that holds all journeys. The duck's purity was never wrong; it was just early.
