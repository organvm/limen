# Conference of the Birds, Section 6: The Hawk's Devotion / The King's Wrist and the King of Kings

## The Problem of Having Already Surrendered

The hawk's objection is the subtlest in the assembly. He does not resist the idea of service —
he has already given his life to a king, hunts on a king's fist, wears the jesses and the
hood and the bell. He has made the submission the other birds are being asked to make. His
question is devastating in its simplicity: I have already surrendered my will to an earthly
sovereign; why would I leave a real king for an invisible one?

The hoopoe's answer is the Assembly phase's most mature teaching. He does not tell the hawk
that his king is unworthy — that would be a lie, and a dangerous one. He tells the hawk that
his king is a veil of the Simurgh, a real presence through which the divine sovereign has
been disclosing itself all along. The hawk's service has not been wasted; it has been
training. The earthly king's wrist prepared the hawk for a wrist that holds the entire
cosmos. The hawk does not need to leave his king; he needs to see through his king to the
reality his king himself serves.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Walton — Orb and Sceptre — Coronation March** — *kingship*

The hawk's opening speech carries the authority of someone who has lived discipline. He does
not boast; he reports: I serve a king. I have given my life to his wrist. I am not free, and
I do not desire freedom — I desire to be used by what is greater than myself. Walton's Orb
and Sceptre, written for the coronation of Elizabeth II, is sovereignty music at the highest
level of formality: the brass intervals, the processional rhythm, the sense of a world ordered
by allegiance. The hawk's position sounds reasonable because from inside hierarchy it is
perfectly reasonable.

**2. Elgar — Pomp and Circumstance March No. 1 (Trio)** — *desire*

The hawk describes the physical texture of his service: the glove, the leather, the weight
of the king's arm, the moment of release when the prey is spotted and the king's hand opens.
Elgar's 'Land of Hope and Glory' trio is the melody of loyalty to something larger than the
self: the strings in full voice, the tune anarc of devotion that has become inseparable from
the one who sings it. The hawk's desire is not for freedom — it is for the structure of
service itself, the hierarchy, the discipline, the joy of being the tool of a worthy will.

**3. Messiaen — L'Ascension, 'Prière du Christ montant vers son Père'** — *revelation*

The hoopoe's answer: every earthly king is a veil of the Simurgh. The hawk's service has been
real and true, but it has been service to the trace, not the source. Messiaen's 'Prayer of
Christ ascending to the Father' is revelation as vertical movement: the organ sustains long,
suspended chords that rise through a series of plateaus, each higher than the last, each
preparing the next. The hawk's king is such a plateau — a real height from which the next
height becomes visible. He has not been wrong; he has been incomplete.

**4. Stravinsky — Symphony of Psalms, I. 'Exaudi orationem meam, Domine'** — *sacrifice*

The hawk's world breaks. The service of his life has been pointed beyond itself, and he did
not know it. Stravinsky's Symphony of Psalms opens with a choked cry — the winds and brass
in a single dissonant attack, the sound of a prayer that is also a wound. The hawk's
realization is a sacrifice: not of his life but of the meaning he has built around his life.
'Hear my prayer, O Lord' is his unwilling cry — the prayer of one who has just discovered
that the God he did not know he was serving may be the only one he has ever truly served.

**5. Handel — Music for the Royal Fireworks, Overture (Adagio)** — *kingship*

The hoopoe's rejoinder: the Simurgh is not a different kind of ruler — the Simurgh is what
every ruler is ruling toward when they rule well. Handel's Royal Fireworks Overture is
sovereignty in the French overture tradition: the dotted rhythms, the ceremonial gravity,
the brass (no strings in the first scoring) announcing a presence that exceeds the occasion.
The hoopoe is not diminishing the hawk's king; he is elevating the category of kingship to
include its own source.

**6. Bach — Mass in B Minor, 'Agnus Dei'** — *supplication*

The hawk's prayer pivots. He does not want to abandon his king, but he wants to serve the
reality his king was serving. Bach's 'Agnus Dei' is the most concentrated supplication in
the Mass: the alto voice above the continuo, the 'Agnus Dei, qui tollis peccata mundi'
repeated with the economy of absolute necessity. The hawk is not bargaining; he is laying
down his rank, his pride, his duty, before a king he has not yet seen, asking only to be
used rightly.

**7. Bach — The Art of Fugue, Contrapunctus I** — *law*

The hoopoe's final tale: a king who removed his crown, entered the mosque as a beggar, and
found that the prayer of a beggar reaches a different God than the prayer of a king. The
hawk must learn the form of service that asks for nothing — not even the dignity of serving.
Bach's Contrapunctus I is law without lawgiver: a subject, a countersubject, an architecture
of musical logic that does not argue but simply is. The fugue is submission to form; the
hawk's next step is submission to the form that underlies all service.

**8. Bruckner — Symphony No. 7, IV. Finale (coda)** — *ascent*

The hawk removes his jesses. The leather straps fall. The assembly is silent. Bruckner's
Seventh finale coda is the sound of arrival: the brass restating the theme, the strings
surging upward, the harmony resolving into C major with the force of a journey that has
reached its structural destination. The hawk's act is not renunciation — it is the next
step in his service. He has not stopped serving; he has begun to serve the reality his king
was pointing to.

**9. Dvořák — String Quartet No. 12 ('American'), II. Lento** — *desire*

The hawk thinks of his earthly king with new eyes. Not as the end of his service but as a
fellow creature who also — perhaps — is seeking something beyond his crown. Dvořák's Lento
is desire in the key of homesickness: the viola melody over syncopated strings, the harmony
reaching toward a resolution that keeps receding. The hawk's love for his king has not
diminished; it has been released into a larger field. The king is no longer the terminus of
his devotion but a companion in seeking.

**10. Bruckner — Symphony No. 9, III. Adagio (conclusion)** — *salvation*

The section closes in stillness. The hawk has shown the assembly that submission is not the
end of freedom but its beginning — and that the Simurgh rules not by coercion but by being
what the seeker most deeply is. Bruckner's Ninth Adagio conclusion — dedicated 'to the
beloved God,' the horns intoning the theme over a string chorale of almost unbearable
radiance — is salvation as the threshold of a home that was always already there. The birds
are not at the Simurgh. But they are no longer anywhere else.
