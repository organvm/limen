# Conference of the Birds, Section 2: The Parrot's Excuse

## The Cage of Immortality

In the second movement of the assembly, the parrot steps forward. Attar dresses him in a beautiful
green coat, but the humor is pointed: the parrot is a spiritual conservative. He does not want the
Simurgh. He wants Khizr's Water of Life. He wants to live forever, safely, in a cage. The music of
this section must chart the deconstruction of this false safety, moving from the parrot's chromatic,
liquid desires to the hoopoe's unyielding architectural logic, ending in the stark realization that
safety is exactly what is keeping the bird from flying.

This section deals with the most common spiritual trap: mistaking the preservation of the self for
the salvation of the self.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Rameau — Pièces de clavecin, 'Le Rappel des Oiseaux'**
The parrot approaches in his green coat, full of self-importance. Attar's comedy is sharp here:
the bird is undeniably beautiful but completely missing the point. Rameau's harpsichord piece
mimics bird calls with a courtly, slightly ridiculous elegance that perfectly captures the
parrot's vanity. It is the music of someone very pleased with their own plumage.

**2. Szymanowski — Mythes, Op. 30, 'La Fontaine d'Arethuse'**
The parrot's desire is not for the Simurgh but for immortality — the Water of Khizr. Szymanowski's
shimmering violin writing captures this exact liquid obsession. It is beautiful, chromatic, and
ultimately static, circling around an unattainable object rather than moving toward it. It sounds
exactly like a beautiful illusion.

**3. Bach — The Art of Fugue, Contrapunctus I**
The hoopoe answers with structural clarity: what good is living forever if you are not alive?
Bach's opening fugue is the absolute statement of architectural law. It does not argue; it simply
demonstrates how things are constructed. The hoopoe applies this unyielding logic to the parrot's
false premise, laying it bare step by step.

**4. Sibelius — The Swan of Tuonela**
The hoopoe points out that the parrot's green coat is exactly what puts him in an iron cage. His
safety is his prison. Sibelius's dark, wandering English horn solo embodies this profound isolation:
a creature trapped in a static, twilight world, cut off from the sky. This is the true sound of the
parrot's desired immortality.

**5. Fauré — Élégie, Op. 24**
Attar inserts a sub-tale of a man who found the water of life but realized it meant watching everyone
he loved die forever. Fauré's Élégie carries this exact weight of endless, weary recall — the burden
of outliving one's time. The cello line remembers without the possibility of closure, turning the
dream of eternal life into an endless grief.

**6. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 6 ('Pathétique'), IV. Adagio lamentoso**
The illusion of safe immortality is finally crushed by the hoopoe. Fate cannot be outrun by staying
in a cage. Tchaikovsky's hammer blows of despair in the Pathétique finale represent the inevitable
collision with reality. The parrot must face that he will die, and that his cage will not save him.

**7. Pärt — Fratres (for violin, strings and percussion)**
The hoopoe does not leave the parrot in despair, but commands him to let go of his small life for the
sake of the vast one. Pärt's Fratres is built on repeated, suspended supplication — it strips away
ornament and leaves only the raw need to surrender. It is the sound of ego beginning to dissolve.

**8. Bach — Chorale Prelude 'Ich ruf zu dir, Herr Jesu Christ', BWV 639**
The parrot finally accepts the hoopoe's logic. He lets go of the cage. Bach's chorale prelude is not
triumphant, but deeply resolved. The continuous, steady flow of the left hand is the sound of a choice
made in the quietness of the soul, turning toward the true destination.

## The Dominant Arc

```
vanity → false desire → structural rebuke → the reality of isolation → the burden of eternity →
the blow of reality → the necessity of surrender → quiet resolution
```

The parrot's arc is the dismantling of safety. The music must show the cage becoming unbearable, so
that flight becomes the only remaining option.
