# Conference of the Birds, Section 2: The Parrot's Objection / The Cage of Immortality

## The Problem with Wanting to Live Forever

The parrot is the first bird to speak in the assembly after the nightingale, and his objection
is the most radical: he has heard the hoopoe's description of the Simurgh, and he wants it
immediately — not as presence, not as transformation, but as more of what he already is. The
parrot wants eternal life without realizing that the "life" he wants to extend is precisely
the condition the journey is designed to dissolve.

The section turns on a profound distinction: between the desire for immortality (which is the
parrot's position) and the desire for the Simurgh (which is the destination). The parrot
thinks they are the same thing. He calls his desire spiritual because its object is eternal.
The hoopoe shows him, through two embedded tales, that the desire for eternal life is still
a desire for the self — just the self projected into infinite time. The Simurgh does not
offer extended selfhood. It offers the only thing that outlasts the self: the dissolution of
the self into the pattern of transformation itself.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Szymanowski — Symphony No. 3 ('Song of the Night'), I. Moderato (opening)** — *desire*

The parrot's opening is not greed in the vulgar sense — it is longing that has mistaken its
object, desire that calls itself spiritual because the thing it wants (eternal life) is
unlimited. Szymanowski's Third Symphony, setting the 13th-century Persian poet Rumi's 'Song
of the Night,' opens with a tenor voice suspended above a shimmering, chromatically shifting
orchestra. The music sounds like desire that has forgotten it is attached to anything. That
is the parrot's exact condition: he wants the Simurgh the way a miser wants gold, but he
calls it immortality. The Szymanowski holds the register of wanting so refined that the singer
(whether the parrot or Rumi's night-struck poet) does not yet know what he is singing for.

**2. Rossini — Il barbiere di Siviglia: Overture** — *comedy*

The other birds laugh. The laughter is structurally necessary: the poem needs release after
the intensity of the parrot's demand, and the birds' collective comic sense is Attar's
guarantee that the spiritual teaching will not become oppressive. Rossini's Barber Overture
is comedy as precision engineering — the crescendi timed to the millisecond, the thematic
material incisive, the rhythm driving toward a release that never fully arrives because the
music is already laughing at its own momentum. The birds are not mocking the parrot; they
are laughing with the recognition of absurdity that only a community of flawed creatures
can share.

**3. Debussy — Prélude à l'après-midi d'un faune** — *revelation*

The hoopoe does not answer the parrot's argument. He tells a story. The tale of the Prince
and the Phoenix begins; the register shifts from disputation to narrative. Debussy's prelude
opens with the most famous flute solo in the repertoire — a line that rises out of silence
as if narrative were being invented at this moment, in this breath. The quality of revelation
here is not information but disclosure: the faune's afternoon, the hoopoe's tale, the
opening of a world that was not present until the telling began. Revelation in Attar's poem
works exactly like this: not by presenting evidence but by shifting the ground of what the
listener considers real.

**4. Mahler — Kindertotenlieder, 'Nun will die Sonn' so hell aufgeh'n' (No. 1)** — *grief*

The prince's journey costs him everything. Mahler's song about a mother who has lost her
children — the sun rising on a day the children will not see — is grief that has left behind
all drama. The voice is quiet, the orchestra sparse, the emotional weight in the spaces
between the notes. The prince's losses accumulate this way: not as tragedy but as the slow,
irreversible subtraction of everything that seemed essential. The grief is the shape of what
it costs to seek something beyond the self without yet knowing that the self is what must
be spent.

**5. Scriabin — The Poem of Ecstasy, opening (Lento)** — *desire*

The phoenix appears. It does not offer immortality. It burns. Scriabin's Poem of Ecstasy is
desire pushed to the point where it ceases to be personal — the famous opening trumpet call,
the gathering orchestral texture, the sense of a force that is no longer attached to any
individual will. The prince, facing the phoenix's fire, experiences desire as what it truly
is: not wanting something but being something. The phoenix's fire is desire itself, burning
the distinction between the one who wants and the thing that is wanted.

**6. Bach — Cantata BWV 60, 'Es ist genug' (chorale)** — *supplication*

The parrot interrupts: I wanted life, not death. The confusion is honest. The chorale 'Es
ist genug' (It is enough) is Bach's most radical statement of surrender — the harmonies
moving through suspensions that never quite release, the text announcing that the speaker
has arrived at the limit of what the self can carry. The parrot's 'It is enough' is different
from the chorale's: the parrot means he has had enough of this difficult teaching; the
chorale means the soul has had enough of itself and is ready to be held. The gap between
the two meanings is the gap between the parrot's current understanding and the one the
journey will open.

**7. Bruckner — Symphony No. 4 ('Romantic'), III. Scherzo (Trio)** — *ascent*

The hoopoe's answer to the parrot's protest is the poem's first full teaching on fana: the
self is not to be preserved but outlived. Bruckner's Fourth Scherzo Trio is Bruckner at his
most lifted — a Ländler that seems to float, the horns and strings in a dance that has
surrendered its weight. The ascent here is not effortful; it is Bruckner's specific gift to
the spiritual repertoire: the sense that rising is what we were made for, that gravity is a
temporary condition. The parrot cannot yet enter this dance, but he hears it, and the hearing
plants something.

**8. Fauré — Pavane, Op. 50** — *memory*

The hoopoe returns to the feather. The trace of the Simurgh, fallen in China, is the poem's
central image before the mirror: not proof, not presence, but evidence that something beyond
the known has left its mark on the known world. Fauré's Pavane is memory that has become
formal — the melody repeats, the bass repeats, the structure is a dance that remembers itself.
The feather is like this: a pattern that can be recognized because it was always already
there, waiting to be seen.

**9. Ravel — Daphnis et Chloé, Suite No. 2, 'Lever du jour'** — *metamorphosis*

The parrot has not decided to go. But something has shifted beneath his certainty. Ravel's
dawn music from Daphnis et Chloé is metamorphosis as natural process: a flute line in the
silence, then another, then a slow layering of strings, woodwinds, the sun rising not because
anyone wills it but because morning happens. The parrot's change is like this: not a
conversion but a dawn, a crack in the shell of his old understanding through which a light
enters that he did not invite and cannot control.

**10. Tavener — The Protecting Veil, I. The Protecting Veil (opening)** — *revelation*

The hoopoe closes with a prayer: may you die before you die, so that the death that comes
after death is not the first death. Tavener's The Protecting Veil opens with a cello melody
of immense stillness — a single line above a held chord, the melody not going anywhere
because it has found its home. The hoopoe's prayer is this threshold: the door through which
the birds must pass, but which none of them can enter until they are ready to leave
themselves behind. The parrot is not ready. But the door is visible now.

## The Dominant Arc

```
longing that mistakes its object → the assembly's laughter → narrative as disclosure →
the cost of seeking → desire as fire → honest confusion → fana as the soul's natural motion →
the trace as clue → change that precedes choice → the threshold held open
```

Section 2 is the poem's first extended teaching on the difference between wanting the eternal
and being made eternal through dissolution. The parrot is the most difficult case because his
desire is the closest to the right thing — and therefore the easiest to confuse with it.
