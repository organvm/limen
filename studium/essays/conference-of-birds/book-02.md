# Conference of the Birds, Section 2: The Nightingale and the Rose

The nightingale does not begin by wanting to stay. It begins by being in love. This is Attar's
crucial distinction: the objection to the journey is not an intellectual choice but a confession
of a state so total, so particular, that the very idea of leaving seems like annihilation. The
nightingale loves the rose. The rose is beautiful. The rose is enough. Why would a creature with
a heart full of song and a beloved within reach trade that certainty for a journey toward
something incomprehensibly distant?

This is where Attar places the hoopoe's first real test of wisdom. The hoopoe does not dismiss
the nightingale's love. He does not say love is illusion or that the rose is unworthy. Instead,
he reveals a teaching that is far more difficult: the rose is real, the beauty is genuine, but
it is a *veil* — a manifestation of something infinitely larger. The nightingale's love is not
misplaced; it is simply incomplete. The fire that burns in the nightingale is the same fire that
will burn in the seven valleys, but the nightingale has mistaken the particular object for the
universal principle.

The dominant force is **desire** — but desire now understood not as weakness or attachment but as
the soul's engine. The nightingale must move from loving *a rose* to understanding that love
itself — that longing, that reaching toward beauty — is the mechanism of ascent. The seven valleys
are not an escape from desire; they are desire deepened, spiritualized, pointed toward its true source.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Schumann — *Dichterliebe*: 'Im wunderschönen Monat Mai'** — *desire*

The nightingale does not apologize for its love. Nor is its love merely romantic sentimentality.
In Sufi poetry, the beloved — whether figured as the rose, the beloved's face, or the divine —
is the object that concentrates all the soul's longing. To love is to be chosen by love; the
beloved does not decide whether you will feel this way, and you cannot simply decide to feel
otherwise. Schumann's opening to *Dichterliebe* captures this involuntariness perfectly. Spring
comes; longing awakens; the beloved appears in imagination. The harmony does not resolve. The
longing is suspended, incomplete, and wholly binding. This is the nightingale's state: not chosen,
not escapable, and — in its moment — sufficient. The beauty of this opening is that it does not
diminish the love. Attar's poem will ask the nightingale to transcend this attachment, but not
before establishing that the attachment is real, particular, and worthy of honor.

**2. Mahler — Symphony No. 2: V. 'Urlicht'** — *supplication*

The nightingale's refusal is not a rhetorical flourish; it is a cry of the soul. How can I leave?
How can I abandon what my entire being has become? The nightingale does not say the journey is
difficult; it says the journey is impossible — not because the bird lacks courage but because
love has restructured the bird's entire ontology. To leave the rose would be to leave itself.

Mahler's setting of 'Urlicht' — 'O light of origin' — is a soul's supplication to the divine,
asking for understanding, for mercy, for a way out of an impossible bind. The exposed melodic
line, the way the voice searches and cannot find rest, the profound sorrow of the accompaniment
— all of this embodies the nightingale's condition. The supplication is not weakness. It is the
highest form of honesty: a creature admitting that it cannot move, that it is too heavy with
love to fly.

**3. Debussy — *Clair de lune*** — *desire*

Here the rose itself must be present in the music — not as symbol but as sensory fact. The rose
is not an excuse; it is a specific, irreplaceable, beloved thing. In Sufi tradition, the beloved
is often imaged as a face, and the face is always particular: this face, not beauty in general,
but *this* beloved's eyes, this beloved's smile, this beloved's presence in the world.

Debussy's 'Clair de lune' does something that much romantic music fails to do: it gives absolute
beauty without sentimentality. The moonlit piano writing does not ask you to feel sentimental or
lose yourself in reverie. Instead, it presents the beauty as a fact, as sufficient, as enough
reason for a being to remain. The piece is precise, crystalline, and utterly convincing. This is
the point: the rose is genuinely beautiful, genuinely worthy, genuinely sufficient as an object
of devotion.

**4. Messiaen — *Quartet for the End of Time*: 'Louange à l'Éternité de Jésus'** — *revelation*

The hoopoe's teaching is the turning point of the section, and it is not a dismissal but a
revelation. The hoopoe does not tell the nightingale that the rose is unworthy or that the love
is misplaced. Instead, he teaches that the rose is a *veil* (*hijab*) — a manifestation, a
particular expression of something infinitely larger. The Simurgh is the source from which all
beauty flows, including the rose's beauty. The nightingale is not wrong to love the beautiful;
the nightingale is simply incomplete in its understanding of what it loves.

Messiaen's movement from the Quartet for the End of Time is the closest Western music comes to
expressing this layering of revelations. The eternal is beneath the temporal; the infinite face
is behind the beloved's face; the principle is behind the manifestation. The music has a vertical
quality — it does not move horizontally through time but opens downward/upward into depth. The
violin melody unfolds with infinite patience, and beneath it, the piano holds a kind of eternal
stillness. The space of the music is vast. The revelation here is not that the beloved is false
but that the beloved is a window, and the view behind the window is incomparably large.

**5. Bruckner — Symphony No. 7: I. Allegro moderato (opening)** — *ascent*

At this moment, the nightingale begins to understand something crucial: the very longing that
binds it to the rose is not what holds it back. Longing is the mechanism of ascent. The hoopoe
has not asked the nightingale to stop loving; he has asked the nightingale to redirect that love
toward its source.

This is the poem's great paradox: the seven valleys are not a path away from desire but a path
deeper into desire, rightly understood. The nightingale's love does not diminish on the journey;
it expands. The fire that burns for the rose will burn for the Simurgh, and the burning will be
infinitely more intense.

Bruckner's long-arching cello theme that rises without rush, without aggression, without
destination anxiety — it simply ascends as if upward motion were natural law — captures this
perfectly. The nightingale is not being forced. It is recognizing something it always was: a
creature built for flight, for ascent, for movement toward something larger than itself. The
rose did not create the longing; the longing created the rose. Now the nightingale understands
where the longing truly leads.

**6. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*: Prelude** — *desire*

But the rose is still there. Teaching does not erase the beloved. The nightingale has heard the
hoopoe's wisdom and recognized the truth of the path, but the rose has not ceased to be beautiful.
Desire is still present, still binding, still absolute.

This is the delicate moment in the section. Many spiritual traditions teach renunciation as the
extinguishing of desire, as if the goal were to stop wanting. Attar teaches something more
sophisticated: desire does not disappear; it deepens. The nightingale will carry the longing for
the rose through the entire journey, but increasingly it will understand that the rose is not the
destination, merely a way-station on the path.

Wagner's Tristan Prelude — that unresolved chord, that forward-pulled harmonic structure, that
sense of desire that cannot discharge itself in satisfaction — is exactly right. The Prelude is
famous for its harmonic irresolution, and that irresolution is the texture of the mystical path.
The nightingale cannot have the rose and the Simurgh. It must choose. But the choosing does not
happen in a moment; it happens across the entire journey, and the desire does not disappear, it
transforms.

**7. Fauré — *Requiem*: 'Sanctus'** — *grief*

Underneath the revelation, underneath the recognition of the path, is a grief. The nightingale
will have to lose the rose. Not because the rose is unworthy, but because the journey cannot be
undertaken while one is rooted in a single beloved. The hoopoe's teaching does not deny this
loss. In fact, the beauty of Attar's poem is that it takes losses seriously. The journey is not
portrayed as liberation from constraint; it is portrayed as a willing sacrifice.

Fauré's 'Sanctus' from the Requiem is not lamenting, not harsh, but it is present to grief. It
witnesses the threshold between the life one is leaving and whatever comes after. The music is
gentle because the grief is the grain of a true opening, not a punishment. The nightingale grieves
not because it is weak but because it is awake. To leave the beloved is to undergo a small death.
Fauré's music honors this without asking the griever to deny the beauty of what is being left behind.

**8. Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel*** — *salvation*

The section ends with the nightingale's commitment: I will go. I will leave the rose. I will
follow the path whose end I cannot imagine. This is not achieved through the extinguishing of
desire but through the transformation of desire. The nightingale has learned that it can trust
the longing that moves through it, because the longing is not the rose's but the Simurgh's.

Pärt's *Spiegel im Spiegel* — 'mirror in the mirror' — is a simple, almost austere piece: two
voices (violin and piano), moving in still, infinite space, each reflecting the other. There is
no resolution in the romantic sense; there is no climax or catharsis. Instead, there is a return
to simplicity, to clarity, to the fundamental structure of being that lies beneath all the
complication of particular loves. The nightingale has surrendered the rose, not as a loss but as
a return to the source from which all beauty comes. In that surrender, there is a strange peace,
a salvation that is not escape but return.
