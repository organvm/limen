# Deuteronomy: Devarim — Law Reinterpreted Through Love

Deuteronomy is not a legal code. It is Moses' *hortatory address* to Israel on the eve of
entering the promised land. The entire book is a speech—sometimes didactic, sometimes pleading,
always urgent. The law given at Sinai is now restated, but the frame has fundamentally changed.

The fundamental reinterpretation is stated in Deuteronomy 6:4–5, the Shema:

> Hear, O Israel! The LORD our God, the LORD is One! And you shall love the LORD your God with
> all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your might.

Love is not added to law. Love is revealed as the *ground* of law. Suddenly, covenant is not
obligation imposed but *relationship chosen through love*.

---

## The Hermeneutical Revolution: Love as Key to Law

The law delivered at Sinai (Exodus 20–23) is primarily *imperative*: "You shall not kill. You
shall not steal. You shall not bear false witness." These are commandments imposed by sovereign
decree.

Deuteronomy takes these same laws and reframes them through the logic of love. Why shall you not
steal? Because you love your neighbor, and you understand their property as an extension of their
personhood. Why shall you release slaves in the sabbatical year? "Remember that you were a slave
in the land of Egypt, and the LORD your God redeemed you" (15:15). You keep this law because you
love the stranger with the same love by which God loved you.

The emotional register shifts from *obedience* to *gratitude-driven choice*. The law is not
oppressive; it is the expression of love's logic made concrete.

Consider Deuteronomy 10:17–19:

> For the LORD your God is God of gods and Lord of lords, the great, the mighty, and the
> awesome God, who is not partial and takes no bribe. He executes justice for the fatherless
> and the widow, and loves the sojourner, giving him food and clothing. Love the sojourner
> therefore; for you were sojourners in the land of Egypt.

Justice is not abstract law; justice is the *enactment of love toward the vulnerable*. God loves
the sojourner; therefore Israel must love the sojourner. The law is simply the specific form
that love takes when love encounters need.

---

## The Structure: Pleading as Pedagogy

Deuteronomy moves through distinct emotional registers:

**Chapters 1–4: Wilderness Recapitulation**
Moses recounts the wilderness journey. This is not mere history; it is *testimony*. He is inviting
the people to remember who they are—a people saved by God, led through the wilderness, sustained
by God's provision. Memory becomes the ground for commitment.

**Chapters 5–11: Law Repreached with Love as the Key**
The Ten Commandments are restated (5:4–21). The Shema is given (6:4–5). The people are exhorted
to love God with all their heart, soul, and might. Every subsequent law is explained not as
external obligation but as enactment of love.

**Chapters 12–26: Specific Laws Reframed**
Cultic law (sacrifice, pilgrimage), food law, family law, judicial law, social law—all are
retranslated through the lens of love and remembrance. Why keep this law? Because you love God.
How do you love God? By loving your neighbor, the widow, the orphan, the poor.

**Chapters 27–30: The Choice Set Before Them**
Moses lays out the stakes. "I set before you this day a choice between blessing and curse. A
blessing, if you obey...a curse, if you do not obey" (28:1, 15). By chapter 30, the choice
becomes absolute: "I call heaven and earth to witness against you this day, that I have set
before you life and death, blessing and curse; therefore choose life, that you and your
offspring may live" (30:19).

**Chapters 32–34: Final Blessing and Departure**
Moses sings a song (32:1–43), blesses the tribes (33:1–29), and departs. He ascends Mount Nebo,
sees the promised land, and dies. Joshua is appointed to lead. The book ends not with conquest
but with succession—leadership passing from Moses to Joshua, the torch passing to a new generation.

---

## Why Deuteronomy Is Distinct

Each Torah book has a different theological work to do.

**Genesis** establishes *origins* (creation, ancestry, promise).
**Exodus** establishes *covenant* (revelation, liberation, binding agreement).
**Leviticus** establishes *ritual maintenance* (protocols for living with God).
**Numbers** establishes *consequence* (refusal leads to irreversible judgment).
**Deuteronomy** establishes *choice* (covenant reinterpreted through love).

Only Deuteronomy frames covenant as *decision*. The people are not forced into covenant; they
are *invited* to choose it. They are not commanded to obey; they are *pleaded with* to obey.

Moses is old. He will not enter the land. But before he departs, he makes one final appeal: "I
have set before you life and death, blessing and curse; therefore choose life." The people must
choose. The choice is theirs. But the stakes—life or death, blessing or curse—are total.

---

## Love as Hermeneutical Key

The innovation of Deuteronomy is that it makes *love* the interpretive lens for the entire legal
tradition. This is not romantic love, but *covenantal love*—the love that binds obligation and
sustains relationship.

God loves Israel: "The LORD set his heart in love on your fathers and chose their descendants
after them" (10:15).

Israel must respond with love: "You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart, and with
all your soul, and with all your might."

And this love, when it is genuine, will necessarily issue in how you treat other people. The
greatest commandment (love God) cannot be separated from the second commandment (love your
neighbor). The law is simply the specification of what love looks like in concrete situations.

Why shall you leave gleanings in your field for the poor? Because you love God, and you
understand God's character as one who advocates for the vulnerable. Why shall you release slaves
in the sabbatical year? Because you were slaves in Egypt, and you remember God's liberation.
Memory and love together generate law.

This is revolutionary. The law is no longer external imposition. The law is *love given form*.

---

## The Hortatory Register: Urgency and Pleading

Throughout Deuteronomy, Moses uses direct address: "I set before you." "You shall love." "You
shall choose." The text is not describing law; it is *commanding* in the mode of *pleading*.

This is the emotional register of Deuteronomy: urgency. Moses knows his time is limited. The
people must decide before he dies. The choice cannot be deferred. The stakes are immediate.

Listen to the tone of Deuteronomy 30:15–19:

> See, I have set before you this day life and good, death and evil. If you obey the
> commandments of the LORD your God which I command you this day, by loving the LORD your God,
> by walking in his ways, and by keeping his commandments and his statutes and his ordinances,
> then you shall live and multiply, and the LORD your God will bless you...But if your heart
> turns away, and you will not hear, but are drawn away to worship other gods and serve them, I
> declare to you this day that you shall surely perish...I call heaven and earth to witness
> against you this day, that I have set before you life and death, blessing and curse; therefore
> choose life, that you and your offspring may live.

This is not cool legislation. This is a man at the end of his life, begging his people to choose
rightly. The urgency is palpable.

---

## Why This Matters: Freedom and Obligation

Deuteronomy solves a profound problem: How can covenant be both *binding obligation* and *free
choice*?

Exodus gives covenant as binding obligation—God commanded, Israel must obey. Numbers shows the
consequence of refusing binding obligation—death in the wilderness. But what happens to human
freedom? How can the people genuinely choose if they are already bound?

Deuteronomy answers: through *love*. Covenant is binding because God bound it (that cannot be
undone). But covenant is also *chosen* because love cannot be compelled. God loves Israel; Israel
must *choose* to love God in return. The law is binding, but it is obeyed not from coercion but
from love.

This is the deepest theological claim of Deuteronomy: **Freedom and obligation are not
opposites; they are reconciled through love**. I am bound to you, yet I choose you. I choose
you, yet I am bound to you. These are the same thing when the bond is love.

## The Covenant Model Completed

Genesis establishes *origin*. Exodus establishes *covenant establishment*. Leviticus establishes
*ritual maintenance*. Numbers establishes *consequence*. Deuteronomy completes the arc by making
clear that covenant, after all is said and done, rests on *choice*.

God has chosen Israel. But Israel must *choose* God in return. The choice happens through love.
The love is enacted through law. The law is binding because love is binding, and love is binding
because it is freely chosen.

This is why Moses' final words carry such weight. He has not come to compel; he has come to
plead. The people must choose. Everything—their future, their children's future, their
relationship with God—depends on whether they choose life (obedience through love) or death
(refusal).

And they stand at the edge of the promised land, ready to enter under Joshua's leadership, with
the law in their hearts and the choice before them: Will they choose life?
