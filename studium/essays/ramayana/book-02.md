# Ramayana Ayodhya Kanda: The Boon, the Banishment, the Sandals on the Throne

Ayodhya Kanda is the most intimate book of the Ramayana and the one in which the most is
destroyed. There is no war here, no forest, no demon-king — only a palace, a family, and a
single corrupted desire that brings all of it down. Bala Kanda ended in a wedding; this kanda
opens with a coronation being prepared and turns, in the space of one conversation, into a
fourteen-year exile, a father's death, and a kingdom emptied of its king. The horror of it is
that everyone behaves well. Dasharatha keeps his word though it kills him; Rama obeys his father
without a tremor; Bharata refuses the throne he is handed. The law is kept perfectly — and the
law, kept perfectly, produces a catastrophe. The music here must hold the grief inward and the
dharma audible, and it must refuse, at the end, to resolve. The kanda closes not on a death but
on a pair of sandals set upon an empty throne.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Parry — *I was glad*** — *kingship*

Feeling his age, Dasharatha resolves to consecrate Rama as heir-apparent, and Ayodhya is
transformed overnight: banners, water-pots, garlands, the streets washed and strewn with
flowers, the people half-delirious that their beloved prince will be crowned at dawn. The poem
lets this joy be complete before it takes it away. Parry's *I was glad*, written for a
coronation and opening with that immense upward choral surge on the very first word, is sincere
processional gladness — the whole city's joy gathered into one rising chord. It must be played
without irony, because the people feel none. The shadow lives entirely in our foreknowledge: we
know, as they do not, that the coronation will not come. This is the last unclouded ceremony of
Rama's life, and it exists in the kanda precisely so that we can measure everything against it.

---

**2. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 10: II. Allegro** — *plague*

Kaikeyi is happy at Rama's elevation — she loves him more than her own son. Then her servant
Manthara begins to talk. Rama crowned means Bharata enslaved, means Kaikeyi reduced to a
co-wife's servant, means her line extinguished. The poison works slowly, then all at once; by
the end of the scene Kaikeyi is unrecognizable, lying in the "chamber of wrath," stripped of her
jewels, weaponized against the husband and the prince she loved an hour before. This is
corruption as contagion — one bitter voice infecting a healthy mind until it turns. Shostakovich's
Tenth Symphony scherzo is the most concentrated portrait of malevolent driving force in the
orchestral literature, relentless and vicious, often heard as a portrait of tyranny itself. It
is the whisper that will not stop, the thought that takes over the host. Plague requires
descending motion and dark orchestration; the scherzo is the poison spreading through Kaikeyi
faster than she can resist it.

---

**3. Verdi — *La forza del destino*: Overture** — *fate*

Years before, in a battle, Kaikeyi saved Dasharatha's life, and he granted her two boons, then
left unredeemed. Now Manthara reminds her of them, and she calls them in: Bharata crowned in
Rama's place, and Rama exiled to the forest for fourteen years. The boons are a debt that cannot
be refused — a mechanism set running long ago that now closes with absolute finality. Verdi's
overture opens with the brass hammering its fate-motif and drives through the restless, recurring
theme of a doom that pursues and cannot be outrun. This is exactly the structure of the boons: a
promise made in the past, sprung in the present, irreversible. The catastrophe of Ayodhya Kanda
is not random and not even, strictly, anyone's sudden malice — it is a contract being honored.
Fate requires an inexorable motif and hammered insistence; Verdi gives us a destiny agreed to
long ago, arriving precisely on schedule.

---

**4. Bruckner — Symphony No. 7: II. Adagio** — *grief*

Dasharatha collapses. He begs, he rages, he faints, he offers Kaikeyi anything but this; he
cannot make himself say the words that will banish his son. But a king's word, once given, is
the foundation of dharma — if the king lies, the cosmos itself is unmoored — and so he is
destroyed by the very thing that makes him a king. He must keep his promise though it kills him.
Bruckner's Seventh Adagio, composed in anticipation of Wagner's death and crowned by its great
cymbal-clash of grief before the long subsidence, is the sound of a vast, slow, immovable sorrow.
It does not rage; it sinks. That is Dasharatha: not a man fighting his fate but a man crushed
beneath the weight of his own integrity. Grief requires a slow tempo and an inward line, and
Bruckner gives this grief monumental scale because what is breaking is not only a father's heart
but the keystone of a kingdom.

---

**5. Bach — *Ich habe genug*, BWV 82: I. Aria** — *law*

Rama is told that he is to be exiled rather than crowned, and his response is the moral center of
the kanda and one of the most extraordinary moments in world literature: he feels no grief at
all. Losing a kingdom and gaining a forest are equal to him; what matters is only that his
father's word be kept true. His calm terrifies everyone around him, because it is the calm of a
man wholly identified with dharma rather than with his own fortune. Bach's *Ich habe genug* — "I
have enough" — is the serenity of complete acceptance, a man laying down the world without
struggle, oboe and voice in unhurried, contented surrender. It is not resignation and not grief;
it is peace. Law requires formal clarity and architecture, and Rama's acceptance is law made
flesh: the self dissolved so completely into obedience that the loss of everything produces no
disturbance at all. Bach's aria is that impossible tranquility, scored — and it should sound,
against the surrounding grief, almost frightening in its stillness.

---

**6. Strauss — *Morgen!*, Op. 27 No. 4** — *desire*

Rama tries to leave Sita behind in the palace, safe. She refuses — in one of the epic's great
speeches, she argues that a wife's place is beside her husband, that the forest with him is
heaven and the palace without him is exile, that she will walk gladly on thorns if he is there.
It is not erotic desire but its deepest form: the longing to be where the beloved is, to share
his fate entirely. Strauss's *Morgen!* — "and tomorrow the sun will shine again, and on the path
we will take, we will be united" — is the most luminous song of quiet union in the repertoire,
violin and voice meeting over a hushed accompaniment as if the world had narrowed to two people
and one shared road. Desire requires chromatic longing and deferred resolution; here the longing
is for togetherness itself, and Strauss resolves it into the radiant certainty of Sita's choice.
Wherever he goes, she goes. The forest, to her, is not exile; it is marriage.

---

**7. Beethoven — *Coriolan* Overture** — *wrath*

Lakshmana, Rama's brother and shadow, erupts. He wants to take up arms, depose the doddering
king, kill whoever stands in the way, and crown Rama by force — the injustice is so naked that
violence seems the only honest answer. Rama calms him: dharma is not served by a coup. Lakshmana's
rage is righteous and useless, and he swallows it to follow his brother into exile instead.
Beethoven's *Coriolan* Overture is precisely this — the portrait of a warrior's fury with no
permissible outlet, the C-minor agitation surging against a restraint that finally grinds it
down to silence. Coriolanus, like Lakshmana, is a man whose wrath is real and whose action is
forbidden. Wrath requires rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony; Beethoven gives us the anger
that must be mastered rather than indulged, the fury of the loyal brother who would burn the
kingdom down and is told, instead, to pack for the forest.

---

**8. Górecki — Symphony No. 3: I. Lento** — *supplication*

Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana leave in Sumantra's chariot, and the entire city pours out after them,
weeping, begging Rama not to go, following the chariot until they can follow no farther. To
escape them, Rama travels through the night and slips across the river while the people sleep on
the far bank, so that they wake to find him gone. This is supplication made communal — a whole
population pleading with one man to stay, and the man refusing because he must. Górecki's Third
Symphony, the "Symphony of Sorrowful Songs," opens with a vast, slow, rising canon of strings:
grief made collective, a sorrow sung by many voices as one. Supplication requires a suspended
cadence and an exposed melodic line; Górecki gives the people's plea the quality of a prayer that
goes unanswered — a whole city's voice raised, and the chariot receding into the dark regardless.

---

**9. Mahler — Symphony No. 5: IV. Adagietto** — *exile*

They cross the Ganga with Guha the boatman, leave the chariot and Sumantra behind, put on bark
garments, and walk into the Dandaka forest. The threshold is exact and irreversible: on one side
the kingdom, on the other fourteen years of wilderness. This is the kanda's title-image — the
moment the banishment stops being a sentence and becomes a life. Mahler's Adagietto, scored for
strings and harp alone, is the most concentrated exile-grief in the repertoire: a slow, aching
line that walks forward without resolving, beautiful and bereft at once. It was held in reserve
for exactly this scene — not the death of a father, not the rage of a brother, but the quiet
crossing of the threshold into the years of wandering. Exile requires wandering tonality and
open intervals; the Adagietto walks the three of them across the river and into the trees, and
the strings never once look back at the kingdom they have left.

---

**10. Wagner — *Götterdämmerung*: Siegfried's Funeral March** — *grief*

Sumantra returns to Ayodhya alone. Dasharatha, unable to bear the loss of Rama, dies that night —
and as he dies he remembers why. Years before, hunting in the dark, he killed by accident a young
ascetic drawing water, mistaking the sound for an elephant, and the boy's blind parents cursed
him to die one day grieving for a son, as they grieved for theirs. The death is grief and the
closing of an old account at once. Wagner's Funeral March from *Götterdämmerung* is the supreme
orchestral cortège, the brass intoning the hero's themes one last time over the tread of the
death-march, monumental and final. Grief requires a slow tempo and an inward line, but a king's
death also requires public weight, and Wagner supplies both — the private sorrow of a father who
died of love and the state funeral of a monarch whose kingdom is now leaderless. The curse is
paid. The king is gone. The throne is empty.

---

**11. Brahms — *Ein deutsches Requiem*: II. "Denn alles Fleisch, es ist wie Gras"** — *sacrifice*

Bharata, summoned home, learns that his mother schemed to win him the crown, that it cost his
father's life and his brother's exile. He is horrified. He denounces Kaikeyi, refuses the throne
absolutely, performs Dasharatha's funeral rites, and then marches to the forest to beg Rama to
return and rule. Rama refuses — the exile must be honored — and Bharata gives up his own claim
entirely rather than profit from a crime. Brahms's "Denn alles Fleisch, es ist wie Gras" ("For
all flesh is as grass") is a slow, immense processional in triple meter, a funeral march that
becomes a renunciation: the dead are mourned and the living are stripped of their illusions of
permanence. Sacrifice requires ritual weight and processional build; Brahms gives Bharata's
refusal the gravity of a man laying down a crown he was never willing to wear, walking behind his
father's bier into a renunciation as total as his brother's exile.

---

**12. Elgar — *Enigma Variations*: IX. "Nimrod"** — *memory*

Rama gives Bharata his golden sandals. Bharata carries them home, sets them on the throne, and
rules for fourteen years not as king but as the sandals' regent — governing from the village of
Nandigrama, wearing bark like an ascetic, holding the kingdom in trust for an absent man whose
footwear is the only sovereign present. It is one of the most haunting images in the epic: rule
as pure fidelity, the throne occupied by a memory. Elgar's "Nimrod" is the music of noble absence
held in honor — a slow, swelling elegy that is also a kind of vow, grief transfigured into
steadfastness. Memory requires nostalgic recall and faded recapitulation; "Nimrod" gives the
sandals their due. Not triumph, not mourning exactly, but the long faithful holding of a place
for someone who is gone — the kingdom kept warm against a return that is still thirteen years
away. The kanda ends here, on a vigil rather than a death.

## The dominant arc of Ayodhya Kanda

```
coronation-joy → the poison → the claimed boons → the king's collapse → dharmic acceptance →
shared exile → swallowed wrath → the people's plea → the threshold crossed →
the king's death → the refused throne → the sandals' vigil
```

Ayodhya Kanda is the Ramayana's book of **exile** — not the forest exile that follows, but the
act of banishment itself, traced through everyone it touches: the prince who accepts it, the wife
who insists on sharing it, the brother who follows it, the father who dies of it, the brother who
refuses to profit from it. Its terrible coherence is that it is driven entirely by people keeping
faith. Kaikeyi's boons are honored, Dasharatha's word is kept, Rama's obedience is absolute,
Bharata's renunciation is total — and the sum of all this fidelity is catastrophe. The kanda
refuses to let us locate a simple villain (even Kaikeyi is a loving mother poisoned into one) and
refuses, at the close, to resolve: it ends not with the death of the king but with the image of
his son ruling on behalf of an absent brother, sandals on the throne, the whole kingdom waiting.
You cannot feel the long ache of the forest years, or the fury of the war to come, unless you
have first felt — here — exactly what was given up, and how perfectly, and at what cost.
