# Ramayana Kishkindha Kanda: The Pact, the Arrow, the Monsoon, the Search

Kishkindha Kanda is the book where the grief of the empty forest is converted into a machine for
return. The man who, at the end of Aranya Kanda, could only rave at the trees becomes here the
ally of a king and the commander of a search that spans the world. He meets Hanuman, the perfect
minister and the truest heart of the epic; he swears friendship with the exiled monkey-king
Sugriva at a sacred fire; he kills Vali to restore Sugriva's throne — the most morally vexed act
in the whole poem — and he waits out a long monsoon while his new ally, crowned and comfortable,
forgets the vow entirely. Then the rains end, the armies are summoned, and the search parties go
out to the four directions. The kanda is bent entirely toward the launching of the quest, and it
ends with Hanuman swelling on the southern shore to leap the sea. But it refuses to be simply
triumphant: the alliance is sealed with an arrow shot from hiding, and the poem will not let us
forget it. Score the turn toward return — and score the wound at its center.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Vaughan Williams — *Fantasia on a Theme by Thomas Tallis*** — *revelation*

Wandering south, the brothers reach Rishyamukha, where Sugriva, the exiled monkey-king, sends his
minister Hanuman to assess the strangers. Hanuman approaches in the form of a mendicant and
speaks — and his speech is so perfect, so learned, so exactly judged, that Rama marvels: here is
eloquence itself, the ideal servant, devotion that has not yet declared itself but is already
total. It is a recognition scene, Rama meeting the being who will become the truest heart of the
epic. Vaughan Williams's Tallis Fantasia, scored for divided strings that answer one another
across a luminous space, is the sound of something sacred recognized — antique, radiant,
devotional without dogma. Revelation requires stillness, chant, and verticality; the Fantasia
gives the meeting of Rama and Hanuman its proper register of quiet, luminous recognition. The
greatest devotee in the tradition has just walked onstage, and the music knows it before the plot
does.

---

**2. Handel — *Music for the Royal Fireworks*: I. Overture** — *law*

Hanuman brings Rama to Sugriva, and the two make a formal compact around a sacred fire, circling
it together: Rama will kill Vali, who has stolen Sugriva's kingdom and wife, and Sugriva will
marshal the monkey nations to find Sita. It is a treaty, a sworn alliance, law made binding by
ritual. Handel's *Music for the Royal Fireworks* overture is ceremonial law at its most
architecturally grand — the dotted rhythms of the French overture, the brass and timpani of a
state occasion, formality as the very substance of the music. Law requires formal clarity,
repetition, and architecture; Handel gives the fire-pact the weight of a treaty between
sovereigns, the alliance that organizes everything to come rendered as ceremonial structure. From
this fire forward, the recovery of Sita is no longer one man's grief but a contract between
nations — and like all contracts in this kanda, it will be tested by how it is kept.

---

**3. Dvořák — Symphony No. 9 (*From the New World*): II. Largo** — *exile*

Sugriva tells his story. His brother Vali, the mighty monkey-king, wrongly believed Sugriva had
betrayed him, seized his kingdom and his wife, and drove him into hiding on the one mountain
Vali's curse forbids him to climb. Sugriva lives in fear, a king reduced to a fugitive, mirroring
Rama's own exile back to him — which is part of why the alliance feels fated. Dvořák's "New
World" Largo, with its great cor anglais melody, is the most famous music of exile and
homesickness in the repertoire: the longing of one driven far from home, tender and bereft. Exile
requires wandering tonality and open intervals; the Largo gives Sugriva's dispossession its ache —
the wronged brother in hiding, longing for the kingdom and the life that were taken from him,
recognizing in Rama a fellow exile and binding his fate to his. Two banished kings, each holding
the other's remedy.

---

**4. Prokofiev — *Romeo and Juliet*: Dance of the Knights (Montagues and Capulets)** — *wrath*

To draw Vali out, Sugriva challenges him to single combat. The two enormous monkey-kings meet and
grapple — a fight of brother against brother, so evenly matched that Rama, hidden, cannot at first
tell them apart. It is wrath between kin, the most dangerous kind: two titanic forces locked in a
combat neither can win outright. Prokofiev's "Dance of the Knights" is the most implacable music
of armored menace in the repertoire — the heavy, stamping theme of two houses' enmity grinding
forward with brutal weight. Wrath requires rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony; Prokofiev's
relentless tread is the duel of Vali and Sugriva, two near-equal powers crashing together. But
the fight will not be decided by either of them. It will be decided by an arrow from the trees —
which is what makes the next scene the kanda's moral wound.

---

**5. Mahler — Symphony No. 9: IV. Adagio** — *fate*

As the brothers grapple, Rama shoots Vali from hiding. Vali falls — and with his dying breath he
reproaches Rama bitterly: by what dharma does a prince kill a stranger from concealment, in
another's quarrel, striking down one who had no fight with him? It is the most debated act in the
epic, and the poem does not resolve it cleanly. Rama answers (Vali had wronged his brother;
monkeys may be hunted; the deed serves a larger justice), Vali half-accepts, and the arrow
remains a shadow on the hero's honor. The killing had to happen for the alliance to hold — fate
as dark necessity — but it costs something never quite repaid. Mahler's Ninth Adagio is the
supreme music of farewell and the wound of mortality, a long, broken, heart-stopping leave-taking
that fades into silence at the edge of dissolution. Fate requires an inexorable motif and hammered
insistence, but here fate wears the face of a dying king's reproach; Mahler gives Vali his death
the way the poem does — necessary, sorrowful, and not without a stain on the one who caused it.

---

**6. Johann Strauss II — *Wine, Women and Song* (Wein, Weib und Gesang), Op. 333** — *comedy*

Restored to his throne and his wives, Sugriva promptly forgets everything. He sinks into the
pleasures of the recovered kingdom, drinking and reveling through the season, and entirely
neglects his sworn promise to find Sita while Rama waits in the forest. It is a lapse almost comic
in its human ordinariness: the rescued king, drunk on relief and luxury, letting the debt slide.
Johann Strauss's *Wine, Women and Song* — a glittering, indulgent Viennese waltz whose very title
names Sugriva's distraction — is courtly pleasure as gorgeous excess. Comedy requires dance rhythm
and courtly exaggeration; the waltz gives Sugriva's dissipation its exact color: not villainy but
the seductive forgetfulness of a man who has gotten what he wanted and lost sight of what he owes.
Lakshmana will have to come, furious, to remind him — and the contrast between this waltz and the
grief on either side of it is the kanda's one breath of dark comedy.

---

**7. Chopin — Prelude No. 15 in D-flat major (*Raindrop*)** — *grief*

The rainy season comes, and with it the kanda's famous interlude: Rama, unable to act while the
rains make war and travel impossible, sits in a cave through the long wet months and grieves for
Sita. The lightning reminds him of her, the rain is of a piece with his tears, every swollen river
and dripping tree becomes an emblem of his longing. It is grief stretched across a season, waiting
as a form of suffering. Chopin's "Raindrop" prelude, with its endlessly repeating single note
beneath a melody that darkens and returns, is the most literal and the most inward music of rain
and melancholy in the repertoire — the drop that will not stop, the sorrow that has nowhere to go.
Grief requires a slow tempo and an inward line; Chopin gives Rama's monsoon-vigil its precise
texture: the patient, dripping, inconsolable wait for a season to end so that the search can
finally begin.

---

**8. Beethoven — Symphony No. 7 in A major: II. Allegretto** — *return*

Roused at last — Lakshmana's anger reminding Sugriva of his vow — the monkey-king summons the
nations, armies beyond counting, and sends them out to the four quarters of the earth to find
Sita, each party given a direction and a deadline. The lone grief of the empty hut has become a
planetary search: this is the machinery of return assembling and setting out. Beethoven's Seventh
Allegretto, with its inexorable processional tread building from a single rhythm into a vast,
measured march of many voices, is the sound of a great purposeful movement gathering and going
forward. Return requires circular form and a recognition motif; the Allegretto's relentless,
accumulating procession is the search-armies marching out to the horizons, the whole world
mobilized to bring one woman home. From here the epic's direction is fixed: everything now moves
toward recovery.

---

**9. Strauss — *Also sprach Zarathustra*: Sonnenaufgang (Sunrise)** — *ascent*

The southern party, Hanuman among them, reaches the edge of the sea — and learns from the old
vulture Sampati, Jatayu's brother, that Sita is held in Lanka, across a hundred leagues of ocean.
The party despairs: who can cross such a gulf? Then Hanuman, reminded of his divine parentage and
his own forgotten power, begins to swell — growing vast upon the mountain, gathering himself for
the impossible leap that will open the next kanda. It is the kanda's final image and its upward
thrust: the servant rising into the hero he was always meant to be. Strauss's "Sunrise" is the
most elemental music of rising in the repertoire — the low rumble, the climbing trumpet, the
blazing arrival of full orchestra and organ, dawn breaking over the world. Ascent requires a
rising sequence and luminous orchestration; Strauss gives Hanuman's swelling on the shore the
exact shape of a sunrise, and ends Kishkindha Kanda not on the ground but on the verge of flight.

## The dominant arc of Kishkindha Kanda

```
the perfect minister → the sworn pact → the exiled brother → the duel of kin →
the arrow from hiding → the forgotten vow → the monsoon vigil → the search dispatched →
the hero rising to leap
```

Kishkindha Kanda is the Ramayana's book of **return** — not the homecoming itself, which is many
kandas away, but the building of the machine that will make return possible. Aranya Kanda ended
with a man alone and bereft; this kanda ends with that man commanding the armies of the world and
with his greatest servant rising on the shore to leap an ocean. The conversion of private grief
into collective purpose is the kanda's whole work. But the poem refuses to let the turn be clean.
The alliance that powers the return is sealed by the killing of Vali — Rama's arrow shot from
concealment into another's quarrel — and the Ramayana, unlike a lesser epic, does not file the
deed away as justified and move on. It lets Vali reproach the hero with his dying breath and lets
the reproach stand, a permanent question mark over the most righteous man in the poem. That is the
mark of the kanda's seriousness: even as it builds the engine of return, it insists that the
engine was built at a cost. The book ends on a sunrise — but a sunrise that the listener has
earned by passing through a death no one quite answers for.
