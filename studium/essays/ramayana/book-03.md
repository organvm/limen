# Ramayana Aranya Kanda: The Golden Deer, the Abduction, the Empty Forest

Aranya Kanda is the book where desire — wanting what is not yours to have — becomes the hinge of
the cosmos. Two desires drive it. Shurpanakha, the rakshasi, wants Rama and is mutilated for
pressing her claim; Ravana, her brother and king of the demons, wants Sita and tears the world
apart to take her. Between these two appetites the kanda moves from a pastoral peace that has
become a genuine way of life, through a sudden eruption of violence, into the most famous trap in
the epic: a golden deer that is a demon, a cry for help in the husband's voice, a protective line
crossed at the worst possible moment, a hut left undefended. Its terrible lesson is that desire
indulged — even a single demon's lust — can collapse an entire order. The music must keep the
early forest luminous so the rupture lands, must make the abduction sound like overreaching crime
rather than heroic flight, and must give Jatayu — the one creature who does the right thing and is
destroyed for it — the full weight of doomed sacrifice. The kanda ends not in rescue but in
absence: Rama returning to an empty hut, the forest silent. Score the silence.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Vaughan Williams — Symphony No. 5 in D major: I. Preludio** — *exile*

The three exiles move through the Dandaka forest, visiting the ashrams of sages, living simply,
the years passing in a kind of grave pastoral. The poem dwells on the beauty of the wilderness
and the holiness of the hermits — it is exile, but exile that has become a way of life, even a
kind of peace. Vaughan Williams's Fifth Symphony, written in the depths of war but radiating an
almost unbearable serenity, opens with horns over a quiet modal landscape: English pastoral
raised to the visionary. Exile requires wandering tonality and open intervals; the Preludio
wanders through an open, luminous country without urgency, which is exactly the register of the
forest years before Shurpanakha arrives. The listener should feel settled here — at home in the
exile — so that the rupture to come registers as the loss of a peace that had become real.

---

**2. Allegri — *Miserere mei, Deus*** — *supplication*

The forest hermits come to Rama with a plea: the rakshasas are defiling their sacrifices,
devouring ascetics, making holy life impossible. They show him the bones of the sages already
eaten. Rama vows to protect them — the vow that will, by the kanda's own logic, bring the entire
rakshasa world down on his household. This is supplication in its sacred form, the defenseless
holy appealing to the one who can defend them. Allegri's *Miserere* — the penitential psalm sung
in alternating plainchant and soaring polyphony, the treble line arching impossibly high — is the
sound of pure plea raised toward a power above. Supplication requires a suspended cadence and an
exposed melodic line; the *Miserere* gives the hermits' petition its proper register of trembling
holiness, a voice reaching up out of vulnerability toward a protection it cannot compel, only ask
for. Rama grants it, and in granting it sets the catastrophe in motion.

---

**3. Saint-Saëns — *Samson et Dalila*: "Mon cœur s'ouvre à ta voix"** — *desire*

Ravana's sister Shurpanakha sees Rama and is overwhelmed with desire. She takes a beautiful form
and propositions him directly, offers to devour Sita so as to have him to herself, presses her
claim with mounting heat. This is desire as appetite — frank, predatory, refusing the answer
"no." Saint-Saëns's "Mon cœur s'ouvre à ta voix" ("My heart opens to your voice") is the great
seduction aria of the repertoire, Delilah's voluptuous chromatic line winding around its target
with total confidence of conquest. Desire requires chromatic longing and deferred resolution;
here the longing is rapacious rather than tender, and the aria's gorgeous, encircling melody is
exactly Shurpanakha's pitch — a desire that assumes it will be satisfied and turns murderous when
refused. The refusal, and the mutilation that follows it, is what sets the rest of the kanda
running: a wounded rakshasi running to her brother with a story about a beautiful woman.

---

**4. Walton — Symphony No. 1 in B-flat minor: I. Allegro assai** — *wrath*

Rebuffed, and turning on Sita, Shurpanakha is seized by Lakshmana, who cuts off her nose and
ears. Humiliated and bleeding, she runs to her brother Khara, who sends fourteen thousand
rakshasas to annihilate the three exiles — and Rama, alone, destroys the entire host, Khara with
it. This is the first true eruption of wrath in the kanda: a mutilation answered by an army, an
army answered by a single archer. Walton's First Symphony first movement is one of the most
ferociously driven things in the orchestral literature — relentless rhythmic pressure, grinding
dissonance, an energy that never relaxes. Wrath requires rhythmic pressure and unresolved
harmony; Walton's Allegro assai is sustained combat-fury without release, one man against a horde
and the horde breaking on him. But the wrath has a cost not yet visible: Shurpanakha, twice
wronged in her own eyes, runs next to the one rakshasa who can answer Rama — Ravana.

---

**5. Stravinsky — *The Firebird*: Dance of the Firebird** — *metamorphosis*

Inflamed by Shurpanakha's account of Sita's beauty, Ravana plots her abduction. He compels the
demon Maricha to take the form of a marvelous golden deer, jeweled and shimmering, and let it
stray near the hut. Sita, enchanted, begs Rama to catch it for her. The deer is a shape-shift — a
demon wearing beauty as a lure, the trap disguised as a gift. Stravinsky's "Dance of the
Firebird" is the supreme orchestral portrait of a magical, shimmering, not-quite-real creature:
iridescent, darting, impossibly bright, gorgeous and uncanny at once. Metamorphosis requires
changing orchestral color and fluid harmony; the Firebird's music shimmers between registers
exactly as the deer shimmers between animal and demon. The beauty is the bait. Sita wants it,
Rama goes after it, and the trap is sprung the instant the deer is desired — desire again, now
displaced onto an object, but with the same fatal mechanism.

---

**6. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 5 in E minor: I. Andante – Allegro con anima** — *fate*

Rama chases the deer deep into the forest and finally shoots it; as it dies, Maricha cries out in
Rama's own voice — "Ah, Sita! Ah, Lakshmana!" — a perfect imitation of a man in mortal danger.
Sita, panicking, orders Lakshmana to go to Rama's aid. Lakshmana, who knows it is a trick,
refuses — until Sita, frantic, accuses him of wanting Rama dead so as to have her himself, and he
leaves in wounded fury, drawing a protective line around the hut and forbidding her to cross it.
Every link is a small, comprehensible human choice, and together they form an unbreakable
mechanism. Tchaikovsky's Fifth opens with the brooding "fate" motto that returns, transformed,
to govern every movement — a destiny-theme that stalks the whole symphony. Fate requires an
inexorable motif and hammered insistence; the Fifth's motto is the trap closing one click at a
time: the deer, the cry, the accusation, the line drawn, the hut left open.

---

**7. Scriabin — *The Poem of Ecstasy*, Op. 54** — *desire*

With the hut undefended, Ravana comes — first as a wandering ascetic begging alms, then, when
Sita steps across the line to give him food, in his true towering form. He seizes her, mounts his
flying chariot, and carries her screaming into the sky toward Lanka. This is the kanda's
catastrophe and the desire that drives the entire epic: Ravana wants Sita and will unmake the
world to possess her. Scriabin's *Poem of Ecstasy* is desire as consuming overreach — a vast,
overheated, chromatic surge toward a climax it pursues with megalomaniac intensity, the
trumpet-theme straining ever upward toward an apotheosis of pure wanting. Desire requires
chromatic longing and deferred resolution; Scriabin gives us desire that has lost all restraint,
the appetite of a being who recognizes no law but his own craving. This is not love. It is
possession as cosmic crime — and the whole remaining epic is the cost of it. That the same force,
desire, scores both Shurpanakha's proposition and Ravana's seizure is the point: the kanda is a
study in what appetite, ungoverned, does to the world.

---

**8. Strauss — *Tod und Verklärung* (Death and Transfiguration), Op. 24** — *sacrifice*

The aged vulture-king Jatayu, an old friend of Dasharatha, hears Sita's screams and hurls himself
at Ravana's chariot — one ancient bird against the demon-king. He fights heroically, breaks the
chariot, tears at Ravana, and is at last cut down, his wings severed, left dying on the forest
floor. He is the one being in the kanda who does the right thing at the right moment, and he is
destroyed for it. Strauss's *Death and Transfiguration* traces exactly this arc: the struggle of
a dying man, the fevered battle against the end, and finally the transfiguration-theme rising in
radiance as the spirit is released. Sacrifice requires ritual weight and an immolation build;
Strauss gives Jatayu's doomed defense its full tragic dignity — not a futile death but a noble
one, the old hero spending his life in the single act of resistance the kanda allows, dying so
that someone may later learn the truth from him.

---

**9. Purcell — *Dido and Aeneas*: "When I am laid in earth"** — *grief*

Borne helpless through the air, Sita does the one thing she can: seeing a band of monkeys on a
mountain far below, she gathers her jewels in her garment and lets them fall — a trail of tokens
scattered for whoever may come to find her. It is grief made into the only possible action, a
captive woman marking her passage with the last things she owns. Purcell's "When I am laid in
earth," Dido's lament over its slow descending ground bass, is the supreme song of a woman's
sorrow at being torn from love: "Remember me, but ah! forget my fate." Grief requires a slow
tempo and an inward line; the lament's inexorable falling bass is the jewels falling, the descent
of everything Sita has, released into the air as the one message she can send. She does not
despair, and that is what makes the scene unbearable. She drops her rings and trusts that they
will be found.

---

**10. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 6 in B minor (*Pathétique*): IV. Adagio lamentoso** — *grief*

Rama returns from the deer-hunt to find the hut empty, Sita gone, no answer in the silent forest.
His grief breaks every restraint he has shown until now: he raves, he searches, he questions the
trees and the river and the deer, he half-loses his mind. The most composed man in the epic — the
man whose serenity at his own exile terrified everyone — comes apart. Tchaikovsky's *Pathétique*
finale is the most desolate movement in the symphonic literature, a slow descent into a grief
that finds no consolation, the strings sinking finally into near-silence and stopping. Grief
requires a slow tempo and an inward line, and the Adagio lamentoso refuses the comfort of any
resolution at all. This is the bottom of the kanda — not the abduction itself but the discovery
of it, the empty hut, the collapse of Rama's superhuman calm into a human being who has lost
everything and does not yet know where it has gone. Two grief-tracks meet here: Sita's, falling
through the air, and Rama's, on the ground beneath where she fell.

---

**11. Messiaen — *Quatuor pour la fin du temps*: V. "Louange à l'Éternité de Jésus"** — *revelation*

Searching, the brothers find Jatayu dying. With his last breath the old vulture tells them what
happened: Ravana, king of Lanka, has carried Sita to the south. Then he dies, and Rama cremates
him with the rites due a father. Out of the kanda's lowest moment comes its first light — a
direction, a name, the thread the whole rest of the epic will follow. Messiaen's "Louange à
l'Éternité de Jésus," the slow movement for cello and piano at the heart of the Quartet for the
End of Time, is a single endless line of unearthly stillness — a praise that seems to come from
outside time itself. Revelation requires stillness, chant, and verticality; Messiaen gives the
dying Jatayu's testimony the quality of sacred disclosure, truth spoken from the threshold of
death. The bird's last words are the kanda's gift to everything that follows: now Rama knows
where to go. The search can begin.

## The dominant arc of Aranya Kanda

```
pastoral exile → the hermits' plea → Shurpanakha's appetite → the eruption of wrath →
the shimmering lure → the trap closing → Ravana's seizure → Jatayu's doomed stand →
the falling jewels → the empty hut → the dying bird's revelation
```

Aranya Kanda is the Ramayana's book of **desire** — disordered desire, wanting-what-is-not-yours,
traced from a rakshasi's proposition to a demon-king's abduction and shown, at every step, to be
the force that collapses order. The kanda is built so that desire and its consequences rhyme: the
same force scores Shurpanakha's lust and Ravana's, and both end in catastrophe. Around that
central appetite the poem arranges its other forces with great care — the pastoral exile we are
made to love, the holy plea we hear Rama answer, the wrath that mutilation provokes, the
shape-shifting lure, the fate-mechanism of the false cry, and finally the two griefs (the captive
woman's and the bereft husband's) that meet across the empty forest. It is the engine-room of the
whole epic: everything after this — the alliance, the leap, the war — exists because, in this
book, one being wanted what he could not have and took it. The kanda ends not with rescue but
with a direction. Out of the silence of the empty hut, a dying bird points south.
