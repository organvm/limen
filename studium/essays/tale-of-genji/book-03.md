# Utsusemi: The Shell of the Locust
## A music arc for *The Tale of Genji*, Chapter 3

*The Tale of Genji*'s third chapter is the shortest and tightest in the early sequence: a single night's pursuit, a single evasion, a single abandoned robe. Genji, aided by the boy Kogimi, sneaks into Utsusemi's house, spies on her playing Go, and attempts to surprise her — but she catches the scent of his robes and flees, leaving only her outer garment behind. The chapter is a comedy of errors that keeps a straight face: the wrong woman, the raised alarm, the elderly servant with poor eyesight, the poem that substitutes for the person. But it is also the chapter where the novel discovers its central erotic figure: the trace, the remnant, the shape left behind when the living thing escapes. The title "Shell of the Locust" — the discarded exoskeleton after the insect has flown — is the novel's first explicit statement that absence may be more powerful than presence, that what Genji ultimately desires is not the woman herself but the form she leaves behind.

Scoring this chapter means following its arc from stealth through comedy through frustration to the strange tenderness of its final exchange. Debussy opens with veiled movement; Rameau gives the theatrical comedy of the Go-game voyeurism; Scriabin provides the febrile intensity of desire that has lost its object; Debussy returns for the mist of the poem; Fauré gives the unheard voice of the woman; Bach closes with the ritualized devotion of the fetishist. The chapter is light in tone but heavy in implication: it is the novel's first treatment of erotic substitution, a theme that will run through all fifty-four chapters.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Debussy — *Préludes*, Book I: 'Voiles'**

The chapter opens with Kogimi, Utsusemi's young brother, agreeing to help Genji. Their midnight approach is stealthy, veiled, conducted in darkness. Debussy's 'Voiles' is a whole-tone prelude that drifts without tonal gravity, suspended in a medium that is neither air nor water. It captures the quality of the chapter's first movement — figures moving through darkness, intentions concealed behind screens, a world where knowledge is always partial and mediated.

**2. Rameau — *Platée*: Ouverture**

Genji spies on Utsusemi and her stepdaughter playing Go. The scene is one of the novel's most frequently illustrated: the two women absorbed in their game, the concealed observer, the lamp illuminating their profiles. Rameau's Platée Overture is theatrical, slightly absurd court music — the opera is about a marsh nymph who is tricked into believing Jupiter loves her. The parallel is exact: Genji is the concealed observer, the women are absorbed in their world, and the whole situation has the quality of an operetta whose participants do not know they are in one.

**3. Scriabin — Piano Sonata No. 4 in F-sharp major, Op. 30: I. Andante**

Utsusemi catches Genji's distinctive scent, recognizes him, and flees — leaving only her robe behind. Genji picks it up, presses it to his face. Scriabin's Fourth Sonata first movement is desire music of a specific kind: not the suspended longing of Tristan but a reaching that is already turning inward, becoming its own object. The Andante has an almost febrile intensity, a chromatic interiority that matches Genji's condition: he holds the robe, breathes the vanished scent, and the woman herself is already gone.

**4. Debussy — *Préludes*, Book II: 'Brouillards'**

Genji composes the poem that gives the chapter its name: the cicada's empty shell. Debussy's 'Brouillards' ('Mists') is a prelude about forms that dissolve as you approach them — a melody that keeps losing its outline in harmonic fog. It is the sound of something that was present and is now only its trace, exactly the register of Utsusemi's robe and the poem that mourns its emptiness. The piece also introduces the idea of transformation — the mists that obscure forms also allow new forms to emerge — which is the chapter's quiet philosophical content.

**5. Fauré — *Requiem*, Op. 48: 'Agnus Dei'**

Utsusemi copies a poem by Lady Ise in reply: it speaks of sleeves wet with tears, of love that must remain secret. This is the chapter's emotional revelation — the woman who resisted is also the woman who feels. Fauré's Agnus Dei is a piece about sacrifice that asks for rest, not vindication. It is not the Pie Jesu (used in Chapter 1 for the Kiritsubo Lady's death scene) but the communion text, quieter, more resigned. It gives the unheard music of Utsusemi's interior world — a woman who has made a difficult choice and may regret it, but who has preserved her honor.

**6. Bach — Cello Suite No. 1 in G major, BWV 1007: Prélude**

Genji keeps the robe, returns to it night after night, pressing it for its vanished scent. The chapter ends with desire converted into ritual. Bach's Cello Prélude is an arpeggiated journey through the G major scale — a piece that finds order and resolution through sustained focus on a single instrument. It gives the chapter's strange final note: not tragedy, not triumph, but a settled, almost monastic devotion to an object that substitutes for a person. The shell of the locust has become a relic.

## The Dominant Arc

Utsusemi moves from stealth through comedy through frustrated desire to the tenderness of absence. Debussy's veils give the atmosphere of the pursuit; Rameau gives the theatrical comedy of the voyeur; Scriabin gives the fever of desire that has just lost its object; Debussy returns with mists and dissolution; Fauré gives the woman's lament; and Bach gives the strange peace of erotic substitution. The dominant force is desire — but desire that has discovered it may prefer the trace to the thing itself.
