# The Cicada Shell: The Woman Who Sheds Herself to Escape
## A music arc for *The Tale of Genji*, Chapter 3

*Utsusemi* is the shortest of the early chapters of *The Tale of Genji* and the most perfectly shaped. Genji, still smarting from the refusal he met in the previous chapter, returns by night to take the governor's wife again. She senses him coming through the dark of the house, rises, slips off a single thin summer robe, and steals away — leaving the garment behind like the empty husk a cicada sheds when it molts and flies. Unwilling to leave empty-handed, Genji lies instead with her young stepdaughter, Nokiba no Ogi, taking the substitute almost without deciding to; and he carries off the discarded robe as his only trophy, writing a poem on the cicada-shell image that gives both the chapter and the woman their name. The whole meaning of the chapter is concentrated in that central image: escape by self-shedding. What is scored here is the woman who survives desire by molting out of its reach, and the man left holding the husk.

Scoring this chapter means refusing two temptations, the same two the arc has refused from the start, but adapted to a chapter that is essentially a miniature. The first is Japonisme — the reach for atmospheric "Easternness," koto-flavored exoticism, anything that substitutes the sound of a culture for the precision of a scene. Murasaki Shikibu is writing the psychology of evasion and substitution with a sophistication that has no cultural ceiling; the music must meet it from the other direction. The second temptation, here, is wrong-scale grief: Utsusemi's sorrow is small and self-contained, a woman managing a diminished life, not a death. To reach for the vast valedictory Adagios that scored the Kiritsubo Lady's dying would crush this chamber chapter flat. Mahler's Ninth belonged to a death; nothing dies in *Utsusemi* but a possibility.

So the register must be kept hushed, nocturnal, miniature: a spied game of Go, a robe slipped off in the dark, a substitute woman taken almost absentmindedly, a keepsake that is only an emptied skin. The governing force is not transformation toward something but transformation as evasion — Utsusemi becomes an absence in order to keep her self. This is a chamber chapter, and it must be scored in chamber scale: water-music, keyboard miniatures, things that recede rather than declare. The emotional truth is that Genji's desire is answered with vanishing, and that the vanishing is the woman's only victory.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Scriabin — *Piano Sonata No. 4 in F-sharp major*, Op. 30**

Utsusemi's refusal in the previous chapter has done what refusal always does to a man like Genji: it has made her the only thing he wants. Scriabin's Fourth Sonata is desire conceived as upward flight — a languid, yearning first movement that breaks into an ecstatic, accelerating pursuit, the whole compact two-movement piece reaching toward a star it names but never quite touches. It is the sound of fixation that mistakes itself for love, the will to possess accelerating precisely because the object keeps its distance.

This is the right opening because Genji is not returning out of love. He is returning because she eluded him, and the eluding is the engagement; the chase has become its own object. Scriabin gives that hunger its glittering, slightly airless intensity — a yearning so concentrated it begins to seem starved of air, all aspiration and no rest. The sonata's flight toward its unreachable light is the exact shape of a desire that is really about the distance it cannot close.

**2. Couperin — *Pièces de clavecin*, Book III: 'Le Tic-Toc-Choc, ou Les Maillotins'**

Hidden in the house, Genji watches Utsusemi and her young stepdaughter Nokiba no Ogi playing a game of Go by lamplight — a domestic genre-scene of two women at ease, placing stones, scoring points, teasing each other in the unguarded way of people who do not know they are observed. Couperin's 'Le Tic-Toc-Choc' is the perfect miniature for it: a crossed-hands keyboard piece of ticking, interlocking figures, light and precise and entirely self-contained, each hand answering the other like two players setting down stones in turn.

The music gives the scene its true charm — the intimacy of women at play — without ever reaching for grandeur, because nothing grand is happening, and that is the point. It also carries the scene's faint comedy: the careful, complete little world that Genji is about to disturb for no good reason at all. The piece is closed and whole the way the women's evening is closed and whole, and the watcher at the gap is the only thing in the room that does not belong.

**3. Couperin — *Pièces de clavecin*, Book II: 'Les Barricades mystérieuses'**

This is the chapter's quiet center. Genji, peering through a gap — the *kaimami*, the "gap-glimpse" that is Heian fiction's signature erotic device — studies Utsusemi and realizes that she is not even beautiful: thin, unremarkable, her whole appeal residing in her self-possession. And he wants her more. The desire is organized entirely around what she withholds.

'Les Barricades mystérieuses' is the great enigma of the French keyboard repertoire: a gentle, veiled piece in *style brisé*, everything suggested and nothing disclosed, whose very title — the "mysterious barricades" — no one has ever convincingly explained. It is the sound of desire built around a screen, where the barriers are themselves the attraction. What Genji loves in Utsusemi is precisely the barrier she keeps up; the veil is not in the way of the desire, it is the desire's object. Couperin's veiled, interlocking figures never quite resolve into a melody you can hold, which is exactly what cannot be held here.

**4. Ravel — *Gaspard de la nuit*: I. 'Ondine'**

This is the chapter's defining act. Utsusemi, feeling Genji approach through the dark, rises and steals away, leaving behind a single light robe like a cicada's shed shell. She escapes by becoming, in effect, water — ungraspable, already elsewhere, gone before the hand closes.

Ravel's 'Ondine' is the supreme musical image of exactly that: the water-sprite's shimmering, seductive song over a trembling watery accompaniment, and then her sudden laughing dissolution into a spray of droplets the moment she is refused. Here the genders invert — in Bertrand's poem the water-sprite courts a mortal man; in the chapter it is the woman who slips away from the man — but the mechanism is identical: a being that cannot be held because holding is not its element. To grasp at it is to find your hand full of water and your quarry already laughing somewhere else. The robe is the shell; Utsusemi is the creature gone. Ravel scores not the escape's drama but its physics — the way the thing pursued was never solid enough to seize.

**5. Berg — *Lyric Suite*: III. Allegro misterioso**

Rather than leave empty-handed, Genji lies with the stepdaughter he had not come for, taking the substitute almost without deciding to, in the dark, where one body can stand in for another. Berg's 'Allegro misterioso' is desire gone furtive and uncanny: muted, whispering, hurtling figures that flicker past too fast to grasp, an entire movement played *sotto voce* and built, as Berg's own annotated score reveals, on a hidden cipher of a forbidden name.

It is the sound of an erotic act that is also a substitution and an evasion of the real object — desire spending itself on whatever is present because what it wanted has fled. The hidden name beneath the music is the perfect analogue for the wrong woman in the dark: an act addressed to one person and carried out upon another, the true object encrypted and absent. The mistaken night is not played for comedy here, the way Heian substitution-bed scenes often are; it is slightly hollow, slightly cold, a heat that has been displaced onto the nearest available body and knows, somewhere, that it is in the wrong room.

**6. Janáček — *On an Overgrown Path*, Series I: X. 'The Barn Owl Has Not Flown Away'**

Behind her escape is a real woman's sorrow, and the chapter does not let the cleverness of the vanishing obscure it. Utsusemi is married to a coarse, older provincial governor; Genji is a prince. She knows that to yield would be to become first a kept secret and then a discarded one, and she chooses instead the smaller dignity of refusal — a victory that is real and that costs her everything tender.

Janáček's late miniature is grief in the Moravian idiom: terse, plain-spoken, shadowed by an omen — the barn owl of the title, a death-bird, that lingers and will not fly off. It carries the specific weight of a life narrowed almost to nothing and held together by sheer will, which is precisely Utsusemi's condition. This is why the chapter must not be scored with the vast Adagios that grieved the Kiritsubo Lady; her sorrow is not a death but a managed diminishment, and Janáček's small, clear, omened piece is the correct size. He refuses to sweeten the cost of her dignity, and the refusal is the truthfulness.

**7. Schumann — *Fantasiestücke*, Op. 12: III. 'Warum?'**

Genji carries off the abandoned robe and keeps it, breathing its faint residual scent, and writes a poem on the *utsusemi* — the cicada-shell — image that names the chapter and the woman both. He is left loving a keepsake: a thing she wore, not the woman who wore it. Schumann's 'Warum?' — 'Why?' — is a tiny, questioning piece that asks and never answers, a melodic phrase that lifts in interrogation and falls back unresolved, over and over, finding no reply.

It is the sound of a man turning an unanswerable absence over in his hands. Why did she go; what did the robe hold; why does the husk move him more than the living woman ever permitted. Schumann's phrase rises into its question mark and subsides without resolution exactly as Genji's longing does, because there is no resolution available — the keepsake is all he gets, and the not-getting is what he is left holding. He treasures it, and the treasuring is itself the question 'Warum?' asked of an empty robe.

**8. Debussy — *Préludes*, Book I: 'Des pas sur la neige'**

The chapter ends on its own central paradox: Genji possesses the trace and not the thing. Debussy's 'Des pas sur la neige' — footprints in snow — is music made entirely of an absence: a bleak, repeating two-note ostinato that the composer marks should sound *comme un tendre et triste regret*, "like a tender and sad regret," a whole landscape defined by the marks something left as it walked away.

It is the perfect close, and it keeps the arc's voice consistent with the Debussy that ended Chapter 1. The cicada has molted and flown; the shell lies in the snow; what remains is the shape of a departure, the cooling print of a body no longer there. Utsusemi survives the entire novel by this single act of vanishing — she is one of the very few women in the book to escape Genji intact — and the music is the print she leaves behind: a possession that is only the negative space of the thing possessed. Debussy does not dramatize the absence; he simply sounds it, two notes repeating in the cold, and lets the emptiness be the whole content. (Takemitsu's *Rain Tree Sketch II* was weighed for this coda — genuinely modernist, water-and-trace in its very aesthetic, no Japonisme about it — but 'Des pas sur la neige' renders the precise idea, possession of a trace and not a thing, with an unmatched bleak exactness, and holds the arc to a single voice.)

## The Dominant Arc

*Utsusemi* moves through desire, comedy, desire again, metamorphosis, desire, grief, and memory, and resolves at last into metamorphosis: Scriabin gives the starved, accelerating fixation that drives Genji back; Couperin's 'Tic-Toc-Choc' gives the unguarded, self-contained world of women at Go that he is about to spoil; 'Les Barricades mystérieuses' gives the veiled desire organized around the very screen Utsusemi keeps up; Ravel's 'Ondine' gives the defining act, the woman dissolving into water and gone; Berg gives the hollow substitution in the dark with the wrong body; Janáček gives the small, omened sorrow that is the real cost of her dignity; Schumann's 'Warum?' gives the man left questioning a keepsake; and Debussy's footprints in snow give the close — possession reduced to a trace, the print of a departure. The arc ends not in seduction achieved but in disappearance accomplished. The dominant force is metamorphosis — not transformation toward anything, but transformation as evasion: Utsusemi becomes an absence in order to keep her self, sheds herself like a husk, and flies. What Genji holds at the end is the shell. The creature is gone, and that, in this chapter, is the woman's only and complete victory.
