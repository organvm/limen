# Young Murasaki: The Found Child and the Forbidden Mother
## A music arc for *The Tale of Genji*, Chapter 5

Chapter 5 is the hinge on which the whole of *The Tale of Genji* turns. Genji, wasted by a recurring fever, withdraws to a hermitage in the northern hills for an ascetic's healing rites, and there, through a brushwood fence, he glimpses a weeping ten-year-old girl whose pet sparrow has flown its cage. He cannot look away — not only because she is lovely, but because she is the living image of Fujitsubo, his father's young consort and the forbidden love of his life, whose niece she turns out to be. In the same chapter, interwoven with the discovery, runs the novel's central transgression: Genji forces his way to a single stolen night with Fujitsubo herself, who conceives a child — the boy who will one day reign as emperor, his true father hidden forever. Genji carries the orphaned girl, Murasaki, off to his Nijō mansion to raise into his ideal woman. Two secrets are planted here that drive everything after: the substitute child, and the hidden heir.

To score this chapter as a single love story would be to miss it entirely. It is two desires for the same unreachable face, running in counterpoint. On the surface: the enchanting discovery of a child who mirrors Fujitsubo, whom Genji will take and shape into the woman he wants — desire sublimated into cultivation. Underneath: the forbidden consummation with Fujitsubo and the pregnancy that becomes the novel's buried engine — desire as transgression and fate. The arc must hold both registers at once: the luminous, fairy-tale brightness of the mountain discovery, and the dark, guilt-stricken weight of the sin against the emperor-father. The two are not separate plots. They are one longing, reaching twice for the same lost face.

The standing temptation to refuse is Japonisme — any reach for orientalist atmosphere or exotic spectacle simply because the chapter is set partly in Japanese mountains. *Wakamurasaki*'s power is in the psychology of substitution and transgression, not in scenic "Easternness." Murasaki Shikibu is writing obsession and its consequences with a sophistication that has no cultural ceiling, and the music must meet that from the other direction: not the sound of Japan, but the sound of a man fixing his desire onto a child because he cannot have her aunt, and acting at last on the love that has organized his whole life.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Beethoven — *String Quartet No. 15 in A minor, Op. 132*: III. 'Heiliger Dankgesang'**

The chapter opens in the stillness of spring mountains and prayer. Genji, his body worn down by a fever that keeps returning, has come to a hermitage in the northern hills to submit to an ascetic's healing incantations, and before any desire enters, the music must give the clean, rarefied air of the retreat. Beethoven's 'Heiliger Dankgesang' is uncannily exact: it is, by its own inscription, the "holy song of thanksgiving of a convalescent to the Divinity," written in the Lydian mode — literally a sick man's hymn of recovery. It is music of pure verticality and stillness, time suspended in gratitude and healing, chant-like and hushed.

This is the chapter's sacred ground note, and the register is revelation: not the revelation of a secret yet, but of a still place from which everything will fall. By giving the mountain its sanctity first, the arc makes the worldly enchantment that follows feel exactly like what it is — a fall out of the suspended air of healing into the gravity of wanting.

**2. Debussy — *Préludes*, Book I: 'La fille aux cheveux de lin'**

Through the brushwood fence Genji watches a household of women, and among them a lovely child of about ten, in tears because her pet sparrow has escaped its cage. He cannot look away. Debussy's 'La fille aux cheveux de lin' — 'The Girl with the Flaxen Hair' — is a portrait of innocent, fresh, uncomplicated beauty: a simple pentatonic melody, tender and clear, utterly without irony. It is the right music for the surface of the moment, which is simply a man enchanted by a child's loveliness.

The disquiet is deliberately withheld. This enchantment is the opening move in a project of possession, but the music does not say so. It supplies only the brightness; the shadow — that the child is already being chosen, already being seen as something to be taken — is left for the listener to feel, exactly as the chapter leaves it. The force is desire, but desire wearing the face of pure delight.

**3. Debussy — *Images*, Book I: I. 'Reflets dans l'eau'**

The enchantment turns electric the instant Genji understands why the child moves him: she is the very likeness of Fujitsubo, his forbidden love — in fact her niece. The girl is a reflection of the unreachable woman, and in that recognition Genji's desire transforms its object, fixing on a child he can legally take and shape into a copy of the original. Debussy's 'Reflets dans l'eau' is the music of precisely that operation: a shimmering surface in which an image forms, dissolves, and re-forms, water reflecting something that is not itself.

The force here is metamorphosis — desire changing its own shape to find a permissible vessel for the impermissible. It is not the brightness of Track 2 and not yet the dark consummation to come; it is the sound of a longing quietly relocating itself, finding in a reflection a way to keep the original. The whole tragedy of Murasaki's life begins in this substitution, and Debussy's water gives it without a word of editorial: an image that is loved precisely because it is the image of someone else.

**4. Mendelssohn — *A Midsummer Night's Dream*, Op. 61: Scherzo**

Around the discovery is a world of gentle comedy: the elderly nun who is the child's grandmother, the bustling rustic clerics, the small domestic fuss of a country retreat, the nurse scolding the girl over her lost sparrow. Mendelssohn's Scherzo is fleet, gossamer, faintly absurd fairy music — the chatter of a small enchanted world going about its ordinary business. It lightens the chapter at exactly the right moment.

The lightness does real work. It frames the child in her proper element — a real little girl in a real, slightly comic household, not yet the object she is about to become — and that ordinariness is the truth the rest of the chapter will erase. The Scherzo's mercy is one the narrative withholds from her future: this is the last time we see Murasaki as a child simply among her own people, before she is folded into someone else's design.

**5. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*: Act II, 'O sink hernieder, Nacht der Liebe'**

The chapter cuts to the transgression itself. Back in the capital, Fujitsubo lies ill and withdrawn from the palace, and Genji forces his way to a single stolen night with his father's consort — the deepest taboo in his world. Where Chapter 1 used the Tristan Prelude to give desire-as-premise, the consummation here demands the Act II love-duet, 'O sink hernieder' — the great nocturne of lovers who can exist only in darkness, who beg the night to swallow them and never bring the day. It is forbidden union made music: ecstatic, doomed, drunk on its own secrecy.

This is the deliberate rhyme across the whole work. The same composer, the same world of unsatisfiable longing, but advanced from premise to act — Chapter 1 reached, Chapter 5 grasps. The desire that has organized the entire novel finally moves, and in moving plants its irreversible consequence. Wagner's nocturne knows what the lovers will not say aloud: that a night which refuses the day is a night that has already chosen its own ruin.

**6. Mahler — *Rückert-Lieder*: 'Ich bin der Welt abhanden gekommen'**

For Fujitsubo there is no ecstasy, only dread. She has betrayed the emperor who loves her, and she withdraws into guilt, terror of exposure, and a grief that severs her from the world. Mahler's 'Ich bin der Welt abhanden gekommen' — 'I am lost to the world' — is the supreme song of withdrawal: a soul retiring from the living world into a still, private grief, its long lines sinking inward toward "I live alone in my heaven, in my love, in my song."

This is Fujitsubo exactly — a woman who must vanish inwardly in order to survive what has happened, carrying a secret that isolates her absolutely. The pairing with Track 5 is the chapter's moral pivot: the same night is desire for Genji and grief for her, and the arc, by following Wagner's nocturne with Mahler's farewell, takes her side. The music refuses to let the transgression stay beautiful. The cost is borne by the one who did not want it, and Mahler gives that cost its own unbroken, inward line.

**7. Verdi — *La forza del destino*: Overture**

Fujitsubo conceives, and the chapter quietly sets the novel's deepest machinery in motion. The child she carries, passed off as the emperor's, will one day reign — his true paternity a secret that will warp Genji's entire fate, raising him impossibly high and shadowing him with the dread of discovery. Verdi's 'Force of Destiny' overture names this fate-force outright: the hammering, recurring destiny-motif that returns through every key, an unstoppable momentum loosed by a single irreversible act.

The choice of Verdi over the obvious alternative is deliberate. Beethoven's fate-motif is the reflex pick, but its heroic, will-driven striving is wrong for a fate that is secret, shameful, and passive — a consequence no one can act against. Verdi's destiny-motif, recurring helplessly through the overture, is the truer note: the sound of a doom set in motion by concealment rather than struggle, a consequence that has already escaped its authors' control. Nothing in the remaining novel is free of what is conceived here.

**8. Dvořák — *Gypsy Songs*, Op. 55: IV. 'Songs My Mother Taught Me'**

Back in the mountain strand, the old nun who has raised Murasaki sickens and dies, leaving the child truly alone — her mother long dead, her father distant and unsuitable, no protector left. That exposure is precisely what makes Genji's scheme possible. Dvořák's 'Songs My Mother Taught Me' is tender, aching grief for a vanished maternal world: a melody remembered through tears, the lost warmth of being someone's cherished child.

It mourns exactly what Murasaki has lost and what Genji will never truly replace — the protection of someone who wants nothing from her. Every guardian she has left, or is about to acquire, wants something; the grandmother wanted nothing but the child's good. The force is grief, and it is the purest grief in the chapter because it is the child's own: she weeps for her grandmother, and the music weeps with her, holding the one loss that is entirely innocent.

**9. Ravel — *Ma mère l'Oye*: I. 'Pavane de la Belle au bois dormant'**

Seizing his chance, Genji effectively carries the orphaned girl off to his Nijō mansion before her father can claim her, installing her there to be raised — and slowly transformed — into his ideal wife. She cries for her grandmother and her home; he soothes her with pictures and games. Ravel's 'Pavane of Sleeping Beauty' is the perfect, terrible music for it: a brief, exquisite, archaic miniature — a child laid down to sleep in a strange enchanted house, to be awakened, in time, into someone else's design.

The force is metamorphosis again, but now made literal: a person being turned into an object of cultivation. Richard Strauss's *Metamorphosen*, literally titled for transformation, was considered for this and rightly demoted — its elegiac, valedictory weight, a lament for a destroyed culture, is far too heavy and too adult for a ten-year-old being taken into a strange house. Ravel holds the child-scale and the quiet menace together at once, and that doubleness is the point: his tenderness makes the gentleness and the appropriation inseparable, which is the moral truth of the scene. The lullaby and the abduction are the same music.

**10. Schumann — *Kinderszenen*, Op. 15: XIII. 'Der Dichter spricht'**

At Nijō the girl gradually settles. She plays with dolls and paintings, grows fond of Genji as a kind of father-brother-guardian, and the household arranges itself around her. It is domestic, tender, and quietly uncanny — a childhood resuming inside a design she cannot see. Schumann's 'Der Dichter spricht' — 'The Poet Speaks' — closes his cycle of childhood scenes from the adult's vantage: a backward, loving look at a child's world, shadowed by the knowledge of what the child does not yet know.

That double vision is exactly the chapter's standpoint on Murasaki — the child at play in the foreground, the watcher who knows the whole future standing behind. The force is memory, and it is a strange, anticipatory memory: the narrative is already grieving this childhood as it happens, looking back on it from a vantage the girl will not reach for years. Schumann's epilogue speaks in precisely that voice, loving and elegiac at once, mourning a present moment because it already knows how the story ends.

**11. Mahler — *Symphony No. 4*: IV. 'Das himmlische Leben'**

The chapter closes with both secrets in place: the hidden heir growing in Fujitsubo, the surrogate child growing at Nijō — Genji's life now built on two concealments that will raise and ruin him. Mahler's Fourth finale, 'Das himmlische Leben,' is sung in a child's voice describing heaven: a vision at once domestic and cosmic, innocent and vast, serene on a surface that the orchestra keeps quietly undercutting.

It is the exact register for this ending. The apparent radiance of a child's new life and of a love finally consummated floats over depths the child's voice cannot sound. The ascent is real, and it is shadowed — a horizon of beauty resting on a buried fate. By closing on a child's heaven that the orchestra refuses to let be wholly innocent, the arc states the chapter's final truth without a word of commentary: the novel has set its true machinery turning, and it is turning under music that sounds, on its surface, like peace.

## The Dominant Arc

*Wakamurasaki* moves from revelation through desire and metamorphosis and out the far side into fate, grief, and a shadowed ascent. Beethoven's convalescent hymn gives the mountain its sacred stillness; Debussy's flaxen-haired girl gives the first enchantment and his water-reflections give the substitution that turns the child into a copy of the unreachable woman; Mendelssohn lightens the household with the last of her ordinary world; Wagner's nocturne grasps at last the love Chapter 1 only reached for, and Mahler's 'Ich bin der Welt' answers it with the cost borne by Fujitsubo; Verdi names the secret, passive destiny set loose by that one night; Dvořák mourns the child's innocent loss; Ravel makes the abduction and the lullaby the same music; Schumann looks back on the childhood as it is still happening; and Mahler's child-heaven floats the whole new life over the fate buried beneath it. The arc does not resolve — it deepens into concealment. The dominant force is desire, because the substitute-love and the forbidden-love are not two longings but one, reaching twice for the same lost face, and everything the chapter sets turning is desire's issue.
