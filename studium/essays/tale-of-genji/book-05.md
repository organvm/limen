# Wakamurasaki: Young Murasaki
## A music arc for *The Tale of Genji*, Chapter 5

*The Tale of Genji*'s fifth chapter is the hinge of the entire first half of the novel. Genji, sick with malaria, retreats to the northern hills for treatment by a holy man. During his convalescence, he glimpses a ten-year-old girl through a gap in a hedge — and recognizes in her face the living image of his forbidden lover, Fujitsubo, who is herself the image of his dead mother, Kiritsubo. The recognition produces a decision that will shape the rest of the novel: Genji resolves to possess this child, to raise her, educate her, and make her his ideal woman. He kidnaps her when her grandmother dies, installs her in his Nijō palace, and begins her education. This is also the chapter where Genji consummates his love for Fujitsubo, producing the son who will become emperor. The chapter is about the metamorphic power of resemblance: the way the dead can be resurrected through the living, the way desire can be transferred from the forbidden to the permissible, from the adult to the child, from the unreachable to the captive. It is the novel's most morally complex chapter, and its music must hold the tenderness and the violence in suspension.

Scoring this chapter means tracking its strange arc: from illness through recognition through forbidden consummation through kidnapping through education. Berlioz gives the fever; Wagner gives the desire awakened by resemblance; Elgar gives the full weight of memory; Debussy gives the metamorphosis; Brahms gives forbidden love under formal pressure; Mahler gives the grotesque funeral-march of the kidnapping; Mozart gives the court's amused commentary; Fauré gives the strange peace of the chapter's close. The music must never settle comfortably — the chapter's moral complexity requires that each piece carry an undertone of what it displaces.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Berlioz — *Les Troyens*: 'Chasse royale et orage'**

Chapter 5 opens with Genji suffering a lingering fever — a physical illness that follows the psychological wound of Yūgao's death. The retreat to the northern hills is both cure and threshold. Berlioz's 'Chasse royale et orage' gives the atmosphere of the fevered mind: the storm that is both weather and interior state, the hunt that is both narrative and metaphor. The music is turbulent, dark, full of motion without direction — the sound of a body fighting illness and a mind preparing for transformation.

**2. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*: Prelude to Act I**

Genji glimpses the little girl through a gap in the hedge. She has rich eyebrows, lovely hair, a child's face — and she reminds him of Fujitsubo. The Tristan Prelude, which opened the novel for the emperor's desire for Kiritsubo, now returns — not reused carelessly but as the same engine of desire, now refracted through memory and substitution. The music recognizes that desire has found a new object that is also an old object, that the chain of resemblance that began with Kiritsubo is continuing through Fujitsubo to this child. The unresolved chord is now the sound of Genji's future being decided.

**3. Elgar — *Enigma Variations*: 'Nimrod' (Variation IX)**

Genji learns that Murasaki is Fujitsubo's niece. The recognition produces a complex emotional fusion: the dead mother, the forbidden stepmother, the living child who carries both their likenesses. Elgar's 'Nimrod' returns from Chapters 1 and 4 — but here it takes a different coloration: not grief or premonition but the dawning realization that loss has produced a new object, that memory can become flesh again. The music builds from near-silence to a sustained tenderness that is also, in this context, deeply troubling.

**4. Debussy — *Préludes*, Book I: 'La cathédrale engloutie'**

The submerged cathedral, used in Chapter 1 for Genji as his mother's legacy, now returns: the same luminous structure emerging from a different depth. Where Chapter 1 used it for the birth of radiance from grief, Chapter 5 uses it for the birth of a relationship that will be the novel's longest thread. The prelude sounds different now — what was emergence is now enclosure, what was promise is now possession.

**5. Brahms — Symphony No. 3 in F major: III. Poco allegretto**

Genji's night with Fujitsubo is the chapter's most transgressive scene: the son of the emperor sleeping with his father's consort, his stepmother. Brahms's third movement is passion under formal pressure — a cello melody that sings with extraordinary tenderness over a strict rhythmic structure. It is the sound of desire that knows it is forbidden and proceeds anyway, with full awareness of the cost. The music never breaks its composure; the emotion is all in the melodic line, held in by the orchestration like a secret inside a formal room.

**6. Mahler — Symphony No. 1 ('Titan'): III. Feierlich und gemessen**

When Murasaki's grandmother dies, Genji acts: he rushes to the house, wakes the child, and carries her off. The Mahler First's third movement is a funeral march based on 'Frère Jacques' in a minor key — simultaneously solemn and grotesque, a death march that becomes something else. It gives the chapter's most ambiguous scene: a kidnapping that is also a rescue, a theft that is also an adoption, a funeral that is also a birth. The music refuses to settle into a single emotional register, just as the scene refuses a single moral category.

**7. Mozart — *Così fan tutte*: Overture**

Genji's household and friends react to his sudden adoption of a ten-year-old girl with amusement, curiosity, and scandalized fascination. Mozart's Così fan tutte Overture — 'All women do it' — is bright, witty, and fundamentally cynical: the comic register the Heian court would use to process the spectacle of the Shining One raising his own bride from childhood. The comedy here is knowing and worldly, shaded by the chapter's darker currents.

**8. Fauré — *Requiem*, Op. 48: 'In Paradisum'**

The chapter ends with Murasaki settled in Genji's Nijō palace, her education begun. He teaches her calligraphy, poetry, music — shaping her into the woman he wants her to become. Fauré's 'In Paradisum' returns from Chapter 4 (Yūgao's release) transformed: no longer a farewell to the dead but a welcome for the new. The music is the sound of a soul being conducted into a garden — and Murasaki's new life is precisely that, a garden enclosed, a protected space where she is groomed for her future. The darkness of the chapter's moral complexity is not resolved but suspended in the luminous peace of Fauré's final cadence.

## The Dominant Arc

Wakamurasaki moves from illness through desire through forbidden consummation through kidnapping through the strange peace of the garden house. Berlioz gives the fever; Wagner gives the desire awakened by a child's face; Elgar gives the recognition of inheritance; Debussy gives the metamorphic emergence; Brahms gives transgressive love under formal constraint; Mahler gives the grotesquerie of the abduction; Mozart gives the court's amusement; Fauré gives a peace that is also a cage. The dominant force is metamorphosis — the transformation of absence into presence, of child into wife, of loss into possession.
