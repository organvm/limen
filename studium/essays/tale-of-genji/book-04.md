# Yūgao: The Evening Face
## A music arc for *The Tale of Genji*, Chapter 4

*The Tale of Genji*'s fourth chapter contains the novel's first death since the opening — but where Kiritsubo's death was a slow attrition, Yūgao's is a sudden catastrophe. A young woman in the fullness of a secret romance, at the height of tenderness, is killed by a spirit of jealous rage while Genji sleeps beside her. The evening-face flower that opens the chapter — delicate, white, blooming at dusk — becomes the emblem of a life that opens, blooms, and is extinguished in a single night. The chapter is also a love story told in the space between other loves: Genji is simultaneously courting Lady Rokujō, corresponding with Utsusemi, and falling for Yūgao, and the jealous spirit that kills Yūgao is the accumulated violence of an erotic economy in which women's claims on men are structurally unequal. Yūgao dies because she occupies space that someone else's jealousy has already claimed.

Scoring this chapter means tracking the arc from nostalgia through erotic discovery through foreboding to sudden, senseless death. Elgar opens with the love that is about to become memory; Debussy and Ravel give the brief, tender romance; Beethoven introduces the inexorable motif; Penderecki shatters the peace with the sound of death occurring; Barber provides the funeral music for a death that cannot be mourned aloud; Fauré releases the spirit into peace. The chapter's challenge is that the most beautiful music must precede the most terrible, so that the listener — like Genji — does not see the catastrophe coming until it is already upon them.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Elgar — *Enigma Variations*: 'Nimrod' (Variation IX)**

Genji visits his old nurse, who has become a nun and is near death. The scene is saturated with his childhood, his mother's death, the nurse's devoted care. Elgar's 'Nimrod' — used in Chapter 1 for the emperor's grief and in Chapter 5 for Murasaki's recognition — here takes a third coloration: love that is about to cross into memory, not yet grief but its premonition. The nurse is still alive; the love is still present; the music gives the tenderness of that threshold.

**2. Debussy — *Estampes*: 'Pagodes'**

Genji sees white flowers blooming at dusk, sends for one, and receives a fan inscribed with a poem. The exchange is pure Heian erotic protocol — oblique, poetic, charged. Debussy's 'Pagodes' is pentatonic, delicate, exquisitely decorative: the sound of an encounter that is entirely aesthetic on the surface and entirely urgent underneath. The music reveals the mystery slowly, note by note, like the veiled woman emerging through poetic correspondence.

**3. Ravel — *Pavane pour une infante défunte***

Genji and Yūgao begin their secret affair. She is gentle, passive, infinitely yielding — the woman Tō no Chūjō abandoned for being too meek. Ravel's Pavane was demoted from Chapters 1 and 2; here it finds its natural home. The piece is nostalgic even in the present tense, as if the happiness is already being remembered, already touched by the fatal quality of a flower that blooms only at dusk. The Pavane is a dance for a dead princess; Yūgao is a living woman who will be dead before dawn.

**4. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5 in C minor: I. Allegro con brio**

Genji, in an impulsive decision, takes Yūgao to an abandoned villa in a desolate quarter. The journey is shadowed by foreboding. Beethoven's Fifth opening — the famous four-note motif — is fate knocking, here used with restraint: not the full movement but the inexorable pattern being set in motion. The chapter has seeded Yūgao's vulnerability; the journey to the villa is where vulnerability crosses into danger.

**5. Penderecki — *Threnody for the Victims of Hiroshima***

The apparition appears. Yūgao dies in Genji's arms, silently, without warning. Penderecki's Threnody is the sound of a life being erased in real time: string clusters, glissandi, the texture of consciousness being torn apart. It is not the Mahler Ninth Adagio (reserved for the Kiritsubo Lady's dignified death); it is uglier, more violent, more senseless — which is the exact register of Yūgao's death, a death that has no meaning, only cause.

**6. Barber — *Adagio for Strings*, Op. 11**

Genji falls physically ill from shock. He cannot explain his collapse, cannot reveal the night's events, cannot mourn Yūgao publicly. Barber's Adagio was reserved in Chapter 1 for a moment of collective grief; here it finds its place — the funeral music for a death that cannot be mourned aloud, the terrible interior of grief that has no social outlet.

**7. Fauré — *Requiem*, Op. 48: 'In Paradisum'**

The chapter ends with final poems and the narrator's apology. Fauré's 'In Paradisum' is not a plea for the dead but an escort — a musical guide leading the soul into peace. It gives the chapter its final note: Yūgao is gone, but the music does not end in tragedy; it ends in a kind of sad, luminous acceptance. The choir's ascent into the upper register is Yūgao's spirit being released from the story, as the evening face fades into the night.

## The Dominant Arc

Yūgao moves from the tenderness of recollection through the delicacy of new love through the inexorable approach of disaster. Elgar gives the premonition of loss; Debussy gives the beauty of the encounter; Ravel gives the love that is already ghosted; Beethoven gives fate entering the narrative; Penderecki gives the violence of the death; Barber gives the grief that cannot be spoken; Fauré gives the release. The dominant force is grief — but grief that arrives without warning and leaves without explanation, the grief that is the novel's most insistent theme.
