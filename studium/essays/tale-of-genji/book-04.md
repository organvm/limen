# Yūgao: Evening Faces, and the Jealous Spirit
## A music arc for *The Tale of Genji*, Chapter 4

Chapter 4 is one of the supreme chapters of world fiction, and it earns that standing by doing something the novel has not yet dared: it lets Genji's recklessness kill someone. Visiting his ailing old nurse, he notices white moonflower gourds — *yūgao*, "evening faces" — blooming on a humble fence at the magic hour; a fan with a poem comes back over the wall; and he falls into a secret affair with the gentle, mysterious woman who lives there. They court incognito, neither knowing the other's name. She is, unknown to him, Tō no Chūjō's vanished love from the rainy-night talk of Chapter 2 — the novel folding its own past back into the present. Wanting her wholly to himself, Genji carries her off at dawn to a deserted, decaying villa. In the deep of the night a vengeful spirit — *mononoke*, the living jealous wraith of the proud Lady Rokujō, whom he has been neglecting — appears at the pillow, and Yūgao dies in his arms, instantly, of pure terror. The rest is secret cremation in the hills and a grief that has no public name.

The chapter is not a ghost story, and it must not be scored as one. Its real subject is the collision of two forces Genji believes he can keep apart: the tender, anonymous idyll with a low-born woman who asks nothing of him — no rank, no name, no future — and the wounded pride of a great lady whose jealousy has become, in the world of this novel, literally lethal. So the arc runs enchantment → intimacy → dread → supernatural irruption → sudden death → secret grief, and the register must darken by degrees: hushed and moonlit at the start, then the eerie "night music" of the haunted house, then the irruption, then a sorrow Genji cannot even speak because the whole affair was a secret.

Scoring it means refusing two temptations. The first is Japonisme — Puccini's Butterfly-flavored nocturnes, any orientalist "mood" that would substitute atmospheric Easternness for psychological precision; the chapter's power is metaphysical, not picturesque. The second is horror-movie scoring of the supernatural, the cheap frisson of the jump-scare. The terror here is intimate and metaphysical; the death is quiet and irreversible. Murasaki Shikibu treats the *mononoke* as terrifyingly real, not as a special effect, and the music must meet it on that ground.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel***

The chapter opens in a register of plain gentleness. Genji calls on his old wet nurse, gravely ill and about to take Buddhist vows, and her gratitude and tears at the sight of him are overwhelming — a scene of unguarded human devotion, of a plea for blessing over a dying woman. Pärt's *Spiegel im Spiegel* is supplication distilled to nothing but a rising arpeggio and a held line: patient, exposed, devotional, asking quietly and continuously without any expectation of an answer. It establishes the chapter's tender baseline, the warmth against which all the coming dread will register.

It also quietly tells us who Genji is at his best — a man capable of real care, attentive to the dying, moved by gratitude he has done little to earn. That is precisely what makes his recklessness with Yūgao the more culpable. The music does not accuse him; it simply shows the better self he is about to betray by leading another woman into harm.

**2. Debussy — *Images*, Book II: II. 'Et la lune descend sur le temple qui fut'**

Next door, on a shabby fence, white *yūgao* flowers bloom in the dusk; charmed, Genji asks for one, and a fan bearing a teasing poem comes back over the wall. The whole enchantment of the chapter begins here, in moonlight and anonymity, with neither party knowing the other. Debussy's 'And the moon descends on the temple that was' is hushed, modal, suspended — slow bell-like chords falling through still air over a ruined, beautiful place. It is the sound of a man drawn by something modest and luminous in the half-dark, the lure of a lowly house glimpsed at the magic hour.

Crucially the desire here is gentle, almost pastoral, with none of the predatory heat the word "affair" might suggest. That softness is exactly what makes its cost so shocking: nothing in this music forecasts a death. The piece's stillness is the stillness of a held breath before anyone knows there is reason to hold it.

**3. Fauré — Nocturne No. 6 in D-flat major, Op. 63**

The affair deepens under cover of disguise. Genji hides his rank and even his face, and Yūgao — pliant, trusting, unalarmed by the mystery — receives him with a softness unlike any woman he has known. Fauré's Sixth Nocturne is his greatest, a long-breathed nocturnal song that opens in tender reverie and grows steadily more impassioned without ever turning hard or grasping. It is the music of a love that feels like refuge precisely because it asks no questions: no rank, no name, no future, only the present hour.

The tragedy the music does not yet know is that this refuge is being built in the open. Genji mistakes secrecy for safety, intimacy for protection, when in fact he is conducting the affair within easy reach of a jealousy he has badly underestimated. The Nocturne's growing ardor is the sound of two people sinking deeper into a shelter that has no walls.

**4. Schumann — *Fantasiestücke*, Op. 12: I. 'Des Abends'**

The chapter quietly lets us understand what Genji does not: this gentle woman is the lover Tō no Chūjō mourned on the rainy night of Chapter 2 — the "wild pink" who bore his child and fled his principal wife's jealousy. The novel folds its own past back into its present, and we hold a memory the lovers themselves lack. Schumann's 'Des Abends' ('In the Evening') is a gently rocking, dusky reverie in cross-rhythm, a piece that seems to remember even as it happens, its present already tinged with elegy.

That is the exact mechanism of the scene's dramatic irony. The music's evening tenderness is shadowed by everything we know is coming — and by everything the lovers cannot see about who they are to each other. It is not foreboding in the obvious sense; it is the soft ache of recognition that arrives a beat too early, the past humming under a present that does not yet hear it.

**5. Scriabin — Poème, Op. 32 No. 1 in F-sharp major**

Wanting her wholly to himself, away from prying eyes, Genji spirits Yūgao at dawn to a decaying, isolated villa — a fatal romanticism, privacy bought at the price of safety. Scriabin's Op. 32 Poème is brief, hovering, voluptuous desire suspended out of time: a few minutes of harmonic floating that feels like a stolen interval the world has stopped to allow. It is the sound of lovers who believe they have escaped into a private hour entirely their own.

But the piece's very weightlessness is the warning. Nothing this ungrounded can hold; the harmony never settles because it has no floor beneath it. The lovers have walked, hand in hand, into an empty house that already belongs to something else — and the music's gorgeous suspension is exactly the absence of solid ground that the next track will turn into dread.

**6. Bartók — *Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta*: III. Adagio**

As night falls the villa reveals itself: derelict, echoing, wrong. Yūgao is frightened; the air thickens with something neither of them will name. Bartók's Adagio is the founding masterpiece of "night music" — sparse, shivering string textures, the celesta's cold glints, sounds that seem to leak from the dark corners of an empty room. It is the sound of dread without an object, of a place that is occupied by an absence.

The force here is plague in its purest engine sense: descending motion, dark orchestration, the slow approach of something that kills, the malign atmosphere closing in before it has yet taken a face. This is the hinge of the whole chapter, the bar where the idyll curdles. Everything tender that came before — Pärt's devotion, Debussy's moonlight, Fauré's refuge, Scriabin's stolen hour — is here being quietly surrounded.

**7. Mussorgsky — *Night on Bald Mountain* (original 1867 version)**

In the dead of night Genji wakes to see a beautiful, furious woman standing at the pillow, reproaching him for lavishing love on this nobody while neglecting her — the materialized jealous spirit, which the text leaves as the living wraith of Lady Rokujō, his proud, abandoned mistress. The lamp gutters; the supernatural breaks the natural world open. Mussorgsky's *original* 1867 *Night on Bald Mountain* is raw, jagged, malevolent manifestation — not the sanitized Rimsky-Korsakov concert version but the savage first draft, all sudden brass and clawing strings.

This is the engine's 'divine-intervention' in its dark mode: a sudden irruption of the other world that shatters the human scene. The original version is the right choice precisely because Berlioz's *Symphonie fantastique* finale — the obvious witches'-sabbath candidate — pulls toward burlesque, its *Dies irae* parody and grotesque theatricality making the supernatural picturesque. Mussorgsky's first draft is more savage and far less ironic, closer to the genuine metaphysical violence of a *mononoke* the chapter insists is terrifyingly real. The other world has reached in, and it is not amused.

**8. Mahler — Symphony No. 10: I. Adagio (the dissonant nine-note climax)**

Genji turns to wake Yūgao and finds her already cold — dead of sheer terror, instantly, without illness or wound. The death is impossible to absorb: a healthy young woman gone in a heartbeat, in his arms, in a place he chose. Mahler's Tenth Adagio builds to the most harrowing sound in the symphonic repertoire — a single dissonant chord of nine piled-up notes that shrieks and refuses to resolve, the sound of a mind colliding with something it cannot contain.

That is the exact register of this death. It is not the slow, quiet withdrawal of the Kiritsubo Lady in Chapter 1, which the Mahler Ninth Adagio gave as a consciousness letting itself go; it is the opposite — a violent, vertical shock, grief as the collision with the unthinkable. And then the chord must subside into numb aftermath, the body cooling, the room emptied of everything but the fact. The death is over before Genji can understand that it has begun.

**9. Bach — *St. John Passion*, BWV 245: 'Es ist vollbracht' (No. 30)**

Genji, near collapse, alone in the haunted dark with a corpse and a few terrified servants, must somehow act. He sends for his trusted man Koremitsu and begins the agony of concealment, because the affair and the death must never be known. 'Es ist vollbracht' ('It is finished') — the viola da gamba's grieving lament beneath the alto voice — is the sound of a thing accomplished that cannot be undone, sorrow that has passed beyond pleading into exhausted acceptance.

It is supplication at its lowest ebb: the prayer of someone with nothing left to ask except the strength to carry the body out. The Christian text is incidental here, and would be the wrong cultural filter to impose literally on a Buddhist-inflected scene; what is exact is the emotional mechanism. This is grief that has stopped resisting and only wants to be able to move its hands — to lift, to hide, to endure the next hour.

**10. Schubert — *Winterreise*, D. 911: No. 20 'Der Wegweiser'**

Yūgao is carried in secret to a mountain temple and cremated almost furtively; Genji, unable to mourn her publicly without exposing everything, falls gravely ill with shock and guilt. His is a grief with no rites, no name, no one to share it. Schubert's 'Der Wegweiser' ('The Signpost') is the heart of *Winterreise*: a trudging, frozen tread toward the one road "from which no man has ever returned," grief walking alone through a dead landscape.

It is the sound of bereavement that must keep moving and cannot stop to weep, sorrow privatized into a solitary winter journey. This is why Barber's *Adagio* is held back here as it was in Chapter 1: Barber is monumental funeral music for shared, public, collective loss, and Yūgao's death is the precise opposite — a secret Genji must hide even as he rides back from the hills carrying a death no one may know he is carrying. The Schubert holds both the privacy and the trudging compulsion to go on.

**11. Strauss — 'Morgen!', Op. 27 No. 4**

As Genji slowly recovers he is left permanently marked. He keeps the memory of Yūgao like a wound that will not close — and, as the novel will remember, her little daughter survives somewhere, to surface decades later. Strauss's 'Morgen!' ('Tomorrow') is the supreme music of love after loss: a long violin meditation over which the voice enters, almost too late, on the word "and," as though joining a sentence already in progress. The song is all afterglow, a tenderness addressed to a tomorrow the lovers were to spend together and now never will.

It is the right close — not resolution but a luminous, grieving memory, the evening face that bloomed for a single night and was gone by morning. The dominant force of the whole chapter is grief, and this is where it goes to rest: not extinguished, not consoled, but turned at last into something Genji can carry quietly inside him for the rest of the book.

## The Dominant Arc

Yūgao moves from enchantment through irruption to a grief that has no public name. Pärt gives the tender baseline and the better self Genji betrays; Debussy and Fauré give the moonlit, anonymous desire that feels like refuge; Schumann folds the novel's own past back in as dramatic irony; Scriabin gives the weightless, ungrounded stolen hour; Bartók turns that lack of ground into "night music" and the approach of something lethal; Mussorgsky's savage first draft gives the supernatural irruption without making it picturesque; Mahler Ten gives the vertical shock of a death that arrives all at once; Bach gives the exhausted, hands-only supplication of concealment; Schubert gives the secret, trudging bereavement that cannot stop to mourn; and Strauss closes on love after loss, the evening face remembered. The arc ends not in resolution but in a luminous wound. The dominant force is grief — because everything tender in this chapter, every flower and stolen hour and anonymous kindness, was only ever grief preparing its ground.
