# The Broom Tree: The Rating of Women, and the Woman Who Refuses
## A music arc for *The Tale of Genji*, Chapter 2

*The Tale of Genji*'s second chapter is a comedy that goes cold. Hahakigi is two movements welded together. The first is the famous "rainy-night ranking of women" — the *amayo no shinasadame* — in which Genji, Tō no Chūjō, and two other young courtiers, penned indoors by the long seasonal rains, drift into the kind of talk that only bored, privileged men have: who are the truly desirable women, and how would one know one? It is witty, idle, faintly absurd, an elaborate apparatus of taxonomy built to make desire orderly. The second movement is the reality that defeats every theory: Genji's clumsy, coercive night with Utsusemi, the provincial governor's wife, who refuses to become what he wants her to be. The broom tree of the title is a legendary tree visible from a distance that vanishes when you approach it. That is the chapter's true subject — desire constructing a system of ranks and types, and the one real woman who steps out of the system and is gone.

Scoring this chapter means refusing two temptations. The first, common to any Genji chapter, is Japonisme — Puccini's Butterfly-flavored orientalism, anything that substitutes atmospheric "Easternness" for psychological precision. Hahakigi is about how men talk, how desire systematizes itself, and how a woman refuses; none of that is atmosphere. The second temptation is to over-monumentalize — to score a chapter that is mostly four young men chatting indoors as though it were grand opera or tragedy on a stage. The rainy-night session is conversational intimacy, not spectacle, and Utsusemi's defeat of Genji is quiet, interior, withheld. The music must stay the size the text is: chamber-scaled, ironic, and exact.

The arc therefore moves from comedy to desire to grief. It begins in the urbane self-amusement of clever men theorizing women, passes through the ache beneath the theory — the perfect woman who does not exist, the real woman really lost — and lands in a coercive encounter that yields its perpetrator nothing. The gap the chapter measures is the gap between the systems men build to manage longing and the women who escape them. The dominant force is desire, and desire here is something that builds taxonomies and then breaks against a person.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Poulenc — *Sextet for Piano and Winds*, FP 100: I. Allegro vivace**

The chapter opens with Genji, Tō no Chūjō, and two friends penned indoors by the seasonal rains, drifting into the conversation that only bored, privileged young men have: who are the truly desirable women, and how would you know one if you found her? Poulenc's Sextet is the exact register — urbane, quick, comically self-assured, full of wit that thinks itself wiser than it is. The winds chatter over and around the piano like courtiers topping one another's stories, each entrance a fresh boast, the whole texture a sparkle of cleverness amusing itself. This is what the rainy-night session is before it pretends to be philosophy: talk for talk's sake, performed worldliness, men enjoying the sound of their own discernment.

Not Rameau, whose ceremonial idiom would be too public and stately for this private, joking intimacy — the scene is four men shut in a room, not a court at function. Poulenc has the specific tone the moment needs: the sound of people who are sure they are interesting, which is the precise comedy Murasaki Shikibu is staging.

**2. Bach — *The Art of Fugue*, BWV 1080: Contrapunctus I**

Tō no Chūjō proposes the chapter's governing conceit: women, like rank and office, can be sorted into upper, middle, and lower grades, each with its characteristic virtues and its characteristic traps. It is the impulse to make desire into a system — to replace the chaos of attraction with a taxonomy that can be discussed, defended, and admired. Bach's *Art of Fugue* is the purest music of that impulse. A single subject is worked out with total architectural control, beautiful precisely because it is lawful, its symmetry a closed and self-sufficient perfection. The irony the chapter intends is audible in it: the men's confidence that desire can be made this orderly is as elegant, and as abstract, as a fugue. Reality will not honor the schema, but the schema is gorgeous while it lasts.

Not Handel, whose grandeur would be too sovereign — this is not power decreeing but theory admiring itself. The Art of Fugue's cool, closed, almost mathematical beauty is the right image of a system that has refined its object until the object has nothing to do with any living woman.

**3. Rossini — *Il barbiere di Siviglia*: Overture**

The men trade cautionary anecdotes, and the anecdotes are frankly funny: the hyper-competent wife so consumed by jealousy that she bites her husband's finger in a fit; the woman who overplays her refinement until refinement becomes a fault. These are domestic farces dressed up as wisdom. Rossini's *Barber* overture is comic machinery in its ideal form — the celebrated crescendos that build and build to nothing in particular, the patter that mistakes speed for substance, the whole glittering apparatus of opera buffa running on its own momentum. It gives the rainy-night anecdotes their true genre: not moral instruction but entertainment, men performing worldliness for one another and laughing at their own examples.

The laughter is real, and so is the faint cruelty underneath it. The women in these stories are types, not people — the jealous one, the affected one, the over-refined one — and the comedy depends on their never being granted an interior. Rossini's brilliance has exactly that quality of surface delight that does not look too closely at what it is laughing at.

**4. Debussy — *Préludes*, Book I: 'Voiles'**

Beneath the comedy runs a real ache. When Uma no Kami holds forth on the ideal woman, what he is describing is a woman who does not exist — the perfect compound of accomplishment, discretion, and warmth, visible like the broom tree from a distance and gone the moment one draws near. Debussy's 'Voiles' is built almost entirely on the whole-tone scale: it floats without a tonic, ungrounded, beautiful and impossible to stand on. There is no key to come home to, no firm ground beneath the shimmer. That is the ideal woman of the rainy-night discussion exactly — a glimmer with no center, a desire that has refined its object out of reach by demanding everything of it at once.

The prelude's veiled, weightless suspension is the sound of longing for something that recedes by definition. 'Voiles' can mean *veils* or *sails*; either way the object is screened, in motion, never quite arrived at. The men have talked themselves into wanting a phantom, and Debussy gives the phantom its proper music: lovely, drifting, and unreachable.

**5. Fauré — 'Après un rêve,' Op. 7 No. 1**

For a moment the talk turns genuinely sad. Tō no Chūjō tells of a gentle, undemanding woman he once loved and neglected, who bore him a child — a little girl, "the wild pink" — and then simply disappeared, frightened off by his principal wife's jealousy. He has never found her again. The reader does not yet know that this is Yūgao, whose death will devastate Chapter 4; here she is only a regret, a softness the speaker let slip away. Fauré's 'Après un rêve' is the song of a tenderness recalled too late — a melody that rises toward a remembered closeness and then sinks back into the cold of waking, the dream's warmth dissolving as the eyes open.

It punctures the comedy at exactly the right moment. Under all the ranking and the patter is a real woman, really lost, whom the system could not hold and the man did not protect. The song's ache is not theoretical: it is grief for a specific absence, and it briefly shames the whole enterprise of sorting women into grades by reminding the room that one of them once had a person and lost her.

**6. Wagner — *Wesendonck Lieder*, WWV 91: V. 'Träume'**

The session ends as the rain lets up, and Genji, who has mostly listened, is left with his own unspeakable longing: Fujitsubo, his father's young consort, the one woman he cannot have and cannot stop wanting. 'Träume' is Wagner's study for the *Tristan* love-music — the dream-song of a desire that can only be lived at night, in secret, half-asleep, a longing too dangerous to be spoken aloud in daylight. It gives the private interior that the rainy-night comedy has been circling without ever naming. All the talk of ranks and types has been displacement; the real object of Genji's desire is forbidden, and the chatter about other men's women was a way of not saying her name.

Using the Wesendonck rather than the *Tristan* Prelude itself keeps the register intimate. This is not the engine of an entire opera's tragedy declared at full orchestral pressure; it is a confession murmured in a single voice, a man alone with a wish he must not have. The scaled-down Wagner is the right size for an interior that the chapter shows us only obliquely — desire kept under the breath.

**7. Stravinsky — *Apollon musagète*: 'Apothéose'**

A divination forbids Genji's direct route home — a *kata-tagae*, the directional taboo — so he must lodge for the night at the house of the Governor of Kii, and that small social rule, obeyed without thought, delivers him to Utsusemi. Stravinsky's neoclassical Apollo is the music of pure formal constraint made beautiful: strings moving in clean, lawful lines, a cool diatonic order that takes evident pleasure in its own restraint. It is the right sound for the way Heian life is governed by rules that look arbitrary on their face and turn out to be fate's delivery system. The taboo is law on its surface; underneath, it is the mechanism that routes Genji to the one encounter that will frustrate him more completely than any other.

The 'Apothéose' is serene, ordered, and entirely trustworthy-sounding — which is exactly the wrong thing to trust, and exactly why it fits. The music's poise is the poise of a system that appears to be merely procedural and is in fact steering a man toward the night that will defeat him. Stravinsky lets the order sound innocent so that the irony of where the order leads can do its work.

**8. Szymanowski — *Myths*, Op. 30: I. 'The Fountain of Arethusa'**

In the unfamiliar house Genji learns that the governor's young wife is sleeping nearby, and — on impulse, in the dark — he goes to her. Szymanowski's 'Fountain of Arethusa' is shimmering, predatory desire in motion: the violin spins iridescent figuration over a restless, glinting piano, water that is also pursuit. The myth could not be more exact. Arethusa is the nymph who flees the river-god Alpheus and is transformed into a fountain to escape him — a woman literally becoming water to slip a god's desire. Genji crosses the dark rooms toward Utsusemi with that same glamour and that same heedlessness, certain that his beauty and rank make the outcome a foregone conclusion.

The track's glittering surface carries the predation inside it; the music is gorgeous and it is a chase. And the myth foretells the chapter's result: Utsusemi will spend this night and the next chapter becoming, in effect, something Genji cannot hold — slipping his grasp not by flight but by refusal, water running through the fingers of a man who assumed she would stay where he reached.

**9. Schubert — Impromptu in G-flat major, D. 899 No. 3**

The encounter is not romance. It is coercion softened by Genji's beauty and his rank, and it gives him nothing he came for. Utsusemi cannot physically refuse him — he is a prince and she is a provincial governor's wife — but she refuses inwardly: she will not pretend to be moved, will not perform the pleasure expected of her, will not give him the woman he imagined when he crossed the dark. Her dignity is a kind of grief. She knows exactly what she is and exactly what she cannot escape, married to a coarse older man from the provinces and now visited by a prince who sees her as a conquest rather than a person.

Schubert's G-flat Impromptu is that interior precisely: a long, flowing cantabile of almost unbearable restraint, sorrow held in perfect composure and never once breaking the surface. The right hand sings on and on above the shimmering accompaniment, contained, dignified, withholding its grief from any display. It is the sound of a woman keeping her self intact by the only means left to her — by withholding it, by declining to give the encounter the assent that would make it mean what Genji wants it to mean.

**10. Brahms — Intermezzo in A major, Op. 118 No. 2**

Genji departs having taken everything and won nothing. Utsusemi has not yielded the one thing he actually wanted — her assent, her surrender, the confirmation that he is irresistible — and her refusal has converted his conquest into a defeat he can barely name. He enlists her young brother Kogimi as a go-between, intent on a second attempt, but the chapter closes on Utsusemi's resolve to refuse him again, and on the muffled sadness of a life she did not choose and cannot leave. Brahms's A-major Intermezzo is autumnal consolation that knows it cannot console: a tender melody folded around a darker, questioning middle section, then returning to itself, resigned rather than comforted.

It is the music of accommodation — of a person making peace with a diminished life precisely by declining to dramatize it. There is no outcry in the Intermezzo, no scene; there is a quiet that has decided to hold. The chapter ends not in conquest and not in tragedy but in a woman's composed, intact No — and Brahms gives that No its real weight, which is the weight of a sorrow that refuses to perform itself.

## The Dominant Arc

Hahakigi moves from comedy through desire into grief, and at every stage desire is the force underneath. Poulenc gives the idle, urbane talk of men with nothing to do but theorize; Bach gives the taxonomy itself, desire dreaming it can be made lawful; Rossini gives the farcical anecdotes that turn women into types; Debussy gives the impossible ideal woman who floats out of reach; Fauré punctures the comedy with a real woman really lost; Wagner names the forbidden longing the whole session was displacing; Stravinsky gives the small social law that delivers Genji to his defeat; Szymanowski gives the glamorous, predatory crossing toward Utsusemi; Schubert gives her withheld, composed refusal inside submission; and Brahms gives the resigned sorrow of the No she will not take back. The arc ends not in possession but in a woman's escape from the entire apparatus built to contain her. The dominant force is desire — because desire is what builds the system, what crosses the dark room, and what breaks, finally, against a person who declines to be what it imagined.
