# Hahakigi: Taxonomy, Longing, The Broom Tree
## A music arc for *The Tale of Genji*, Chapter 2

*The Tale of Genji*'s second chapter is the novel's first extended demonstration that its subject is not love but the knowledge of women — the compulsion to classify, rank, and possess them, and the comedy that ensues when reality refuses the categories. The rainy-night conversation at the palace is a philosophical symposium disguised as gossip: four men trying to bring the chaos of erotic experience under rational order. Tō no Chūjō, the Guard Officer, the Junior Secretary, and the sleeping Genji together produce a taxonomy of women that will be tested, and found wanting, by every subsequent chapter. The second half of the chapter then stages the test: Genji's pursuit of Utsusemi, the wife of the Iyo Deputy, who resists, evades, and finally escapes, leaving only an empty robe. The broom tree of the title is a mythical tree that fades as you approach it. The chapter is about that recession — the space between the category and the real, between the desire to know and the impossibility of knowing.

Scoring this chapter means finding the right register for two very different kinds of comedy: the intellectual comedy of the taxonomy and the situational comedy of the failed seduction. Rossini opens the chapter with the pure energy of male conviviality; Bach gives the fugal architecture of the classification system; Fauré provides the emotional pivot in Tō no Chūjō's story of the abandoned woman; Debussy and Szymanowski give the nocturnal, chromatic register of Genji's desire; Mozart and Rameau provide the comic exits. The arc moves from the confidence of taxonomy through the deflation of experience, ending not in wisdom but in rueful self-awareness.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Rossini — *Il barbiere di Siviglia*: Overture**

The rainy-night conversation begins with Genji confined to the palace by a directional taboo during the rainy season. Boredom, proximity, and the arrival of love letters produce the conversation. Rossini's overture captures the social mood: it begins with a quiet, expectant introduction — the desultory evening before anyone speaks — and then accelerates into the famous comic presto when the conversation ignites. It is the sound of male conviviality: talkative, competitive, self-amused, never quite serious about the enormous claims it is making. Rameau would be too courtly; Mozart too elegant; Rossini exactly hits the register of young men talking, late at night, about the subject that most interests them.

**2. Bach — *The Art of Fugue*: Contrapunctus I**

The taxonomy scene — Tō no Chūjō's three categories of women, the detailed discussion of virtues and defects — is the novel's most explicit exercise in male epistemology. Bach's Contrapunctus I is pure architectural clarity: a subject stated, inverted, developed, resolved. It sounds like what the men believe they are doing: bringing fugal order to the chaos of erotic desire. The comedy is that the system is entirely theoretical and will immediately fail when confronted with an actual woman. Bach does not know this is funny; the listener does.

**3. Fauré — *Élégie*, Op. 24**

Tō no Chūjō's story of the woman he abandoned because she was too meek and forgiving — the woman who bore his child — is the chapter's emotional pivot. It is the first hint of Yūgao, and it introduces the novel's concern with the women who are discarded by the very qualities that make them lovable. Fauré's Élégie for cello and orchestra is quiet, private grief — not the grand mourning of Mahler but the particular regret of a man who is telling a story he does not fully understand about a woman he has wronged. The cello's line is the unheard voice of the abandoned woman.

**4. Debussy — *Préludes*, Book I: 'Les sons et les parfums tournent dans l'air du soir'**

At the Governor of Kii's house, Genji overhears the young wife Utsusemi speaking about him. Her voice, heard through a screen, moves him. Debussy's prelude is the music of knowledge that comes through the senses rather than sight — sounds and perfumes, atmosphere rather than encounter. It captures the specific quality of Genji's attraction: not the immediate desire of the emperor for Kiritsubo but something more indirect, more curious, more shaded by the social forms that surround it.

**5. Mozart — *Le nozze di Figaro*: Overture**

Genji carries Utsusemi off to his room, but she resists. The next morning he is sent away — a comic defeat for the Shining One. Mozart's Figaro Overture is the sound of a comic plot moving too fast for its hero: a breathless presto that keeps arriving at doors that have just been closed. The music knows that desire is funny when it fails, and Genji's frustration is the novel's first genuine comic deflation. Not Rossini's pure farce — Mozart has more psychological shading, more awareness that comedy and humiliation are close neighbors.

**6. Szymanowski — *Mythes*, Op. 30: 'La fontaine d'Aréthuse'**

Genji's letters to Utsusemi become increasingly urgent; her replies remain carefully evasive. Szymanowski's 'La fontaine d'Aréthuse' is desire music in the chromatic, post-Wagnerian register: the violin line is all reaching and ornament, never quite arriving at rest. It captures the specific quality of this pursuit — refined, increasingly desperate, and shaded by the knowledge that the object is escaping. The fountain of the title is a nymph who was transformed to escape a pursuer; the parallel to Utsusemi, who keeps slipping through Genji's hands, is exact.

**7. Sibelius — *Valse triste*, Op. 44**

Genji's second visit ends with Utsusemi having moved to a different pavilion. Defeated, Genji sleeps with her young brother Kogimi — a strange, melancholic substitution that the novel treats with eerie matter-of-factness. Sibelius's Valse triste is a waltz that has lost its partner, a dance that goes through the motions without joy. It gives the nocturnal mood of desire that has not so much failed as dissolved into something awkward and unresolved — not tragic, not comic, but strange.

**8. Rameau — *Les Indes galantes*: 'Rondeau des Sauvages'**

Genji's final poem compares Utsusemi to the broom tree that vanishes as you approach it. Rameau's 'Rondeau des Sauvages' is his wittiest dance movement: a tune that seems simple and cheerful but has an intricate harmonic structure underneath. It captures the chapter's final mood — comedy that has learned something, laughter that is slightly rueful. The taxonomy has met its first failure; the novel has found its method.

## The Dominant Arc

Hahakigi moves from the confidence of classification through the comedy of failure to the rueful self-awareness of its closing poem. Rossini gives the energy of talk; Bach gives the architecture of system; Fauré gives the first wound; Debussy and Szymanowski give the texture of desire; Mozart gives comic deflation; Sibelius gives the strange aftermath; Rameau gives the concluding dance, wiser and sadder than the opening. The dominant force is comedy — not the comedy of mockery but the comedy of epistemology, the laughter that comes when systems meet reality and reality wins.
