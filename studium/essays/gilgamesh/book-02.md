# Gilgamesh Tablet II: Wrestling, Recognition, Brotherhood

Tablet II is where the constructed equals become witnessed equals. Enkidu arrives in Uruk and immediately confronts Gilgamesh. The wrestling-match that follows is not a battle for dominance — it is the physical enactment of two beings discovering they cannot overcome each other. Out of that impasse, something unexpected emerges: not rivalry, but friendship. The poem's genius is to place this recognition in the body first, in the exhaustion and contact of wrestling, before it becomes articulate speech. By the time Enkidu speaks his vow of companionship, we already know what his body has testified: Gilgamesh is no longer alone.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Debussy — "Pagodes"**
Enkidu has never walked through a city. The shepherds bring him to Uruk. He experiences human architecture, crowds, ritual, walls. Everything the wilderness taught him — solitude, animal time, open horizons — is suddenly contained within constructed space. Debussy's Pagodes captures this: the piece emerges from harmonic mist into patterns that suggest an entire ordered world. The pentatonic colors borrowed from gamelan music suggest a civilization perceived as *strange and complete* — alien to him, but undeniably present. Pagodes is not celebratory. It is the sound of astonishment before an organized world.

**2. Messiaen — "Liturgie de cristal"**
The people of Uruk see Enkidu. A being arrives who is neither wild animal nor entirely human. His appearance is a revelation — something unknown made suddenly visible. Messiaen's opening movement of the Quartet combines bird-song (the voice of the unseen, the natural world) with instrumental voices that are crystalline and absolute. The piece establishes a new world without argument, and there is no compromise between the natural and the structured. Enkidu's appearance has that same quality: an interruption that changes everything.

**3. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5, III**
Gilgamesh goes to his customary right: he will sleep with a bride on her wedding night. Enkidu blocks his path. This is not chance — it is Aruru's creation working exactly as designed. Beethoven's Scherzo is the sound of cosmic machinery: rhythmic, relentless, driving forward. The third movement of the Fifth captures fate as something inevitable and yet active — not passive destiny, but the collision of two forces that cannot avoid meeting. The music has no option for mercy or detour.

**4. Holst — "Mars, the Bringer of War"**
The wrestling-match begins. Neither combatant holds back. The tablets describe their strength with extraordinary specificity — this is not metaphorical struggle but the physical exertion of two beings at the limit of human power. Holst's Mars is hostile energy in 5/4 time: asymmetrical, driving, unable to settle into rest. The piece has no resolution because the wrestling has none — it is pure wrath, the force that animates two wills in direct collision. No victor appears yet; only the intensity of the struggle itself.

**5. Stravinsky — "The Ritual of the Abduction"**
Still wrestling. Still locked together. Stravinsky's abduction scene is violence organized into ritual pattern: the music is brutal but structured, raw but ceremonial. This is the second phase of the match — no longer the initial shock, but the sustained, intricate contact of two equal forces. Each move meets resistance; the wrestling has become a dance without beauty, a ritual without mercy. The piece captures wrath that has lost all urgency and become pure, sustained pressure.

**6. Bach — *The Art of Fugue*, Contrapunctus I**
The wrestling resolves. One yields (the tablets give varying accounts; the narrative point is that yielding happens). In that moment, a structure becomes audible. Bach's first Contrapunctus is two voices in perfect mathematical counterpoint — neither leads, both follow, neither dominates. The law discovered in wrestling is the law of equality itself: neither Gilgamesh nor Enkidu can impose force, so both must accept form. The fugue is not imposed from without; it emerges from the voices' necessity to relate to each other.

**7. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*, Prelude to Act III**
The wrestling ends. Gilgamesh and Enkidu look at each other. In that moment, something fundamental shifts from battle into recognition. This is not the Prelude to Act I (Shamhat's unresolved sexual desire). This is Act III's prelude: desire that has moved through suffering and arrived at threshold. The music is yearning, but yearning that has become absolute and quiet. Wagner here captures what the tablets enact: the moment wrath transmutes into something that has desire's intensity but a different quality — the desire to remain with, to witness, to be witnessed.

**8. Mahler — Symphony No. 5, IV. Adagietto**
Gilgamesh weeps. In finding someone he cannot defeat, he grieves. The grief is complex: for his solitude before this meeting, for the knowledge that equality requires vulnerability, for the bond he has just entered that will bind him to another's fate. Mahler's Adagietto is the sound of opening to another — not eagerly, but inevitably. The movement is private, inward, and absolute: not the public grief of lament, but the interior recognition that something final has shifted.

**9. Schubert — String Quintet in C Major, IV. Allegretto**
Enkidu speaks. He who was wild, who has only recently learned human language, now articulates a vow: he will be Gilgamesh's companion. The tablets present this speech simply, without embellishment — yet it is the turning-point of the whole narrative. Schubert's final string quintet slow movement is the most deeply human sound in chamber music: affection without sentimentality, strength without aggression, vulnerability that is also solidity. The Allegretto captures what Enkidu's words testify: desire in a new register — not eros, not conquest, but the desire to remain, to serve, to be true.

**10. Fauré — Pavane**
They embrace. The movement has become formal, ceremonial, structured. Fauré's Pavane is a dance and a procession at once — it moves with weight and dignity. The embrace itself is the moment friendship becomes social: what was individual recognition becomes a bond with legal weight. From this moment forward, Gilgamesh and Enkidu are obligated to each other. The Pavane's stately progress captures this: the bond is now enacted in the public sphere, confirmed, given form.

**11. Brahms — Symphony No. 3, III. Poco allegretto**
Gilgamesh brings Enkidu back to the palace, to his mother Ninsun. The episode moves toward return and homecoming. But this is not a simple homecoming — Gilgamesh is returning with someone who was not there before, and he himself has been changed by the wrestling. Brahms' third symphony slow movement is gentle, grateful, and shadowed: you return to a place, but you are not who left it. The music acknowledges both the familiarity of home and the impossibility of truly returning to it.

**12. Messiaen — *Harawi*, "La Ville Celeste"**
Ninsun sees Enkidu and recognizes him immediately. Her divine knowledge confirms what the mortals have enacted. The friendship that emerged from wrestling is now blessed and affirmed by the divine order itself. Messiaen's celestial city is luminous and absolute — not questioning, but declaring. The piece has the temperature of divine certainty: what the gods set in motion, what mortals have enacted, and what the divine order confirms all align. But Ninsun's blessing, which seems final and joyous, will later be revealed as the prelude to devastation — she is blessing Enkidu into his death.

## The Dominant Arc of Tablet II

```
metamorphosis → revelation → fate → wrath → wrath → law →
desire → grief → desire → law → return → divine-intervention
```

The arc moves from astonishment through conflict into recognition. Enkidu's arrival in Uruk is a revelation that displaces Gilgamesh's solitude. The wrestling-match is wrath at its purest — two forces unable to overcome each other must discover a new grammar. That grammar emerges as law and desire in tandem: the law of their equality, the desire to remain together. The dominant force is **desire**, but not the desire of conquest or eros. This is desire as the binding force that creates loyalty, companionship, and the vulnerability that comes with witnessing another being. By the end of Tablet II, Gilgamesh has found his match, his mirror, his doom.
