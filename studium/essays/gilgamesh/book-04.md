# Gilgamesh Tablet IV: The Journey and the Dreams

Tablet IV is the march. The heroes cover impossible distances, crossing from the known world of Uruk into the mythic space of the Cedar Forest. But the physical journey is secondary to the psychological one. Each night, Gilgamesh—the fearless king—is plunged into nightmares of crushing mountains, wild bulls, and raining fire. Enkidu, the former wild man, becomes the anchor, interpreting the nightmares as divine promises of victory. By the time they reach the edge of the forest, the balance of power has shifted: the king is terrified, and the wild man must urge him forward.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Vaughan Williams — *A London Symphony*, II**
They travel an inhuman distance: fifty leagues a day, crossing vast tracts of wilderness. The music needs a sense of endless, wandering space, distant from the walls of Uruk. Vaughan Williams's Lento is expansive, open, and slightly desolate.

**2. Fauré — *Pavane***
At the end of three days, they stop to pitch camp and dig for water. The rhythmic repetition of the journey gives way to the fading light and the quiet rituals of survival. Fauré's Pavane offers a nostalgic, faded recapitulation, a brief moment of gentle humanity before the terrors of the night.

**3. Bach — *The Art of Fugue*, I**
Enkidu draws a magic circle of flour on the ground to protect Gilgamesh while he seeks a dream from Shamash. This is the application of ancient, geometric ritual to ward off chaos. Bach's precise, architectural counterpoint is the perfect sonic equivalent to drawing a circle of order in the dirt.

**4. Mahler — *Symphony No. 6*, I**
Gilgamesh wakes in terror: a mountain fell on them. The sheer, crushing weight of the omen requires inexorable, hammering force. Mahler's 6th opens with exactly this relentless, fatalistic march.

**5. Pärt — *Cantus***
Enkidu turns the terror into promise: the mountain is Humbaba, and it falls because they will defeat him. Pärt's descending bell and strings provide a chilling but luminous stillness—the omen is translated from fear into divine truth.

**6. Wagner — *Das Rheingold***
Gilgamesh dreams of a terrifying wild bull, but someone brings him water. Enkidu interprets: the bull is not their enemy, it is Shamash the protector. The sudden realization of divine presence shielding them demands bright, massive brass.

**7. Stravinsky — *The Firebird***
The sky goes dark, thunder rolls, fire rains down and turns to ash. This is the pure, chaotic violence of the coming battle with Humbaba manifesting in sleep. Stravinsky's Infernal Dance provides the jagged, rhythmic pressure and unresolved terror.

**8. Sibelius — *Tapiola***
They finally reach the gate of the forest. Gilgamesh's courage falters; they stare into the dark, towering cedars. Sibelius's Tapiola is the ultimate evocation of ancient, terrifying, non-human forest spirits. It is ominous, vast, and completely indifferent to the heroes.

## The Dominant Arc of Tablet IV

```
exile → memory → law → fate → revelation → divine-intervention → wrath → fate
```

The arc traces the long wandering (exile) into the nightly rituals (law), before descending cyclically into the nightmares of the king (fate, wrath) and the saving interpretations of his companion (revelation). The dominant force is **fate**, pressing down on them with every league they travel and every dream they suffer.
