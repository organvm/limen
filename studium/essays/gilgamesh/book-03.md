# Gilgamesh Tablet III: The Journey Begins — Toward Humbaba

Tablet III marks the moment when the domestic drama of Uruk gives way to the heroic narrative proper. Gilgamesh and Enkidu, newly bound in friendship, choose to undertake a quest that is not motivated by need, hunger, or necessity. They choose to walk toward a known danger: the Cedar Forest, guarded by Humbaba, a monster whose very voice can shatter mountains. This is the moment when friendship becomes tested by the possibility of shared death. The tablet is suffused with a peculiar knowledge: they are walking toward something that may destroy them, and they are walking together. The dominant movement is fate — the inexorable pull of destiny that draws them forward — alongside exile, the knowledge that departure from Uruk means entering a world governed by different laws.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Stravinsky — *The Firebird*, "The Sacrifice" Suite**
Gilgamesh announces the plan with the weight of decree: he and Enkidu will journey to the Cedar Forest and slay Humbaba. This is not a suggestion or a proposal — it is a pronouncement that establishes a new order. Stravinsky's Firebird is ceremonial and magical, introducing the world of supernatural trial that the narrative is entering. The piece has ritual weight without grandeur, establishing that what follows is formal and irreversible. The decision to depart is law in the sense that it establishes the rules by which the rest of the story will proceed.

**2. Strauss — *Salome*, Dance of the Seven Veils**
Enkidu counsels caution. He knows the Cedar Forest; he knows Humbaba's voice, which is described as a terrible force. His warning is not a refusal but a revelation: in warning Gilgamesh of what awaits, Enkidu is also testifying to the knowledge he carries from his wild life. Strauss's dance reveals hidden knowledge with each gesture — it is sensuous, knowing, and utterly serious. Enkidu's desire to protect Gilgamesh, to warn him while remaining committed to him, is captured in this music: protective vigilance, not fear, but awareness of danger held in tandem with loyalty.

**3. Bruckner — Symphony No. 4, I. Allegro (Romantic)**
The expedition assembles. Soldiers, companions, servants, craftsmen — each addition to the company changes what the journey is. The enterprise grows from personal quest to public enterprise. Bruckner's Romantic Symphony first movement is the sound of something expanding into grandeur: the brass enters gradually, the full orchestra assembles, and there is a sense of mounting momentum that cannot be stopped. The gathering is ascent in the sense that each addition lifts the journey toward something greater and more public than what Gilgamesh and Enkidu could accomplish alone.

**4. Wagner — *Die Walküre*, "Ride of the Valkyries"**
Gilgamesh addresses the assembled company. His words are not gentle or inspirational in the modern sense. He is asking men to walk toward danger, toward a confrontation with a force that has survived and conquered every warrior who came before. Wagner's Valkyries is the sound of human will organized into collective military force — not peaceful, not hesitant, but determined. The wrath here is the militant energy that transforms fear into resolve. The music captures the moment when a leader speaks to those who will follow him toward possible death.

**5. Sibelius — Symphony No. 4, I. Tempo molto moderato**
They depart. The gates of Uruk close behind them. The city recedes. There is a threshold being crossed that cannot be recrossed — from the governed, the known, the familiar, into wilderness. Sibelius's fourth symphony is stark and austere: angular harmonies, sparse orchestration, a landscape that offers no comfort. The exile force is activated the moment the gates close — they are no longer citizens of Uruk with the protections that citizenship offers. They are wanderers in a world that has its own laws.

**6. Beethoven — Symphony No. 3, I. Allegro con brio (Eroica)**
The journey unfolds. They walk. Days pass. The landscape changes. With each step, they move closer to Humbaba. Beethoven's Eroica is the sound of an inexorable momentum: not questioning whether the path is wise, but enacting it as necessary. The music is grand and heroic, but not because heroism is celebrated — because the forces that drive them forward are too large to resist. Fate in Beethoven is not external destiny imposed on unwilling subjects; it is the natural unfolding of powers that were set in motion long before this moment.

**7. Elgar — Cello Concerto, IV. Allegro**
As they travel, Gilgamesh remembers his dreams from Tablet I: the meteorite he loved, the axe he carried to his mother. Those dreams foretold this journey. Enkidu was the companion promised; this quest is the trial foretold. Memory here is not sentimental reflection but the understanding that the present fulfills what was foreseen. Elgar's concerto finale is intimate and personal — a solo voice in conversation with the orchestra, reflecting on hard-won understanding. The memory force captures how retrospection becomes clear the moment it arrives at proof.

**8. Barber — Adagio for Strings**
Enkidu prays. In the wilderness, approaching Humbaba's domain, he calls upon the gods for protection. It is a plea without certainty of answer — a supplication made in the face of darkness. Barber's Adagio is the sound of human vulnerability before forces larger than itself: exposed, unadorned, holding no false hope of rescue, yet dignified and unbroken. The prayer goes unanswered — the gods do not intervene. But the act of praying testifies to something essential in the human confrontation with the divine and the unknown.

**9. Penderecki — Threnody for the Victims of Hiroshima**
The Cedar Forest comes into view. With it comes the knowledge of Humbaba's presence — the guardian is real, is vast, is terrible. This is revelation as terror: something unknown becomes suddenly visible, and what is visible exceeds human scale. Penderecki's Threnody has no beauty in the conventional sense; it is the sound of forces overwhelming human capacity. The approach to Humbaba's domain has that register: not excitement, but the moment when abstract danger becomes overwhelmingly, undeniably real.

**10. Gluck — *Iphigénie en Aulide*, Prelude to Act I**
They stand at the threshold. The moment of sacrifice is approaching. They know that crossing the forest's edge may mean death. The sacrifice has not yet been enacted, but it has been chosen. Gluck's Iphigénie prelude is the music of the threshold: solemn, aware of what is being given up, but resolved. The music does not negotiate or hesitate. It acknowledges what is being offered and moves toward it with ceremonial weight.

**11. Mahler — Symphony No. 8, Part II (Mystical Chorus)**
Gilgamesh walks first into the Cedar Forest. His companions follow. This is ascent toward the trial that will define him. Mahler's eighth symphony second part is the sound of movement toward transcendence: there is triumph here, and also the knowledge that transcendence may be indistinguishable from annihilation. Gilgamesh walks into darkness knowing he may not return. The ascent is toward something greater than himself, and that greatness may destroy him.

**12. Tchaikovsky — Symphony No. 4, I. Andante sostenuto**
The forest has closed behind them. The way back is obscured. The machinery of their fate is fully engaged. Tchaikovsky's fourth symphony opens with a fateful motif: the sound of inescapable destiny that was not chosen but is now being enacted. They have crossed the threshold. The Cedar Forest will yield only to conquest or death. Fate is sealed.

## The Dominant Arc of Tablet III

```
law → desire → ascent → wrath → exile → fate →
memory → supplication → revelation → sacrifice → ascent → fate
```

The arc moves from decision through assembly to departure and beyond into the unknown. Gilgamesh announces the quest as law — it is formal, binding, irreversible. The companions gather in ascent, the company swells. But with departure comes exile: the moment the gates close, they are no longer citizens of Uruk. Then fate takes hold — inexorable, grand, indifferent to their desires. They remember, they pray, they approach the revelation of danger. And by the end, they are fully committed to a fate from which there is no turning back. The dominant force is **fate**, but a fate that is actively chosen. They walk toward Humbaba knowing what awaits, and they walk together.
