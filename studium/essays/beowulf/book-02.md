# Beowulf Fitt II: The Sea-Road and the Coastguard
## A Music Arc for the Second Division

Fitt II shifts the poem from the paralyzed grief of Denmark to the deliberate, kinetic action of the Geats. The movement is defined by forward motion and institutional collision. We see Beowulf's quiet decision, the swift sea-crossing over wind-driven waves, and the sudden, bureaucratic confrontation with the Danish coastguard on the beach. This is not yet combat—it is the assertion of presence, fate organizing itself into military and diplomatic reality.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Sibelius — *En Saga***
The decision to cross the sea is not presented as hot-headed heroism but as an inevitable aligning of the man with the moment. Sibelius's tone poem captures this brooding, inevitable gathering of momentum. It sounds like a legend beginning to take physical shape.

**2. Stravinsky — *Symphony of Psalms*, I. Exaudi orationem meam**
The loading of the ship is described with technical, almost ritualistic care: the carrying of bright weapons, the securing of the armor. This is not chaos but deep military order. Stravinsky's austere, driving rhythmic blocks convey the strict, deliberate geometry of men preparing for war.

**3. Debussy — *La Mer*, I. De l'aube à midi sur la mer**
The sea in Beowulf is both a highway and a hostile expanse. The voyage itself is triumphant, described as a bird flying over the water. Debussy's ascent into light captures the sheer elemental speed and lift of the crossing, an unironic forward rush out of the dark.

**4. Pärt — *Fratres* (for strings and percussion)**
They see the high hills and broad headlands. The voyage is over. Pärt's *Fratres*, with its sudden stillness and ancient, ringing chords, gives exactly this feeling of arriving at a massive, silent geography. It is the sudden drop in kinetic energy as the ship strikes sand.

**5. Prokofiev — *Alexander Nevsky*, The Crusaders in Pskov**
A Danish watchman sees armored men landing and immediately rides his horse down to the beach. This is the state responding to an incursion. Prokofiev's heavy, rhythmic brass matches the weight of a lone, armed rider coming down the cliff to demand order. It is institutional machinery functioning exactly as it should.

**6. Wagner — *Lohengrin*, Prelude to Act III**
The coastguard does not attack; he speaks. He demands to know their lineage and purpose, noting Beowulf's sheer physical majesty. Wagner's Prelude is martial but highly formal, aristocratic, and proud—the exact register of two nobles assessing each other's worth across the length of a spear.

**7. Verdi — *La Forza del Destino*, Overture**
Fitt II closes suspended in the tension of the standoff, before Beowulf speaks. The air is thick with the possibility of violence, though the protocol holds. Verdi's overture provides the necessary driving, hammered insistence of an encounter that must now be resolved by words or blood.

---

## The Dominant Arc

The arc of Fitt II moves: **fate → law → ascent → revelation → law → kingship → fate**. The dominant force is law—not the law of courts, but the strict order of men, ships, and borders. The Geats operate with immense procedural discipline, matching the disciplined watchfulness of the Danes. The arc begins and ends with fate, framing human order within the broader inevitability of the encounter.
