# Beowulf Fitt II: Grendel in Full
## A Music Arc for the Creature's Dominion

Fitt II is structured as a perverse mirror of Fitt I. Where Fitt I established human civilisation rising to its zenith (Scyld's glory, Heorot's building, Hrothgar's kingship), Fitt II traces a counter-rise: Grendel's establishment of his own dominion. This is not the monster as obstacle but the monster as a theological figure — descended from Cain, exiled from light and fellowship, whose exclusion from human community is written into his genealogy. When Grendel hears the creation-song from inside Heorot, he experiences the precise measure of what he cannot be. The fitt then moves into action: the eruption, the taking of men, the establishment of his terrible kingship. For twelve years, Grendel rules the night; Hrothgar rules the day. Neither can dislodge the other. The music arc traces this arc: from curse through exile to wrath, then the settling into a grim equilibrium of unrelenting grief.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 8, I. Adagio**

Grendel is introduced in the context of his genealogy, not his deeds. He is described as descended from Cain — the first murderer, cursed by God to wander the earth, marked with a sign so that no one who found him would kill him. This genealogical weight is theological: Grendel's exclusion is not circumstantial but metaphysical, written into his origin. Shostakovich's First Movement of the Eighth Symphony carries exactly this sense of ancient, inherited doom — the music opens in chromatic darkness, slow, pressed, with the weight of a history that has already determined its conclusion before any action occurs. The Eighth was written in Soviet extremity; it knows the sound of a life cursed before it could be lived.

**2. Sibelius — *Tapiola***

Grendel dwells in the marshes and fens, on unreclaimed moorland where no Dane would venture. Sibelius' only tone-poem captures the essence of this exile made geography: the music is not beautiful but austere, vast, ancient — the sound of a wilderness that precedes and will outlast human civilisation. Tapiola is notable for having no triumphant moment; it simply *is*, indifferent, older than the listener. When Grendel inhabits this landscape, the music establishes that his exile is not punishment imposed by human choice but a state that exists prior to and beyond human judgment. He belongs to the moors the way darkness belongs to midnight — it is not a metaphor but a fact.

**3. Stravinsky — *The Rite of Spring*, 'The Sacrificial Dance'**

The turning point: Grendel hears the feast in Heorot — the joy, the music, the warmth — and feels his heart swell with wrath. This is the moment where his condition transforms from static misery into active rage. Stravinsky's Sacrificial Dance is the right music for this ignition: its irregular, driving rhythms, its sense of primitive power building toward inevitable violence, its ritualistic quality. The piece does not sound like human anger but like the eruption of a force that will consume its object. Grendel will become, in effect, a priest offering sacrifice — the bodies of Danes as his counter-eucharist to Heorot's feast.

**4. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*, Prelude**

Grendel leaves his lair and crosses the dark moors toward Heorot, drawn by light and sound. The Tristan Prelude — Wagner's masterpiece of unresolved longing — carries the dread tension of this approach. The music is suspended, climbing toward a resolution that never quite arrives, the chromatic harmony suggesting a crossing of thresholds that cannot be uncrossed. Grendel moves toward light he cannot ever enter, toward a feast he will violently claim. The prelude's characteristic suspension and impossible resolution are the sound of Grendel's predicament made audible: the thing he hears is the exact measure of what he will never be.

**5. Bartók — *Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta*, II. Allegro**

The violence itself is not described in detail in the poem — the poem shows the aftermath, the bodies, the grief. Bartók's second movement is violence as *structure*: driving ostinatos, pizzicato strings like percussion, the movement relentless and terrible and utterly controlled. Unlike the Adagio (used in Fitt I for the silence after attack), this movement *is* the action — Grendel moving through the sleeping hall, taking thirty men. The music is implacable, exact, the sound of fate executing itself through Grendel's will.

**6. Penderecki — *Stabat Mater***

The taking is followed by the removal: Grendel drags the bodies back to his lair, establishing his dwelling-place as a repository of the dead. Penderecki's Stabat Mater — a work of profound rage at human suffering — inverts itself here: it becomes the music of the sufferer becoming the taker, the one who stands at the foot of the cross becoming the one who puts the bodies on the cross. The work's dissonant harmonies and its quality of grief made violent capture the moment where Grendel transforms a mere den into a throne-room, where violence becomes possession.

**7. Grieg — *In the Hall of the Mountain King***

Dawn comes, and the Danes discover the horror: the blood, the torn bodies, and Grendel's severed arm (he escaped but left proof of his mortality). The discovery is an absolute reversal of the order established in Fitt I. The greatest hall in the world reveals itself as penetrable, as a place where the Danes' might and wisdom have been catastrophically inadequate. Grieg's cavern-music — menacing, vast, its regular rhythms suggesting something non-human and relentless — transforms Heorot from a place of safety into a violated space. The music carries the weight of the Danes' understanding: something entered the hall, took what it wanted, and left. The threshold between inside and outside, civilisation and wilderness, has been breached.

**8. Mahler — Symphony No. 6, IV. Finale**

But the attack does not end. It *begins* the reign. Grendel returns night after night for twelve years — the same duration that dominates Fitt I, now inverted. Where Fitt I focused on the Danes' sustained grief under Grendel's attacks, Fitt II tracks the establishment of Grendel's own dominion: he is a king, ruling the night, his subjects the creatures of darkness. Mahler's Sixth Finale — one of the great collapse-musics in the canon, with its repeated hammer blows, its relentless downward motion — captures this long occupation. The music does not dramatize; it *endures*, pressing further and further downward with implacable force.

**9. Vaughan Williams — *The Lark Ascending***

The great hall, built as a monument to civilisation and joy, stands empty at night. The Danes sleep elsewhere; Heorot becomes a tomb, a place of terror. Vaughan Williams' Lark Ascending — one of the most luminous pieces in music, celebrating English pastoral beauty — is used here as inversion: the soaring melody and the clean orchestration are rendered uncanny by context. The music asks: what does beauty sound like when it is exile? What does an ascending melody sound like in a hall that has been abandoned to darkness?

**10. Messiaen — *Oraison pour le silence de l'amour***

The fitt closes not with action but with stasis: twelve years without remedy, without hope. Grendel's dominion is absolute. Messiaen's Prayer for the Silence of Love is among his most desolate works — a prayer offered to void rather than to divinity, language addressed to the absence of response. No counsel, no prayer to heathen gods, no human resource has produced any path forward. The fitt ends with Grendel in complete and unquestioned reign. The music holds this silence.

## The Dominant Arc

The arc of Fitt II moves: **fate → exile → wrath → wrath → fate → wrath → fate → grief → fate → wrath**. The dominant force is wrath — not the wrath of battle but the perverse eruption of a being whose entire existence is condemnation. The playlist traces this trajectory: from the inherited curse in Shostakovich, through the exile-landscape of Tapiola, into the double eruption (Stravinsky's ritual wrath and Wagner's impossible approach), then into the structured violence of Bartók, the possession-music of Penderecki, the discovery-shock of Grieg, the long grinding of Mahler, the inversion of Vaughan Williams, and the desolate prayer of Messiaen. The arc does not resolve. It establishes. By the close of Fitt II, Grendel is not a problem to be solved but a *condition* to be endured.
