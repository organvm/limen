# Beowulf Fitt V: Arrival and the Law of Hospitality
## A Music Arc for the Threshold of the Court

Fitt V is the transition from the external world (the sea, the shore) to the internal world (the hall, the court, the seat of civilised authority). The fitt opens with the coast-guard — the sentinel who guards Hrothgar's realm — challenging the strangers who have landed on the Danish shore. This is the first test: not of strength but of courtesy, language, and rightful purpose. Beowulf answers with perfect clarity: he names himself, his father, his lineage, his errand. He does not boast but declares himself with absolute authority. The poem emphasizes the hero's courtesy alongside his confidence — he asks for the right to attempt the trial rather than claiming it by force. The coast-guard, recognizing the quality of the stranger, grants passage and directs the heroes toward Heorot. The fitt then traces the approach: seeing the hall in its glory, standing at its doors, entering and addressing Hrothgar formally. Hrothgar accepts the hero's petition and grants him the right to fight Grendel. The law is restored in this acceptance: the king exercises his authority to receive aid, and the hero exercises his right to offer it. The music arc traces this entire threshold-crossing, from the external challenge to the internal acceptance.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Brahms — Symphony No. 4, III. Scherzo**

The coast-guard of Denmark sees the warriors landing on the shore and immediately moves to challenge them. This is not hostility but law: the sentinel who guards the realm at its boundary. Brahms' Scherzo from the Fourth — rigorous, alert, with a quality of formal inspection and seriousness — captures the moment of challenge. The music is the sound of law being asserted at a threshold. It is not welcoming, but it is clear: the boundary is guarded, and those who cross it must declare themselves.

**2. Handel — *Zadok the Priest***

Beowulf answers the coast-guard's challenge: he names himself, his father (Ecgtheow), his grandfather (Hrethel, king of the Geats), and his errand. There is no boasting here — the poem carefully marks Beowulf's courtesy and clarity — but there is absolute authority. When Beowulf speaks, his words carry the weight of genuine kingship behind them. Handel's Zadok — the coronation music, the sound of legitimate authority and rightful lineage — is exactly right for this moment. The coast-guard hears a man of consequence and recognizes it immediately.

**3. Fauré — *Pavane***

Though Beowulf is confident and of high lineage, his approach to Hrothgar is formally supplicatory. He comes to *ask* for the right to fight Grendel, not to claim it by force or right of birth. This balance is crucial to the poem's logic: even the hero must work within the law of kingship and courtesy. Fauré's Pavane — slow, measured, deeply respectful — captures the quality of formal petition and approach. It is not weakness but grace: strength held in courtesy, confidence balanced with deference.

**4. Bach — *The Art of Fugue*, Contrapunctus I**

The coast-guard makes his judgment: these are warriors of genuine quality, their purpose is righteous, and they may pass. The judgment is made according to law — the evaluation of character, excellence, and purpose. Bach's Art of Fugue — the music of absolute logical clarity and structure — captures the moment of evaluation. There is no ambiguity here; the law sees what it requires and recognizes it when it appears.

**5. Scriabin — *Poem of Ecstasy***

The coast-guard grants passage and directs the heroes toward Heorot. The threshold opens. Scriabin's Ecstasy — luminous, ascending, the sense of a boundary falling away — captures the moment where the external test (proving oneself worthy of entry) is passed and the internal trial (the actual encounter with the court) opens ahead. The music is the quality of being received and welcomed into a realm previously closed to strangers.

**6. Elgar — *Pomp and Circumstance*, March No. 1**

Walking inland, the heroes see Heorot for the first time: the great hall blazing with light and gold, Hrothgar's seat of power, the visible proof of what the realm has built. Elgar's Pomp and Circumstance — grand, ceremonial, the music of human achievement and civilisational splendor — captures the sight of the hall in its unironic glory. This is not mockery but recognition: Heorot is worth saving precisely because it is magnificent, and Grendel's attacks are terrible precisely because they threaten this splendor.

**7. Pärt — *Tabula Rasa***

The heroes reach the doors of Heorot and stand before them. The poem marks this moment with silence and weight: they are about to enter the hall where Grendel has killed so many, the place where the greatest warriors of Denmark have died. Pärt's Tabula Rasa — stark, clear, the sound of approaching something sacred and dangerous at once — captures the quality of standing at this final threshold. The door is about to open; everything will change.

**8. Barber — *Knoxville: Summer of 1915***

Beowulf enters Heorot and addresses Hrothgar formally. He recounts his lineage, his knowledge of Hrothgar's suffering, his determination to fight Grendel. He does not demand the right but petitions for it. Barber's Knoxville — lyrical, earnest, the music of human clarity and grace — captures the quality of this formal speech. Beowulf is utterly confident in his capacity, but he approaches the king with perfect courtesy. The music is strength held in deference, confidence expressed through proper form.

**9. Giovanni Gabrieli — *Canzona per sonare 'La Spiritata'***

Hrothgar accepts Beowulf's petition. The king recognizes the quality of the man and grants him the right to fight Grendel. The law is restored: the king exercises his authority to receive aid, and the hero exercises his right to offer it. Gabrieli's brass canzona — brilliant, clear, the sound of ceremony and proclamation — marks the moment where kingship re-establishes order through acceptance. The law is working properly; justice is beginning to move.

**10. Messiaen — *Visions of the Amen*, 'Amen of the Desires'**

The fitt closes as Beowulf and his companions enter Heorot, accepted, received, ready for the night that will come. Messiaen's Amen — ecstatic, ascending, the sense of moving toward something transcendent — marks the completion of the formal threshold and the opening of the actual trial. The law has been satisfied; the way is clear. The hero stands in the hall; Grendel comes.

## The Dominant Arc

The arc of Fitt V moves: **law → kingship → supplication → law → desire → kingship → supplication → supplication → law → ascent**. The dominant force is law — the orderly framework within which heroism becomes possible and meaningful. The playlist traces this arc: from Brahms' assertion of boundary through Handel's declaration of lineage, Fauré's formal petition, Bach's evaluation, Scriabin's opening of passage, Elgar's vision of the hall, Pärt's sacred threshold, Barber's formal speech, Gabrieli's proclamation, and finally Messiaen's ecstatic acceptance. The fitt does not resolve the trial; it opens the way toward it. The hero is accepted; the night waits.
