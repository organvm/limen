# Beowulf Fitt IV: The Boast and the King
## A Music Arc for the Fourth Division

Fitt IV is the political center of the first act. Beowulf stands before Hrothgar and makes his formal boast, pledging to fight Grendel bare-handed. Hrothgar replies not with triumph but with memory, recalling Beowulf's father and the terrible toll Grendel has taken. The arc contrasts the young hero's kinetic certitude with the old king's heavy, accumulated sorrow.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Fauré — *Requiem*, In Paradisum**
Before Beowulf even enters, Hrothgar hears his name and declares that God has sent him as a rescue against Grendel's terror. It is a moment of profound, sudden hope breaking through a twelve-year nightmare. Fauré's resolving radiance perfectly captures this quiet, almost disbelieving salvation—the first ray of light in the hall.

**2. Walton — *Crown Imperial***
Beowulf walks into the hall, his ring-mail shining, and stands before the king. This is sovereign display at its most brilliant: the greatest warrior of his age presenting himself to the court. Walton's march is unapologetically regal, matching the sheer physical and visual impact of Beowulf taking the floor.

**3. Stravinsky — *Dumbarton Oaks*, I. Tempo giusto**
Beowulf does not just promise to fight; he lays out the precise terms of the combat. Because Grendel uses no weapons, Beowulf will use no weapons. It is a binding legal and heroic contract. Stravinsky's neoclassical clarity reflects this rigid, structured commitment—it is the sound of a vow being locked into place.

**4. Gluck — *Iphigénie en Tauride*, O Toi, Qui Prolongeas Mes Jours**
Having made his boast, Beowulf calmly acknowledges that he might die, instructing Hrothgar to send his armor back to Hygelac if Grendel takes him. There is no fear, only ritual weight. Gluck's aria carries exactly this noble, sacrificial readiness—a willingness to be consumed by the act without complaint.

**5. Rachmaninoff — Symphony No. 2, III. Adagio**
Hrothgar does not immediately celebrate. Instead, his mind drifts back decades to when he settled a feud for Beowulf's father. It is a deeply nostalgic, paternal speech, anchoring the present crisis in old loyalties. Rachmaninoff's Adagio provides the necessary faded recapitulation, the long, sweeping backward glance of an old man remembering a younger world.

**6. Mahler — Symphony No. 5, IV. Adagietto**
Hrothgar then speaks of his own depleted ranks, the men who boasted they could stop Grendel and were found dead in the morning, the hall stained with blood. This is intimate, inward pain. Mahler's Adagietto, unlike the collective grief of Barber used in Fitt I, fits this personal, exhaustion-hollowed confession of a king who has outlived his protectors.

**7. Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel***
The formal speeches conclude. Hrothgar bids Beowulf sit at the feast. A bench is cleared, a cup-bearer pours bright mead, and a scop sings clearly in Heorot. It is a restoration of the hall's true purpose, an island of stillness before the night falls. Pärt provides this breathless, suspended peace, a moment of grace functioning against the dark.

---

## The Dominant Arc

The arc of Fitt IV moves: **salvation → kingship → law → sacrifice → memory → grief → revelation**. The dominant force is memory. Hrothgar's response grounds the hero's present bravado in past obligations and past losses, pulling the narrative backward before it can move forward. The music descends from the high regality of Walton into the intimate sorrows of Rachmaninoff and Mahler, finally resting in Pärt's luminous stillness.
