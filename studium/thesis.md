# Working Thesis for the Whole Project

You are not just reading "great books." You are studying **how civilizations encode**:

```
violence · law · memory · fate · sacrifice · speech · revelation ·
empire · desire · death · return · transformation · salvation · comedy
```

The daily composition is not background music — it is a **second commentary system**.
The handwriting is not decorative — it is a **third commentary system**.
A film that *relates* is not illustration — it is a **fourth commentary system**: the same force
(wrath, fate, grief, law) given a body in image and time, often by a film from anywhere — Vietnam,
the Eastern Front, a samurai apocalypse — that encodes it more truthfully than any adaptation could.
Translation is the place where the book stops being an object and becomes an **encounter**.

Each text in the canon is one tradition's answer to one of these encodings — and read together,
across cultures and in their own scripts, they become a single conversation the species has been
having with itself. The Eastern works are not "extra world literature"; they are **counter-systems**
to the Greek-Roman-Abrahamic-medieval line — the Gita against Achilles, the Dao against force, the
Heike against the Iliad's martial glory.

And this is not, finally, a solitary act. A professor uses the class as a **breeding zone and a war
zone** for the ideas of the book they are writing. The Studium is built to become that — a multi-medium
**seminar** where the same passage is read, copied, translated, scored, and *screened*, and where a
claim about it is **staged, defended, and battle-tested** against the canon's own counter-systems. Every
interaction across those mediums is captured as **data** (`analysis/`), so the conversation the species
has been having with itself becomes one a community can have in public — and learn from, measurably. The
mediums are the surfaces of interaction; the trials are the breeding of ideas; the data is the notebook.
