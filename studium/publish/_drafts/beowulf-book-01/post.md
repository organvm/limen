# Beowulf Fitt I: Glory, Grendel, Twelve Years of Night
## A Music Arc for the Opening Division

Beowulf's opening is the moment where civilisation states its own fragility. Within roughly 190 lines, the poem builds a world — a founding king, a genealogy, a golden hall — and immediately begins to unmake it. The great mead-hall Heorot rises at Hrothgar's command and is emptied within months. The scop sings the creation of the world inside a hall that a creature of darkness is already circling outside. Fitt I is not a prologue to the poem's conflict; it *is* the conflict, stated in full, before Beowulf has spoken a word. The music arc follows this double movement: ascending into civilisational grandeur, then descending into grief that endures twelve years without relief.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Elgar — *Enigma Variations*, 'Nimrod' (Variation IX)**

The poem opens not with Beowulf, and not with Grendel, but with Scyld Scefing — an ancestor who arrived from the sea as a foundling and grew to greatness, who forced tribute from neighbouring peoples, who was mourned by the whole nation when he died. 'Hwæt!' is a hall-cry, a demand for attention from a live audience in a mead-hall, and what it demands attention for is *reputation* (dom): the record of the great. 'Nimrod' carries exactly this quality. It is not triumphant or coronational — it is commemorative, civic, retrospective. The slow swell is how a people sound when they are remembering someone who mattered. Elgar's 'Enigma' was composed in another late, elegiac culture; the variation's warmth is indistinguishable from the warmth of the poem's opening tribute. A more fanfare-like piece (Walton's *Crown Imperial*, Handel's *Zadok the Priest*) would be wrong: we are not watching a coronation but hearing praise-song for the already-dead.

**2. Grieg — *Peer Gynt Suite No. 1*, 'Åse's Death'**

The funeral of Scyld Scefing is among the most beautiful passages in Old English: the king laid out in his ship, surrounded by weapons and treasure, sent out to sea alone, his destination *uncuð* — unknown. The Danes themselves confess they cannot say where the ship went. This is grief as ceremony — not lamentation but the dignified performance of the community's knowledge that kings, ultimately, belong to the sea from which they come. Grieg's processional descent — bare strings, harmonic simplicity, an emotional weight that refuses the operatic — matches this exactly. The piece does not weep; it *proceeds*. Chopin's Funeral March (from the B-flat minor Sonata) would be too interior, too nineteenth-century in its grief-architecture; this is collective, public, coastal mourning, and Grieg is the right tradition for it.

**3. Sibelius — Symphony No. 7 in C major**

After the funeral, the poem unfolds the genealogy: Scyld Scefing → Beow → Healfdene → Hrothgar. This is not list-making; it is the accumulation of time, the slow working of fate through generations toward a crisis none of them could foresee. Sibelius's Seventh is a single-movement world — it unfolds from something ancient and undifferentiated toward something inexorable, then subsides. The trombone theme appears, withdraws, returns with greater weight each time: this is what the genealogy does. Four generations of kings, each consolidating the last, until one builds a hall so magnificent that it triggers its own counter-force. Beethoven's Fifth would be too sudden for this; fate in *Beowulf* is not a knock at the door but a long geological pressure.

**4. Handel — *Music for the Royal Fireworks*, Overture**

Hrothgar commands the greatest mead-hall ever built. Heorot rises — gold-bright, torchlit, the height of what secular power can make — and the Danes gather in it to receive gifts, drink mead, hear songs. This is the poem's proudest moment of human achievement. Handel's overture is the right music for this moment: ceremonial, sovereign, genuinely grand. The Royal Fireworks was written for public secular celebration; Heorot is public secular celebration. The same piece scores Olympus in the Iliad arc, but there it is undercut by Olympian absurdity; here it is unironic. Fitt I has not yet earned irony. The ruin is coming; for now, the hall blazes, and the music should let it.

**5. Hildegard von Bingen — *O virtus Sapientiae***

Inside Heorot, the scop (the court poet) sings a creation-song: light dividing from darkness, land from water, sun and moon set in their courses, all living creatures made. This is the poem's Genesis-moment, and its placement — joy inside a hall while darkness gathers outside — is the poem's central structural irony. Hildegard's music has a quality no secular composer can replicate: it is genuinely singing the world into being, open vowels climbing toward sacred clarity. This is not anachronistic. The Beowulf-poet was a Christian author writing of a pagan past and threading Christian theology through a heroic tradition; the syncretism is the poem's own. Hildegard captures the medieval Christian luminosity that the Beowulf-poet would have heard as the underside of all heroic song: *God made this world. Grendel knows it. He is outside.*

**6. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 8, II. Allegretto**

The transition from the scop's creation-song to Grendel's introduction is the poem's most devastating move. Grendel is presented immediately after: condemned to darkness, descended from Cain, hearing the music he cannot enter. His exclusion is defined precisely by what he can hear but cannot have — the joy, the warmth, the fellowship of the lit hall. Shostakovich's second movement of the Eighth has exactly this quality: it is march-like but grotesque, sardonic, relentless, possessing a terrible anti-joy. It sounds like something moving toward a light it cannot possess. The Eighth Symphony was written in extremity; its humor is the humor of the condemned, of the figure pressing its face against glass. Penderecki would be too abstractly horrific — Grendel is not a horror-film creature but a figure of theological exile, and theological exile requires this darkly satirical register.

**7. Bartók — *Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta*, III. Adagio**

Grendel comes on the first night, takes thirty thanes while they sleep, carries them to his lair. The horror of this passage in the poem is not physical description but the silence that follows — the discovery at dawn, the grief of Hrothgar, the emptied benches. Bartók's Adagio is night music of controlled menace: chromatic string-clusters, the celesta introducing something non-human and eerie, percussion mapping footfalls in the dark. It is music that knows what happens in the night without showing it — the same narrative economy the poem employs. Stravinsky (Rite of Spring) would make this ritual-sacrificial; Bartók keeps it uncanny, specific, and fateful. The night is not ceremony; it is simply what comes.

**8. Barber — Adagio for Strings**

This is Fitt I's most sustained movement and the emotional centre of the whole arc: not one attack but twelve years of attacks. The poem dwells on this duration. Heorot stands empty every night. No thane dares sleep there. No counsel finds a remedy. Hrothgar's men die season after season. The poem names Grendel's occupation of the night-hall as a kind of permanent counter-kingship — he rules the darkness; Hrothgar rules the daylight; neither can dislodge the other. Barber's Adagio is the most precisely calibrated piece in the canon for civilisational, sustained, politically total grief. Its characteristic motion — pressing upward, intensifying, pressing further — is not tragedy's single catastrophe but twelve years of accumulated loss. The long climax and the silence that follows it are the right analog to the poem's most despairing lines. Note the distinction: Mahler's Adagietto (used for Achilles and Thetis in the Iliad) is private, inward grief. The Barber is public, sustained, communal — an entire people's exhaustion. They are not interchangeable.

**9. Penderecki — *Threnody for the Victims of Hiroshima***

In desperation, the Danes turn to heathen shrines and make offerings, praying at idol-temples for relief. The Beowulf-poet notes this with his characteristic theological double-vision: he pities them (they knew no better), but he records that their counsel was wrong and God's grace was absent from it. This is a moment of civilisational collapse into superstition — human institutions failing, a people's spiritual resources exhausted, turning to sources they suspect will not help. Penderecki's Threnody captures this precisely: it is not horror music but the sound of structure itself failing, the human acoustic going beyond the threshold of form. It is used once, briefly, for this single dark passage — not for Grendel's attack (which is fateful but controlled) but for the moment human counsel breaks down entirely. Its use here is meant to be uncomfortable: the Threnody was written for the victims of a catastrophic human failure; placing it at a civilisation's spiritual collapse honours the comparison.

**10. Vaughan Williams — Symphony No. 5, I. Preludio**

Fitt I closes not with rescue but with widening: the news of Heorot's suffering travels the sea-roads to Geatland, where a man named Beowulf hears it. The arc of the fitt does not resolve — it turns. The problem has been stated in full (twelve years, no counsel, no remedy); now the possibility of a response opens, tentatively, at the edge. Vaughan Williams' Fifth Symphony Preludio has exactly this quality. It was written in 1943, when the outcome of the war was not yet known, and it carries the texture of a world in serious trouble that might — possibly — be answered. It is searching music, liminal, a sustained open question. It does not promise resolution; it creates readiness. This is the emotional posture Fitt I closes on: not rescue, but the first breath of its possibility.

## The Dominant Arc

The arc of Fitt I moves: **kingship → grief → fate → kingship → grief → fate → grief → fate → fate → fate**. The dominant force is grief — not the grief of a single death but the grief of an institution that cannot protect itself, of a people who built the greatest hall the world had seen and cannot use it. The playlist correspondingly moves from the commemorative grandeur of Elgar and the processional dignity of Grieg, through Handel's unironic flourish and Hildegard's sacred clarity, into the progressive darkening of Shostakovich, Bartók, Barber, and Penderecki, before ending on the liminal openness of Vaughan Williams. The arc does not resolve. It turns.
