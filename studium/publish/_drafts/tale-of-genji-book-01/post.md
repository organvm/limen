# Kiritsubo: Desire, Ruin, Radiance
## A music arc for *The Tale of Genji*, Chapter 1

*The Tale of Genji*'s opening is the moment where desire and grief are discovered to be the same force. The emperor's love for the Kiritsubo Lady is absolute and absolutely destructive — not because he wills her death, but because the court's social machinery converts his preference into her persecution, and her delicacy cannot survive it. She dies in Chapter 1, before the novel has found its hero. What remains is her son: Genji, the Shining One, who carries her beauty forward like an inheritance he did not choose and cannot put down. Chapter 1 is not a prologue to the novel. It is the novel's emotional key — everything that follows lives inside the space that the Kiritsubo Lady's death opens.

Scoring this chapter means refusing two temptations. The first is to reach for Japonisme — Debussy's *La mer*, Puccini's Butterfly-flavored exoticism, anything that substitutes atmospheric "Easternness" for dramatic precision. The second is to over-monumentalize the death, to reach for Barber's *Adagio* or Wagner's funeral marches when the text's mode is interior, quiet, deliberately underdramatic. Murasaki Shikibu does not dwell on the death itself; she moves through it. The music must honor that restraint.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*: Prelude to Act I**

The Tristan Prelude is the architecture of desire that cannot be satisfied without destroying the world that contains it. The famous unresolved opening chord — the "Tristan chord" — defines the piece: it reaches, suspends, reaches again. This is the correct opening for Genji because the emperor's love for the Kiritsubo Lady is not a marital preference or a political alliance. It is an obsession that bends every convention of Heian court life, that overrides considerations of rank (she is lower-ranking than protocol permits for such concentrated imperial favor), and that, by its very excess, makes her the target of every socially powerful woman who stands to lose by her preferment. Wagner does not dramatize this — he gives its interior pressure. The tragedy is already present in the opening bars, the way it is already present in the chapter's first sentence.

Wagner also works here because of what it displaces: any purely "Japanese" musical atmosphere would be a category error. Murasaki Shikibu is not writing an ethnographic document — she is writing the psychology of desire and court cruelty with a specificity and sophistication that has no cultural ceiling. The Tristan Prelude reaches the same depth from the other direction.

**2. Schubert — String Quartet No. 14 in D minor ('Death and the Maiden'): II. Andante con moto**

The court's cruelty toward the Kiritsubo Lady is not personal — it is structural. Every time the emperor favors her rooms over those of more senior consorts, the social order is being visibly distorted, and the women who represent that order respond with practiced, graduated humiliation: soiled hems, blocked passages, whispered contempt on her route through the corridors. Schubert's 'Death and the Maiden' variations give the sound of that slow attrition. The melody in the Andante is serene, continuous, and relentless — it does not rage, it does not stop. The structural beauty of the form is part of the horror. The Kiritsubo Lady is not being destroyed by anything chaotic or arbitrary; she is being destroyed by a social machine that functions perfectly, smoothly, according to its own logic. Schubert understood exactly that relationship between beautiful form and lethal content.

**3. Fauré — Requiem, Op. 48: 'Pie Jesu'**

Fauré's *Pie Jesu* — the soprano solo at the heart of his Requiem — is not a funeral piece. It is a petition for rest, sung in advance, in the register of exhausted love. The text asks for perpetual rest and light; the music gives a sustained, hovering quality, a line that seems to float slightly above the ground. This is the right music for the long middle section of the chapter where the Kiritsubo Lady is simply failing — not dying dramatically, not being killed, but wearing down under the accumulated weight of the court's hostility and her own constitution's limits. The emperor cannot protect her; his love cannot touch what is happening to her body. Fauré gives the sound of a world that is already, quietly, releasing someone — before anyone has formally acknowledged that the release is coming. The *Pie Jesu* is also not Barber's *Adagio* (too grand), not Pärt (too cool), not Bach (too devotional in a Christian-theological sense that would impose the wrong cultural filter on a Buddhist-inflected Japanese text). Fauré lands the grief without translating it.

**4. Mahler — Symphony No. 9: IV. Adagio (opening)**

The Mahler Ninth's final movement is the piece in the Western orchestral repertoire that most precisely scores the experience of consciousness preparing to relinquish itself. It does not do this dramatically — no climax, no announcement, no funeral procession. It extends a melodic line past the point where resolution seems available, keeps extending it, and then, without fanfare, does not resolve. Mahler completed it knowing he was dying; its autobiographical weight is audible. 

The Kiritsubo Lady's death in the novel is treated with the same quality of restraint. Murasaki Shikibu moves through the death without dwelling — a few sentences, and it is accomplished. What she dwells on is the emperor's response, the mourning, the atmosphere of the court in the weeks after. The Mahler Adagio is not funeral music in the public sense — it is the interior sound of departure, which is what the chapter requires. It is emphatically NOT the register of the Resurrection Symphony's finale (which belongs to Genji's ascents later in the novel) or Mahler's Fifth Adagietto (save that for a private, wounded-inwardness scene like Achilles with Thetis). The Ninth Adagio is where death is confronted without rhetoric.

**5. Elgar — *Enigma Variations*: 'Nimrod' (Variation IX)**

After the death, the emperor cannot recover his equilibrium. The palace is full of the Kiritsubo Lady's absence: her rooms remain as she left them, her infant son is at court, every object in the emperor's daily life has become a conduit for grief. 'Nimrod' is the musical shape of love that has been converted by death into memory without losing its force. It grows from near-silence — a quiet string texture that expands and swells without ever breaking into theatrical declaration — and then subsides, never having arrived at anything you could honestly call consolation. The effect is sustained tenderness with nowhere to go. 

In the novel, the emperor's grief is not just personal — it has political consequences. He neglects his duties. He neglects other consorts who have legitimate claims on his attention. He keeps Genji at court past the point where it is appropriate, because the boy's face is his dead mother's. Elgar's 'Nimrod' gives the interior of that condition: love that has become obsessive memory, beautiful and slightly ungovernable.

**6. Rameau — *Les Indes galantes*: 'Musette en rondeau'**

The Kokiden Lady — mother of the Crown Prince, the legitimate imperial heir — and her faction represent the court's structural resistance to the Kiritsubo Lady's elevation. Their opposition is not merely personal jealousy; it is a defense of Heian social architecture, of the protocol that determines who has priority access to imperial favor, whose sons become crown princes, whose bloodlines are consolidated. Rameau's court dance forms are the right music for that world's texture: graceful, formulaic, beautiful in their proportions, and absolutely about rank, precedent, and political maneuvering. The 'Musette en rondeau' has the aristocratic lightness and the coiled political edge that the Heian court's surface requires — everyone is behaving correctly, every gesture is calibrated, and the most dangerous people smile.

Not Handel's *Royal Fireworks* (too sovereign, too masculine, wrong court culture); not Couperin (too French-intimate). Rameau's dance-opera idiom has the combination of sensual surface and social hardness that matches the Heian milieu from the outside.

**7. Sibelius — Symphony No. 5: III. Allegro molto (finale)**

The Korean diviner who reads infant Genji's face makes one of the chapter's most structurally important pronouncements: this child has the features of imperial destiny, but making him a prince could bring the realm to ruin. The emperor absorbs this and resolves to register Genji as a commoner — a prince by nature who will not sit on the throne. The diviner's reading is not a curse and not a prophecy in the Delphic sense; it is a recognition of something already present in the child's face, already written into the conditions of his existence.

Sibelius's Fifth finale gives the right texture for that moment of destiny-naming. The famous hammer blows at the end — six chords separated by measured silences — are what it sounds like when a conclusion is being stated rather than argued for, when the future is acknowledged rather than chosen. Sibelius works in the modal idiom of natural inevitability; the Fifth's finale does not dramatize fate, it embodies it. Tchaikovsky or Beethoven would impose too much willful rhetoric; Sibelius's conclusion is quieter and more massive.

**8. Bach — *St. Matthew Passion*, BWV 244: 'Erbarme dich' (No. 39)**

After the Kiritsubo Lady's death, the emperor summons her mother and draws out the conversation past any normal limit, asking about her daughter's final days, her voice, her habits, the small details that grief converts into relics. He is trying to maintain contact with someone who is already gone. This is supplication in its most naked form — not to a god, not to authority, but to absence itself, through the medium of someone who also loved her.

'Erbarme dich' — Peter's aria after the betrayal and the cock's crow — is the great aria of supplication in the Western canon. The solo violin plays an ascending, sinuous, unanswered line while the alto voice pleads below it. What Bach understood about supplication is that it has a musical structure: the plea goes up, is not answered, goes up again, is not answered. The two lines weave without resolving into comfort. This is exactly what the emperor's conversation with the Kiritsubo Lady's mother sounds like from inside: two people united by grief, unable to give each other what they most need, each trying anyway.

**9. Debussy — *Préludes*, Book I: 'La cathédrale engloutie'**

Genji's beauty is the chapter's metamorphic fact. He is the Kiritsubo Lady continued in a new form — her loveliness surviving her, concentrated, made more intense by the grief-context that frames it. Murasaki Shikibu calls him 'Hikaru Genji,' the Shining One, and describes him in terms that suggest something not quite of the ordinary human register: a beauty so extreme it is slightly uncanny, slightly inhuman, a form of light rather than of flesh.

Debussy's submerged cathedral emerges from its watery medium very slowly, as if surfacing from a depth where it has been for a long time. It has the quality of something vast and luminous that is not entirely present in the normal sense — both there and not-there, both above water and below. That is precisely the ontological register of Genji's beauty in this chapter: it is his dead mother, made visible. He is a presence that is also an absence, a radiance that is also a wound. The *cathédrale engloutie* gives this without editorializing — it doesn't explain the metamorphosis, it sounds like what a metamorphosis sounds like from the inside.

**10. Mahler — Symphony No. 2 ('Resurrection'): I. Allegro maestoso (opening)**

Chapter 1 ends with Genji named, positioned, and destined. The emperor has made his decision; the court has arranged itself around the new configuration; the child who will be the novel's subject has been given his social existence. The first movement of the Resurrection Symphony begins with a cello-bass unison that sounds like a world being called into being from below — an immense, unresolved inaugural statement, something vast being set in motion rather than completed.

This is not the Resurrection's apotheosis — that finale belongs to different chapters, perhaps to Genji's great triumphs or his final retirement. The first movement is all inaugural weight and unresolved destiny. It knows that what it is introducing will be enormous; it does not yet know how. That is the right register for the end of Chapter 1: the novel has named its hero, established his origins in desire and grief, and now must carry him forward into the fifty-three chapters that follow. The music announces that horizon without claiming to fill it.

## The Dominant Arc

Kiritsubo moves from desire through grief through metamorphosis: the Tristan Prelude gives the engine; Schubert and Fauré give the slow violence; Mahler Nine gives the death; Elgar gives what the palace sounds like afterward; Rameau gives the political context that made the death possible; Sibelius names the destiny; Bach gives the supplication; Debussy gives the child who carries his mother's beauty forward like a light; and Mahler Two announces the horizon of a life that has only just begun under extraordinary conditions. The arc ends not in resolution but in inception. The dominant force is grief — because everything in this chapter, including Genji himself, is grief's issue.
