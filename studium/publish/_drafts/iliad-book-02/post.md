# Iliad Book II: The False Dream, the Assembly, the Catalogue

Book II is the moment where the war almost ends before it begins — and doesn't. Zeus sends a
lying dream to Agamemnon. The dream tells him to attack; it is designed to destroy him. He
tests the army, telling them to go home. They go. Ten years of siege, ten years of suppressed
longing for ships and wives, and the army dissolves toward the water faster than anyone thought
possible. Odysseus stops them — not by inspiring them, but by reasserting rank. He beats
Thersites with a scepter. The crowd laughs. The laughter is the hinge: comedy restores what
exaltation couldn't. Then Nestor speaks from memory, Agamemnon sacrifices (the omen is dark),
and Homer calls on the Muses for the Catalogue — 1,186 ships, every captain, every place-name
from the Greek world, then the Trojan world in mirror. Then the armies move.

This is not the Trojan War as spectacle. This is the Trojan War as institution — how it gets
assembled, what holds it together, how close the whole thing came to ending on a beach in
Aulis before a single Trojan saw it. The dominant force is **law**: not law as statute, but
law as the force that reassembles broken social form. Assembly, oratory, rank, memory, the
great catalogue itself as a juridical act of memory — these are what Book II is made of.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Berlioz — *Symphonie fantastique*: IV. "March to the Scaffold"** — *divine-intervention*

Zeus sends Oneiros, the False Dream, in the shape of the trusted elder Nestor. The dream enters
Agamemnon with all the pomp of divine authority — and is a lie. Berlioz's "March to the
Scaffold" is the right piece because it is ceremonial and sinister simultaneously: a procession
toward catastrophe, dressed in the clothes of official occasion. The brass arrival, the
grinding march rhythm, the sense of something magnificent moving you toward ruin — that is
exactly the register of the False Dream. Divine-intervention requires a sudden color-shift and
brass/strings contrast; Berlioz delivers it with the added layer of menace the scene demands.

**2. Holst — *The Planets*: "Uranus, the Magician"** — *wrath*

Not "Mars" — that belongs to Book I's quarrel between Achilles and Agamemnon, and re-using it
here would flatten what is a completely different kind of force. The army's rout toward the
ships is not martial wrath — it is ten years of suppressed frustration releasing at once, a mob
movement, chaotic and almost comical in its suddenness. "Uranus" is Holst's most grotesque
movement: massive, lurching, rhythmically irregular, brutally loud then suddenly collapsing
into silence. It captures the scene's strange texture — the violence of collective dissolution,
not of individual heroic fury. Wrath requires rhythmic pressure and unresolved harmony; Holst's
Uranus has both, plus the tinge of the grotesque that this particular scene cannot be without.

**3. Strauss — *Also sprach Zarathustra*: "Von den Hinterweltlern"** — *divine-intervention*

Hera watches the Greeks breaking for the ships and cannot endure it. She turns to Athena
immediately: go down, stop them. The gods operate at speed here — a pivot in the mechanism of
history that takes three lines in Homer but represents the entire difference between a war and
an abortion of a war. Strauss's "Von den Hinterweltlern" (Of the Backworldsmen) is not the
famous opening — it is hushed, searching, strings dissolving into uncertainty, a meditation on
forces operating from above. That register of remove and recalibration is exactly the gods
here: not thundering into the scene but redirecting it from a height. The brass/strings
contrast that divine-intervention demands arrives in the transition as Athena descends.

**4. Bach — Brandenburg Concerto No. 5: I. Allegro** — *law*

Odysseus takes Agamemnon's scepter — the physical emblem of legitimate authority — and moves
through the army. To the chiefs he speaks with restraint; to the common soldiers he speaks with
force, striking them physically if they resist. This is law as rhetoric, as structural
intervention: authority being re-articulated through rank-calibrated speech. The Brandenburg
Concerto No. 5's first movement is exactly this — formal clarity driving relentlessly forward,
the ensemble held together by an architectonic logic, and then the harpsichord cadenza: the
moment when one voice rises above the whole, not by force but by mastery of the form itself.
Law requires formal clarity, repetition, architecture. Bach at this scale is its fullest
realization.

**5. Mozart — *Le nozze di Figaro*: Overture** — *comedy*

Thersites is the poem's great problem figure: physically ugly (Homer is specific — bald,
lame, hunched), socially low, rhetorically shrewd, and politically accurate. He is right that
Agamemnon keeps the prizes while others die. He is right that the war serves the commanders.
He says so, loudly, before the whole army. Odysseus beats him with the scepter. The army
laughs. The laughter is what restores order — not the speech, not the beating alone, but the
crowd's shared comic relief at the demagogue's humiliation. The Figaro Overture captures this
register exactly: bourgeois cunning, verbal velocity, class comedy with a sharp edge, the
servant who almost wins and doesn't. Comedy requires dance rhythm and courtly exaggeration;
Mozart's Figaro is the repertoire's clearest example of wit as social mechanism.

**6. Handel — *Messiah*: "And the Glory of the Lord"** — *law*

After Thersites, Odysseus delivers his full oration. Nestor seconds it with his own long
speech, invoking his authority as the oldest Greek present. The army returns to assembly —
seats itself, listens, reformulates itself as a political body capable of decision. This is
the restoration of social form after comic chaos, and it needs music that sounds like many
voices falling into one shape. Handel's choral writing at this scale — diatonic, architecturally
clear, voices entering in sequence and building to a single formal mass — is the closest
musical equivalent to the Greek assembly reconstituting itself. Law requires formal clarity and
architectural repetition; Handel's chorus is its most literal enactment.

**7. Elgar — *Enigma Variations*: IX. "Nimrod"** — *memory*

Nestor's counsel is always the same structure: I have seen greater men than you, and they
listened to me, and they prevailed. He names Pirithous, Dryas, Caeneus, Exadius, Polyphemus —
heroes of a prior age that no one in the assembly can contradict because no one was there.
This is memory deployed as authority, the past as political weight. "Nimrod" is the paradigm
case of memory as force in music: the nostalgic recall of something irretrievably past,
rendered not as loss but as gravity. Elgar gives Nestor his exact register — not sentimental
vanity, but the genuine weight of what an old man has actually seen and what that seeing costs
to carry.

**8. Verdi — *Requiem*: "Dies Irae"** — *sacrifice*

Agamemnon sacrifices a fat five-year-old ox to Zeus before the army advances. Zeus accepts the
fat and burns the thighs — and rejects the prayer. The omen is already dark before the
Catalogue even begins. This is sacrifice at full ritual weight: the priests, the fire, the
formal compact with a god who is already arranging betrayal. Verdi's "Dies Irae" is the
repertoire's most powerful statement of sacrifice as immolation-build — the processional gravity
of a world-historical act, the full mass of human organization arrayed before a force that will
not answer on human terms. Sacrifice requires ritual weight and processional build; Verdi at
this scale is the only answer.

**9. Elgar — Symphony No. 1: I. Andante opening** — *memory*

The Catalogue of Ships is the poem's act of supreme memorial invocation. Homer calls on the
Muses directly — no mortal memory could hold these names. Then he counts: 1,186 ships, 29
contingents, every captain from every part of the Greek world. It is not drama. It is the poem
insisting that all of this happened, that these places and people were real, that the weight of
that world must be registered before it can be destroyed. Elgar's First Symphony opens with a
melody of such plain, broad inevitability that it functions as archetype — noble recollection
elevated to sovereign gravity. Memory requires nostalgic recall and faded recapitulation; here,
in the Catalogue, memory becomes something closer to law.

**10. Walton — *Crown Imperial*: March** — *kingship*

The Trojan catalogue mirrors the Greek: ally after ally, kingdom after kingdom, the Eastern
world arraying itself under Priam and Hector. This is Trojan power described at its maximum —
before the walls fail, before Hector falls, before any of what we know must happen has happened.
Walton's Crown Imperial is ceremonial grandeur without irony or shadow: sovereignty on full
display, the full weight of rank and territory in procession. Kingship requires ceremonial
grandeur and sovereign display; Walton is the 20th century's most unambiguous realization of
it. The shadow only comes when you remember what the Catalogue is arraying toward.

**11. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5: I. Allegro con brio** — *fate*

Zeus sends Iris to the Trojans: the Greeks are moving. The armies converge on the plain for
the first time. This is the mechanism engaging — not a choice, not an escalation, but the
thing that was set in motion by the False Dream and the assembly and the Catalogue now beginning
to move with its own logic. Fate requires inexorable motif and hammered insistence; the Fifth's
first movement is the canonical instance of fate as rhythm, as inevitability made audible. The
armies begin to move and the music cannot stop. It will not stop for twenty-two more books.

## The dominant arc of Book II

```
divine-deception → mob dissolution → divine counter-move → law restored →
comedy as social mechanism → memory as authority → sacrifice with dark omen →
the great memorial act → kingship on display → fate engaged
```

Book II is the book of **law** — law as the only thing standing between ten thousand men and
the ships home. The arc moves from divine manipulation (a lying dream) through the near-collapse
of all military order, its comic restoration, its memorial foundation (the Catalogue), and
finally into the machinery of fate clicking into gear. The war does not begin in Book I with
the quarrel. It begins here, when the armies actually move — and only after it almost didn't.
