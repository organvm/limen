# Shahnameh Cycle 1: Creation, Kingship, the Line of Fate

The Shahnameh's opening is the moment where the world acquires its first sovereign — not through conquest, not through election, but through ontological necessity. Keyumars does not earn the kingship; he is the kingship, as the first human who stands upright on a mountain and finds the world organized around him. Ferdowsi begins not with action but with wisdom (kherad), and not with a king but with praise of the faculty that makes kings legible. This is already a different register from Homer, from Gilgamesh, from Valmiki: the Shahnameh opens by telling us what kind of mind is required to read what follows. Then it installs the first man-king, gives him a son, takes that son away, and announces the long line of kings who will bear the same cycle — glory, darkness, grief, restoration — until the poem ends. Cycle 1 is creation as prologue to catastrophe: everything bright with first light, everything already aimed at loss.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel***
The Shahnameh does not begin with a king. It begins with kherad — reason, wisdom, the faculty that allows the world to be read. Ferdowsi's prologue is genuinely theological: God, wisdom, the moral duty to use one's mind before one's sword. Before any hero, before any battle, the poem establishes its epistemology. Pärt's mirror-canon is the most literal musical embodiment of that opening: piano and violin moving in parallel thirds, neither dominating, generating a space of perfect vertical calm. The title is exact — mirror of mirrors, a reflection of a reflection. The Shahnameh's claim is that the wise reader and the wise king share the same faculty; the music should feel like a mind, at the beginning of a long task, settling into its own gravity.

**2. Haydn — *The Creation*, "Representation of Chaos"**
Ferdowsi, following Zoroastrian cosmology, describes a world that forms itself from the four elements — a movement from undifferentiated substance to ordered existence. Water, fire, earth, wind: the classical Persian four take on their natures and then take on their stations. Haydn's Chaos introduction is the greatest musical depiction of creation-in-process in the Western repertoire: tonal centers dissolve and form, the orchestra cannot decide what key it is in, matter seems to be trying to cohere. Nothing else holds the *process* of creation — as opposed to its triumph — with this specificity. Bruckner and Mahler give majesty already achieved; Haydn gives the moment just before the "Let there be light" resolves into C major, and that moment of pressure is exactly Ferdowsi's cosmological prologue.

**3. Elgar — "Nimrod"**
Keyumars descends from no one. He is the first. He wears animal skins and lives on the mountain, and yet the world recognizes him as its sovereign — the animals bow, the world orients around him. This is kingship as ontological fact rather than political achievement. Elgar's Nimrod is the sound of sovereign gravity at rest: not ceremony (that is Handel's register), not imperial pomp (that is Walton's), but the deeper thing — the weight of a person whom the world accepts as necessary. It builds without urgency from a quiet opening to a full orchestral mass and then does not linger. Keyumars is ancient and simple and immovable. Nimrod is what that looks like from the outside.

**4. Bach — *Goldberg Variations*, Aria**
Under Keyumars, the world learns culture. What to eat, what to wear, how to organize life. This is the Persian equivalent of Enkidu learning bread and beer from Shamhat — civilization instilled not as conquest but as pattern, repetition, grammar. Bach's Aria is the ground beneath the Goldberg's thirty variations: a bass-line so stable that the entire architecture rests on it without that ground ever shifting. That is what Ferdowsi describes in Keyumars' rule: the first laying-down of human order, modest and total. The register is dignified but not grand. Civilization at its first, plainest moment has no need for ceremony; it only needs to establish the repeating pattern that will make everything else possible.

**5. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5, I. Allegro con brio**
The Shahnameh's cosmology is Zoroastrian dualism: Ahura Mazda (light, order) against Ahriman (darkness, chaos). As soon as Keyumars establishes order, Ahriman moves against it — not yet as catastrophe, but as the grammar of opposition that will structure every cycle that follows. Beethoven's Fifth announces an adversarial force with the specificity Ferdowsi requires: it is not random menace but organized, structural opposition, a pattern that returns and returns. The four-note fate-hammer is the correct register because it describes *persistent* darkness, not dramatic surprise. Ahriman in the Shahnameh is not a monster who appears and is defeated; he is a force that is always already in motion. Tchaikovsky's fate-motif was considered and rejected — it is too personal, too Romantic. Beethoven's is cosmological.

**6. Gluck — *Iphigénie en Aulide*, Overture**
Siamak is the first sacrifice of the Shahnameh: Keyumars' beloved son, sent against Ahriman's army, killed. His death is not a battle-death in the Homeric sense — it is the world's first demonstration that the light has a price. Gluck's overture was composed for exactly this situation: a father of sovereign authority whose child is about to be consumed by the machinery of cosmic necessity. The piece holds both the formal gravity of the command-world and its human wound simultaneously, without choosing between them. There is no melodrama in Gluck here, no operatic wringing — just the weight of what a civilization requires from its sons. Siamak does not survive the requirement. The music should carry the weight of that precedent, which will recur.

**7. Mahler — *Kindertotenlieder*, "Nun will die Sonn' so hell aufgeh'n"**
Keyumars weeps for a year. The Shahnameh is explicit: the first king tears his clothes, gathers the world in mourning, and grief becomes a cosmic event — armies of creatures, humans, the natural world all mourning alongside him. This is not private inwardness (which calls for the Mahler Adagietto used in the Iliad arc). It is a father's grief that has the quality of a natural disaster. Mahler's Kindertotenlieder opens its first song with a cruel structural irony: the sun rises brightly on the morning after the child dies. The voice and the orchestra are not aligned — the world continues as if nothing has changed, and the father must live inside that gap. That gap is Keyumars' mourning exactly. The funeral-march register belongs later, to Sohrab, to Esfandiyar — to losses the reader has lived alongside. In Cycle 1, Siamak is not yet beloved by us; Keyumars' grief is the grief, and it needed the Kindertotenlieder's combination of scale and isolation.

**8. Handel — *Music for the Royal Fireworks*, Overture**
Hushang is Siamak's son, Keyumars' grandson. He rides out, marshals his forces, defeats the demon's army, and the world rights itself — kingship replenished through righteous vengeance. This is ceremonial restoration: the throne that was wounded is sealed. Handel's Royal Fireworks Overture is court music at its most ceremonially exact — not war music, not funeral music, but the music of order publicly re-established after legitimate victory. Sovereign display with swagger. The register is somewhat lighter than Elgar's Nimrod precisely because Hushang has earned his standing through action, not through primordial necessity; there is brass-and-drums confidence in Handel that Keyumars' mountain-sovereignty did not need and could not have used.

**9. Verdi — *Requiem*, "Dies Irae"**
Ferdowsi closes the Opening by announcing the kings who will follow: Hushang, Tahmuras, Jamshid — names carrying their own fates already sealed. The Shahnameh's structure is a king-list disguised as an epic; every name in the prologue is a fatedness. Verdi's Dies Irae is not death music in the narrow sense — it is the sound of a vast ledger being read aloud, of fate operating at cosmological scale, of a roll-call whose outcomes are already inscribed. The Shahnameh's opening plants exactly that weight before a single heroic deed has been narrated. These kings will achieve; they will also fall, be corrupted, be destroyed by Zahhak, by their own hubris, by Ahriman's endless patience. The Dies Irae is the register in which their ledger opens. This is not the terminal weight of an apotheosis — hold that for Rostam's final hours. It is the opening of a vast and unforgiving account-book.

**10. Bruckner — Symphony No. 7, II. Adagio (opening)**
The Shahnameh's first cycle ends not with a battle-cry or a funeral but with a declaration: Ferdowsi has set the world in motion, aligned his material under heaven's grammar, and is about to narrate the full arc of Persian royal history. The closing music must hold the horizon of what is about to unfold — vast, unhurried, not yet tragic, fully aware of its own scope. Bruckner's Seventh Adagio opens with a Wagner-tuba and horn choir rising in a long, unhurried theme — an ascent of the purest kind, without argument or urgency, simply the opening of scope. The movement does not arrive anywhere in its first minutes; it simply opens, and keeps opening. That is the Shahnameh's first cycle: the world has been placed under kingship and fate, Ferdowsi has done his cosmological work, and now the poem — all fifty cycles, all the heroes and their dooms — begins.

## The Dominant Arc of Cycle 1

```
revelation → divine-intervention → kingship → law → fate → sacrifice → grief → kingship → fate → ascent
```

The arc moves from origination through installation into the first wounding and its repair. The world is made, a man becomes its king, a son is sacrificed to darkness, the darkness is answered by the next generation, and then the poem looks forward at the line of kings who will carry this pattern — creation, order, threat, sacrifice, restoration — until the Shahnameh's own final exhaustion. The dominant force is **kingship**: not kingship as triumph or as ceremony, but kingship as a structural fact that the world requires and that costs the kings everything. Keyumars is the first demonstration. He will not be the last.

Fate is the other current, always running beneath. The first cycle of the Shahnameh is the moment when fate is planted as a grammar, before anyone has learned what kind of sentences it will write.
