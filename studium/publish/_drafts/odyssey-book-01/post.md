# Odyssey Book I: The Man of Many Turns

The Odyssey's opening is the moment where a poem about return refuses, for as long as it can, to begin returning. Book I is diagnosis before therapy: it names a wound (the hero is absent), catalogues its effects (a son who cannot confirm his father exists, a wife besieged, a house in slow dissolution), and then — only then — sends the machinery of divine favor into motion. The poem begins not with Odysseus but with the space he used to occupy.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Schubert — Symphony No. 8, I. Allegro moderato ('Unfinished')**

The proem's first word is ἄνδρα — "a man" — not a god, not a war, not a place. The Odyssey announces itself as a study in a particular human type: πολύτροπον, "of many turns." But before we meet him, Homer does something structurally remarkable: he rehearses the whole story in ten lines. Troy, wandering, suffering, companions lost through their own folly, the gods' anger. We are told the ending before the beginning. The poem does not start at the beginning; it starts at the end, running backward into its own past.

Schubert's Unfinished Symphony is the right instrument for this. It is formally incomplete — not because Schubert died, but because he chose not to finish it, or could not find the third movement that would close what the first two opened. It begins moving, reaches a pitch of urgent beauty, and cannot arrive. Book I has this structure: it is a book of approach, not arrival. The opening cello theme — low, hunting, restless in B minor — is the sound of a man-shaped gap that everything else is organized around.

**2. Elgar — Enigma Variations, 'Nimrod'**

The proem's second paragraph recapitulates the wandering in the voice of memory: 'he saw the cities of many men and knew their minds; he suffered many griefs at sea.' This is not action; it is inventory. Homer is not narrating events yet — he is taking stock of a life spent elsewhere. That is the voice of elegiac retrospection.

Elgar wrote 'Nimrod' as a portrait of his friend August Jaeger — not a description but a feeling, the residue of affection and time shared. It is music saturated in tenderness and irreversibility. The Odyssey's proem carries the same quality: this man went to many places and learned much, and we are hearing about it as one hears of a long journey from someone who has finally returned and can only name what was lost. The Enigma Variations' subtitle is fitting too — these are characters glimpsed from the outside, variations on a hidden theme. Odysseus is the hidden theme.

**3. Handel — Music for the Royal Fireworks, Overture**

The gods assemble. Poseidon is conveniently away eating Ethiopians' cattle — Homer needs him offstage for the council to work. Zeus opens the proceedings not with a plan for Odysseus but with a speech about Aegisthus, who ignored divine warning and paid for it. The speech is mildly self-congratulatory: we warned him, he didn't listen, he got what he deserved.

This is Olympus as court. Not the dark cosmic machinery of Das Rheingold, not the tragic grandeur of Götterdämmerung — Olympus here is a council chamber with a presiding magistrate who has prepared remarks. Handel's Fireworks Overture is sovereign display at its most ceremonial: brass fanfares, processional dignity, the pomp of a power that takes itself rather seriously. There is something faintly comic about Zeus's opening lecture. Handel registers that.

**4. Bach — St Matthew Passion, 'Erbarme dich, mein Gott'**

Athena's appeal to Zeus is the book's first formal supplication. 'My heart is rent for wise Odysseus, the hapless one, who far from his friends this long while suffereth affliction in a sea-girt isle.' The appeal is tender, slightly exasperated ('you, sir, take no heed of this'), and it works.

Bach's 'Erbarme dich' — Peter's lament after the denial of Christ — is the ur-text of musical supplication: a single exposed melodic line, the violin obbligato above a slowly descending bass, an unresolved petition held in suspense. The structural parallel is not religious but formal: both are a voice making a sustained appeal on behalf of someone who is not present, holding the petition open until it is answered. Athena's appeal is granted. Bach's aria holds the emotional state before the answer arrives.

**5. Sibelius — Symphony No. 7 in C major, opening**

Poseidon is not at the council, but Poseidon is everywhere in Book I. He is the reason Odysseus cannot come home. The sea — the poem's governing element, its antagonist and medium — is introduced here not as adventure but as obstruction. Odysseus is on Ogygia, surrounded by water, 'yearning to see if it were but the smoke leap upwards from his own land, longing to die.'

Sibelius's Seventh Symphony opens with a trombone theme of unusual loneliness: tonally restless, emerging from a texture of slowly-shifting string harmonies, never quite resolving into a stable home key. The symphony is in a single movement and cycles through its own material without arriving at a conventional finale. It is the acoustic equivalent of open ocean with no landfall — wandering that is also structured, going somewhere without getting there.

**6. Wagner — Das Rheingold, Prelude**

Zeus's consent is the mechanism engaging: 'let us all consider how he may return.' The administrative decision — Hermes to Ogygia, Athena to Ithaca — is the book's first real turning point. It is not a dramatic event; it is a decree. But the Odyssey is built on exactly such divine decrees: the gods setting things in motion that will take years to manifest.

Das Rheingold's Prelude is the sound of a world-system coming into being. From a single held E-flat, the bass line grows over four uninterrupted minutes into a full orchestral statement of the primordial. It does not begin dramatically — it begins cosmically. The gods of Book I are not tragic or ironic here; they are functional, and their function is to restart the poem's stalled mechanism. Wagner's Prelude is the machinery of a world engaging its gears.

**7. Debussy — Prélude à l'après-midi d'un faune**

Athena's descent to Ithaca is one of the poem's most characteristic movements: a goddess arrives in disguise, transforming herself into a stranger — specifically into Mentes, a Taphian guest-friend of Odysseus. She carries a bronze spear. She looks, to everyone including Telemachus, like a man.

Debussy's Faune opens with a solo flute phrase of radical ambiguity — neither quite in a key, neither quite a melody, suspended between states. The arrival is real but blurred; the figure is present but indeterminate. This is Athena-as-Mentes: she is there, she is materially present (holding a spear, being offered wine and bread), but she is not what she appears. The scene has a quality of dream precision — everything is vivid and slightly off. The flute is the disguise.

**8. Stravinsky — The Rake's Progress, Prelude**

Telemachus sees the suitors before he sees Athena-Mentes. He is sitting among them, moodily, thinking about his father. The suitors are not yet fully characterized as villains — they are simply present, filling the space of absence with appetite and games. They have killed oxen. They are playing draughts on the hides. It is a scene of occupation: other bodies filling a house that belongs to someone who is not here.

Stravinsky's Rake's Progress Prelude is neoclassical in surface — refined, slightly ornate — but with a sourness in the harmony that undercuts the elegance. The opera is about a man who wastes his inheritance and loses his mind. The Prelude announces that theme: pleasure-seeking with disorder underneath, appetite dressed in form. The suitors are not tragic; they are a symptom. The Prelude is the right register for a symptom.

**9. Fauré — Requiem, 'In Paradisum'**

Telemachus tells the stranger — Athena in disguise — that he cannot confirm his own parentage. 'Men say he is my father; I cannot know — no one knows his own begetting.' This is the book's deepest wound, stated quietly and without melodrama. The son's knowledge of his father is second-hand, reported, possible but unverified. His father exists as rumor.

Fauré's 'In Paradisum' is not grief — it is the music of the barely remembered, the almost-there, the state of holding a memory you cannot fully trust. The soprano chorus enters above the organ as if from a distance, luminous but faded, not resolving into presence. It is what it sounds like to hold someone who is gone: not crying for them but attending to them quietly, because the alternative is worse.

**10. Messiaen — Quatuor pour la fin du temps, I. Liturgie de cristal**

The disguised Athena says what Telemachus most needs to hear: your father will return. 'He is not dead yet.' This is the book's pivot — the first time νόστος, homecoming, is made into a promise by an authority beyond the ordinary world. The stranger speaks with the certainty of someone who has seen the future.

Messiaen's 'Liturgie de cristal' opens the Quartet for the End of Time with four instruments playing in simultaneous but non-synchronizing loops — a crystalline lattice, each line at its own tempo, creating a texture that is outside ordinary time. The birdsong of the clarinet, the cycling piano chords, the cello's long tones: this is revelation heard sideways, from a perspective that is not human. Athena's prophecy comes from this angle. It is not a prediction based on evidence; it is a statement made from eternity into time.

**11. Beethoven — Symphony No. 7, II. Allegretto**

Telemachus begins to act. He announces he will call an assembly. He tells the stranger the suitors eat his substance and he cannot stop them — but something in the conversation with Mentes/Athena has shifted. He is not yet his father; he is twenty years of absence embodied in a young man who is only now beginning to refuse.

Beethoven's Seventh Allegretto is one of the strangest pieces in the repertoire: it is processional without being a march, grave without being funereal, purposeful without being triumphant. It accumulates through repetition — the famous repeated-note motif building layer by layer — until it has mass. This is the sound of Telemachus gathering himself: not dramatic awakening, but the slow, patient accumulation of will. The decision to move is made in the Allegretto's tempo, not in any more heroic register.

**12. Rachmaninoff — Vocalise, Op. 34 No. 14**

Penelope descends from her room when she hears Phemius singing of the Greeks' homecoming from Troy. She asks him to stop: the song causes her pain. 'I have lost a brave man who was foremost among the Greeks.' Telemachus intervenes, and she withdraws — but the scene has done its work. We understand the household's emotional situation: the bard sings of return, and the one person for whom that return is most personal cannot bear to hear it yet.

Rachmaninoff's Vocalise is a wordless voice. There is no text — only melody and breath, longing without a name, a line that cannot find its cadence. Penelope's grief in Book I is not performed; it is private, deflected, interrupted by her son. The Vocalise holds the emotional truth of a scene the poem gives us only in glimpses: a woman who has been waiting for twenty years for a song that keeps being sung in the wrong tense.

**13. Brahms — Symphony No. 1, IV. Adagio — opening chorale transition**

The book closes with Telemachus asserting his authority over his mother and, implicitly, over the suitors. 'Speech belongs to men.' He tells Penelope to go to her rooms. He sends the bard back to his singing. He announces an assembly. For the first time in the book, someone is giving orders in Odysseus's house who has the right to give them.

Brahms's First Symphony finale opens not with the famous walking theme but with a slow string chorale — formal, structured, measured, reclaiming authority from the darkness of the third movement. It is the sound of order being stated before it has been fully established: the rule of law announced into a situation where law has been in abeyance. Telemachus is not Odysseus; the order he asserts is fragile and provisional. But the chorale is a beginning.

---

The dominant arc of Book I moves through three forces in orbit: **return** (announced in the proem, decreed by the gods, promised by Athena, embodied in Telemachus's awakening), **memory** (the retrospective structure of the proem, Penelope's grief, Telemachus's half-known father), and **divine-intervention** (the Olympian council, Athena's descent, her prophecy). What is withheld — Odysseus himself, the sea-voyage, the long wandering — is the poem's engine. Book I earns its place by being, formally, the longest of the poem's absences: everything that must be set in motion before the man of many turns can begin to turn toward home.
