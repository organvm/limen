# Iliad Book V: The Wounding of Gods
### *A Transmission Curriculum Essay — Studium Classical Music Arc*

---

Book V is the moment the Iliad breaks its own governing rule. Every other book operates under a horizon the audience understands: mortals fight; gods intervene; divine flesh is inviolate. Book V dismantles that horizon twice in the same afternoon. Diomedes — ordinary Argive captain, neither Achilles nor Ajax — is filled with divine menos by Athena, granted the ability to see gods on the field, and then, following the logic of his empowerment to its conclusion, drives his spear through Aphrodite's wrist and into Ares' belly. The war-god bellows like ten thousand men and flees to Olympus to complain.

The arc is not heroic triumph. It is transgression, then farce. Diomedes is terrifying because he believes, at the moment of greatest inflation, that the rules of the cosmos do not apply. He is right, temporarily. Then Olympus reabsorbs the shock — heals Ares, restores Aphrodite, closes ranks — and the war continues as though nothing happened. The comedy is that the gods are embarrassed, not that mortals won. Nothing has changed. Everything has been revealed.

The playlist scores this specific arc: divine inflation → wrath unleashed → the wounding of a goddess → the wounding of a god → Olympian farce and self-repair. It does not score "a battle." It scores the book's governing movement: the moment a mortal exceeds the human register, and the universe's embarrassed correction.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Strauss — *Also sprach Zarathustra*: Opening Sunrise** — *divine-intervention*

Book V opens not with soldiers but with a transformation. Athena descends to Diomedes, pours menos into his chest, and lifts the divine mist from his eyes — a mist Homer says normally prevents mortals from seeing gods moving on the field. This is a consciousness being upgraded past its species limit. Strauss's sunrise is the piece that sounds most like that: a single sustained low C, then the organ, then brass breaking into light. It is not military preparation; it is revelation. The famous chord does not announce a hero going to battle. It announces a perceptual threshold being crossed. That is precisely what Athena does to Diomedes at the opening of Book V.

**2. Holst — *The Planets*: "Mars, the Bringer of War"** — *wrath*

Book I used Mars for the verbal war between Agamemnon and Achilles — the quarrel before combat, violence held in speech. Book V is where Mars arrives at its proper referent. Diomedes in aristeia is compared by Homer to a winter torrent overflowing its banks, to wildfire crossing a plain. The 5/4 pulse is not uncomfortable friction here — it is asymmetrical annihilation, a rhythm that refuses to settle because Diomedes refuses to stop. Where Book I's Mars was wrath as compressed energy, Book V's Mars is wrath discharging. The same piece, the same meter — but the difference between the quarrel and the rampage.

**3. Nielsen — Symphony No. 4 "The Inextinguishable": IV. Finale** — *wrath*

The aristeia in Book V is not individual duels — it is a harvesting. Diomedes moves through the Trojan field as mechanism, not as man. Homer gives us name after name: Astynous, Hypeiron, Abas, Polyidus, Xanthus, Thoon. Each one dies. Nielsen's finale is the one passage in the symphonic repertoire where two sets of timpani battle each other in open space — not violence as passion but violence as inextinguishable process, a force that cannot be stopped because stopping is not in its nature. That is the aristeia's inner logic: not rage, but inevitability. The timpani are Diomedes. The field keeps breaking under them.

**4. Debussy — *Prélude à l'après-midi d'un faune*** — *divine-intervention*

The turn. Aeneas takes Diomedes' spear through the hip and falls; Aphrodite descends from Olympus to cradle her son in her arms and carry him off the field. She is love-divinity on a killing ground — wrong category, wrong register, beautiful and dangerously exposed. Debussy's Faune is the only piece in the Western orchestral tradition that sounds like divine feminine presence on the edge of the dangerous: shimmering, susceptible, not quite real in the daylight of violence around it. The opening flute is Aphrodite before she understands what Diomedes will do when he sees her. She is the mist; he has been given eyes that cut through mist.

**5. Bartók — *Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta*: III. Adagio** — *revelation*

This is the transgression. Diomedes cuts through the crowd, sees Aphrodite holding Aeneas, and drives his spear through her wrist. Ichor flows — the divine fluid that substitutes for blood in the immortal body. She cries out and drops her son. Bartók's Adagio from the Music for Strings is the sound of a membrane rupturing: night harmonics, celesta hovering over muted strings, a texture that is structurally wrong in the way a universe violating its own principles is wrong. It does not sound like triumph. It does not even sound like violence in the ordinary sense. It sounds like a wound the world does not know yet how to categorize. The ichor is in the celesta.

**6. Berlioz — *La Damnation de Faust*: "Ride to the Abyss"** — *divine-intervention*

After Aphrodite is wounded, the divine emergency machinery activates. Iris and Ares swoop to Olympus and carry the goddess off the field. Berlioz's Ride to the Abyss is headlong, barely controlled, vertical — the orchestral embodiment of divine beings in panicked transit, a situation that was never supposed to exist happening at maximum speed. This is not ceremonial divine intervention; it is emergency response. The brass is not majestic; it is frantic. The gods are moving fast because what just happened should not have happened, and they have not yet assembled a response. The Ride is the moment between the wound and Olympus figuring out what it means.

**7. Shostakovich — Symphony No. 10: II. Allegro (Scherzo)** — *wrath*

The second transgression, and the greater one. Athena herself guides Diomedes' spear, and the bronze enters Ares' belly. The war-god bellows — Homer says he bellows like ten thousand men — and abandons the field in pain, ascending to Olympus leaving a vacuum where the Trojan advantage had been. Shostakovich's 10th Scherzo is the most concentrated violence in the symphonic repertoire: two minutes, no preparation, no development, no mercy. It is not military pomp; it is violence moving faster than comprehension. The bellowing is in the brass; the flight is in the disintegration at the end. When it stops, something enormous has left the field.

**8. Rameau — *Les Indes galantes*: Entrée des dieux** — *comedy*

The war-god arrives on Olympus holding his wound and complaining. Homer gives us Ares speaking in the voice of an injured child, cataloguing grievances against Athena, asking Zeus why she is permitted to do this. Zeus listens, barely conceals his contempt, and sends Ares off to be healed. The god of war is ridiculous. He is costumed authority whose costume has been torn. Rameau's court spectacle — theatrical, ornate, performed grandeur over a fundamentally absurd situation — is the precise register: gods maintaining the forms of dignity while the substance of divine invulnerability has just been publicly disproven. The harpsichord is Ares pretending he still has the floor.

**9. Haydn — Symphony No. 45 "Farewell": IV. Presto / Adagio** — *comedy*

Paeon, the divine physician, heals Ares the way you might treat a bruise: quickly, efficiently, as routine maintenance. The cosmos seals over the wound. Order reassembles. The Trojans regroup. The war continues. Haydn's Farewell Symphony ends with musicians leaving the stage one by one — a performance of decorum quietly dismantling itself, with structural irony built in. That is exactly how Book V closes: the divine household restoring its procedures, the anomaly absorbed, the curtain drawn. The comedy is not that Diomedes won. It is that the gods needed twenty minutes to recover their composure, and now they have, and the whole episode will be treated as an unfortunate administrative matter rather than the shattering of a cosmic principle. Nothing has changed. Everything has been seen.

## Demoted

**Wagner — *Götterdämmerung*: "Siegfried's Funeral March"** — not before Book XXII. The terminal-monumental-grief register opens with Hector's death. Book V is transgression and farce; no major Achaean hero dies; the dominant emotion at the close is Olympian embarrassment, not human mourning.

**Beethoven — Symphony No. 3 "Eroica": II. Marcia funebre** — demoted. The Eroica funeral march is public mourning for a dead titan. Book V ends with the war-god running away and being healed. Wrong register entirely.

## The Dominant Arc of Book V

```
divine-inflation → wrath unleashed → first transgression (goddess wounded)
  → second transgression (god wounded) → Olympian farce → cosmic self-repair
```

That is why the playlist moves Strauss → Holst → Nielsen → Debussy → Bartók → Berlioz → Shostakovich → Rameau → Haydn. The arc is not military. It is the Iliad's universe being violated by one man, then quietly patching itself, then pretending the hole was never there.
