# Gilgamesh Tablet I: Kingship, the Wild, Desire

Gilgamesh's opening is the moment where the problem of power, left without a mirror, generates its own solution — not through divine punishment, but through divine craft. The gods do not diminish Gilgamesh; they build his equal out of clay. Tablet I is the engineering of a friendship that does not yet exist: three movements — a king without limit, a wild man formed from nothing, a woman who opens the door between those two worlds. It is civilizational genesis disguised as a hero story.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Elgar — "Nimrod"**
The Standard Version of the epic opens not with action but with commemoration: *He who saw the deep* — the one who knew all things, who traveled to the ends of the earth, who brought back knowledge from before the flood, who built the walls of Uruk. This is not narrative; it is vaunt, the poem declaring its hero complete before telling us who he was. Elgar's Nimrod opens in exactly this way: quiet sovereign gravity that builds, without urgency, into architectural weight. By the time it reaches full orchestration it is the sound of a civilization declaring itself. The walls of Uruk are the image; Nimrod is what those walls sound like from the inside.

**2. Handel — *Zadok the Priest***
Gilgamesh is two-thirds divine and one-third mortal. He takes the daughters at the door. He exhausts the young men. His energy is not malevolent — it is *unbounded*. The Urukians do not cry "remove him" but "match him." Handel's coronation anthem stages power as a right that fills all available space: the long unison strings before the choral entry are the silence before a force arrives; the entry is the force itself. Sovereign excess staged as glory. That is Tablet I's paradox — the king's power is real and legitimate and ruinous because it has no counterpart yet.

**3. Verdi — "Va, pensiero"**
The Urukians' lamentation is not revolution. They do not riot. They cry upward, to Anu, asking the great sky-god to notice what his city endures. Collective voice, suppressed, rising. *Va, pensiero* is the formal sound of that: a people's chorus, contained and enormous at once, grief organized into harmony. The Holst "Mars" option was considered and rejected — Mars is military aggression, and this is not a revolt. It is a people's sustained appeal to a higher order. The difference is everything.

**4. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5, I**
Anu and Aruru do not deliberate for long. Aruru takes clay, pinches it, casts it on the steppe — Enkidu exists. This is cosmological decision-making: inevitable, economical, without argument. The Fifth Symphony's opening does not invite discussion. It arrives. Four notes that contain the mechanism of a world. The creation of Enkidu is not miraculous in the Mesopotamian register; it is what the gods do when the grammar requires it.

**5. Sibelius — Symphony No. 2, II**
Enkidu lives with the animals. He eats grass. He runs with the gazelles to the waterhole. He has no name for bread or beer or clothing or Uruk. He exists in a pre-human temporality: unresolved, unhurried, adapted. Sibelius's second symphony slow movement is the sound of a landscape before history has organized it — tonal, but open-ended, wandering without resolution. Not lost (Enkidu is perfectly at home), but outside human grammar. Dvořák's New World Symphony was the obvious option; it was rejected as too romanticized, too elegiac about the wild from the outside. Sibelius stays closer to the ground.

**6. Holst — "Saturn, the Bringer of Old Age"**
The trapper returns to his father pale. Something in the wilderness fills his traps, tears his nets, releases the animals he catches. It is not attack — it is the slow, systematic undoing of human craft by a force operating at a different scale. Saturn is the right planet precisely because it is not Mars. Mars is war; Saturn is the pressure of something larger and older that simply doesn't recognize the trapper's world. Enkidu's presence in the wild is ominous without being violent. The music needs that heaviness — patient, inevitable, operating below the level of human intention.

**7. Wagner — *Tristan und Isolde*, Prelude to Act I**
Shamhat unveils herself. For six days and seven nights. The tablets are explicit, unashamed, and entirely serious: this is not an erotic episode but the central mechanism of the tablet. Desire in the Gilgamesh epic is the force that crosses the threshold between the animal and the human, between the wild and the city. Wagner's Tristan Prelude is chromatic longing that neither resolves nor ends — desire held in a state of permanent deferral that nonetheless *transforms*. The Iliad arc uses this for Achilles' rage; the parallel is intentional. Both wrath and desire are forces that hold their subjects suspended, altered, unable to return to what they were. The Prelude is the correct register because it does not conclude. It opens a world.

**8. Schubert — "Der Leiermann"**
After seven days, Enkidu turns back to his animals. The gazelles flee. He cannot keep pace. The legs that once equaled theirs are changed. He has crossed something that cannot be uncrossed. Winterreise ends with a hurdy-gurdy player at the edge of a frozen road, turning his instrument that no one hears — the purest sound of dispossession in the Western song tradition. Enkidu is not grieving in the human sense; he has not yet learned grief. But something has been taken from him that he cannot name, and something is opening that he cannot yet see. Schubert's final song holds both: loss without self-pity, the turning of a wheel that keeps going.

**9. Debussy — "La cathédrale engloutie"**
Shamhat begins to speak. She names Gilgamesh. She describes the city. She offers bread, beer, oil, clothing, human companionship. Each word is a layer of human culture settling over Enkidu — a whole grammar of civilization becoming perceptible to someone who had no name for any of it. Debussy's sunken cathedral rises from the water slowly, through harmonics that accumulate without hurry, as if a whole civilization were surfacing from depth. The structure of the piece is emergence: submerged, then visible and vast, then receding. That is Enkidu's arc through this scene: something enormous is becoming legible. No other piece in the Western repertoire describes the emergence of a world from beneath formlessness with this specificity.

**10. Bach — *Goldberg Variations*, Aria**
The shepherds dress Enkidu in clothes. They set bread before him. They show him how to do the work of the camp, to guard the flocks at night. Enkidu, who was wild, is now learning the repeating patterns of human social life: food-ritual, labor, hierarchy, schedule. Bach's Aria is the ground-bass of a variation form: a pattern that will change and return and change again, but whose deep structure is stable. This is what the shepherd's camp gives Enkidu — not just bread, but a grammar of repetition, the architecture of human dailiness. Law in the sense of *form that makes culture possible*.

**11. Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel***
Gilgamesh dreams twice. First a meteorite falls from the sky, heavy as the firmament itself; he tries to lift it and cannot; yet he loves it as a wife. Then an axe lies in the street, beautiful, and he carries it to his mother Ninsun. Both dreams are the same: something is coming whose weight and beauty he will love before he understands. Pärt's mirror-canon is two lines moving in parallel — never quite resolving, suspended in the same harmonic space, each reflection of the other. The exposed piano against sustained violin is the sound of a waiting that is not anxiety but attention: something is approaching. The dreams are prophetic stillness; the music is prophetic stillness.

**12. Messiaen — *Quartet for the End of Time*, V. "Louange à l'Éternité de Jésus"**
Ninsun, Gilgamesh's divine mother, interprets the dreams: the companion will come, he will love him as a wife, the companion will never forsake him. This is the tablet's first divine speech, the first prophecy. Messiaen's fifth movement — solo cello over sustained piano chords, extreme slowness, the longest duration in the Quartet — is the sound of divine speech when it has nothing to prove. It does not argue; it declares. The slowness is not meditative but overwhelming: time stretched until it touches something outside time. Ninsun is a goddess telling a king the shape of his life. The music needed the temperature of the eternal, not the dramatic.

## The Dominant Arc of Tablet I

```
memory → kingship → supplication → fate → exile → fate →
desire → desire → metamorphosis → law → revelation → divine-intervention
```

The arc moves from establishment through disruption into transformation. Gilgamesh's world is presented at its fullest, then strained, then answered by the creation of its opposite — a being of pure nature who is then slowly drawn across the threshold into human culture. By the end of Tablet I, Enkidu has not yet met Gilgamesh. The two movements (the king's excess, the wild man's civilization) are on a trajectory of collision. The dominant force is **metamorphosis** — but not the metamorphosis of combat or catastrophe. Tablet I is the metamorphosis of formation itself: a wild thing becoming the mirror a king did not know he needed.

Desire is the hinge. It always is.
