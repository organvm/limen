# Iliad Book I: Rage, Plague, Olympus

_Do not score Book I as "the Trojan War." Score it as the moment where private insult becomes historical catastrophe — plague, quarrel, hierarchy, divine leverage, withheld murder, wounded honor, withdrawal, supplication, Olympian pettiness.

**1. Wagner — Tristan und Isolde — Prelude** (wrath) — Book I begins with rage as causality, not battle as spectacle. The Tristan Prelude is unresolved pressure — it suspends, does not discharge. That is Achilles' mēnis: a force introduced before it becomes visible. "Mars" begins too externally; Homer begins deeper.


**2. Gluck — Iphigénie en Aulide — Overture to Act I** (sacrifice) — The Achaean world is already morally contaminated by sacrifice, rank, command, forced exchange. Book I turns on a brutal grammar of exchange: Chryseis, Briseis, ransom, honor-prizes, plague as counterpayment.


**3. Berlioz — Les Troyens — 'Malheureux roi!'** (fate) — Berlioz as mythic atmosphere — Cassandra's prophetic dread, doomed royal knowledge, not generic epic grandeur.

**4. Berlioz — Les Troyens — Act II Introduction** (plague) — Chryses' prayer is a successful appeal to divine order; Apollo enforces insulted sacred hierarchy. A world where human arrogance triggers a force above human law.

**5. Holst — The Planets — 'Mars, the Bringer of War'** (wrath) — The 5/4 pulse is hostile, asymmetrical, hard to settle into — a broken heroic economy of prizes, status, command, possession, visible honor. The scene is verbal, but already war.

**6. Stravinsky — The Rite of Spring — 'The Augurs of Spring'** (sacrifice) — The ritual violence underneath heroic society. Holst gives military aggression; Stravinsky gives ritualized social violence — both levels of the quarrel.

**7. Brahms — Symphony No. 4 — I. Allegro non troppo** (law) — Achilles grips the sword and does not draw it. Brahms is form under pressure, rage under grammar — Athena redirects wrath from murder into speech, then withdrawal. The poem is haunted by the murder Achilles does not commit.

**8. Debussy — La mer — 'De l'aube à midi sur la mer'** (exile) — After the masculine assembly, Achilles returns to the shore and calls Thetis. The sea is maternal, pre-human, divine, liminal — a living threshold.

**9. Mahler — Symphony No. 5 — IV. Adagietto** (grief) — The funeral march belongs later (Patroclus, Hector, Book XXIV). In Book I, Achilles is insulted, wounded, alive, loved by his mother. The Adagietto is private, suspended, self-enclosed grief — wounded inwardness, not public heroic death.

**10. Wagner — Das Rheingold — Prelude** (divine-intervention) — Grows like a world-system coming into being — Thetis converts Achilles' private injury into cosmic policy. Her appeal is geopolitical; Zeus' nod becomes a mechanism that will kill men.

**11. Handel — Music for the Royal Fireworks — Overture** (kingship) — Ceremonial authority, court splendor, sovereign display — Zeus' world, power staged as grandeur. Olympus needs court music: rank, order, display, rule.

**12. Rameau — Platée — Ouverture** (comedy) — The Olympian ending is grand and ridiculous at once: Zeus and Hera quarrel; Hephaestus mediates; the gods laugh; mortals suffer below. Book I ends in divine comedy.

**13. Offenbach — Orpheus in the Underworld — Overture** (comedy) — Only at the end, after Hephaestus restores laughter — it exposes the scandal of the Olympians: immortals laugh, drink, flirt, quarrel while humans are arranged for death.
