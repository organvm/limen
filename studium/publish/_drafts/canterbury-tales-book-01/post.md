# Canterbury Tales — General Prologue: Spring, the Company, the Portraits

# Canterbury Tales — General Prologue: Music Arc

The General Prologue's opening is the moment where spring becomes an argument. Chaucer does not begin with a character, a conflict, or a setting — he begins with a season, and asks why seasons move people. 'Whan that Aprille with his shoures soote / The droghte of March hath perced to the roote' is cosmological before it is social: the earth is being changed by forces above and below it, and those forces act on human beings the way they act on the buds — not by command but by nature, by the pricking of their *corages*. By the time Chaucer reaches the Tabard Inn and the 'nine and twenty' pilgrims gathered there by chance, he has already told us everything we need to know about them: they are the earth in April. They are going somewhere because something cosmic requires them to.

Then he describes them, one by one. And this is where the Prologue becomes something no estate-portrait tradition had been before: a comedy that works by affection rather than condemnation. The Monk violates his Rule with cheerful authority. The Friar sells absolution with twinkling eyes. The Prioress wears a brooch that says *Amor vincit omnia* over a habit that says she is above love. None of them is denounced. All of them are alive. The dominant force of the Prologue is comedy in its formal literary sense — the mode that holds the social world in view and finds it ridiculous and forgivable in the same gesture.

## Per-Track Reasoning

**1. Debussy — Prélude à l'après-midi d'un faune** — spring as cosmic engine.
The Prologue's first eighteen lines are not scene-setting; they are argument. The dry earth is pierced, bathed, engendered. Zephyrus breathes into groves and heaths. Small birds sleep with open eyes because 'nature priketh hem in hir corages.' Debussy's Faun Prelude opens with the same logic — not a scene but a state of becoming, warmth arriving from below and above simultaneously, the flute tracing a figure that is almost an idea before it is a melody. Metamorphosis here is not transformation-into-something-else; it is the world waking from a contraction. This is the only register that matches Chaucer's opening clause.

**2. Monteverdi — L'Orfeo, Sinfonia (Act I)** — the world made young.
'And smale foweles maken melodye / That slepen al the nyght with open ye / (So priketh hem Nature in hir corages).' The poem pivots from the vegetative world to the animal world to the human world in six lines, and the force governing all three is the same: desire structured as impulse. Monteverdi's pastoral sinfonia — light brass, strings, a world before complication — is the sound of a spring morning in which everything is in agreement. Orfeo is the poet who moves the world by singing; the birds who sing all night in Chaucer's April are doing the same thing by other means.

**3. Handel — Messiah, 'He shall feed his flock' (Part I, No. 20)** — pilgrimage as social desire.
The transition from spring to pilgrimage in Chaucer is instantaneous and grammatically seamless: 'Thanne longen folk to goon on pilgrimages.' The *thanne* (then) is the poem's hinge — it converts cosmology into sociology in a single word. Handel's aria belongs to Part I of the Messiah, a moment of gathering and pastoral care, a voice calling people toward a destination that is both holy and restful. It catches the double register of Chaucer's pilgrimage: genuinely pious and genuinely social at once, without the latter undermining the former.

**4. Haydn — String Quartet Op. 33 No. 3 'The Bird,' I. Allegro moderato** — the fellowship at the Tabard.
The narrative frame shifts abruptly from the world at large to 'In Southwerk at the Tabard as I lay.' The narrator has become specific: one person, one evening, one inn. Into this inn falls a company of twenty-nine strangers 'by aventure,' by accident. Haydn's 'Bird' quartet is named for the ornamental grace-notes that flit through its opening — a social music, alert to company, full of small recognitions and polite surprises. Four voices arranging themselves is the social logic of Chaucer's Tabard scene; the tone is genial, the comedy of arrangement not yet satire.

**5. Elgar — Enigma Variations, 'Nimrod'** — the Knight.
The Knight is the type at full formal extension. Twenty-seven lines, fifteen mortal battles, every Christian theater of war from Alexandria to Turkey, 'trouthe and honour, fredom and curteisye' — and then, quietly: his coat is stained from his armor, his horse is not fine, and he is 'meeke as is a mayde.' Elgar's Nimrod is warm retrospection — not triumphant, not elegiac, but the sound of genuine respect for something that is also, in some sense, already past. Chaucer is not satirizing the Knight; he is honoring the type while showing us a world that the type alone cannot contain.

**6. Scarlatti — Keyboard Sonata in D major, K. 119** — the Squire.
The Squire arrives immediately after his father, and the contrast is the point: the Knight is measured, the Squire is musical. 'Singynge he was, or floytynge, al the day; / He was as fressh as is the month of May.' He is twenty years old, his coat is embroidered like a meadow full of flowers, and he has not slept because of love. Scarlatti's D major sonata is ornamental bravura — quick, decorative, self-delighted, technically accomplished. The harpsichord's ornamentation mirrors the embroidery on the Squire's coat almost literally. He is the spring poem made into a person.

**7. Mozart — Così fan tutte, Overture** — the Prioress.
Madame Eglentine is Chaucer's most sophisticated comic portrait because the comedy is invisible to its subject. She speaks French learned at Stratford-upon-Bow (not Paris). She weeps for a mouse. She wears a brooch that reads *Amor vincit omnia* — love conquers all — over a religious habit that is supposed to have conquered love. She is not a hypocrite in the simple sense; she is a person who has successfully convinced herself that her performance of refinement is refinement. Mozart's Così overture is the sound of that — elegant, slightly knowing, the music of people who are playing roles they believe in completely and which the audience sees through entirely. The joke requires both levels at once.

**8. Rossini — The Barber of Seville, Overture** — the Monk.
The Monk's portrait is Chaucer's first great monologue-through-description: we are given the Monk's own reasoning in indirect form. Why should he study? Why should he labor? Let Augustine keep his work. 'And I seyde his opinion was good.' The narrator's assent is the deadpan center of the joke. Rossini builds overtures from pure energy — the famous crescendo from nothing to overwhelming volume — and there is something of the Monk's cheerful self-justification in it, the accumulating momentum of a man who has decided that his appetite is a philosophy and is presenting it with increasing confidence.

**9. Rameau — Platée, Suite: Tambourins** — the Friar.
Huberd the Friar is charm as occupation. He lisps for wantonness, his eyes twinkle 'as doon the sterres in the frosty nyght,' he knows every tavern keeper better than any leper or beggar (for he wouldn't be seen with such poor types), and he has arranged many a marriage of young women 'at his owene cost.' Rameau's *Platée* is his comic ballet, a work built entirely on burlesque — pretension celebrated and punctured in the same breath. The Tambourins are dance-driven, light, irresistibly kinetic, the music of a performer who has elevated charm into a system. The Friar performs piety the way Platée performs dignity: with consummate style, no self-awareness, and an audience too delighted to object.

**10. Bach — Well-Tempered Clavier I, Prelude No. 1 in C major (BWV 846)** — the Clerk of Oxford.
'Noght o word spak he moore than was neede, / And that was seyd in forme and reverence, / And short and quyk and ful of hy sentence.' The Clerk is the Prologue's intellectual conscience: threadbare, pale, riding a horse as lean as a rake, and spending every coin on books. He has twenty volumes of Aristotle where others have fine robes. Bach's C major Prelude is structural minimalism at its most complete — every note is weight-bearing, there is no ornament, the form IS the meaning. It is the musical translation of 'gladly wolde he lerne, and gladly teche.'

**11. Offenbach — Orpheus in the Underworld, 'Galop infernal'** — the procession moves.
The Prologue ends when the Host proposes the storytelling game and the company agrees. The frame is set. They rise early, they mount, they ride toward Canterbury. Offenbach's Galop — the famous Can-Can, originally depicting the sinners of Hades in gleeful chaos — is the sound of a crowd in motion, too fast, too cheerful, slightly ridiculous, irresistibly alive. It exposes the General Prologue's secret, which Chaucer never states and never conceals: this pilgrimage is a party. The Host knows it. Chaucer knows it. The Monk and the Friar and the Wife of Bath (waiting in the wings) definitely know it. The Canterbury Tales begin the moment everyone admits they are going to have a very good time on the road to a holy place.

## The Dominant Arc

The General Prologue moves from cosmological metamorphosis (spring forces everything to move) through the comedy of social type (each portrait a category made individual) to comic procession (the company in motion, the storytelling game begun). The dominant force is **comedy** — not the comedy of ridicule but of the social world held in clear-eyed, affectionate, critical view. Chaucer sees the Monk's contradiction, the Friar's charm, the Prioress's self-performance, and does not condemn any of them. He just describes them. That is what social speech does: it sees the world with accurate warmth, which is the hardest thing.
