# Iliad Book XXIV: Ransom, Weeping, Burial

Book XXIV is the moment where the war stops being war. Not because peace comes — it does not
come, the war will resume, Troy will fall, Achilles will die — but because for one night, in
one tent, two men who are enemies sit together and weep for their own dead, and that is enough.
Priam has crossed the plain in the dark, guided by a god in disguise, to clasp the hands that
killed his sons and beg for the body of his greatest. Achilles has been commanded by Zeus
through Thetis to give it back. The scene should not work. It does. What Book XXIV gives us —
the poem's last gift — is mercy, which is distinct from forgiveness and reconciliation and
peace, and which is, in its way, harder. The arc is divine restraint, a journey through the
night, the supplication that should be impossible, shared humanity across the body of the
enemy, the threefold lament, and burial. This is the coda of the *Iliad*, and it is scored
accordingly: the funeral-march register opens fully here, everything held back from Books I
through XXIII finally arrives, and the three women's voices close what the quarrel between two
men began.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Strauss — *Also sprach Zarathustra*: "Von den Hinterweltlern" — *divine-intervention***

Apollo has held the gods' collective anger at Achilles for twelve days — twelve days during
which Achilles has dragged Hector's body around Patroclus' tomb at dawn and left it in the
dirt. Now Zeus acts. He sends Thetis to Achilles with a command: the gods are angry; release
the body. This is the gods not as battle-managers but as moral limit — they are imposing a
boundary on grief gone feral. Strauss's "Von den Hinterweltlern" (Of the Afterworldly, the
second section of *Also sprach*) is not the famous opening sunrise. It is the hushed passage
that follows it: strings dissolving into a searching uncertainty, forces calibrating from a
height before descending. We have used this piece twice before in the cycle for exactly this
function — Books II and XVI, each a divine pivot that recalibrates the poem's mechanism — and
it earns its third appearance here as the third and largest divine-pivot of the *Iliad*. The
brass/strings contrast that divine-intervention demands arrives in the transition out, as the
order is issued and the divine will descends from deliberation into command.

**2. Handel — *Solomon*: "The Arrival of the Queen of Sheba" — *divine-intervention***

Zeus sends Iris to Priam with the instruction: go to Achilles with gifts, alone, at night.
The instruction is not comfort — it overrides every rational instinct of self-preservation
the old king has left. Handel's "Arrival of the Queen of Sheba" is chosen not for triumph
but for its specific color: the two-oboe brightness against strings, a sudden fleet clarity,
the sense of a message arriving from a higher order with perfect certainty. This is what a
divine instruction feels like — not dark and searching (Strauss) but specific, clear, almost
cheerful in its authority. The divine-intervention requirement is sudden color shift, brass/
strings contrast; Handel delivers it in its most transparent, daylit form. After the weight
of the divine deliberation, Iris's message to Priam is brief and certain: *go*.

**3. Mahler — Symphony No. 9: IV. Adagio (closing pages) — *grief***

Before Priam leaves, he and Hecuba argue. She tells him he will die. He tells her he does not
care — he wants to see Hector's face one final time, and he wants to touch the man who killed
his sons. This is grief operating as recklessness: the wish to die is genuine in Priam. He
has already survived what no parent should survive, and the world no longer holds him in the
way it once did. Mahler's Ninth Adagio in its closing pages — not the whole movement, but the
passage where the music begins to thin, where the phrases come farther and farther apart with
increasing silence between them — is the right scale for this. This is not the private wounded
grief of the Adagietto (Achilles at the shore, Book I) and not the cosmic divine grief of the
Ninth's full arc (Sarpedon in Book XVI). This is the grief of a very old man who has decided
that grief is now the whole of his reason for living, and that is enough reason to walk into
the enemy's camp. Mahler's closing pages do not resolve; they thin, as if losing their grip
on existence. That is Priam preparing to ransom his dead son: exhausted, grief-reckless, past
fear, already partly on the other side.

**4. Schubert — String Quintet in C major: II. Adagio — *supplication***

Hermes comes in the shape of a young man and guides Priam's wagon through the night — past
the sentries, across the plain that has been a killing-ground for ten years, to the edge of
Achilles' compound. This passage is among the strangest and most tender in Homer: the old king
moving through enemy territory in total silence, guided by a god who looks like a boy, in a
night that feels outside of time. The supplication force requires suspended cadence and exposed
melodic line — the long arc of a request still in transit, still crossing the space between
asking and being answered. Schubert's C major Quintet Adagio is the most naturally apt piece
in the repertoire for this transit. The addition of a second cello to the quartet creates a
warmth and depth no quartet achieves — an intimate resonance that feels held rather than
performed — and the melody suspends over a harmonic space that never quite resolves, unhurried,
impossibly tender, moving through the dark toward something it cannot yet reach. The crossing
of the plain is night music, and this is its score.

**5. Bach — *St. Matthew Passion*: "Erbarme dich, mein Gott" — *supplication***

Priam enters Achilles' tent and takes his hands — the hands that killed his sons — and kisses
them. He invokes Peleus, Achilles' own father: pity me as you would pity him. Think of your
father and look at me. This is the poem's supreme act of supplication, the one that should not
work by any logic available and does. Bach's aria was used in Book XVI for Patroclus' weeping
before Achilles — a younger man begging a friend out of mercy — and it earns its return here
because Homer himself creates the rhyme: Patroclus wept and clasped Achilles' knees; Priam
weeps and clasps his hands. The two supplications are the moral architecture of the second half
of the poem, and the same piece scores both because they are the same gesture at different
scales. Peter's aria is the right vehicle: the exposed solo violin sustaining a long-breathed
ache over a walking bass that will not stop, the melodic line making its plea without ornament,
the cadence suspending — always suspending — because the supplication force's requirement is
exactly that: the plea still in the air, not yet answered, hanging in the interval between the
asking and the response. The hinge of the entire *Iliad* is here, and it sounds like this.

**6. Wagner — *Götterdämmerung*: "Siegfried's Funeral March" — *grief***

This is the moment. From Book I's demoted list — "save for Patroclus, Hector, Book XXIV" —
through every subsequent book that held this piece back, refusing to spend its register before
it was earned: the funeral march opens here, and only here. Achilles weeps for Patroclus.
Priam weeps for Hector. They do not weep for each other or comfort each other or persuade each
other — they weep alongside each other, each inside his own catastrophe, each recognizing in
the other a form of grief that mirrors his own without being identical to it. Achilles speaks
first of his father, then of Patroclus. Priam weeps and then is still. Homer gives them both
the same gesture: they weep until the weeping is done, and then they look at each other.
Wagner's Funeral March builds from a single tolling figure in the low strings into one of the
largest orchestral monuments in the Western canon — not one death but the idea of heroic
mortality itself, the full cost of the world the *Iliad* has spent twenty-four books
constructing. This is not private grief (the Adagietto's register) or divine grief (Mahler's
Ninth) or civic grief (what comes at the walls). This is the grief of the entire poem
condensed into a single room with two men, each of whom has lost the thing that made living
worth continuing, and who can see that fact in each other's face. The Funeral March is at once
intimate and monumental. So is this scene. The register opens here because it has nowhere left
to be saved for.

**7. Barber — Adagio for Strings — *grief***

After the weeping, Achilles acts. He will give back the body. He has Patroclus' women wash
and wrap Hector in a shroud so that Priam cannot see the wounds — so that the sight of what
he has done to Hector will not rekindle his own rage and break what has just been accomplished
between them. This is Achilles functioning as a human being rather than an instrument of
wrath: he is protecting the grief of his enemy's father from himself. The Barber Adagio was
used in Book XVI for the terminal image of Patroclus' body — and it returns here for the same
function one register lower, after the Funeral March has passed through. Barber's Adagio rises
and does not arrive; it builds to a climax that cuts off rather than lands. That structural
quality — the grief that cannot complete itself — is exactly what Achilles' act of preparation
is: an incomplete mercy, a kindness that costs something real, performed by a man who is not
finished grieving and knows it.

**8. Fauré — *Requiem*: "In Paradisum" — *salvation***

Achilles tells Priam that even Niobe ate, after all her children died. They sit together at a
table in the enemy's camp and share food, two men who have spent the better part of a decade
as instruments of each other's destruction. Homer gives us the detail: Priam marveled at
Achilles' size and beauty; Achilles marveled at the old man's face and words. This is not
reconciliation — the war does not end, Achilles does not promise to spare Troy, Priam does not
forgive. It is recognition: the moment when the human form of the other becomes visible through
the role of enemy, when the other ceases to be a category and becomes a person whose suffering
you can see from the inside. Fauré's "In Paradisum" is not the Dies Irae grief of the Requiem's
center — it is the grief that comes after grief has been fully accepted, the radiant stillness
at the end of the liturgy, the prayer for the departed soul as it crosses. The force here is
still grief, but grief resolved into presence rather than lamentation. The *Iliad* does not
offer transcendence — but at this table, in this scene, it offers something that functions like
grace: two enemies eating together, each seen, each real, before the world resumes.

**9. Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel* — *supplication***

Priam sleeps in the enemy's compound, guarded by no one but a god. The scene is suspended,
almost dreamlike — the old king has accomplished the impossible and is simply asleep, exhausted,
in the place that should be most dangerous to him. Hermes wakes him near midnight: the Greeks
will demand ransom too when they find him; go before they discover you. Pärt's "Spiegel im
Spiegel" — mirror in the mirror — is pure suspended cadence at its most elemental: a single
piano arpeggio and a violin line that extends without arriving, time held outside of time, a
held breath. The supplication force's requirement is suspended cadence and exposed melodic
line; here that requirement is realized with the fewest possible notes, in the music's most
exposed register. "Mirror in the mirror" is the exact image the title suggests and the scene
contains: Priam and Achilles have become, for this one night, reflections of each other — two
men whose grief is structured the same way, who recognize that structure in the other. The
violin sustains, the piano moves beneath it without landing, and the old king sleeps in his
enemy's tent under a god's protection.

**10. Berlioz — *Les Troyens*: "Malheureux roi!" (Cassandre) — *grief***

Hermes guides Priam back across the plain before dawn. Cassandra sees the cart from the walls
of Troy and raises the cry; the people pour out of the gates, and Homer is precise about what
they are doing: they should be going to meet a man returning, but they are going to meet a
corpse. Berlioz scored the opening of the Trojan register in Book I — Cassandra's prophetic
dread, doomed royal knowledge — and it earns its return here because the poem's first Trojan
note and its last Trojan note are the same note. Book I's Cassandra prophesied what was coming;
Book XXIV's Cassandra laments the first installment of its fulfillment. The cycle closes: the
music that opened the Trojan doom now opens the Trojan public mourning. Grief here is crossing
from the private register (the tent, the two men, the night) into the civic register — the
people, the walls, the first collective act of lamentation.

**11. Purcell — *Dido and Aeneas*: "When I am laid in earth" — *grief***

Andromache's lament is the first of the three and the most specific. She does not address the
crowd or the gods or the abstraction of loss — she addresses Hector directly. She describes
exactly what has been destroyed: not a category but a particular man, the warmth of a particular
bed, a child who will grow up in a doomed city without a father's protection. Purcell's Dido's
Lament is the paradigm of grief as direct address: a woman speaking to what she has lost, the
ground bass descending in measured, inexorable steps that do not stop while the voice above
sustains the long line that cannot find its close. The grief force's formal requirement — slow
tempo, inward line — is nowhere more completely realized in the canon than in Purcell's
construction here: the descending ground gives grief its formal structure while the voice above
it keeps reaching toward someone who is no longer there. Andromache's lament in Homer is almost
exactly this structure: the descending enumeration of what will never return, the sustained
address to the absent, the refusal to close.

**12. Verdi — *Otello*: "Willow Song" (Salce, salce) — *grief***

Hecuba's lament differs from Andromache's in a specific way: she marvels. The gods have
preserved Hector's body, kept it incorrupt through everything Achilles did to it. Her grief is
therefore not only loss but also a bewildered recognition of divine care — her son was beloved
of the gods even after death, even through all the fury. Verdi's "Willow Song" (Desdemona's
lament in *Otello*, Act IV) carries exactly this register: grief that is shot through with a
reaching tenderness, the voice moving between lamentation and a kind of amazement at the
beloved, a grief that keeps touching what it has lost rather than only marking its absence.
Desdemona sings of Barbary's maid and the willow she planted in memory — grief that keeps
noticing beauty in the midst of its own extinction. That is Hecuba's note in Homer: the mother
who lost the greatest of her sons and grieves, and also marvels, and the marveling does not
contradict the grief.

**13. Brahms — *Four Serious Songs*: No. 4, "Wenn ich mit Menschen und mit Engelszungen redete" — *grief***

Helen's lament is the last, the most isolated, and the most unexpected in structure: she was
the cause of the war, and she grieves for the one man in Troy who never blamed her for it. She
says she has not had a friend in Troy in all the years since she came — only Hector stood
between her and the contempt of everyone else. Her grief is shot through with exile and guilt:
the grief of the person who knows herself to be the origin of the disaster, mourning the one
person who refused to treat her accordingly. Brahms's Fourth Serious Song sets a text from 1
Corinthians — "Though I speak with the tongues of men and of angels" — arriving at the verse
on love: the love that endures all things, suffers all things, never fails. The baritone voice
alone against the piano in Brahms's late style — formal gravity, the inward line, the sense of
a final reckoning — is the right register for Helen's voice because her grief has a specific
texture no other lament in the poem shares: guilt, isolation, and the strange testimony that
this man was kind to her when no one else was. Brahms's architecture gives grief the grammar of
endurance, and endurance is what Helen has been left with. Of the three laments, hers is the
loneliest. And it is the last voice the poem gives us before the burial that closes everything.

## The dominant arc of Book XXIV

```
divine-restraint → the night journey → supplication across the impossible gap →
grief meeting grief → the body given back → shared mortality → the threefold lament → burial
```

Book XXIV is the book of **grief** — but grief in its most mature form: grief that recognizes
itself in the enemy, grief that can still perform mercy, grief that holds its formal shape
(the threefold lament, the eleven-day truce, the pyre) even when the world that generated it
is already ending. The arc moves from the gods imposing a limit on grief gone feral (divine-
intervention) through the long supplication that traverses impossible distances (night, enemy
territory, the hands that killed your son) into the central scene where two men simply weep —
and then, through the act of giving the body back and the feast and the sleep, into the great
coda: Cassandra on the walls, Andromache, Hecuba, Helen, each in her own voice, each making
the same discovery in a different register. The funeral-march register opens fully here because
this is the only place in the poem where it belongs: not one death but the full cost of the
heroic world, held in a room with two men who can see it in each other's face.
