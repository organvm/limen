# Ramayana Bala Kanda: Origin, Kingship, the Breaking of the Bow

# Ramayana Bala Kanda: Origin, Kingship, the Breaking of the Bow

Bala Kanda is the moment where the Ramayana is not yet tragic. It is the one kanda that ends
in light — a wedding, a procession, a city that has not yet been undone. That is its precise
moral weight: not innocence (the poem is already dense with divine machinery), but the last
fully unclouded moment. A reader who does not feel the cost of Bala Kanda's beauty — who does
not hear, in the wedding procession's return to Ayodhya, the silence that follows — is not yet
reading the poem. The music chosen here tries to hold both registers at once: the luminous and
the already-shadowed, the ceremonial and the barely-heard undertone of what the next kanda will
unmake.

## Track Reasoning

**1. Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel*** — *revelation*

The Ramayana begins inside a layer of recursion. The sage Narada arrives at Valmiki's ashram
and recites the whole story in condensed form — the story already exists, the poet has not yet
written it. Then Brahma appears and commands Valmiki to give the tale its full body in verse.
And then Valmiki sits down and sees — in a sudden vision at the riverbank, watching a hunter
kill a mating bird — the meter itself (shloka, the couplet of 32 syllables) arising unbidden
from his grief. The poem does not originate in a poet's invention. It descends into one.

Pärt's *Spiegel im Spiegel* ("mirror in mirror") is a single piano note — a sustained,
repeating pulse — and a violin line that moves by semitone, endlessly resolving into and away
from the tonic. It does not accumulate toward anything. It reflects. That is exactly the
structure of Valmiki's invocation: the poem already exists, and the poet receives it from
within the still center of his attention. Revelation requires stillness, chant, and verticality;
Pärt gives all three without invoking a Western Christian sacred that would misframe the opening
of this Hindu text.

---

**2. Handel — *Zadok the Priest*** — *kingship*

Valmiki describes Ayodhya as a city twelve yojanas long, populous and prosperous, its streets
filled with music, its king the beloved of gods and men. Dasharatha's court is enumerated at
length: the ministers, the priests, the treasury, the army, the abundance of every provision.
This is not ironic grandeur — it is the poem's sincere attempt to establish what a good kingdom
looks like before it is unmade. Bala Kanda is the only kanda where the poem means its praise
of Ayodhya without reservation.

Handel's *Zadok the Priest* is the paradigm case of sincere ceremonial kingship in music. The
opening string texture builds for almost a minute — quiet, sustained, expectant — and then the
choir arrives with an authority that sounds like a fact of nature. There is no shadow in it.
This is not Walton's knowing pomp or Elgar's valedictory gravity: it is the real thing,
kingship as the poem presents Ayodhya before the plot has touched it. That sincerity is the
point. When Ayodhya Kanda undoes everything Bala Kanda establishes, the reader must have felt
the thing being unmade. Handel provides the thing, at full weight.

---

**3. Stravinsky — *Symphony of Psalms*: II. Fugue** — *divine-intervention*

Dasharatha has three wives and no heir. He summons the priest Rishyashringa and commissions
the putrakameshti yagna — a great fire sacrifice, elaborate and expensive, designed to compel
the gods to grant male children. From the sacrificial fire rises Agni himself, bearing a golden
vessel of payasam, which the king distributes with precise ritual calculation among his queens.
This is not a prayer: it is a technology. Fire is the medium through which heaven is accessed,
and the yagna is the protocol. The scene moves from political need (no heir, succession crisis)
through ritual obligation and into divine machinery, which responds.

Stravinsky's Fugue from the *Symphony of Psalms* is not pious but architecturally exact: the
theme in oboe and flute, then the structure builds voice by voice, and the full orchestra
arrives like the fire taking hold. There is something compulsive in it — sacred pressure as
formal procedure — and something vast at the center when the voices converge. That convergence
is the fire bearing the vessel. Divine-intervention requires sudden color shift and brass/strings
contrast; the Fugue delivers this inside a formal architecture that matches the yagna's own
grammar of accumulation and climax.

---

**4. Elgar — *The Wand of Youth Suite No. 1*: "Serenade"** — *kingship*

Four sons are born in rapid succession. Rama carries the divine fullness — he is the avatara,
Vishnu made flesh — but the poem describes his birth quietly: a luminous child, an auspicious
moment, the king beside himself with joy. There are no lightning-bolts. The divine descent is
not an apocalypse but a quiet arrival: a child who is more than a child, sovereignty in the
form of a newborn. The scene asks for music that is radiant without being thunderous, luminous
without being heavy.

Elgar's "Serenade" from *The Wand of Youth* — a suite he began as a child and returned to in
maturity — is the sound of something beginning in its most perfect form. It is tender and clear,
not yet tested, not yet shadowed. Kingship here is not the pomp of *Zadok the Priest* but the
intimate sovereignty of potential: everything is in front of this child, and the music knows it
without imposing that knowledge on the scene. Elgar at his most luminous, before a trace of
*Nimrod*'s grief enters the room.

---

**5. Beethoven — Symphony No. 5: I. Allegro con brio** — *fate*

The arrival of the sage Vishwamitra at Dasharatha's court is the hinge of Bala Kanda. He
demands Rama — a boy of perhaps sixteen — to accompany him into the forest to guard his fire
sacrifices from rakshasas. Dasharatha's resistance is total: he offers his entire army, he begs,
he weeps, he offers himself. Vishwamitra's rage rises — this is a brahmin's request, which the
Vedic order treats as a command from a superior. The guru Vasishtha intervenes and tells
Dasharatha to comply. The outcome was never in doubt, but the king's anguish is not a mistake
or a weakness: it is the accurate measurement of what is being set in motion.

Beethoven's Fifth begins with fate as rhythm — the four-note motif that does not threaten so
much as declare. It is not dark exactly; it is inexorable. Vishwamitra is not a villain but a
mechanism, and Dasharatha's grief is real and will not be answered. The hammered insistence of
the Fifth's first movement captures exactly this: something has arrived that cannot be negotiated
with, only endured. The fate machine starts here, in Bala Kanda, and will not stop running.

---

**6. Schubert — *Winterreise*: "Gute Nacht"** — *exile*

Rama and Lakshmana leave Ayodhya with Vishwamitra at dawn. This is the first departure, and
the poem handles it quietly — the princes go, Dasharatha watches, the city recedes. It is not
yet the great exile of Ayodhya Kanda, but it is its exact rehearsal: the same sons, the same
city, the same threshold. A reader coming back through the poem for the second time feels this
clearly. The departure scene carries the weight of what it prefigures.

Schubert's "Gute Nacht" opens *Winterreise* with departure without sentimentality. The wanderer
goes in the middle of the night; the household does not wake; he leaves quietly because there
is no other way to leave. The minor key, the walking bass, the voice that states its grief and
then keeps moving: this is the structure of Rama's departure from Ayodhya, both the small one
here and the larger one to come. Exile requires wandering tonality and open intervals;
Schubert's 'Gute Nacht' is a departure song in its bones — the wanderer goes and does not
return, and the walking bass is the sound of feet crossing a threshold that closes behind them.

---

**7. Bartók — *Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta*: III. Adagio** — *sacrifice*

In the forest, Vishwamitra leads the princes to his ashram. Rakshasas — demons whose very
presence defiles sacrificial fire — are polluting every ritual the sages attempt to complete.
Rama kills the demoness Tataka: his first killing, at his guru's explicit instruction, and
morally freighted in the telling. She was once a beautiful yaksha woman; a brahmin's curse
transformed her. Rama hesitates — there is a moment where the poem acknowledges the strangeness
of killing a woman, even a monstrous one — and then he acts. The scene is the night forest as
moral terrain: ancient, strange, and the killing necessary in ways that are not simple.

Bartók's Adagio from the *Music for Strings, Percussion and Celesta* is the closest Western
music has to the ancient forest at night: xylophone and muted strings, an atmosphere of deep
unease in which something known is not quite visible. The movement is not threatening but
genuinely liminal — it is the forest where rules apply differently, where the beautiful becomes
monstrous and the monstrous must be killed with a clear mind. Sacrifice requires ritual weight;
Bartók gives it the moral weight of the act performed in the dark that is both necessary and
not without cost.

---

**8. Bach — *The Art of Fugue*: Contrapunctus I** — *law*

Vishwamitra transmits to Rama the bala and atibala — sustaining mantras, protective formulae —
and then, over successive nights, the divine weapons: the pashupatastra (Shiva's weapon), the
brahmastra, weapons of fire and wind and storm, each named, each associated with a specific
deity, each received through a precise ritual invocation. This is not simple gift-giving. Each
astra carries conditions: it can only be used against a worthy opponent, it must be returned to
its source, it cannot be misused without devastating consequences. The transmission is
law — the grammar of sacred obligation encoded in weapon-form.

Bach's Contrapunctus I from *The Art of Fugue* takes a single subject and unfolds every
permitted transformation: it is formal mastery made audible, the rule-governed system
demonstrating its completeness from a single point. This is what Vishwamitra's transmission is:
not a list of weapons but an introduction to a complete formal system, each weapon a variation
on the subject of dharmic combat. Law requires formal clarity, repetition, and architecture;
Bach's contrapuntal architecture is its purest and most demanding form.

---

**9. Walton — *Crown Imperial*: March** — *kingship*

Vishwamitra brings the princes to Mithila, city of King Janaka, where Sita's swayamvara is
being held. Mithila is the second great royal tableau of Bala Kanda — different from Ayodhya
(grander in its ritual strangeness, built around the impossible object of Shiva's bow), but
fully sovereign in its own register. Princes and kings have come from across the known world
to compete for Sita. Janaka presides. The court is arranged with the full ceremonial weight
of the occasion: rank, order, display, the barely-suppressed knowledge that no one present has
been able to lift the bow and no one expects to.

Walton's *Crown Imperial* is ceremony without irony: the march that knows its own grandeur and
does not apologize for it. This is the right music for Janaka's court at the moment of maximum
pomp, before Rama makes the bow irrelevant. The shadow comes later — now, the court performs
itself at full display, and the music should honor that rather than ironize it. Kingship requires
ceremonial grandeur and sovereign display; Walton delivers it at the scale of an event that the
whole world has come to witness.

---

**10. Wagner — *Lohengrin*: Prelude to Act I** — *desire*

Rama alone can lift Shiva's bow. He receives permission from his guru, approaches the bow as
the assembled kings watch in silence, strings it, draws it to its full tension — and it shatters
with a crack like thunder. The earth shakes. In the silence that follows, Sita crosses the
floor and places her flower garland around his neck. The poem has been building toward this
moment since the kanda began: the avatar and his shakti, divinity recognizing divinity, the
marriage that is not merely royal but cosmic.

Wagner's *Lohengrin* Prelude begins above the threshold of audibility — violins so high and so
soft that the music seems to arrive from outside the concert hall — and builds through a long
arc of luminous accumulation to a full orchestral arrival, then retreats again to silence. That
arc is the exact shape of this scene: the enormous latent tension of Shiva's bow, the world
holding its breath, the moment of shattering and recognition, the garland placed in silence.
Desire requires chromatic longing and deferred resolution; Wagner's Prelude gives us desire
resolved into recognition — not catastrophe, not the Tristan Prelude's unassuaged ache, but
the thing finally arriving that was always on its way.

---

**11. Vivaldi — *Gloria in D major*, RV 589: "Et in terra pax"** — *divine-intervention*

The weddings are performed: Rama-Sita, and the three brothers to their queens. The gods approve
from above — flowers fall from the sky, the omens are auspicious, celestial musicians play.
This is divine-intervention in its benevolent register: heaven ratifying rather than
commandeering, the machinery that has been running (the yagna, the departure, the combat, the
bow) delivering its promised result. The scene is joyful and collective, many voices sharing
in a ceremony that concerns the entire cosmos.

Vivaldi's "Et in terra pax" from the *Gloria* is choral celebration at exactly this pitch:
formal joy that is also a kind of peace, voices joining in major-key clarity that does not
overreach into apotheosis. It is not thunderous enough to steal the scene from the wedding
itself, and it is not so quiet as to undercut the moment's genuine radiance. Divine-intervention
requires sudden color shift and brass/strings contrast; the Gloria's choral arrival provides
the brightness that the flower-shower demands without tipping into the terminus that belongs
to a later kanda.

---

**12. Ravel — *Pavane pour une infante défunte*** — *memory*

Bala Kanda closes with the return procession to Ayodhya. Rama and Sita enter the city as the
poem's ideal couple: the prince who broke the impossible bow, the queen who carried the divine
shakti, both of them young, both beloved, the city celebrating their return. This is the last
scene in the Ramayana that is not shadowed by what is coming. A first-time reader may not feel
it; a rereader cannot miss it. The poem holds its breath at this procession. What Ayodhya Kanda
opens with is already visible, barely, at the edge of this scene — the queen Kaikeyi's chamber,
the boons she has not yet claimed.

Ravel's *Pavane pour une infante défunte* was named, by Ravel himself, for a piece he might
have heard a long-dead Spanish princess playing in a palace — something radiant that is already
past at the moment of its imagining. It is not grief, but it knows about grief. The tune is
simple and beautiful and carries within its simplicity the awareness that the beauty is
temporary, that what is being described existed once and does not now. That is exactly Bala
Kanda's final register: the procession is real, the joy is real, the city is full of light —
and the poem already knows what will unmake all of it. Memory requires nostalgic recall and
faded recapitulation; Ravel gives us the procession as radiant-already-past — the unclouded
moment held in the amber of its own impermanence, known as precious precisely because it will
not last.

## The dominant arc of Bala Kanda

```
revelation → kingship → divine-machinery → birth-into-sovereignty → fate arriving →
departure → first combat → transmission of law → rival kingship → recognition →
divine ratification → illuminated return
```

Bala Kanda is the Ramayana's book of **kingship** — not kingship in crisis but kingship as
ideal form, which is why its dominant force can only be what it is before the crisis has begun.
The arc moves from the poet receiving a gift through the two great courts (Ayodhya, Mithila),
through Rama's education in the forest, and closes on the marriage that seals him to both Sita
and to dharma. Every scene in the kanda is an introduction to what will be tested: the king's
love for his sons, the guru's authority over the king, the hero's restraint in combat, the
recognition between two halves of the divine. Bala Kanda establishes the register of the whole
poem — not in spite of its luminosity, but because of it. You cannot feel the cost of what
Ayodhya Kanda destroys unless you have first felt, here, the full weight of what there was to
lose.
