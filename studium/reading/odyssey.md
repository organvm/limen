# Reading Orientation: The Odyssey

**Main question: What is return?**

Return is the Odyssey's subject in the way wrath is the Iliad's subject — it names the force, not the plot. The Iliad tells you in its first word what it is about (*mēnin*, wrath); the Odyssey tells you in its proem's governing word what kind of man it is about (*polutropon*, of many turns), and the action that word implies: turning, circling, trying to find the way back. But return in the Odyssey is not simple homecoming. It is a question: what does it mean to return to a place after twenty years? Is the man who comes home the same man who left? Is the home that receives him the same home? The poem presses on this question from both directions — the difficulties of getting back, and the strangeness of being back.

---

## Structure

The Odyssey is 24 books of hexameter verse, approximately 12,000 lines, and it divides into three architecturally distinct movements:

**The Telemachy (Books 1–4):** The poem begins with Odysseus's absence, not his presence. We see Ithaca — the suitors occupying his house, Penelope in suspension, Telemachus only beginning to become a man — before we see the man they are waiting for. Athena rousing Telemachus to travel to Pylos and Sparta in search of news of his father is the poem's opening gambit: the son must grow into his father's heir before the father can return.

**The Returns (Books 5–13):** Odysseus himself finally enters the poem in Book 5, on Ogygia, weeping. Books 5–12 are the famous wandering narrative (Cyclops, Circe, the Underworld, Sirens, Scylla and Charybdis, the Cattle of the Sun). But this sequence is structurally a retrospective: Odysseus tells his own story at the Phaeacians' court in Books 9–12. He is his own bard. Books 5–8 and 13 are the frame; the wandering is recollected, narrated, already past.

**The Recognition and Return (Books 13–24):** Odysseus arrives in Ithaca incognito. The poem's final movement is a slow, structurally complex recognition sequence — he is recognized by his dog, his nurse Eurycleia, a swineherd, his son — before the climax of the bow and the slaughter of the suitors (Book 22), the recognition by Penelope (Book 23), and the final armistice with the suitors' kin (Book 24).

## What to Watch in Book 1

Book 1 is not yet Odysseus's book — it is Telemachus's, and the household's, and the gods'. Read it as a diagnosis:

- **The proem (lines 1–10):** Homer condenses the whole poem into ten lines before the action begins. He names the hero's type (*polutropon*), the extent of his suffering, and the cause of his companions' death (their own folly, eating the Cattle of the Sun). Read these lines closely. The poem is already over; what follows is the telling.

- **The gods' council:** Notice that Zeus's first speech is not about Odysseus but about Aegisthus — a digression that functions as a principle. The gods are not arbitrary; mortals who ignore divine warning suffer consequences beyond what fate ordained. Odysseus is the counterexample: he is a man of many turns who survives by reading the situation.

- **Athena-as-Mentes:** Track every moment of disguise in this book. Athena's transformation into a Taphian stranger is the poem's first enactment of its central theme — return requires disguise, requires the identity that is known to be hidden beneath the identity that appears. This is Odysseus's method; the poem introduces it first through his patron goddess.

- **Telemachus:** He is, in Book 1, defined entirely by absence. His father is absent; his knowledge of his own birth is second-hand; his authority over his own house is disputed. Watch his transformation over Books 1–4 as a preparation for Odysseus's own transformation later.

## Daily Portion

Book 1 runs approximately 440 lines. A productive daily unit:

- **Day 1:** Lines 1–87 (proem through Zeus's speech on Aegisthus). Read in your chosen translation, then copy lines 1–5 of the Greek by hand (see below).
- **Day 2:** Lines 88–212 (Athena's speech through her descent to Ithaca and Telemachus's first sight of her).
- **Day 3:** Lines 213–323 (the conversation of Telemachus and Mentes — Athena's visit, the discussion of Odysseus, her prophecy of return).
- **Day 4:** Lines 324–444 (the bard Phemius, Penelope's grief, Telemachus's assertion, the suitors and their night).

After each session: write one Greek line from the day's passage, gloss it word by word, and note which translator renders it best — and why.

## The Greek Opening

```
ἄνδρα μοι ἔννεπε, μοῦσα, πολύτροπον, ὃς μάλα πολλὰ
andra  moi  ennepe   mousa  polutropon  hos  mala  polla
```

| word | translit | gloss |
|------|----------|-------|
| ἄνδρα | andra | **a man** — accusative (the object: "tell me of a man"; note: NOT his name first) |
| μοι | moi | **to me** — dative of interest ("for me," "on my behalf") |
| ἔννεπε | ennepe | **tell, recount** — aorist imperative (command to the Muse) |
| μοῦσα | mousa | **Muse** — vocative |
| πολύτροπον | polutropon | **of many turns / much-traveled / resourceful** — accusative, modifying ἄνδρα; the poem's key epithet |
| ὃς μάλα πολλὰ | hos mala polla | **who very many things** — relative clause beginning; "who suffered many things indeed" |

Compare immediately with the Iliad's opening: μῆνιν ἄειδε — 'wrath, sing.' The Iliad's first word is an abstraction (wrath); the Odyssey's first word is a person (man). The difference is the poem.

## Translations to Compare

The corpus holds four translations alongside the Greek original:

- **Butler (1900):** "Tell me, O Muse, of that ingenious hero who travelled far and wide." Butler is readable, conversational, and slightly domesticating — good for narrative momentum. His 'ingenious' for *polutropon* is interesting but thin.

- **Lang (1879) / Butcher & Lang:** "Tell me, Muse, of that man, so ready at need." More archaic in diction, closer to the formulaic repetition of the original. 'Ready at need' is a good rendering of *polutropon*'s practical dimension.

- **Pope (1726):** Pope's Odyssey is in heroic couplets and is frankly a poem about Pope's England as much as Homer's Greece. Read it for cadence and wit; do not use it as a gloss. His Telemachus is a young Augustan gentleman.

- **Cowper (1791):** Blank verse, more literal than Pope, more measured than Butler. Useful as a control translation — when Butler domesticates and Lang archaizes, Cowper usually gives you the middle register.

**Recommended exercise:** Look up *polutropon* in all four. Note what each translator chose — 'ingenious,' 'ready at need,' 'much-experienced,' 'of many arts.' This single word is a small seminar in translation theory. The word does not mean only one thing; the poem is about all of them.
