# Reading Orientation: The Divine Comedy

**Main question: What is ordered ascent?**

Ordered ascent is the Divine Comedy's subject the way wrath is the Iliad's subject — it names the force, not the plot. The Comedy does not ask whether souls are saved or damned; it asks what the structure of ascent *toward* right order looks like when the pilgrim begins in disorder. And it insists on a sequence: you cannot ascend before you understand, intimately, what is below. Inferno is not a prelude to the real poem. It is the first and necessary movement. The pilgrim who arrives at Paradiso's final circle has earned the sight because he has looked at everything beneath it.

---

## Structure

The Divine Comedy is 100 cantos of *terza rima* (interlocking three-line stanzas, ABA BCB CDC…), divided into three cantiche:

**Inferno (Cantos I–XXXIV):** Descent through nine concentric circles of Hell, from the dark wood of Canto I to the frozen center where Satan is locked in ice. Each circle corresponds to a category of sin, and each encounter is a portrait of a soul fixed in the disposition that condemned it. The sinners of Inferno are not simply being punished; they *are* what they chose, permanently and without remainder. Canto I is technically the prologue — the journey proper begins in Canto II when Dante and Virgil enter the gate — but it is the whole poem's premise.

**Purgatorio (Cantos I–XXXIII):** Ascent of a mountain island in the southern hemisphere, its terraces corresponding to the capital sins (pride, envy, wrath, sloth, avarice, gluttony, lust). The souls of Purgatory are moving; they are changing; they are learning. Purgatorio is the canticle of time and penitence and hope, and it contains some of Dante's most intimate encounters (Casella's song, Statius's theology of embryology, the reunion with Beatrice at the summit, which is also the final disappearance of Virgil).

**Paradiso (Cantos I–XXXIII):** Ascent through the nine celestial spheres to the Empyrean, where the pilgrim sees — briefly, at the limit of language and memory — the unified form of the universe and the mystery of the Incarnation. Paradiso is Dante's most difficult book and his most ambitious: a canticle whose subject is a vision that cannot be transmitted, only approached by the accumulated pressure of simile, negative theology, and light.

## What to Watch in Inferno, Canto I

**The three temporal layers:** Dante is writing in exile (after 1302) about a journey that the poem's fiction dates to Holy Week 1300, "midway through" a life of 70 years = age 35. He is describing past crisis from a position of recovered orientation. This means Canto I's disorientation is retrospective; he knows what the dark wood was, and still cannot say precisely when he entered it.

**The three beasts and their escalating difficulty:** The leopard circles (compulsion; beatable in principle); the lion advances (pride; requires confrontation); the she-wolf drives backward (avarice as structural social disease; not beatable by the individual, requires the Veltro prophecy). The beasts are not simply obstacles — they are a taxonomy of spiritual blockage, organized from most to least tractable.

**Virgil as what reason can and cannot do:** Virgil is sent by Beatrice is sent by Lucia is sent by the Virgin Mary — reason appears at the pilgrim's lowest point because divine grace arranged it to appear there. But Virgil also cannot take Dante all the way: he will guide through Hell and most of Purgatory, then hand the pilgrim to Beatrice (theology/grace) for the ascent into Paradise. Track this limit from his first appearance — it is the poem's argument about the relationship between classical reason and Christian revelation.

**The turn at the canto's end:** Dante does not enter Hell in Canto I. He turns. He follows Virgil not *into* Hell but in a direction that will eventually lead there. This moment of following — voluntary, without full knowledge of the destination, trusting the guide — is the poem's first and founding act of ordered movement. Every subsequent step of the journey is a repetition of this structure.

## Daily Portion

Inferno Canto I runs approximately 136 lines — a complete daily unit. Suggested approach:

- **Day 1 — Lines 1–27:** The dark wood and the failed ascent (the *selva*, the *colle*, the sunlit morning). Copy the opening terzina by hand. Find the exact line where Dante first says he "cannot well say" how he entered the wood — this is the structural heart of the opening.
- **Day 2 — Lines 28–90:** The three beasts (leopard, lion, she-wolf) and Dante's descent. Note the escalation: leopard delays, lion checks, she-wolf drives back. The order matters. Track the disposition of each beast in your ledger.
- **Day 3 — Lines 91–136:** Virgil appears, introduces himself, explains that he was sent by Beatrice, and the first step of the journey is taken. Read Virgil's self-description twice — once for what he says about his work, and once for what he says about his limitation.

After each session: copy two or three lines of the Italian by hand, look up at least one word's etymology (Dante's Italian is 13th-century Tuscan, often transparent to Latin), and compare how two translators render a single image. The divergences are the interpretation.

## The Italian Opening

```
Nel mezzo del cammin di nostra vita
mi ritrovai per una selva oscura,
ché la diritta via era smarrita.
```

| phrase | literal | note |
|--------|---------|------|
| *Nel mezzo del cammin* | In the middle of the road/journey | *cammin* = way, journey; also road. The phrase is precise: middle of life's road. |
| *di nostra vita* | of our life | Not "my" — "our." Dante universalizes the moment immediately. |
| *mi ritrovai* | I found myself again | *Ritrovai* is reflexive: "re-found myself." Implies prior loss. |
| *per una selva oscura* | through/in a dark wood | *Per* is ambiguous: in, through, by means of. He is already inside it. |
| *ché la diritta via era smarrita* | because the straight way had been abandoned | *Era smarrita*: passive, past perfect — it had already been lost, agent unspecified. |

The opening terzina contains the whole poem's logic in miniature: discovered in the middle, inside something dark, because the straight way had already (passively, agent-unknown, before the poem began) been lost.

## Translations to Compare

The source rail is the Digital Dante at Columbia University (https://digitaldante.columbia.edu/), which provides the Italian alongside multiple English translations with commentary. For daily reading, compare three registers:

- **Allen Mandelbaum (1980):** "When I had journeyed half of our life's way, / I found myself within a shadowed forest, / for I had lost the path that does not stray." Mandelbaum keeps the *nostra* — "our life's way" — and renders *diritta via* as "the path that does not stray." His terza rima approximation is close to the original's music without forcing English into Italian rhyme schemes.

- **Robert Hollander and Jean Hollander (2000):** "Midway in the journey of our life / I came to myself in a dark wood, / for the straight way was lost." The Hollanders are the most literally accurate of modern translators; their edition is accompanied by the most rigorous modern commentary in English. Use them when you want to know what the word says; they do not always tell you what the line sounds like.

- **Seamus Heaney's partial translations / C.H. Sisson (1980) for the whole:** Sisson is undervalued — he renders Dante in a plain, grave English prose-verse that has something of the original's refusal to ornament the catastrophic. Compare Sisson's opening to Mandelbaum's: where Mandelbaum makes music, Sisson makes weight.

**Recommended first exercise:** Look up all four renderings of *la diritta via*: "the straight way," "the right road," "the path that does not stray," "the true path." Each translator's choice is an interpretation of the poem's central question. *Diritta* means straight, right, and correct simultaneously; no English word carries all three. The exercise names the problem the whole poem is solving.
