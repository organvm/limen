# Reading Orientation: The Epic of Gilgamesh

## The Main Question

**What is mortality, and what is the friendship that defies it?**

This is not a rhetorical question. Gilgamesh discovers mortality from the outside — through the death of a friend — before he experiences it himself. The epic's engine is not battle or divine anger but *recognition*: the moment a king who thought he was two-thirds divine understands that the one-third mortal part will kill him just as dead. Everything before that recognition is the world before the question. Everything after is the search for an answer that the poem knows cannot be found, and pursues anyway.

---

## Structure: 12 Tablets

The Standard Babylonian Version (assembled c. 1200 BCE, the Nineveh library tablets) runs 12 tablets. The first two tablets establish the world; the crisis is Enkidu's death at the center (Tablets VII–VIII); the quest for immortality and the return fill the final tablets. The structure is not linear in the modern sense — it is concentrically organized around absence.

| Tablet | Contents |
|--:|--------|
| 1 | Gilgamesh's tyranny; Enkidu created and civilized |
| 2 | Enkidu comes to Uruk; the wrestling-match; friendship |
| 3–5 | Journey to the Cedar Forest; Humbaba |
| 6 | The Bull of Heaven; Ishtar's curse |
| 7–8 | Enkidu's dream; his death; Gilgamesh's lament |
| 9–10 | Gilgamesh's wandering; Scorpion-men; Siduri |
| 11 | Utnapishtim; the Flood; the plant of life |
| 12 | (Late addition, Sumerian) Enkidu in the underworld |

## What to Watch for in the Opening (Tablet I)

**The opening hymn is already a vaunt.** "He who saw the deep" (*sha nagba imuru*) — the first line announces Gilgamesh as complete, retrospectively. You are reading a monument before you read a story. Notice this posture: it is a civilization's way of saying *this happened, and it mattered, and we built stone to prove it.*

**The gods create Enkidu to solve a political problem, not a moral one.** The Urukians are not oppressed in the sense of being wronged — they are *overwhelmed* by royal energy that has no counterpart. The divine solution is not justice but *balance*. This is Mesopotamian theology: the gods engineer equilibria, they do not punish.

**Shamhat is not a seduction scene — it is a creation scene.** The seven days with Enkidu are the moment the epic identifies desire as the mechanism of civilization. When Enkidu returns to the animals and they flee, he has not been degraded — he has been *made*. Watch how the tablets treat this without irony or shame. It is the poem's most counterintuitive argument: that what opens us to culture is desire, not reason.

**The dreams of Gilgamesh at the end of Tablet I are the thesis of the whole poem.** A meteorite he loves as a wife. An axe he carries to his mother. Both are Enkidu. The poem tells you what will happen before it tells you how — and what will happen is love, and what love prepares for is grief.

## How to Portion It Daily

Gilgamesh is short by epic standards — roughly 3,000 lines survive of a probable 3,600. Read one tablet per sitting, or, for Tablets with dense narrative (VII–VIII, XI), half a tablet with time to sit with it. Recommended daily unit: **one column** (approximately 50 lines), read twice — once for narrative, once for the specific words and images that recur (the walls of Uruk, the cedar forest, the flower of immortality, the ferryman Urshanabi, the waters of death).

**Do not skip Tablet XI** — the Flood narrative embedded there is a direct ancestor of the Biblical flood, and reading it in context changes both stories permanently.

## Translations to Compare

**In the corpus (local):**
- Jastrow & Clay (1920) — `corpus/classical/gilgamesh/english_jastrow_1920.txt` — an Old Babylonian version (partial), scholarly and literal; invaluable for seeing the seams where tablets differ.

**Beyond the corpus (source rail: https://www.sacred-texts.com/ane/eog/):**
- **Andrew George** (Penguin Classics, 2003) — the current scholarly gold standard. Reads the Nineveh tablets plus the earlier Old Babylonian fragments in parallel columns; his introduction on the manuscript history is essential.
- **Stephen Mitchell** (2004) — loose, literary, beautiful; occasionally improvises where tablets are broken. Best for reading aloud. Compare against George for any passage that seems too smooth.
- **N.K. Sandars** (Penguin, 1960) — the prose version that shaped most twentieth-century encounters with the poem. Readable, but compresses and homogenizes; use it as a gateway, not an authority.

**The comparison exercise for Tablet I:** find the line in each translation that describes Shamhat's action and Enkidu's transformation. Note what each translator does with *harimtu* (often rendered "harlot," "prostitute," "hierodule," "sacred woman"). The word's range is the poem's range: sacred sexuality, civilizing force, social boundary. What a translator does with it tells you what they think the poem is about.

**On the cuneiform:** the script guide is at `studium/scripts/cuneiform.md` (forthcoming). The first line — *sha nagba imuru* — is worth tracing even without Akkadian: the tablets are clay, the signs are wedge-shaped (cuneus), and holding that fact in mind changes the texture of every line. This poem was written *into* the material of the earth, by a stylus, in a river-city, four thousand years ago. The walls of Uruk are not metaphor.
