# Reading Orientation: The Tale of the Heike (平家物語)

**Main question: What is impermanence in the fall of the proud?**

Impermanence — *mujo* (無常) — is not the Heike's theme in the way that a moral might be a theme. It is the work's epistemology: the mode of attention the work demands. The bells of Gion Shoja do not toll as a warning; they toll as a description. The Taira are not punished for pride as a lesson; they fall because the nature of flourishing things is to fall, exactly as flowers fall. The Heike is not a tragedy in the Aristotelian sense (hubris → nemesis → recognition) — though Kiyomori's overreach has that shape. It is something more Buddhist and more strange: a sustained meditation on the fact that greatness and impermanence are not opposed forces but the same force seen from two angles. To be great is to be fully visible; to be fully visible is to be fully subject to the law that everything visible passes. The question the work asks is not "why did the Taira fall?" The question is: "what does it feel like to live inside that law without knowing you are inside it — and what does it feel like to know?"

## Structure

The Heike Monogatari is 12 books, composed and recited by *biwa hoshi* (blind lute priests) over centuries, reaching something like its received form in the early 14th century. The work is oral literature that has been written down, and it carries that origin in its texture: episodic, formulaic, full of set-piece scenes that recur like musical themes, and organized not by strict chronological plotting but by emotional arc.

**Books 1–3 — Rise and Overreach:** The Taira at their height. Kiyomori's ascent; the clan's splendor; the first signs of overreach (the Gio episode, the arbitrary cruelties, the silencing of dissent). The retired Emperor Go-Shirakawa and the Taira begin their long antagonism. The world feels full, grand, and already slightly wrong.

**Books 4–6 — The Breaking:** The clan's authority challenged. The exile of Mongaku, the conspiracy of monks, the flight from the capital. Kiyomori's death (Book 6) — burning alive with fever, asking for cold water on his body, and dying at the moment of his greatest earthly power. This is the book's structural hinge: the man who built the clan does not live to see it destroyed.

**Books 7–9 — War:** The Genpei War proper begins. The Taira armies are repeatedly outmaneuvered by Minamoto commanders, especially Yoshinaka and the Yoshitsune. The military episodes include the battle of Ichinotani (Book 9), where the Taira are driven into the sea from the cliffs at Ichi-no-Tani — the scene of Atsumori's death, the work's most famous single episode.

**Books 10–12 — Dissolution:** Yashima, then Dan-no-ura (Book 11): the final sea battle, the drowning of the child Emperor Antoku, the Taira clan's elimination. Book 12 is not battle but aftermath — survivors in hiding, Kenreimon'in (the Emperor's mother, Kiyomori's daughter) in a mountain nunnery at Jakko-in, the retired Emperor visiting her. The work ends in stillness.

## What to Watch in Book 1

Book 1 is the argument for the Taira's greatness — genuinely, not ironically. Read it as the text demanding that you understand what is being lost before it is lost.

**The opening sentence:** Copy it by hand in Japanese if you can manage the script; if not, hold it in translation. "The sound of the Gion Shoja bells echoes the impermanence of all things; the color of the sala flowers reveals the truth that the prosperous must decline." This is not poetry decorating the opening — it is the work's thesis, stated as fact, before the narrative begins. Everything that follows happens inside this sentence.

**The genealogical passages:** The Taira lineage is traced with care. This is not pedantry; it is establishing the terms of the fall. The clan is not low-born reaching for what it was never owed — it is legitimate, nine generations royal, and its ascent is a restoration as much as an ambition. The fall will be of something that had a right to exist.

**Tadamori and the wooden sword:** The episode of Kiyomori's father navigating a court assassination plot through wit and restraint is the work's first lesson in what distinguishes samurai excellence from mere force. Tadamori does not fight; he outmaneuvers. Watch for this throughout: the Heike is interested in the difference between power that reads its situation and power that cannot.

**The Gio episode:** Do not skim this. It is Book 1's emotional center and its most precise image of what Kiyomori's power actually costs. Gio the shirabyoshi dancer is not a villain's victim in a melodramatic sense — she is simply used, then discarded, then humiliated when she is asked to return and entertain her replacement. Her departure into Buddhist vows at twenty-one, followed eventually by Hotoke (her replacement) doing the same, is the work's first statement of its spiritual counter-motion: the world of power passes; those who are crushed by it turn toward what does not pass.

**The force of *mujo*:** Every episode in Book 1 is lit, from behind, by the opening sentence. When Kiyomori reads the luckfish as an omen of his own greatness, that is correct and also insufficient. When the text lists the grand ministers of the past who fell — Zhao Gao, Wang Mang — it is not making a political argument; it is annotating the law. The reader who hears the bells on every page will read differently from the reader who encounters them only in the opening.

## Daily Portion

Book 1 is one of the longer books in the received text. A productive daily unit:

- **Day 1:** The Gion Shoja opening through the genealogical survey (the "Giōn Shōja" and "Denjō Yamiuchi" chapters). Read slowly. Copy the opening sentence in whichever script you have.
- **Day 2:** Tadamori's rise and the assassination-attempt episode. Note the logic of the wooden sword — what it says about the relation between appearance and power.
- **Day 3:** Kiyomori's own rise — the Hogen and Heiji uprisings through his appointment as Grand Minister. Track the speed.
- **Day 4:** The "Kaburo" (the shaved-head informers) and the Tokitada speech. Feel the temperature change. This is the first moment where the text's admiration tips into unease.
- **Day 5:** The Gio episode entire — her initial favor, her expulsion, her return and humiliation, her retreat to Sagano, and Hotoke's eventual arrival to join her.

After each session, write one sentence in your ledger: what did impermanence look like in today's passage?

## Translations to Compare

The corpus holds the Japanese original (University of Virginia text, Hobunkan 1933 edition) and the source rail at `https://jti.lib.virginia.edu/japanese/heike/`.

For comparison reading, the two essential modern English translations are:

- **Helen Craig McCullough (Stanford, 1988):** The standard scholarly translation — accurate, readable, with excellent notes on historical context and court culture. The diction is clear without being flat. Her prose catches the episodic, recitative quality of the original better than most. Begin here.

- **Royall Tyler (Penguin Classics, 2012):** More literary, more attuned to the work's emotional registers, with a willingness to let the formulaic repetitions build the way they do in the original. Tyler's Gio episode has a particular intimacy. Read Tyler alongside McCullough when the scenes are emotionally dense.

The Japanese original repays attention even without reading knowledge. The opening sentence in Classical Japanese — 「祇園精舎の鐘の聲、諸行無常の響あり」(*Gion Shōja no kane no koe, shogyō mujō no hibiki ari*) — is one of the most famous sentences in the language. Its sound is not separate from its meaning: *shogyō mujō* (all conditioned things are impermanent) moves in the mouth like a bell's resonance. If you can hear it recited to biwa accompaniment, do so before reading — the work was made to be sung.
