# Reading Orientation: Beowulf

**Main question: What is heroic life under doom?**

This is the poem's irreducible question, and it is set up in Fitt I before Beowulf has arrived. The Danes built Heorot — the greatest hall ever made — and a creature came who could not be stopped. Twelve years passed. The poem does not rush past this. It dwells on the duration, the failed counsel, the emptied benches, to establish that the question is real: given that great men build great things and great things are destroyed, what does it mean to be a hero? The poem's answer is not triumphant. Beowulf defeats Grendel. He defeats Grendel's mother. He rules his people for fifty years. Then a dragon comes, and he dies. The poem knows how it ends when it begins. So does the hero. The question is what you do with that knowledge.

---

## Structure: 43 Fitts

Beowulf is 3,182 lines of Old English alliterative verse, divided by the manuscript into 43 *fitts* (numbered sections) of irregular length. The poem falls into two large movements separated by about fifty years:

**Movement I (Fitts 1–25): The Young Warrior — Denmark**
Fitts 1–2 establish the world (Scyld Scefing's glory, Hrothgar's hall, Grendel's reign). Fitts 3–12 follow Beowulf's voyage to Denmark, his boasts in Heorot, the night fight with Grendel. Fitts 13–18 cover the aftermath — celebration, then Grendel's mother's revenge and the underwater battle. Fitts 19–25 complete the Danish episode: rewards, counsel, Beowulf's return to Geatland.

**Movement II (Fitts 26–43): The Old King — Geatland**
Fitts 26–31 open fifty years later: Beowulf is king, a dragon has been roused, the hoard has been disturbed. The rest of the poem follows the final fight. Beowulf wins and dies. His thanes burn him on a barrow above the sea. His people lament that enemies will now come.

| Fitts | Contents |
|------:|---------|
| 1–2 | Scyld Scefing; Heorot built; Grendel |
| 3–9 | Beowulf sails; boasts; the night in Heorot |
| 10–12 | Grendel's arm; celebration; Finn digression |
| 13–16 | Grendel's mother attacks; the underwater lair |
| 17–18 | Hrothgar's speech (the 'sermon'); Beowulf rewarded |
| 19–25 | Return to Geatland; report to Hygelac; reign begins |
| 26–33 | The dragon; the hoard disturbed; last counsel |
| 34–42 | The fight; Wiglaf; Beowulf's death |
| 43 | The funeral; the lament; the poem closes |

## What to Watch in Fitt I

**The opening word — 'Hwæt!' — is a performance.** This is not a literary text in the modern sense; it is a hall-performance transcribed onto vellum. 'Hwæt' (roughly: 'Listen!' or 'Lo!' or 'Attend!') is the speaker demanding the room's attention. Keep this in mind throughout: the poem was originally heard, not read, and its rhetorical patterns — repetition, variation, apposition — are sound-patterns designed to carry across a hall.

**The poem begins with an ancestor who is not Beowulf.** Scyld Scefing died generations before the poem's main action. His funeral ship, sent out to an unknown destination, establishes the poem's governing image: greatness that came from the sea returns to the sea. Beowulf's barrow, at the end, is built above the sea. The poem is circular in the way fate is circular.

**Heorot's golden moment is also its shadow.** The hall is built in Fitt I, and immediately two shadows fall on it: the poet notes that Heorot will one day be consumed in a feud-fire (an allusion to Hrothgar's family's civil war, history the audience knew); and Grendel, hearing the creation-song from outside, begins his attacks. Do not read the building of Heorot as triumph. Read it as the establishment of a target.

**The creation-song inside the hall is placed against Grendel's genealogy on purpose.** The scop sings that God made the world — light, land, creatures. The next lines introduce Grendel as the descendant of Cain, condemned by God, enemy of light, exiled from human community. The structural irony is the poem's thesis in miniature: creation and the outcast it produces are simultaneous; the hall that celebrates God's creation is surrounded by the evidence of God's judgment.

**Twelve years is not a detail.** The poem insists on the duration of Grendel's reign. This is not a monster-of-the-week problem; it is a civilisational failure spanning over a decade, one that the greatest king of the age cannot solve. When Beowulf arrives in Fitt 3 and boasts he will deal with Grendel, part of the poem's effect comes from how long the Danes waited. Hold that duration in mind.

## How to Portion It Daily

Beowulf is compact — 3,182 lines — but dense. Read it slowly. Recommended unit: one fitt per sitting, with time afterward to sit with any passage that generated a response.

**Day 1:** Fitt I (lines 1–52 in most editions). Read for narrative; note every image of sea, darkness, and fire. Then find line 1 in Old English: *Hwæt! We Gardena   in geardagum* and copy it out (see below on the script).

**Day 2:** Fitt II (lines 53–114). Grendel in full. Note the compound epithets (*grundwyrgenne* — 'the fiend of the deep'; *mearcstapan* — 'wanderer of the waste-lands'). The Old English compounds are one of the poem's primary pleasures.

**Day 3–4:** Fitts III–IV (lines 115–209). The news reaches Beowulf; the voyage. Read against the Odyssey's sea-passages: the sea in Beowulf is a 'whale-road' (*hwælweg*), a 'gannet's bath' (*ganotes baed*). It is both obstacle and kinship; Beowulf comes from the sea to save a people who came from the sea.

Continue at one to two fitts per sitting through Movement I; slow down for Hrothgar's speech (Fitts 17–18), which is the poem's most explicit philosophical statement. Take Movement II (the dragon, Beowulf's death, the funeral) across several days and read the closing lament twice.

## The Old English Opening

The first manuscript line:

```
Hwæt!  We  Gardena   in  geardagum
Hwæt   we  Gar-dena  in  gear-dagum
```

| word | pronunciation | gloss |
|------|---------------|-------|
| Hwæt | hwaet (rhymes with 'flat') | **Listen! Lo! Attend!** — hall-cry, performative opener |
| We | weh | **we** — nominative plural |
| Gardena | gar-DEH-na | **of the Spear-Danes** — genitive plural (*gar* = spear) |
| in | in | **in** |
| geardagum | YAIR-da-gum | **in days of yore / in former days** — dative plural (*gear* = year/age, *dag* = day) |

The second half-line continues: *þeodcyninga* (of the people-kings), *þrym gefrunon* (glories we have heard of). The full sentence runs to line 3: *We have heard of the glory of the Spear-Danes' kings in days of old.*

Old English alliterative verse works on stress, not syllable-count. Each line has two half-lines separated by a caesura; the alliteration falls on the stressed syllables. In this line: Gar-/gear- is the alliterating pair (both /g/). Read the Old English aloud, even haltingly — the alliteration is audible, and the poem changes when you hear it.

**Compound-kenning to learn on sight:** *hronrad* — 'whale-road' = the sea. *beadoleoma* — 'battle-light' = a sword. *goldwine* — 'gold-friend' = a lord who gives treasure. These compounds are the poem's distinctive music; collecting them is one of the joys of reading Beowulf in Old English.

## Translations to Compare

**In the corpus (local):** `medieval/beowulf/old_english_original.txt` — the primary source; all translation decisions should be checked here.

**Source rail:** https://www.sacred-texts.com/neu/ascp/a04_01.htm

**Beyond the corpus — four to compare:**

- **Seamus Heaney (2000, Norton/Faber)** — the standard modern literary translation; Heaney renders 'Hwæt' as 'So,' making it conversational. Brilliant at Anglo-Saxon tone, occasionally softens the poem's bleaker edges. Read for cadence.

- **Michael Alexander (1973, Penguin Classics)** — closer to the alliterative form of the original; sacrifices some readability for authenticity. Best for checking sound-pattern decisions.

- **R.M. Liuzza (2000, Broadview)** — scholarly, literal, annotated, excellent for line-by-line comparison. Heaney's 'So' becomes Liuzza's 'Listen!' — compare the two opening words as a first exercise.

- **Tolkien (completed 1926, published 2014, HarperCollins)** — Tolkien's prose translation is scholarly and strange, rendered in an archaizing prose that sounds like early Genesis. Tolkien was the century's foremost Beowulf scholar; his commentary (in *Beowulf: A Translation and Commentary*) remains essential. His 1936 lecture 'Beowulf: The Monsters and the Critics' is the single most important piece of secondary reading.

**The comparison exercise for Fitt I:** take the passage describing Grendel's exclusion from Heorot (the lines following the creation-song). Find it in Heaney, Liuzza, and Tolkien. Note what each translator does with the theological dimension of Grendel's exile — is he a monster, an outcast, a condemned being, a theological category? The answer tells you what each translator thinks the poem is *for*.
