# Reading Orientation: Bhagavad Gita

## The Main Question

**What is right action in the face of duty and death?**

This is not an abstract philosophical question for the Gita. It arises at a specific moment, in a specific body, on a specific field: Arjuna, one of the greatest warriors alive, standing between two armies, looking at his relatives, and discovering that he cannot move. The entire 18-chapter dialogue that follows is Krishna's answer to the paralysis of one man. If you read the Gita and forget this — forget that it is an answer to a living person's inability to act — you will miss what makes it alive.

---

## Structure

The Gita is 18 chapters (adhyayas), 700 verses total, organized as a dialogue between Arjuna and Krishna embedded within the Mahabharata's Bhishma Parva. The narrative frame: the blind king Dhritarashtra asks his seer Sanjaya to narrate what happened on the field of Kurukshetra. Sanjaya, who has been granted divine sight, tells him. What Sanjaya tells him is the Gita.

The 18 chapters move through a progressive architecture:

- **Chapters 1–2:** Crisis and first doctrine. Arjuna collapses; Krishna begins teaching with the doctrine of the indestructible self (Atman) and the nature of action.
- **Chapters 3–6:** The three paths (Karma Yoga — action; Jnana Yoga — knowledge; Dhyana Yoga — meditation). How to act in the world without being destroyed by the fruits of action.
- **Chapters 7–12:** The nature of Krishna himself. Who he is, how to know him, what it means to worship, culminating in Chapter 11 — the Vishvarupa, the terrifying universal form that is simultaneously the answer to every question and the vision no human can long survive.
- **Chapters 13–18:** The higher doctrine. Matter and spirit, the three gunas (qualities), the different kinds of knowledge and action and renunciation, and the final instruction: abandon all dharmas, surrender to Krishna alone.

## What to Watch for in the Opening (Chapter 1)

Chapter 1 is the only chapter in the Gita that is almost entirely narrative rather than philosophical. Read it slowly.

**The catalog of warriors (verses 4–19):** Duryodhana names heroes; then both armies blow their conchs. This is not filler — it is the setting of the trap. Every name spoken here is someone who will die in the war. The poem knows this. You are being asked to learn these names so that you understand what Arjuna sees when he looks across the field.

**The threshold crossing (verse 21–23):** Arjuna asks Krishna to drive the chariot between the two armies. This request, so simple in its words, is the pivot of the entire poem. The chariot moving into the space between the hosts is the moment before knowing — the last moment in which Arjuna is still a warrior who has not yet looked.

**The body's failure (verses 28–30):** Arjuna's physical symptoms are listed with clinical precision. Do not read past them. The Gandiva — his divine bow, one of the most powerful weapons in the epic — slips from his fingers. This is not metaphor. His body is refusing its trained purpose. The poem is precise about this because the poem knows that the deepest crises arrive first in the body, before the mind has found their names.

**The philosophical argument (verses 31–46):** Arjuna does not simply weep. He argues. He constructs a cascading chain of consequences: kill kinsmen → destroy family rites → spread impiety → corrupt women → mix castes → send ancestors to hell. The argument is not wrong, exactly — it follows from premises that most of his culture would accept. What Krishna will do in Chapter 2 is not refute the argument but dissolve the premises beneath it.

**The final image:** Arjuna casts his bow down and sits. The chapter ends here. No answer yet. Just a man sitting in a chariot between two armies that are waiting to kill each other. The silence between Chapter 1 and Chapter 2 is the structural center of the poem.

## How to Portion It Daily

The Gita's 700 verses across 18 chapters average roughly 39 verses per chapter, but the chapters vary considerably in length and density. A working approach:

- **Week 1:** Chapters 1–2 (the crisis and first doctrine). Read slowly, particularly Chapter 1. Read Chapter 2 twice — it covers enormous ground and introduces most of the poem's key vocabulary.
- **Weeks 2–3:** Chapters 3–6 (the three primary yogas). These chapters are denser; one chapter per two days is not excessive.
- **Week 4:** Chapters 7–10 (Krishna's self-disclosure, building toward the revelation).
- **Week 5:** Chapter 11 alone deserves a full day, then Chapters 12–14.
- **Week 6:** Chapters 15–18. Chapter 18 is the longest (78 verses) and recapitulates the entire poem — read it slowly.

The Sanskrit original divides the 18 chapters into three groups of six — a structure the commentators call sat (six), sat, sat — corresponding roughly to the three paths and their synthesis. Keep this in mind as you read.

## Which Translations to Compare

Three translations are in the corpus, each with a distinct character:

**Kashinath Trimbak Telang (1882) — Sacred Books of the East Vol. 8** (`english_telang_1882.txt`):
Prose translation, scholarly, literal, with extensive footnotes. Best for understanding what the Sanskrit actually says and for following the commentators (Sankara, Sridhara, Madhusudana). Telang's introduction is one of the best short historical essays on the text. Read this as your primary reference when you want to know what a verse means without interpretive overlay.

**Edwin Arnold (1885) — The Song Celestial** (`english_arnold_1885.txt`):
Blank verse, Victorian, aimed at literary effect. Arnold called it "The Song Celestial" and pitched it at English readers who had read Tennyson and Keats. The verse sometimes softens the strangeness of the original; but for passages where you want the poem to sound like a poem — particularly Chapter 11's Vishvarupa — Arnold's controlled blank verse earns its place. Compare Arnold and Telang on Chapter 2's opening verses on the immortal self: Telang gives you the argument; Arnold gives you the music.

**Annie Besant (1895)** (`english_besant_1895.txt`):
Theosophical in orientation, often closer in vocabulary to the Sanskrit technical terms than either Telang or Arnold. Besant preserves the Sanskrit where translation would flatten meaning — she leaves "yoga," "dharma," "karma" untranslated rather than forcing them into English equivalents that inevitably mislead. Useful as a check on the passages where Telang and Arnold diverge most sharply.

**Comparison drill:** Take the opening of Chapter 2 — Krishna's first full speech beginning "How comes it that this delusion..." — and read all three translations in sequence. You will see the problem of translation immediately. Telang's "delusion" versus Arnold's rendering versus Besant's choice of terms opens the entire question of what the Gita is actually saying about *moha* (confusion, delusion, error). That comparison is worth more than a page of commentary.

## Source Rail

The Sanskrit original follows the GRETIL text (Göttingen Register of Electronic Texts in Indian Languages): https://gretil.sub.uni-goettingen.de/gretil.html#BhG — the corpus file `sanskrit_original.txt` contains the Devanagari-transliterated text with Sankara's commentary. The corpus path is `sanskrit/bhagavad-gita/`.

For the Sanskrit: the poem is composed almost entirely in the *anushtubh* meter (eight syllables per quarter-verse, four quarter-verses per verse), the same meter as most of the Mahabharata and the Ramayana. Once you internalize the rhythm — *da da da da / da da da da* — you can hear the verse even through transliteration. Chapter 2's most famous line, *avinashi tu tad viddhi* ("know that to be indestructible"), is worth learning by sound before you read it by sense.
