# Reading Orientation: The Canterbury Tales

**Main question: What is social speech?**

Social speech is the Canterbury Tales' subject in the way wrath is the Iliad's subject — it names the force, not the plot. The poem is not, finally, about pilgrimage or medieval England or the estate system, though it contains all of these. It is about what happens when people talk to each other: the way speech performs social identity, the way a story reveals its teller, the way language is always doing more than it says. Every tale in the Canterbury Tales is a social act before it is a literary one. The Knight tells a tale about honor; the Miller immediately tells a tale about a cuckolded carpenter; the Wife of Bath delivers a five-hundred-line prologue before she even begins her tale. The form is the argument: what people say tells you who they are, and who they are shapes what they say, and neither of these equations is simple.

---

## Structure

The Canterbury Tales is unfinished — Chaucer planned 120 tales (each pilgrim telling four: two going, two returning) and completed somewhere between 22 and 24, depending on which fragments you count. What survives is organized into ten fragments of varying completeness.

**The General Prologue (Fragment I):** The frame is established: spring, pilgrimage, the Tabard Inn in Southwark, twenty-nine pilgrims described one by one, the Host's proposal of a storytelling contest. The Prologue is itself a masterpiece — a gallery of social types each rendered with a precision that makes the type feel individual. It is approximately 858 lines.

**Fragment I (continued):** The Knight's Tale (chivalric romance, Theseus and the cousins Palamon and Arcite), the Miller's Tale (fabliaux, direct subversion of the Knight), and the Reeve's Tale (retaliatory fabliaux). The sequence is deliberate: each tale answers the one before it. Reading Fragment I as a dialogue rather than a sequence reveals the poem's deepest structure.

**Fragments II–X:** The Man of Law's Tale, the Marriage Group (Wife of Bath, Friar, Summoner, Clerk, Merchant, Franklin — tales about marriage and sovereignty, one of the great thematic sequences in medieval literature), the Pardoner's Tale and Prologue (the poem's most chilling performance of self-exposure), the Nun's Priest's Tale (mock epic), and the Parson's long sermon on penitence, which closes the poem in prose.

## What to Watch in the General Prologue

The General Prologue is not a list of characters. It is a theory of social identity disguised as a list.

**The opening (lines 1–18):** Before any person appears, Chaucer gives you a season — and a causal argument. 'Whan that Aprille with his shoures soote...' is structured as a logical proposition: when spring does these things to the earth, THEN people long to go on pilgrimages. The equation of cosmological spring with human motion is not casual. Chaucer is saying that the pilgrimage is as natural and as irresistible as the buds opening. He is also, quietly, framing the social world as subject to forces it doesn't fully control.

**The narrator's position:** Chaucer places himself inside the poem as 'I' — a guest at the inn who joins the company. This narrator is conspicuously unreliable in a specific way: he repeatedly praises things that are obviously satirical, assents to obviously corrupt reasoning, and presents dubious characters with warm enthusiasm. 'And I seyde his opinion was good' (of the Monk's rejection of his Rule) is the template. Never take the narrator's praise at face value; never assume his praise is only irony either.

**Type and individual:** Each portrait begins with a category (A KNIGHT ther was, A MONK ther was) and then immediately complicates it. The Knight is everything a knight should be — and his coat is stained from his armor, and he is meek as a maid. The Prioress is a nun — and she wears a brooch that says *Amor vincit omnia*. The portrait form is itself an argument: categories tell you what something is supposed to be; the details tell you what it actually is. The gap between them is where the comedy lives.

**The Wife of Bath is not in the Prologue proper** — she is listed but not yet described in depth (her portrait in the General Prologue is only a few stanzas). Her real self-portrait comes in the Prologue to her own tale (Fragment III). But she appears in the pilgrimage company from the start; note her when she appears.

**The Host (Harry Bailly):** The Host appears at the end of the Prologue as the architect of the storytelling game. He is loud, cheerful, and shrewd. His proposal — tales told for a prize supper, judged by him — makes him the poem's in-text authority. He is also, inevitably, a character subject to the same social dynamics as everyone else. Watch how he responds to the different tales: his reactions are themselves performances.

## Daily Portion

The General Prologue runs approximately 858 lines. A productive daily unit:

- **Day 1:** Lines 1–42 (the April opening through 'the hooly blisful martir for to seke'). Read the original Middle English aloud — the rhythm is iambic pentameter and comes clear within a few lines.
- **Day 2:** Lines 43–117 (the narrator joins the company; the Knight's portrait).
- **Day 3:** Lines 118–270 (Squire, Yeoman, Prioress, Monk).
- **Day 4:** Lines 271–528 (Friar, Merchant, Clerk, Sergeant of Law, Franklin, the Guildsmen, Cook).
- **Day 5:** Lines 529–714 (Shipman, Physician, Wife of Bath, Parson, Plowman, Miller, Manciple, Reeve, Summoner, Pardoner).
- **Day 6:** Lines 715–858 (the Host's proposal, the company's agreement, the narrator's disclaimer, the departure).

After each session: note which portrait surprised you, and identify one moment where the narrator's praise and the portrait's details contradict each other.

## The Middle English Opening

```
Whan that Aprill with his shoures soote
The droghte of March hath perced to the roote,
And bathed every veyne in swich licour
Of which vertu engendred is the flour;
```

| word | modern English | note |
|------|---------------|------|
| whan that | when | subordinating conjunction; the whole sentence is 'when X, then Y' |
| shoures soote | sweet showers | *soote* = sweet; the adjective follows the noun (common in Middle English) |
| droghte | drought | note the -e ending: Middle English final -e is often sounded, adding a syllable |
| perced | pierced | past tense; the drought is what April's showers have pierced |
| veyne | vein | of the earth, as in veins of moisture; the earth is anatomized |
| vertu | virtue, power | not moral virtue but natural power — the generative force of the liquid |
| engendred | engendered | produced, generated; sexual metaphor latent throughout the opening |
| flour | flower | the output of the whole cosmic process |

Read the opening aloud in Middle English before reading any translation. The meter is iambic pentameter, and Chaucer is strict about it — the final -e is usually sounded. 'droghte' = two syllables (drogh-te); 'soote' = two syllables (soo-te). Once the rhythm comes in, it carries you.

## Translations to Compare

The General Prologue has been translated frequently. The corpus path `medieval/canterbury-tales/middle_english.txt` holds a glossed Middle English text with marginal notes. The Harvard Chaucer source rail (https://chaucer.lib.harvard.edu/) provides the authoritative Middle English text with line numbers.

For modern-English reading alongside:

- **Nevill Coghill (Penguin, 1951):** The standard verse translation — rhyming couplets, readable, occasionally loses the irony in the effort to be accessible. Good for narrative momentum. His Prioress is slightly too sympathetic.

- **A. Kent Hieatt and Constance Hieatt (Bantam, 1964):** Prose translation, accurate, useful for checking meaning when the Middle English stalls you. Not a reading text; a gloss tool.

- **Burton Raffel (Modern Library, 2008):** Verse, looser than Coghill, more willing to modernize idiom. His Monk has the right energy.

**Recommended exercise:** Take the Monk's self-justification (lines 165–188 in the General Prologue) and read it in Middle English, then in two translations. Note what each translator does with 'And I seyde his opinion was good.' The phrase is the poem's comic signature; how a translator handles it tells you everything about how they read Chaucer.

The best long-term practice: read one portrait per session in Middle English first — aloud, tolerating uncertainty — then check your reading against the glossed corpus. The language is far more accessible than it appears. Within a week of daily contact it opens considerably.
