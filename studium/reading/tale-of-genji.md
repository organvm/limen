# Reading Orientation: The Tale of Genji

**Main question: Can a consciousness attended to with enough care outlast the conditions of its own existence?**

Not what Heian Japan looked like. Not whether Genji's behavior is defensible by any ethical standard. The novel's question is whether it is possible to record the precise texture of an inner life — its desires, its evasions, its aesthetics, its griefs — and to do so at a scale and with a discrimination fine enough that the record becomes a form of survival. Murasaki Shikibu is asking whether consciousness, attended to with sufficient care, can outlast the conditions of its own existence. Mono no aware — the pathos of things — is not a decorative theme. It is the novel's method: register everything that is passing, with feeling and without sentimentality, and see what remains.

---

## Structure: 54 Chapters

*The Tale of Genji* divides into three broad movements across its fifty-four chapters, though Murasaki Shikibu did not use those divisions herself. The structure is felt rather than announced.

**Part I — The Shining Years (Chapters 1–33):** The world of Genji's ascent. From his birth in Chapter 1 (Kiritsubo) through his great period of influence at court, his loves, his exile to Suma, his return, and his political and amorous dominance. These chapters are not uniformly triumphant — exile, grief, and loss are woven throughout — but the gravitational center is Genji's radiance and the world's organization around it.

**Part II — The Twilight (Chapters 34–41):** The chapters around Genji's decline and death. Chapter 41 (Maboroshi, "The Wizard") ends with Genji; Chapter 42 (Niou Miya) begins after his death, which is not narrated. The gap between Chapters 41 and 42 — the death that is not written — is one of the most analyzed moments in world literature. Genji simply disappears from the text, and the novel continues.

**Part III — Uji Chapters (Chapters 42–54):** The world after Genji, organized around his son (or possible son) Kaoru and his grandson Niou. The Uji chapters are darker, more psychologically complex, and arguably more modern than the Genji chapters — they show what happens to the world when the organizing radiance is gone. Many readers find them the novel's most interesting and most disturbing section.

## What to Watch for in Chapter 1 (Kiritsubo)

**The first sentence sets the novel's method.** Murasaki Shikibu opens in the middle of a situation already formed: the emperor favors a particular woman above all others, and this is already causing disruption. She does not begin at the beginning; she begins at the intensity. Everything that follows in the chapter is consequence: what desire, at that pitch, costs.

**The court's cruelty is structural, not personal.** The ladies who humiliate the Kiritsubo Lady on her way through the corridors are not individually malicious — they are defending a social order that the emperor's preference is distorting. The novel's political intelligence is visible here from the first pages: Murasaki Shikibu understands that social violence operates through structure, not through villains. The Kokiden Lady's faction is not evil; it is legitimate.

**Genji's beauty is the chapter's metamorphic fact.** When the Korean physiognomist reads the infant's face and describes his imperial destiny, and when Murasaki Shikibu's narration calls him 'the shining one,' she is establishing the novel's central conceit: Genji is his dead mother's beauty continued in a new form. Every romantic relationship in the novel revisits this origin — Genji will spend the rest of his life seeking variations on a loss he did not consciously experience.

**The death is narrated quietly.** Murasaki Shikibu does not dwell on the Kiritsubo Lady's death. She moves through it. The restraint is the point: the emotion lives in the negative space, in the emperor's subsequent mourning, in the palace's changed atmosphere. This is the first instance of the novel's characteristic emotional method — feeling registered in its absence and aftermath, not in its dramatic peak.

**Watch for the first occurrence of mono no aware.** The phrase itself does not appear, but the condition does: the perception that things are beautiful and passing simultaneously, that beauty and transience are the same perception. The Kiritsubo Lady is beautiful; she is passing; her son is beautiful; he will pass. The novel is already doing what it will do for fifty-four chapters.

## How to Portion It Daily

Chapter 1 (Kiritsubo) is relatively short — in most translations, 20–30 pages. Its density warrants three sittings:

- **Day 1:** The court's configuration; the emperor's preference; the Kiritsubo Lady's rank and the protocol violations her favor creates; the social machinery of jealousy. (~pages 1–10 in Seidensticker; 1–12 in Tyler)
- **Day 2:** The Lady's failing health; her death; the emperor's mourning and its political consequences. The palace emptied of her. (~pages 11–20)
- **Day 3:** The Korean diviner's reading; Genji's face and destiny; the decision to register him as a commoner; the novel's opening horizon announced. (~pages 20 to chapter's end)

After each sitting: select one passage where the narration moves through emotion quickly — where you sense Murasaki Shikibu has chosen not to dwell — and sit with it. What is being left out? Where is the grief living if not in the words themselves? This exercise is the novel's reading method in miniature.

## Translations to Compare

**In the corpus (source rail: https://www.aozora.gr.jp/):**
- The corpus holds the Japanese original at `japanese/tale-of-genji/japanese_original.txt` (Aozora Bunko text). Even without Japanese, the visual texture of the kana-kanji manuscript is worth encountering. The script guide is at `studium/scripts/japanese.md` (forthcoming).

**The two essential English translations:**

- **Royall Tyler** (Penguin Classics, 2001) — the current scholarly standard. Tyler preserves the syntactic complexity of Murasaki Shikibu's Japanese: her sentences are long, deliberately ambiguous about who is acting and who is spoken of (Heian court prose avoids naming people by name), and full of embedded poems. Tyler's version is harder than Seidensticker's but more faithful to what reading the original is actually like. Essential.

- **Edward G. Seidensticker** (Knopf, 1976) — for fifty years the default English *Genji*, and still the most readable translation in sustained prose. Seidensticker smooths the ambiguities and modernizes the register; his version reads beautifully and moves with appropriate speed. Use it for the chapter-level narrative arc; use Tyler when you want to sit with a specific scene.

**The comparison exercise for Chapter 1:** find the passage where the Kiritsubo Lady's death is narrated. Compare how Tyler and Seidensticker handle it. Tyler will give you something closer to the original's elliptical restraint; Seidensticker will give you more explicit emotional statement. The difference between the two translations at this moment is the difference between two theories of what this novel is doing. Whichever version moves you more — trust that instinct and note why.

**A third option for the embedded poems:**

The chapter contains *waka* (31-syllable court poems) exchanged between characters at key moments. Both Tyler and Seidensticker translate them into prose or near-prose. For the poems specifically, Helen Craig McCullough's anthology *Classical Japanese Prose* (Stanford, 1990) provides useful context. The poems in *Genji* are not decorative; they are the characters' most direct speech, the moment when the formal social surface gives way to emotional precision. Pay them the attention you would give to lyric passages in Homer.

*The Japanese opening:*

```
いずれの御時にか、女御、更衣あまた侍ひたまひける中に、
Izure no ontoki ni ka, nyōgo, kōi amata saburaitamahikeru naka ni,
```

The first phrase — *Izure no ontoki ni ka* — means roughly "In which reign was it?" or "In some reign." Murasaki Shikibu begins with deliberate temporal indefiniteness: this is not a chronicle; it is not claiming historical specificity. It is declaring, from its first breath, that it is fiction — an account of feeling, not of events. The indeterminacy is the point.
