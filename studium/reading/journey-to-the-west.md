# Reading Orientation: Journey to the West (西遊記)

**Main question: What is pilgrimage, transformation, and comic transcendence?**

*Journey to the West* is asking what it means to change — not to improve incrementally but to undergo
a metamorphosis so complete that the creature arriving at the destination is not the creature that
departed. The novel tracks four pilgrims (and a horse) across a hundred chapters and eighty-one
ordained disasters, from Tang China to India and back, in quest of Buddhist scriptures. But the
scriptures are almost beside the point. What the novel is actually about is what happens to a
personality under sustained trial — and whether transformation that is forced upon you by successive
humiliations can be the same as transformation you would have chosen. The Monkey King begins the
novel as a creature who refuses death, refuses subordination, refuses every limit placed on him.
He ends it, after one hundred chapters, as a Buddha. The question the novel never stops pressing is:
is that a victory or a defeat? And can something be both?

---

## Structure

*Journey to the West* is 100 chapters. The traditional divisions:

**Chapters 1–7 (The Monkey Saga):** Sun Wukong's origin and rebellion. Born from a stone, he
masters immortality and the 72 transformations, steals weapons from the Dragon Kings, erases his
name from Death's register, battles the entire celestial army to a standstill, and declares himself
Great Sage Equal to Heaven (齊天大聖). Chapters 1–7 are essentially a separate novel — the myth of
an anarchic trickster god confronting the cosmic order. They end when the Buddha imprisons the
monkey under Five-Elements Mountain for five hundred years. This is the novel's structural hinge:
the trickster god becomes the prisoner who becomes the pilgrim.

**Chapter 8 (The Setup):** The Buddha commissions Guanyin to find a pilgrim to fetch scriptures
from the west. This transitional chapter introduces the logic of the journey before it begins.

**Chapters 9–12 (Tang Sanzang's Origin):** The human monk Xuanzang (Tripitaka / Tang Sanzang) is
introduced, his past lives explained, and his commission from the Tang Emperor established. He departs
alone. These chapters ground the pilgrimage in the human world before the companions join.

**Chapters 13–99 (The Pilgrimage):** The eighty-one disasters. Xuanzang recruits his companions —
Sun Wukong (Chapter 14), Zhu Bajie / Pigsy (Chapter 19), Sha Wujing / Sandy (Chapter 22) — and
the dragon-prince white horse. Every chapter or chapter-pair presents a monster, a kingdom in
supernatural crisis, or a natural obstacle. The structure is episodic by design: each disaster is
a complete unit of transformation, humiliation, or revelation, and each one teaches something to one
of the four pilgrims (not always the same one).

**Chapter 100 (Arrival and Completion):** The scriptures obtained. The pilgrims retroactively receive
their completed spiritual identities — the monkey is made Victorious Fighting Buddha. The wheel stops.

## What to Watch in Chapter 1

Chapter 1 has an unusual double structure: it begins with a philosophical cosmology (the twelve
cosmic cycles, the origin of Heaven and Earth) that seems to have nothing to do with the monkey —
and then arrives at the monkey as if he is the logical outcome of that cosmology. This is deliberate.
Wu Cheng'en is positioning Sun Wukong as something produced by the universe itself, not by any
individual's intention or any god's design. Watch for:

**The stone's geometry:** The stone is described with precise cosmic dimensions — 36.5 chi high
(matching the 365 degrees of Heaven), 24 chi in circumference (matching the 24 solar terms), with
nine openings and eight holes (matching the Nine Palaces and Eight Trigrams). The monkey is not
accidental. He is the universe's own creature, calibrated to its structure.

**The Jade Emperor's dismissal:** The Celestial Eye and Ear report the gold-light anomaly from the
monkey's eyes. The Emperor says: not remarkable. Note this dismissal — it is the first of many times
the cosmic order will underestimate Sun Wukong. The novel's comedy is largely built on the gap
between how Heaven files him and what he actually is.

**The waterfall as threshold:** The Water-Curtain Cave is both a reward and a type of the journey
itself: there is something beyond the frightening surface (the roaring cataract, death-by-drowning)
that requires only the willingness to leap. The monkey leaps without hesitation. This is his nature,
and it will get him into every disaster on the pilgrimage.

**The sudden weeping:** Three hundred years of perfect happiness, then the pivot: the fear of death.
This is the chapter's most important moment. It reveals that paradise without immortality is not
paradise — it is just a more comfortable form of the prison under Five-Elements Mountain he will
later inhabit. The monkey's refusal to accept mortality is the engine of the whole first arc, and
of everything that follows.

## Daily Portion

Chapter 1 is substantial but self-contained. A productive division:

- **Day 1:** The cosmological opening through the appearance of the stone on the mountain (first
  two poems and the stone's description). Read slowly; this is the novel's key to itself.
- **Day 2:** The stone's birth and the monkey's first days — his obeisance to the four directions,
  the Jade Emperor's dismissal, his life among the troupe.
- **Day 3:** The waterfall — discovery, the dare, the leap, the Water-Curtain Cave. The monkey's
  announcement and the enthronement as Beautiful Monkey King.
- **Day 4:** Three hundred years compressed into a paragraph, then the pivot: the weeping, the
  elder monkey's counsel, the announcement of the quest, and the farewell feast. Close with the
  departure.

After each session: copy one of the chapter's embedded poems by hand (there are four in Chapter 1),
note which English translator renders the verse most faithfully, and write one sentence about what
the poem is doing that the prose cannot.

## The Chinese Opening

The first poem of Chapter 1 (Wu Cheng'en's own framing verse):

```
混沌未分天地亂，茫茫渺渺無人見。
Hùndùn wèi fēn tiāndì luàn, mángmáng miǎomiǎo wú rén jiàn.
```

| phrase | pinyin | gloss |
|--------|--------|-------|
| 混沌 | hùndùn | **primordial chaos** — the undivided state before creation; cognate with the cosmological term Hundun |
| 未分 | wèi fēn | **not yet divided / not yet differentiated** — 未 = not yet; 分 = to divide, separate |
| 天地亂 | tiāndì luàn | **Heaven and Earth [were] chaotic** — tiān = sky/heaven; dì = earth; luàn = disorder, chaos |
| 茫茫渺渺 | mángmáng miǎomiǎo | **vast and indistinct, boundless and dim** — reduplication for scale and vagueness |
| 無人見 | wú rén jiàn | **no person could see [it]** — wú = without/no; rén = person; jiàn = to see/perceive |

The poem continues: since Pangu broke the primordial murk, the clear rose and the turbid sank.
Heaven and Earth support all living things. If you want to know the merit of Creation's great
cycles — you must read this account of the journey that relieves calamities.

**Note:** the novel positions itself as cosmological necessity from its first lines. Reading it,
you are reading what the universe required to happen.

## Translations to Compare

Two primary English translations are in wide use:

**Anthony C. Yu (1977–1983, University of Chicago Press; revised 2012):** The scholarly standard.
Four volumes, complete, with extensive annotation. Yu renders the poetry as verse, preserves the
tonal register shifts between prose and poem, and explains every allusion. The best companion for
deep reading. His Sun Wukong is vigorous and exact.

**W. J. F. Jenner (1982–1986, Foreign Languages Press Beijing):** A three-volume complete translation
that reads fluently, tends to normalize the comic register, and is more accessible on first pass.
Useful for narrative momentum; less useful when the poetry matters.

**Arthur Waley (1942):** *Monkey* — not a translation but an abridgement and retelling (Chapters
1–31 and 98–100, minus most of the verse). Waley's prose is elegant and his monkey is vivid, but
you are reading Waley's synthesis, not Wu Cheng'en's structure. Read it as a way in, not as the
text.

**Recommended exercise on Chapter 1:** Find the waterfall passage ("一派白虹起 / 千尋雪浪飛" — "a
cascade of white rainbow rises / a thousand fathoms of snow-waves fly") in Yu, Jenner, and Waley.
Note how each translator handles the shift from descriptive verse to comic prose. The verse is
cosmological; the prose that follows it (the monkeys clapping, the dare, the boasting) is
burlesque. The gap is the novel's tonal signature.
