# Reading Orientation: The Metamorphoses

**Main question: What is form? What is change?**

Ovid opens his poem with a statement, not a prayer: *In nova fert animus mutatas dicere formas / corpora* — my mind drives me to speak of forms changed into new bodies. This is the *Metamorphoses*'s governing claim: the world is made of matter that cannot hold its shape. Everything that appears to be stable — the cosmos, a species, a body, a name — is already in the process of becoming something else. The poem runs from the creation of the world to Ovid's own present (the Augustan age, Caesar's apotheosis) in fifteen books and approximately 12,000 hexameter lines, and in that span it presents over 250 transformations. But the *Metamorphoses* is not a catalog. It is an argument: that **form is contingent**, that **change is the only constant**, and that **desire, power, and divine will** are the three forces through which the cosmos enacts that changeability on individual bodies. The poem's main question is not "what happened to Daphne?" or "what happened to Narcissus?" — those are the examples. The question is: if nothing holds its form, what does it mean to be a self?

---

## Structure

The *Metamorphoses* is 15 books of continuous hexameter — **not** divided into discrete episodes as the Iliad's books are. Ovid builds narrative chains, stories that flow into stories by means of embedded narrators, bridging characters, and geographical sequences. The poem resists division: one myth ends mid-thought into the next. Nevertheless, scholars recognize three broad movements:

**Books 1–6: Cosmic and divine transformations.** The poem opens at the scale of creation (cosmogony, four ages, flood) and descends toward the scale of gods pursuing mortals. The dominant transformations are punitive or erotic: Daphne, Io, Narcissus, Echo, Arachne. The gods are present as protagonists, not background figures.

**Books 7–11: The heroic age.** The Medea story (Book 7) opens the middle section. Myths now acquire narrative weight — the adventures of Perseus (Book 4–5), the Calydonian Boar (Book 8), the fall of Troy approached through the Trojan cycle (Books 11–13). The register shifts: transformations become more concentrated, more individual, less cosmological. The heroic age is itself a kind of metamorphosis from the divine age of Books 1–6.

**Books 12–15: History, Rome, and Ovid's epilogue.** Troy falls (Book 12). Aeneas arrives in Italy (Books 13–14). Julius Caesar is apotheosized (Book 15). Ovid closes the poem with an explicit claim to his own immortality: *Iamque opus exegi* — now I have finished my work, which neither Jupiter's anger nor fire nor sword nor time will be able to destroy. The *Metamorphoses* ends with a metamorphosis of the poet himself: into permanent literary form.

## What to Watch in Book 1

Book 1 is the poem's thesis demonstration. Every large force in the *Metamorphoses* appears here at maximum scale before the poem narrows its lens toward individuals:

- **The cosmogony (1–88):** Read the first line three times before reading further. *In nova fert animus* is the engine; every transformation in 15 books is already contained in it. Note that Ovid does not name the deity who separates the elements — it is a *deus et melior natura*, a god or better nature. The divine here is a force, not a person. This is characteristic: Ovid's gods are rarely transcendent; they are agents with motives, which is why they are also comic, violent, petty, and erotic.

- **The four ages (89–150):** The decline from gold to iron is Ovid's most explicitly philosophical passage. Read the iron age description carefully — it names the specific crimes that define it (mining, surveying, war, fraud) and notes that the gods have abandoned the earth. Astraea, goddess of justice, is the last divinity to leave. Notice that the iron age is Ovid's own historical present. The poem begins at creation and ends at Augustus; Book 1 announces that the present is the worst of ages.

- **The flood (253–415):** This is Ovid's most sustained narrative in Book 1. Watch the epic simile — the farmer sees fish in his treetops. Ovid's tone here is not purely dark; there is an observational pleasure in the details of catastrophe. This tonal double-ness — disaster rendered with artistic delight — is the poem's signature and its most debated quality. Is Ovid flippant? Is he distancing? The answer is neither: he is Ovidian, which is its own category.

- **Daphne (452–567):** The book's final scene is its most concentrated meditation on the main question. Daphne asks to be changed and is changed; but she is changed *into something permanent* (the laurel, Apollo's tree forever) while the girl who wanted escape is gone. Is this a rescue or a second violation? The metamorphosis preserves and destroys simultaneously. Watch for this double-logic throughout the poem.

## Daily Portion

Book 1 runs approximately 779 lines. A productive daily unit:

- **Day 1:** Lines 1–88 (the first line through the cosmogony's completion: the world ordered, inhabitants assigned). Copy the first couplet in Latin by hand (see below) before reading.
- **Day 2:** Lines 89–252 (the four ages through the Giants and Lycaon's transformation). Pay close attention to Lycaon — the metamorphosis is told from Jupiter's point of view, which is the point.
- **Day 3:** Lines 253–415 (the flood through Deucalion and Pyrrha, the stones becoming people). Note the moment when Deucalion hesitates because he does not understand the oracle.
- **Day 4:** Lines 416–567 (the Python, the Pythian Games, Cupid's revenge, Apollo's pursuit of Daphne, the transformation, the laurel). Read the Daphne scene twice.

After each session: copy one Latin couplet from the day's passage. Write the literal English below it. Then write one sentence about which translator renders the couplet best and why.

## The Latin Opening

```
In nova fert animus mutatas dicere formas
corpora; di, coeptis (nam vos mutastis et illas)
```

| word | gloss |
|------|-------|
| *In nova* | toward new things |
| *fert animus* | my mind drives / carries (me) — *animus* = mind/spirit/impulse, subject of *fert*; Ovid is the agent, not the Muse |
| *mutatas* | changed (perfect passive participle, modifying *formas*) |
| *dicere* | to speak, to tell — infinitive (object of *fert animus*) |
| *formas* | forms, shapes — accusative plural |
| *corpora* | bodies — accusative plural; *formas corpora* = forms of bodies, forms changed into bodies |
| *di* | gods — vocative (address to the gods follows in line 2) |
| *coeptis* | to my undertaking — dative |
| *vos mutastis et illas* | you too have changed them — *vos* = you (gods); *illas* = those forms/bodies; the gods are themselves agents of transformation, and Ovid invokes them *because* they have already done what the poem will describe |

The most important thing in these two lines: Ovid does not ask the Muse for inspiration. He addresses the gods not as sources of the poem but as its *subject matter and evidence*. The invocation is a statement of method.

## Translations to Compare

The corpus holds two translations alongside the original:

- **Riley (1851), via Project Gutenberg (Books I–VII):** The most literal available in the corpus — aimed at scholars, often clumsy, reliable for word-level meaning. Use Riley when you want to know what the Latin says without interpretation. His diction will sometimes flatten Ovid's wit.

- **Perseus/Tufts (source rail):** `https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus:text:1999.02.0029` — the Brookes More prose translation (1922), with the Latin text and morphological parser alongside. Hover any word for case and parsing. This is the recommended working environment for the Latin contact layer.

**Beyond the corpus — recommended for comparison:**

- **Ted Hughes — *Tales from Ovid* (1997):** Not a translation but a re-voicing: Hughes selects 24 tales and renders them in free verse at high intensity. His *Daphne* and *Narcissus* are among the best Ovid in English. Use Hughes to feel what the poem can do in a contemporary register — then return to the Latin to see how different the original is.

- **Rolfe Humphries (1955):** The most readable complete verse translation in English. Humphries finds the poem's speed and tonal range without sacrificing accuracy. Good for reading narrative momentum across many books.

**Recommended exercise:** Compare all three renderings of the Daphne transformation (lines 548–555), the moment the bark closes around her. Riley will give you the literal; Humphries will give you the motion; Hughes will give you the violence. The gap between the three is a seminar in what translation is and cannot be.
