# Reading Orientation: The Iliad

**Main question: What is wrath?**

The Iliad announces its subject in its first word — *mēnis*, wrath — and then spends 24 books refusing to let that word stay simple. It is not anger as a mood; it is *mēnis*, the specific, cosmic, almost sacred wrath that gods feel and that one mortal, Achilles, is permitted to feel like a god. The poem is the anatomy of that wrath: where it comes from (a king's insult, a prize taken), what it costs (countless Achaean dead, the will of Zeus fulfilled), what it becomes (grief, then a more terrible wrath, then — at the very end, over a dead enemy's body — something that is no longer wrath at all). Read the Iliad as a single question held open for 15,000 lines: what is this force, why does it move the best of the Achaeans, and what finally stills it?

---

## Structure

The Iliad is 24 books of hexameter verse, approximately 15,700 lines. The action covers only about seven weeks in the tenth year of the war — the poem is not "the Trojan War" but the consequences of one man's anger inside it. It divides into three movements:

**The Quarrel and the Withdrawal (Books 1–9):** Apollo's plague, the rupture between Agamemnon and Achilles, Achilles' withdrawal from the fighting, and Thetis's appeal to Zeus that the Achaeans be made to suffer for his dishonor. The catalogue of ships (Book 2), the duel of Paris and Menelaus (Book 3), the *aristeia* of Diomedes (Books 5–6), Hector and Andromache at the wall (Book 6), and the failed embassy to Achilles (Book 9) — the Greeks try every means short of justice to bring him back.

**The Battle at the Ships (Books 11–18):** Without Achilles, the Achaeans are driven back to their ships. Agamemnon, Diomedes, Odysseus are all wounded; the wall is breached; the ships are nearly fired. Patroclus, unable to bear it, begs to fight in Achilles' armor. He drives the Trojans back — and Hector, with Apollo's help, kills him (Book 16). The fight over Patroclus's body (Book 17) and Achilles' annihilating grief (Book 18) turn the poem.

**The Wrath Transformed (Books 19–24):** Achilles reconciles with Agamemnon — but his wrath has only changed its object, from a king to a man. He returns to the field, the new shield Hephaestus forged blazing (Book 19), routs the Trojans, fights the river Scamander itself (Book 21), and kills Hector beneath the walls (Book 22). The funeral games for Patroclus (Book 23) and — the poem's astonishing close — Priam's night journey to ransom his son's body, and Achilles' grief meeting the old king's (Book 24).

## What to Watch in Book 1

Book 1 is the engine of the whole poem: every force the Iliad will examine is set in motion here.

- **The proem (lines 1–7):** Homer states the subject (*mēnis*, wrath), its agent (Achilles, son of Peleus), its cost (countless souls of heroes sent to Hades), and its meaning (the will of Zeus was being accomplished). The poem tells you its ending in its first sentence. Everything after is the working-out.

- **The plague (Apollo):** The book opens not with the quarrel but with a *supplication* — old Chryses, a priest, coming to ransom his daughter. Agamemnon refuses him brutally. Apollo answers the priest's prayer with nine days of arrows. Watch how supplication-refused is the seed of everything: the structure of the whole Iliad is a chain of appeals, some honored, most refused.

- **The quarrel:** When Agamemnon is forced to give up his prize Chryseis, he seizes Achilles' prize Briseis to compensate his honor. This is the rupture. Notice that the fight is not about a woman but about *timē* — honor rendered visible as a prize. Achilles' wrath is the wrath of a man whose worth has been publicly denied. Athena physically stops him from drawing his sword (lines 193–222) — the first divine intervention, restraint not rescue.

- **Thetis and Zeus:** Achilles weeps on the shore and calls his mother, the sea-goddess Thetis. She goes to Olympus and supplicates Zeus: let the Achaeans lose while my son is dishonored. Zeus nods (the nod that shakes Olympus, lines 524–530). The mortal quarrel is now a divine plan. The cost of Achilles' honor will be paid in Achaean lives.

- **The comedy of the gods:** The book ends, astonishingly, in laughter — Hephaestus limping about Olympus, the gods' "unquenchable laughter," a feast. Homer answers the mortal catastrophe with divine comedy. This tonal counterpoint — terrible human stakes, gods who can afford to laugh — is one of the poem's deepest structures. Hold it in mind: it returns, inverted, in Book 24.

## Daily Portion

Book 1 runs approximately 611 lines. A productive daily unit:

- **Day 1:** Lines 1–100 (the proem, Chryses' supplication, Agamemnon's refusal, the plague). Read in your chosen translation, then copy lines 1–5 of the Greek by hand (see below).
- **Day 2:** Lines 101–305 (the assembly, the quarrel of Agamemnon and Achilles, Athena's restraint, the seizing of Briseis).
- **Day 3:** Lines 306–492 (Briseis taken, Achilles' lament on the shore, Thetis's consolation, the return of Chryseis and the appeasement of Apollo).
- **Day 4:** Lines 493–611 (Thetis's supplication of Zeus, the nod, the quarrel of Zeus and Hera, Hephaestus and the laughter of the gods).

After each session: write one Greek line from the day's passage, gloss it word by word, and note which translator renders it best — and why.

## The Greek Opening

```
μῆνιν ἄειδε θεὰ Πηληϊάδεω Ἀχιλῆος
mēnin  aeide  thea  Pēlēiadeō   Achilēos
```

| word | translit | gloss |
|------|----------|-------|
| μῆνιν | mēnin | **wrath** — accusative (the object; the poem's first word, and its subject) |
| ἄειδε | aeide | **sing** — present imperative (the command to the goddess) |
| θεά | thea | **goddess** — vocative (the Muse, invoked) |
| Πηληϊάδεω | Pēlēiadeō | **of the son of Peleus** — genitive (a patronymic; whose wrath) |
| Ἀχιλῆος | Achilēos | **of Achilles** — genitive (the name, held back to the line's end) |

Note the architecture of the line: *wrath* first, *Achilles* last, and between them only "sing, goddess, of the son of Peleus." The grammar enacts the poem — the wrath precedes the man, and the man's name is the destination the whole line travels toward. Compare the Odyssey's opening (ἄνδρα μοι ἔννεπε — 'a man, tell me of'): the Iliad begins with an abstraction, the Odyssey with a person. The difference is the two poems.

## Translations to Compare

The corpus holds four translations alongside the Greek original:

- **Lang, Leaf & Myers (1883):** "The wrath sing, goddess, of Peleus' son Achilles." Deliberately archaic, formulaic, close to the Greek word order — it preserves *mēnis* as the first word in English, which almost no other translator manages. The best control text for word-by-word work.

- **Butler (1898):** "Sing, O goddess, the anger of Achilles son of Peleus." Prose, plain, readable — but notice he reorders the line to put the verb first and demotes *mēnis* to "anger." Good for narrative speed; thin on the sacral weight of the word.

- **Pope (1715):** "Achilles' wrath, to Greece the direful spring / Of woes unnumber'd, heavenly goddess, sing!" Heroic couplets — magnificent, and as much Pope's Augustan England as Homer's Greece. Read it for cadence and rhetorical force; do not use it as a gloss.

- **Cowper (1791):** "Achilles sing, O Goddess! Peleus' son; / His wrath pernicious." Blank verse, more literal than Pope, more measured than Butler — the usual middle register when Pope ornaments and Butler flattens.

**Recommended exercise:** Look up *mēnis* in all four. Note who keeps it first, who softens it to "anger," who expands it ("wrath pernicious," "woes unnumber'd"). The single word *mēnis* — used in Greek almost only of gods — is the whole poem's thesis. How each translator weights it tells you what kind of Iliad they think they are translating.
