# The Tanakh — Reading Orientation

## Main Question

**What is covenant, law, creation, exile?**

This is the question that drives the Tanakh's entire arc, from the first speech-act in Genesis to the last book of Ketuvim. The Tanakh is not a unified narrative with a single protagonist; it is a library in conversation with itself — Torah, Nevi'im, Ketuvim (law, prophets, writings), three bodies of literature organized around a single relationship: between a people, a God, and the terms of that relationship, which keep being made, broken, mourned, and re-established. Read the Tanakh as a record of that relationship — its ecstasy, its failures, its silences, its lawsuits.

---

## Structure

The Tanakh comprises 39 books (in the Hebrew canon) organized into three divisions:

**Torah (Five Books of Moses):** Genesis · Exodus · Leviticus · Numbers · Deuteronomy
The origin and law. Creation, the patriarchs, Egypt, Sinai, the wilderness, the second giving of the law on the edge of the promised land. The Torah is both narrative and legal corpus — it does not separate story from statute.

**Nevi'im (Prophets):** Joshua · Judges · Samuel (×2) · Kings (×2) — Former Prophets; then Isaiah · Jeremiah · Ezekiel · The Twelve Minor Prophets — Latter Prophets.
The consequence of covenant: entering the land, the monarchy, the split kingdom, military defeat, the Assyrian conquest of the north, the Babylonian exile of the south. The prophets speak *into* that arc, mostly warning, sometimes weeping.

**Ketuvim (Writings):** Psalms · Proverbs · Job · Song of Songs · Ruth · Lamentations · Ecclesiastes · Esther · Daniel · Ezra · Nehemiah · Chronicles (×2).
The affective and reflective literature: praise, wisdom, erotic poetry, lament, theodicy. The Ketuvim does not advance the main narrative — it meditates on what it costs to live inside it.

## What to Watch for in the Opening (Genesis 1)

Genesis 1 is not a preface to the real story. It is a theological argument disguised as a calendar. Three things to track on your first reading:

**The speech-act structure.** Every day begins with *vayomer Elohim* ("and God said"). Every command is immediately fulfilled. Every fulfilled creation is assessed (*vayar Elohim ki tov* — "and God saw that it was good"). The text is iterating a formula — notice how strict the form is, and then notice what breaks from it on day six (the plural "let us make," the absence of "it was good" for the sixth day's first act, the dual blessing of male and female).

**What is not said.** The text does not explain who or what God is. It does not explain what existed before the formless void, or how God began, or what "image" and "likeness" mean. The Tanakh's theology operates through strategic silence. The questions the text refuses to answer are the ones the tradition will spend centuries arguing about.

**The anti-mythological subtext.** Sun and moon are not named (later verses call them only "the greater light" and "the lesser light") — this is deliberate: the surrounding cultures called them Shamash and Sin, gods to be propitiated. Here they are lamps. The sea monsters (*tanninim*) appear in verse 21 without ceremony — in Babylonian cosmology, Tiamat is the chaos-dragon from whose body the world is made; in Genesis, the tanninim are just another species, made and assessed as good. Genesis 1 is the quietest possible polemic.

## How to Portion It Daily

The Tanakh is organized into a traditional lectionary cycle (*parashiyot*) for weekly Torah reading, but for a reader coming to the text fresh, the following daily rhythm works better than either ploughing through or dipping randomly:

- **Torah (Genesis–Deuteronomy):** One chapter per day is right — the chapters are short, but each chapter of Genesis is a world. Never rush past the genealogies (they are structural arguments about time, not filler).
- **Nevi'im:** One chapter per day for narrative books (Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings); for prophetic books (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel), use thematic blocks — the oracles of judgment, the oracles of consolation, the vision sections.
- **Ketuvim:** Psalm by Psalm; Job as a unit (it requires sitting through the whole argument); Ecclesiastes as a single long meditation, read in one or two sittings.

A good orientation sequence for the first month: Genesis 1–11 (primeval history) · Genesis 12–25 (Abraham) · Exodus 1–20 (Egypt, Sinai, the law) · then pause and read Psalm 1, Psalm 22, and Psalm 137 — three affective anchors from three very different moments in the tradition.

**Source rail:** https://www.sefaria.org/texts/Tanakh (Hebrew text with interlinear, commentary, and cross-references across the whole tradition — the best single online resource for studying the Tanakh seriously)

**The translation encounter:** Compare how these four render the opening verse:
- KJV: "In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth."
- YLT: "In the beginning of God's preparing the heavens and the earth —" (treats the opening as a temporal clause, not an absolute statement)
- Modern Jewish Publication Society (JPS, not in corpus but widely available): "When God began to create heaven and earth —"
- The Alter translation (Robert Alter, *The Hebrew Bible*, W.W. Norton, 2018): "When God began to create the heavens and the earth —"

The difference between "In the beginning" and "When God began" is the difference between *creatio ex nihilo* and a God who begins work on a pre-existing formless substrate. Both readings are grammatically defensible. The argument between them is millennia old. This is what translation means in the Tanakh: every choice is a theological position.

**The Hebrew encounter:** The single most important word to know in Genesis 1 is *bara* (ברא) — to create, but the verb is used in the Hebrew Bible *only* with God as subject. Everything human beings make uses other verbs (*asah*, to make; *yatzar*, to form). This distinction is structural: only God *bara*'s. Copy the opening verse in Hebrew, identify *bara*, and notice that it appears in verse 1, verse 21 (the sea creatures), and verse 27 (humankind) — three times, marking the three moments of genuine ontological novelty in the chapter.
