# Reading Orientation — The Mahabharata

## Main Question

**What is duty (dharma) when every choice destroys something?**

This is not an abstract philosophical question. The Mahabharata earns it through eighteen
parvas and approximately 100,000 shlokas (verses) — roughly ten times the combined length
of the Iliad and Odyssey. Every major character in the poem faces a version of this question
with something real at stake, and no one answers it cleanly. The Pandavas win the war;
they also lose almost everyone they love and reign over a kingdom full of the widows of men
they killed. Bhishma, the most righteous man in the poem, spends half a century upholding
a vow that enables catastrophe. Karna knows who he is and fights for the wrong side anyway.
The Bhagavad Gita — embedded in Book 6 (Bhishma Parva) — is where Krishna gives Arjuna a
direct answer to the question. Read the whole poem first; the Gita lands differently when
you have seen what dharma costs in practice.

## Structure

The poem is organized into 18 parvas (books) plus a supplement (Harivamsa):

| Parva | # | Subject |
|-------|---|---------|
| Adi Parva | 1 | Frame, origins, genealogy, early life of the heroes |
| Sabha Parva | 2 | The dice game; Draupadi's humiliation; exile decreed |
| Vana Parva | 3 | Twelve years in the forest; pilgrimage; encounters |
| Virata Parva | 4 | The year of disguise at King Virata's court |
| Udyoga Parva | 5 | Diplomacy, war preparations, Krishna's embassy |
| Bhishma Parva | 6 | First ten days of battle; the Bhagavad Gita |
| Drona Parva | 7 | Days 11–15; Abhimanyu's death; the chakravyuha |
| Karna Parva | 8 | Days 16–17; Karna as commander; his death |
| Shalya Parva | 9 | Day 18; Shalya as commander; Duryodhana's last stand |
| Sauptika Parva | 10 | The night massacre; Ashvatthama's revenge |
| Stri Parva | 11 | The women's grief; Gandhari's curse on Krishna |
| Shanti Parva | 12 | Bhishma on the bed of arrows; the long teaching on dharma |
| Anushasana Parva | 13 | Bhishma's continued instruction; his death |
| Ashvamedhika Parva | 14 | The horse sacrifice to reconsecrate the kingdom |
| Ashramavasika Parva | 15 | Dhritarashtra and Gandhari go to the forest |
| Mausala Parva | 16 | The destruction of the Yadava clan |
| Mahaprasthanika Parva | 17 | The Pandavas begin their final journey |
| Svarga Arohana Parva | 18 | Ascent to heaven; the poem's last revelation |

The war itself occupies Books 6–10. Before the war (Books 1–5) is as long as the war;
after the war (Books 11–18) is nearly as long again. The poem is not about the battle;
it is about what a civilization does before and after a catastrophe it could not prevent.

## What to Watch For in Adi Parva

**The triple frame.** Three nested narrators: Vyasa composes; Vaisampayana recites to
Janamejaya at the snake sacrifice; Ugrasravas (Sauti) recites that recitation to the
sages at Naimisha. You are the fourth receiver. The poem is formally about its own
transmission — fidelity of telling is as important as the story told.

**The snake sacrifice as the poem's outer wound.** Janamejaya destroys the serpent lineage
in revenge for his father Parikshit's death. The sub-narrative of Astika (whose plea stops
the sacrifice) is the Adi Parva's first demonstration of dharma under pressure: a son doing
his duty to his mother's lineage at the exact moment that duty conflicts with a king's
legitimate grief. The sacrifice stops because Astika's speech is flawless. Remember this
pattern; it recurs.

**The genealogy is not genealogy.** The descent from Brahma through the Prajapatis through
the solar and lunar lineages to the Bharatas is an argument: the closer your origin is to
divine order, the more completely your fall will illuminate what dharma requires. The Pandavas
and Kauravas are not ordinary rivals; they are the final generation of a lineage that began
at the moment of creation.

**Vyasa is everywhere.** He is the poem's author, and he is also a character in it — he
appears at critical moments throughout all 18 parvas to advise, to witness, and occasionally
to intervene. He is the son of Satyavati and the sage Parasara; he is the biological father
(through niyoga) of Dhritarashtra, Pandu, and Vidura. His presence as both narrator and
participant is not a literary inconsistency; it is the poem's argument that memory and action
are not separable.

**Early scenes to mark:** the birth of Bhishma (Devavrata) and his terrible vow of celibacy;
the story of Shakuntala and Dushyanta (Arjuna's ultimate ancestor); the dice game does not
appear until Book 2 (Sabha Parva), but its preconditions are assembled here.

## Daily Portions

The Adi Parva in full translation (Ganguli) runs to approximately 200,000 words.

A reasonable pace for close reading:
- **Week 1–2:** Sections I–XI (the frame, Vyasa's invocation, the cosmogony, the Astika story)
- **Week 3–4:** The sub-parvas — Adivansavatarana (the genealogy), Sambhava (births of the heroes)
- **Week 5–6:** Sambhava continued — early life of Bhishma, the Pandavas' birth and childhood

Do not rush the Sambhava sub-parva. The births of Bhishma, Drona, Karna, and the Pandavas
are not prologues; they are the poem's full first act, each character's origin establishing
the exact terms on which dharma will eventually break them.

## Translations to Compare

**Primary (in corpus):**
- **Ganguli (1883–1896)** — the complete prose translation; literal, sometimes stiff,
  always faithful. Available via the corpus at `sanskrit/mahabharata/english_ganguli_sample.txt`
  and in full via Project Gutenberg. Use Ganguli as your anchor: its fidelity to the original's
  accumulative, encyclopedic quality is unmatched in English.

**Secondary (recommended alongside):**
- **Van Buitenen (Chicago, 1973–1978)** — scholarly, unfinished (Van Buitenen died; covers
  Books 1–5 only); prose that reads as literature. Essential for Adi through Udyoga Parvas.
  Where Ganguli is a faithful mirror, Van Buitenen is a lit window: the same view, clearer glass.
- **Bibek Debroy (Penguin, 2010–2014)** — complete, contemporary, accurate, readable.
  Good for Books 6–18 where Van Buitenen does not reach.

**Source rail for Sanskrit text:** https://gretil.sub.uni-goettingen.de/gretil.html#MBh
(GRETIL holds the critical edition text in Devanagari and transliteration; use the
transliterated version to sound individual shlokas even without reading Devanagari fluently)

**Single-page access:** The Bhagavad Gita (embedded in Book 6) is available in dozens of
standalone editions; hold off until you reach it in sequence. Wrenched from its context —
Arjuna refusing to fight, his chariot between the two armies, his bow fallen — the Gita
is philosophy. Read in context, it is a man being talked back from paralysis by a god
who has already decided the war must happen.
