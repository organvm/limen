# Reading Orientation: The Conference of the Birds
*(Mantiq al-Tayr — Farid ud-Din Attar, c. 1177 CE)*

## The Main Question

**What is the mystical ascent through the self?**

This is not a question about destinations. The Conference of the Birds ends with the thirty
surviving birds looking for the Simurgh — the king they have crossed seven valleys to find — and
discovering that *si murgh* in Persian means thirty birds. The destination was the travelers.
The Simurgh is the unified self that remains after the self has been dissolved through the
disciplines of ascent. Attar's poem is about the journey that is actually a subtraction: valley
by valley, the birds lose what they thought they were, until what remains is the real thing.

This question — what remains when the ego is removed from the pilgrim? — is Attar's contribution
to the global literature of spiritual practice. It connects to Sufi *fana* (annihilation) and
*baqa* (subsistence), to the Buddhist dissolution of the self, to Neoplatonic return to the One.
But Attar's version has something none of the purely philosophical accounts have: birds. The poem
is populated with named, stubborn, excuse-making, beautiful, comic creatures who do not want to
go. Watching them go anyway is watching the ascent happen from inside its resistance.

---

## Structure: 45 Sections

The poem is organized into a prologue and 45 sections, mixing narrative (the frame story of the
assembly and journey) with embedded tales that the hoopoe tells in response to each bird's
objection or question. The embedded tales are Attar's method: he does not argue for the ascent —
he illustrates it, always obliquely, always through a story that answers the bird's fear by
showing a different kind of resolution than the bird expected.

The large movement:

| Phase | Sections | Contents |
|--:|:--|:--|
| Assembly | 1–8 | The gathering; the hoopoe's summons; each bird's objection |
| Seven Valleys | 9–22 | The valleys of Search, Love, Knowledge, Detachment, Unity, Bewilderment, Annihilation |
| The Journey | 23–38 | The birds travel; losses; further stories; the thinning of the group |
| Arrival | 39–45 | Reaching the Simurgh's court; the recognition; the dissolution |

The embedded tales (which constitute most of the poem's length) should not be skipped in favor of
the frame narrative — they *are* the teaching. Each tale is a compressed mystical argument, a
parable whose meaning is always one degree deeper than the surface story suggests.

## What to Watch for in the Opening (Section 1)

**The hoopoe is not the hero — he is already home.** The hoopoe carries the divine letter (he
appeared in the Quran carrying Solomon's message to the Queen of Sheba); he has already seen
the Simurgh's feather. He is speaking from a position of prior arrival, which is why his voice
has the quality of disclosure rather than persuasion. Notice that he does not argue the birds
into the journey — he names it, and the birds feel themselves called.

**The birds' objections are not failures — they are the poem's thesis.** Each bird that objects
(the nightingale who loves the rose, the hawk who loves the king's wrist, the peacock who wants
to return to paradise) is naming a real attachment, a real love, a real reason to stay. Attar
does not dismiss these. His poem takes them seriously. The mystical path is not for people who
have nothing to lose; it is for creatures with beautiful attachments who choose the ascent anyway.

**The feather is the poem's central image before the mirror.** The Simurgh is known through a
single feather fallen in China, from which the whole world was altered. Attar is precise here:
the trace precedes the presence; the image of the divine is available before the divine is
approachable. This is the poem's epistemology — we begin from the impression, the trace, the
rumor of light, and work backward toward the source.

**Watch the comedy.** Attar is genuinely funny. The nightingale is plaintive and romantic; the
parrot wants to live forever; the duck refuses to leave water. The humor is not decorative — it
is the poem's grace, the element that prevents the mystical instruction from becoming oppressive.
Read these sections with amusement intact. The birds are us, and we are often ridiculous.

## How to Portion It Daily

The poem runs roughly 4,500 couplets in Darbandi and Davis's English rendering (the standard
translation). A daily unit of **10–15 couplets** is appropriate — this is dense material, and
each couplet contains more than it shows.

Suggested pacing for the opening division (Section 1):
- **Day 1:** The assembly and the hoopoe's first speech (the naming of the Simurgh)
- **Day 2:** The nightingale and the rose; the hoopoe's reply
- **Day 3:** The parrot, the peacock, the duck — their objections
- **Day 4:** The hoopoe's final summons; the assembly's commitment

For the embedded tales throughout the poem, resist the urge to rush through to the frame
narrative. The frame is thin; the tales are the substance. When Attar interrupts the journey
to tell a story, that story *is* the valley the birds are in.

## Translations to Compare

**Primary (recommended):**
- **Afkham Darbandi and Dick Davis** (Penguin Classics, 1984) — the standard literary translation;
  accurate, readable, and alive. Davis (a poet himself) preserves Attar's rhyming couplets as
  couplets. This is the translation to read daily.
- **Sholeh Wolpé** (Norton, 2017) — a poet's rendering; takes more liberties but captures the
  ecstatic register better than any other English version; particularly strong in the Valley of
  Love sections. Read against Darbandi/Davis for the charged passages.

**For the Persian (source rail: https://ganjoor.net/attar/manteghotteyr/):**
The Ganjoor text is the standard online resource for the Persian original. The opening couplets
are worth copying even without Persian reading ability — the script is *nastaliq*, which has its
own visual music. Persian is a SOV language (subject-object-verb) with a rich tradition of
internal rhyme; each couplet (bayt) rhymes on both halves, which gives the poem a formal
heartbeat. The word *tayr* (birds) and *mantiq* (speech, logic, the faculty of rational discourse)
in the title announce the poem's argument: that birds — creatures of flight, creatures that
sing — are the right vehicles for a discourse about the soul's ascent.

**The comparison exercise for Section 1:** find the hoopoe's first description of the Simurgh
in both Darbandi/Davis and Wolpé. Note what each translator does with the word *morgh* (bird)
and with the phrase *Si-murgh* (thirty birds / the Simurgh). In Davis, the pun is preserved and
annotated; in Wolpé, it is rendered more freely. The quality of each translator's decision here
tells you what they think the poem's ending is *about*.

**On the script:** A Persian script guide is available at `studium/scripts/persian.md`. The
Ganjoor site renders the text with full voweling (harakat) where traditional, and the nastaliq
handwriting tradition is worth examining visually even before any grammar — the calligraphic
tradition that Attar's poem was transmitted through is itself an ascent practice.

## Film Companion: The Seven Valleys in Cinema

A complete film companion mapping each valley to authentic cinematic parallels has been created
at `studium/film/conference-of-birds.yaml`. The companion pairs each spiritual transformation with
films that handle mystical states at the level of *allegory* rather than plot — serious, contemplative
cinema from Tarkovsky, Bresson, Wong Kar-wai, Koreeda, Lynch, Parajanov, and others.

**The seven valleys and their cinematic forms:**

| Valley | Transformation | Dominant Force | Example Films |
|--------|---|---|---|
| **Talab** (Search) | Awakening to the journey; recognition of the call | ascent, longing | *Stalker*, *Sunrise*, *The Holy Mountain* |
| **Eshq** (Love) | The self consumed in the furnace of desire; ecstatic dissolution | desire, dissolution, illumination | *In the Mood for Love*, *Jeanne Dielman*, *Colour of Pomegranates* |
| **Ma'rifat** (Knowledge) | Intellectual vertigo; the mind's annihilation by direct knowing | revelation, memory, dissolution | *Persona*, *A Ghost Story*, *The Turin Horse* |
| **Isteghnā** (Detachment) | Radical relinquishment; the soul stripped of all consolations | salvation, exile, supplication | *A Man Escaped*, *La Haine*, *Come and See* |
| **Tawhīd** (Unity) | The collapse of duality; subject and object as one being | illumination, memory, divine-intervention | *Ikiru*, *The Sacrifice*, *Sans Soleil* |
| **Hayrat** (Bewilderment) | The mind's final confusion; the threshold language cannot cross | fate, longing, metamorphosis | *The Seventh Seal*, *The Night*, *Safe* |
| **Fanā** (Annihilation) | Extinction of self; the traveler becomes the destination (si murgh) | dissolution, illumination, metamorphosis | *Uncle Boonmee*, *Mulholland Drive*, *After Life* |

**How to use the film companion:**

- Read the valley description in Attar's text (usually in Sections 9–22 in translations).
- Then read the corresponding section in `studium/film/conference-of-birds.yaml` to see how cinema enacts
  the same spiritual state through form, duration, image, and sound.
- Notice that these films are not "about" the valleys—they *embody* them. A film is chosen because its
  formal properties (editing rhythm, camera distance, color palette, narrative structure) create a cinematic
  analogue of the mystical transformation.
- The films can be watched in any order, but watching them in sequence (Talab → Fanā) creates a parallel
  cinema-experience of annihilation and return.
