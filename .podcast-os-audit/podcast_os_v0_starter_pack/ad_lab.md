# Commercial Break Lab v0

Advertising is treated as a designed interstitial, not dead air read from a sheet.

## Sponsor intake

Every sponsor brief must contain:

- product truth;
- actual host experience, if any;
- required factual claims;
- prohibited claims;
- legal language;
- one clear call to action;
- visual permissions;
- tone boundaries;
- approval deadline.

## Formats

### Product on Trial
Ari cross-examines the product; the co-host constructs an absurdly overqualified defense. The final answer states the actual utility plainly.

### Worst Possible Spokesperson
One host deliberately embodies the person least qualified to sell the product; the other corrects the failure.

### Impossible Demonstration
A miniature visual scenario exaggerates the problem the product addresses, followed by a factual landing.

### The Overbuilt Explanation
The co-host creates an elaborate intellectual theory of the product. Ari reduces it to the simple reason someone would use it.

### Thirty-Second Film
A self-contained micro-scene, separately shot and inserted as a clearly labeled commercial break.

### Recurring Sponsored Mechanic
A sponsor funds an existing repeatable segment. Editorial control, disclosure, and guest consent remain explicit.

## Production rules

- label sponsorship clearly;
- do not invent personal use or results;
- do not force guests into ads without prior consent;
- record premium ads separately from the interview;
- batch-shoot multiple variations;
- preserve one clean, direct version when a sponsor needs low-risk copy;
- begin with house ads for the studio network, newsletter, or other owned projects to prove the format before pitching it.
